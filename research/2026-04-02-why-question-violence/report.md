# The Violence of "Why"

Every why-question is a small act of world-building. To ask "Why did X happen?" is not merely to request information — it is to assert, before any answer arrives, that X did happen, that X has an explanation, and that the explanation is of a particular kind. These assertions are not made openly. They are carried as presuppositions: silent commitments that enter the conversational record automatically, without negotiation, and that structure everything that follows. When those presuppositions are false or contested, the why-question does not merely fail to find truth — it actively forecloses it. This is what it means for a question to be violent: not that it offends, but that it destroys alternatives before they can be considered.

The single most important finding of this research is that the violence of why-questions can be located with philosophical precision. It lives in what Bas van Fraassen calls the *contrast class* — the implicit "rather than" embedded in every why-question. "Why do Black Americans score lower on IQ tests?" and "Why does this measuring instrument produce different score distributions across racial groups?" look like two ways of asking the same thing. They are not. They are formally distinct questions with different correct answers in different explanatory spaces. The choice between them is made before any evidence is collected, and that choice — not the data, not the analysis, not the conclusion — is where the distortion originates.

## The formal architecture of why-questions

Aristotle drew the foundational distinction. In *Posterior Analytics* II.1 (89b24–30), he identifies four things we seek in inquiry: whether something is the case, what it is, *that* it is the case (*to hoti*), and *why* it is the case (*to dioti*). The ordering is explicit and prescriptive: "When we know the fact we seek the reason why." First establish *that*; only then ask *why*. The planetary example in I.13 makes the logic concrete: demonstrating that planets are near because they don't twinkle gives you only the fact; demonstrating that they don't twinkle because they are near gives you the reason. The direction of inquiry matters — reversing it produces knowledge of a lesser kind, or none at all. [1]

Twenty-four centuries later, van Fraassen gave this principle a formal structure. In *The Scientific Image* (1980), he argues that every why-question is individuated not by its surface grammar but by a triple: Q = <P_k, X, R>. P_k is the *topic* — what happened, presupposed true. X is the *contrast class* — the set of alternatives against which the topic is explained ("why P rather than the other members of X?"). R is the *relevance relation* — the type of factor that counts as an answer (causal, intentional, functional, nomological). [2]

This triple carries three presuppositions. First, the topic is true. Second, only the topic (not the alternatives in the contrast class) is true. Third — and this is unique to why-questions — at least one proposition bearing relation R to the topic actually exists. There *is* an explanation of the right kind. No other standard question type (who, what, when, where, how, whether) carries this third presupposition. A "how" question presupposes only that some process occurred; it does not presuppose that the process is *explicable* in a particular mode. [3] A "whether" question presupposes nothing about the answer at all. The why-question alone asserts, before the conversation begins, that the world is structured in a way that makes a particular kind of explanation available.

Sylvain Bromberger's concept of the *p-predicament* illuminates what this does to the person who accepts the question. Someone in a p-predicament knows that P is true, knows what form a correct answer would take, believes a correct answer exists — and cannot produce one. This is not mere ignorance; it is *structured* ignorance, engineered by the question's presuppositions. [4] The questioner who asks "Why does stress cause ulcers?" places the respondent in a p-predicament that is organized around a specific causal architecture — neuroendocrine, psychosomatic. To answer at all is to work within that architecture. To challenge the architecture is to refuse the question, which is a different and costlier move.

The stickiness of these presuppositions is not merely rhetorical. Satoshi Tomioka (2009) shows that in Japanese and Korean, why-questions exhibit distinctive *intervention effects* — their presuppositions resist the quantificational operations that other wh-words tolerate. [5] The presupposition of "why" is grammatically encoded in a way that makes it harder to subordinate or relativize than the presuppositions of "what" or "who." This is not a feature of English alone; it appears to be a structural property of the question type across languages. Meanwhile, Fabrizio Macagno (2023) demonstrates from argumentation theory that presuppositions function as *dark side commitments* — attributed to a collective voice rather than an individual speaker, reversing the burden of proof so that the hearer must actively challenge what was never explicitly asserted. [6]

## Where the violence lives: the contrast class

Van Fraassen's contrast class is the key to precision. Consider his example of "Why did Adam eat the apple?" Depending on which word carries the stress — *Adam*, *eat*, or *apple* — the question splits into three formally distinct inquiries, each with a different contrast class (why Adam rather than Eve? why eat rather than discard? why an apple rather than a pear?) and each admitting a different correct answer. [2] The surface sentence is one; the questions are many. And the choice of contrast class is not made by evidence. It is made by whoever frames the question.

Peter Lipton refined this into the *fact/foil* structure: every contrastive why-question asks "Why P rather than Q?", where Q is the foil. The relevant explanation is whatever causally differentiates fact from foil. His paresis example makes this vivid: "Why did Smith rather than Jones develop paresis?" gets a different answer (Smith had untreated syphilis) than "Why did Smith rather than Doe develop paresis?" when Doe also had syphilis (here syphilis is common to both, so the differentiating cause must be something else). Same fact, different foil, different explanation. [7] The foil pre-selects the causal space. This is epistemically productive when the foil is well-chosen — Lipton shows how Semmelweis's breakthrough came from choosing the right contrastive question (why Ward 1 rather than Ward 2?). But it is epistemically destructive when the foil is wrong, because the entire search for causes is misdirected before it begins.

Alan Garfinkel, in *Forms of Explanation* (1981), saw the political implications. His Jesse James example: "Why did Jesse James rob banks?" answered "because that's where the money is" becomes a different question when reframed as "Why did Jesse James rob banks rather than work for a living?" — which opens up post-Civil War economic structures, opportunity, desperation. [8] Garfinkel argued that social theory systematically picks individualistic contrast classes when structural ones would be more appropriate. Sally Haslanger (2016) develops the same insight: an individualistic explanation of student grades (what did this student do differently?) and a structural explanation (what does this grading system produce?) differ precisely in their contrast classes, and the individualistic one is treated as default. [9] Neither Garfinkel nor Haslanger uses van Fraassen's formal vocabulary, and neither has been widely absorbed — Garfinkel's book has only about 90 citations. The connection between the formal apparatus of contrastive explanation and the critique of ideological question-framing has not, to my knowledge, been explicitly made. It should be.

Here is what the synthesis looks like, applied to a concrete case. Ned Block's 1995 essay "How Heritability Misleads about Race" is, when reconstructed in van Fraassen's framework, a devastating contrast-class critique of *The Bell Curve*. [10]

Herrnstein and Murray's implicit why-question:
- **Topic**: Black Americans score lower on IQ tests than white Americans
- **Contrast class**: {score lower, no gap, reversed gap} — with racial group as the unit of variation
- **Relevance relation**: genetic/biological causation, indexed to within-group heritability data

This triple commits the researcher, before any data is examined, to treating racial group membership as the natural unit of contrast and heritability statistics as the appropriate type of evidence. Block shows that both commitments are unjustified. Within-group heritability licenses no inference about between-group differences — his corn-seed example (same seeds, different soils, 100% within-group heritability, 100% between-group environmental causation) demolishes the relevance relation. And the contrast class naturalizes race as the unit of variation, obscuring alternatives.

The alternative why-question:
- **Topic**: This measuring instrument produces different score distributions across racial groups
- **Contrast class**: {differential distributions, uniform distributions} — with the instrument as the unit of variation
- **Relevance relation**: construct validity, measurement properties, testing conditions

This is not a rephrasing of Herrnstein and Murray's question. It is a *formally different question* that opens a different explanatory space — one where stereotype threat, test design, standardization samples, and the Flynn Effect are visible as candidate answers. These explanations are invisible from within the first question's contrast class, not because they have been refuted but because the question was never structured to make them relevant.

The same mechanism operates in the framing of poverty. Oscar Lewis's question "Why are the poor poor?" takes as its contrast class {poor, not poor}, with individual poor people as the unit. The relevance relation is behavioral and cultural — what do poor people do that keeps them poor? Harvey and Reed (1996) and Valentine (1969) document how this framing shaped decades of U.S. social policy, directing interventions at individual behavior rather than economic structure. [11] The alternative contrast class — "Why does this economic system produce persistent poverty rather than distributing resources more evenly?" — locates the contrast at the level of systems, making wage structures, housing markets, and credit access visible. Lewis's contrast class made these structurally invisible.

The violence, then, is not in the surface form of the question, not in the conclusions drawn, not in individual researcher bias. It is in the selection of the triple <P_k, X, R> — specifically in the choice of contrast class and relevance relation — which pre-selects the explanatory space before evidence collection begins.

## How presuppositions become invisible: the accommodation mechanism

A loaded contrast class would be harmless if it could be easily noticed and challenged. It cannot. The mechanism that renders it invisible runs through four stages, drawing on independent literatures that have not previously been connected.

**Stage 1: Accommodation.** David Lewis's "Scorekeeping in a Language Game" (1979) establishes the basic mechanism. Conversations have a *score* — a set of shared presuppositions that determine what moves are permissible. When someone says something that requires a presupposition not yet in the score, the presupposition "springs into existence" automatically. [12] The hearer does not evaluate it, debate it, or consent to it. The conversational score adjusts to make the utterance acceptable. Lewis notes that explicit rejection is possible — what Kai von Fintel calls the "Hey, wait a minute" test — but rejection requires active intervention against the default. The default is accommodation.

**Stage 2: Common ground.** Robert Stalnaker (2002) formalizes the space in which accommodation operates. The *common ground* is the body of propositions mutually accepted by discourse participants, represented as a *context set* of possible worlds. [13] Accommodation expands the context set to include worlds consistent with the new presupposition; subsequent assertions then narrow it. The crucial feature: Stalnaker notes that as audiences scale up and become anonymous, the interactive feedback that keeps accommodation in check — someone objecting — is curtailed. Common ground becomes thinner and more attenuated. In institutional discourse, what is "shared" is often what no one has bothered to challenge.

**Stage 3: QUD governance.** The Questions Under Discussion framework (Roberts 2012; Beaver, Roberts, Simons, and Tonhauser 2017) provides the insulation mechanism. [14] In any discourse, a governing *Question Under Discussion* determines what counts as a relevant contribution. Only utterances that constitute (partial) answers to the QUD are relevant; everything else is off-topic. The presuppositions of the governing QUD are *not-at-issue* — they are background conditions for the question to be well-formed. They cannot be challenged from within the discourse the QUD structures; challenging them means proposing a different question, which is changing the subject. When a why-question becomes the QUD of a conversation — or, by extension, of a research program — its presuppositions are structurally off-topic. They are the frame, not the picture. You cannot question the frame from inside the picture.

**Stage 4: Background assumptions.** Helen Longino's *Science as Social Knowledge* (1990) provides the epistemology-of-science counterpart. Her central claim: data is evidence for a hypothesis only relative to *background assumptions* that connect the two. [15] These assumptions are not tested alongside the hypothesis; they are what makes testing possible. They determine which data is relevant, which hypotheses are testable, which results count as confirmation. They are invisible from within normal scientific practice — you cannot simultaneously use an assumption to evaluate evidence and question the assumption. Longino argues that background assumptions can be exposed and criticized, but only through *trans-disciplinary* critique, from outside the practices that take them for granted.

Longino does not cite Lewis. She works in philosophy of science, not philosophy of language. But the structural parallel is exact: Lewis's accommodated presuppositions shape what moves are permissible in conversation; Longino's background assumptions shape what counts as evidence in science. Both are incorporated automatically, both are invisible from within the practice they structure, and both require a meta-level intervention to challenge. The bridge between them — that accommodated presuppositions, when they structure a research program's governing QUD, *become* background assumptions — is, as far as I can determine, not made explicitly in the existing literature. It is this essay's proposed synthesis.

The chain is not merely speculative. Recent work in what Jessica Keiser (2021) calls "QUD-shifting as epistemic injustice" and Fergal Mallory (2025) calls "inquisitive injustice" independently applies QUD theory to questions of power and harm. [16, 17] Neither engages with Longino or Teo, but both confirm that controlling the Question Under Discussion — controlling what is at-issue and what is off-topic — is a fundamental form of social power.

Thomas Teo's concept of *epistemological violence* (2008, 2010) names the harm that sits at the end of this chain. [18] When empirical data underdetermines interpretation — as it always does — and researchers consistently choose the interpretation that constructs the Other as deficient, they commit epistemological violence: harm inflicted through the act of knowledge production itself. Teo works in a hermeneutic tradition and does not cite philosophy of language. His account identifies the harm (inferiorizing interpretation) and its occasion (underdetermination) but does not trace back to the mechanism that makes the harmful interpretation the "natural" one. The accommodation chain provides exactly this prior mechanism. By the time a researcher reaches the interpretive choice Teo describes, the governing QUD has already pre-loaded the common ground with the deficit framing. The harmful interpretation is not freely chosen from a level field of alternatives; it is the one that fits the presuppositions already accommodated.

The weakest link in this chain is the scaling problem: Lewis's accommodation is a theory of conversational dynamics, not disciplinary ones. The claim that a research question functions as a QUD for an entire field requires treating disciplinary discourse as a kind of extended conversation. This is not unreasonable — grant proposal templates, textbook introductions, review article framings, and conference calls for papers are all speech acts that iteratively constitute and reinforce a disciplinary common ground — but it is an analogical extension, not a direct application. It should be taken as such.

## The mechanism is general: lessons from biomedicine

The cases discussed so far — race and IQ, the culture of poverty, gender differences in neuroscience — are all politically charged. This invites the objection that the problem is ideological bias, not the structure of why-questions as such. The stress-ulcer case from biomedicine refutes this objection.

For roughly fifty years, from the 1930s through the 1980s, the dominant research question about peptic ulcers was: "Why does stress cause ulcers?" A 1952 review in *JAMA* stated flatly: "That psychic factors play a prominent role in the causation of ulcer is doubted by no one." [19] The question presupposed that stress was the primary cause. Its contrast class was organized around the psychosomatic register — stressed versus unstressed, emotionally repressed versus well-adjusted. Its relevance relation admitted only neuroendocrine mechanisms: vagal stimulation, acid hypersecretion, the psychoanalytic theory of "oral dependency" elaborated by Franz Alexander at the University of Chicago on the basis of nine cases. [20]

The presupposition was false. In 1982, Barry Marshall and Robin Warren discovered that *Helicobacter pylori* causes more than 90% of duodenal ulcers and up to 80% of gastric ulcers. The 2005 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine confirmed this as the scientific verdict. [21]

The most telling feature of this case is not the discovery itself but the structure of the resistance that preceded it. Multiple researchers had observed spiral bacteria in stomach biopsies before Marshall and Warren. These observations were recoded as "contaminants" or "harmless commensals" — not because the evidence was evaluated and found wanting, but because the dominant why-question's contrast class rendered infectious etiology categorically irrelevant. As Marshall wrote: "my results were disputed and disbelieved, not on the basis of science but because they simply could not be true." [22] The answer existed in the data; the question's structure prevented it from being seen.

Reconstructed in van Fraassen's framework:
- **Topic**: Peptic ulcers occur and recur in certain individuals
- **Contrast class**: Why do they occur *in stressed, emotionally susceptible individuals rather than in well-adjusted ones*?
- **Relevance relation**: Psychosomatic and acid-secretion mechanisms only

This triple made bacterial etiology not merely implausible but invisible — the wrong *kind* of answer. An infectious cause does not differentiate stressed from unstressed patients; it differentiates infected from uninfected ones. But "infected versus uninfected" was not in the contrast class. It was not a candidate foil. The question was structured so that the correct answer could not appear as a relevant answer.

Marshall himself generalized the epistemological lesson: "Everything that's supposedly caused by stress, I tell people there's a Nobel Prize there if you find out the real cause." [22] Stress, he argued, is a placeholder for ignorance — a presupposition that fills the contrast class whenever no real cause has been found. This is the essay's core argument, made from inside the case itself by the person who broke the presupposition.

The pattern recurs in physics and chemistry, though with less granular documentation. The phlogiston theory asked "Why does combustion release phlogiston?" — presupposing phlogiston's existence. Joseph Priestley, who discovered oxygen, never abandoned the phlogiston framework; he called his discovery "dephlogisticated air" and accommodated it within the old question's contrast class. [23] The luminiferous aether theory asked "Why does light propagate through the aether at different speeds depending on direction?" — presupposing the aether. Even Michelson and Morley's null result was initially accommodated via Lorentz contraction hypotheses rather than taken as evidence that the presupposition was false. [24] In each case, the why-question absorbed anomalous evidence by recoding it, rather than allowing it to destabilize the presupposition. The accommodation mechanism operates in natural science as surely as in social science.

## The social force of "why"

The formal analysis establishes that why-questions carry distinctive presuppositions and that these presuppositions can distort inquiry. But the user's original intuition was about *violence* — a word that points beyond logic to the social dimensions of questioning. The pragmatics literature confirms that this dimension is real.

Galina Bolden and Jeffrey Robinson (2011) show through conversation analysis that why-interrogatives are "Janus-faced": they simultaneously request information (the questioner doesn't know why) and convey that the prior event *requires justification* (the questioner knows *that* something happened, and it was accountable). [25] "Why did you do that?" is not informationally neutral — it encodes that the action demands an account. Irene Koshik (2003) goes further: wh-questions, including "why," can function not as information requests at all but as pure *challenges* — the questioner is conveying that no adequate account exists, disguised in interrogative form. [26]

A striking finding from Susanne Egbert and Monika Voge (2008): in German, two near-synonyms for "why" — *warum* and *wieso* — split the function cleanly. *Wieso* is affiliative (a genuine question); *warum* is complaint-implicative (a challenge). In business meetings, complaint-implicative *warum* is correlated with hierarchical status: superiors use it, subordinates do not. [27] The right to demand an account — the right to ask "why" in its challenging mode — is a power prerogative. English "why" is ambiguous between these functions, which gives the questioner deniability ("I was just asking!") while the recipient still receives the challenging force.

This social structure maps onto the research-methodology cases. In research, the scientist who asks "Why does group X have property Y?" occupies a position of epistemic authority analogous to the superior using *warum* in a meeting. The subjects of the question — group X — bear the burden of the presupposition (that they have property Y in a way that requires explanation) without having consented to the question's framing. Miranda Fricker's concept of *epistemic injustice* is relevant here: the taxonomic why-question directed at a marginalized group simultaneously imposes testimonial injustice (deflating the group's credibility as interpreters of their own experience) and hermeneutical injustice (forcing explanation within a conceptual framework the group did not choose and may not recognize). [28] This specific connection — between the formal presuppositional structure of why-questions and Fricker's framework — has, as far as I can tell, not been made explicitly in the literature.

The clinical evidence is equally telling. Motivational interviewing, one of the most empirically supported therapeutic approaches, explicitly prohibits "why" questions. The prohibition is grounded in the finding that "why" creates defensiveness and threatens autonomy — it demands justification rather than inviting reflection. [29] The psychotherapy literature arrived at the same conclusion as the philosophy of language, through a completely different route: "why" is not a neutral request for information. It is an act that restructures the conversational space around accountability.

## Two failure modes

It is important to distinguish two structurally different ways in which premature why-questioning causes harm.

The first is **premature why-questioning about groups**: "Why do Black Americans score lower?", "Why are the poor poor?", "Why do women underperform in STEM?" Here the violence lies in *naturalizing a contested social category as the unit of contrast*. The why-question treats the group as a natural kind whose properties require explanation, when the grouping itself — and the measurement of the alleged property — is what should be interrogated. This failure mode produces what Teo calls epistemological violence: the interpretive construction of the Other as deficient.

The second is **premature why-questioning about mechanisms**: "Why does ego depletion occur?", "Why does stress cause ulcers?", "Why does light propagate through the aether?" Here the violence is not social but epistemic — it lies in *presupposing a causal structure that forecloses the correct answer*. The mechanism being explained may not exist (ego depletion at the originally claimed effect size; phlogiston), may be wrongly identified (stress as primary cause of ulcers), or may rest on a false ontological commitment (the aether). Hundreds of papers on ego depletion asked "why does willpower deplete?" before Hagger et al.'s 2016 multi-lab replication found an effect size of d = 0.04 — functionally zero. [30] The explanatory superstructure was built on a phenomenon that, at the scale presupposed, was not there.

Both are contrast-class errors, but they operate differently. The first naturalizes a social category; the second naturalizes a causal architecture. The first does violence to persons; the second does violence to inquiry. The stress-ulcer case shows that the second can be just as durable and just as damaging as the first — and that it operates identically at the level of presuppositional structure.

## What this argument does not claim

It does not claim that all why-questions are violent, that causal inquiry is inherently harmful, or that "why" should never be asked. Toyota's "5 Whys" in lean manufacturing, Socratic questioning in philosophy, and productive scientific why-questions (Semmelweis's "Why Ward 1 rather than Ward 2?") all demonstrate that why-questions can be epistemically powerful. The violence is in a specific configuration: a why-question whose contrast class naturalizes a contested presupposition, whose accommodation into the common ground renders that presupposition invisible, and whose governance as a QUD insulates it from challenge. Not every why-question has this configuration. But when a why-question does, its violence is structural — it operates below the level of individual intention and cannot be corrected simply by being more careful with conclusions.

The practical implication is Aristotelian: before asking *why*, establish *that*. Before asking why groups differ, establish that the measured difference is robust, that the grouping is the right unit of analysis, and that the measurement instrument is not itself the source of variation. Before asking why a mechanism operates, establish that it does. This is not a prohibition on causal inquiry. It is an insistence on sequence: the *hoti* before the *dioti*, the "whether" before the "why." Every why-question that skips this step is, in a precise and non-metaphorical sense, an act of premature closure — a foreclosing of alternatives that may include the truth.

## Open questions

1. **Can the contrast-class critique be formalized as a diagnostic?** The argument in this essay is that certain contrast classes are ideologically loaded. But the formal apparatus does not itself distinguish good contrast classes from bad ones — that judgment requires normative premises from outside van Fraassen's framework. Is there a principled way to identify when a contrast class naturalizes a contested presupposition, or is this necessarily a substantive philosophical judgment case by case?

2. **How does accommodation scale to institutions, precisely?** The Lewis-to-Longino bridge proposed here treats disciplinary common ground as analogous to conversational common ground. Stalnaker himself flags the scaling problem. A rigorous account of how research questions function as institutional QUDs — through grant templates, editorial scope statements, textbook framings, hiring criteria — would strengthen the chain considerably.

3. **What is the relationship between Teo's hermeneutic framework and the formal-pragmatic chain?** This essay treats them as complementary — the accommodation chain explains a prior mechanism that Teo's account leaves untheorized. But Teo works in a phenomenological-hermeneutic tradition that may resist translation into formal pragmatics. Whether these frameworks are genuinely compatible or merely analogous is an open philosophical question.

4. **Does the "inquisitive injustice" literature (Keiser, Mallory) extend to research methodology?** Both papers focus on conversational and political discourse. Applying QUD-based injustice analysis to scientific research programs — where the QUD governs not one conversation but decades of inquiry — would be a significant extension worth pursuing.
