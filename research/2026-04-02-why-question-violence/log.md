# Research Log — The Violence of "Why" Questions

## Scoping

**User's intuition**: Asking "why" about a phenomenon can be epistemically violent
because it skips the step of confirming that the phenomenon actually exists. For
an observer, the "why" question presupposes what should first be established.

This is richer than it first appears. It touches philosophy of language
(presupposition), philosophy of science (the order of inquiry), rhetoric
(loaded questions), and critical methodology (how premature causal inquiry
distorts fields). The "violence" framing suggests something stronger than a
mere logical error — it implies that the why-question *forecloses* alternative
descriptions of reality.

### Narrowed questions

**Q1. What is the formal structure of presupposition in "why" questions, and how
does it differ from presupposition in other question types?**

"Why did the Roman Empire fall?" presupposes that the Roman Empire fell (in a
specific sense). "Why are women worse at math?" presupposes a performance gap.
Philosophy of language (Strawson, Stalnaker, van Fraassen) has a precise
apparatus for analyzing presupposition. How does the presuppositional load of
"why" questions compare to "how," "what," and "whether" questions? Is the
presupposition in "why" distinctively hard to challenge?

**Q2. What is the Aristotelian distinction between establishing "that" (to hoti)
and explaining "why" (to dioti), and how has it shaped the methodology of
science?**

Aristotle's *Posterior Analytics* insists that you must first establish *that*
something is the case before asking *why*. This ordering principle recurs in
philosophy of science (Hempel, van Fraassen, Salmon). How rigorously has this
principle been theorized? Where has science gone wrong by violating it?

**Q3. In what concrete cases has premature "why"-asking distorted inquiry — in
science, social policy, or public discourse?**

The user's intuition that "why" can be violent suggests real-world stakes. Where
has jumping to "why" caused measurable harm? Candidates: race science asking
"why do races differ in IQ?" before establishing that the measured gaps are
stable, heritable, and not artifacts; medicine asking "why do women report more
pain?" before establishing that reporting differences aren't measurement
artifacts; economics asking "why are the poor poor?" in ways that presuppose
individual-level causes.

**Q4. How does the pragmatics of "why" function as a rhetorical and social act —
what does asking "why" *do* to the conversational space?**

Beyond logic, there's a performative dimension. Asking "why did you do that?"
in everyday life carries accusation. In discourse, "why does group X have
property Y?" can naturalize a contested claim. How have pragmatics, conversation
analysis, and critical discourse analysis theorized the social force of "why"?

### Rationale for these four

Q1 gives us the formal machinery. Q2 gives us the deep philosophical lineage.
Q3 gives us the empirical evidence that this matters. Q4 captures the
specifically *social* and *violent* dimension the user intuited. Together they
should let us write something that moves from formal structure through
methodological principle to real-world consequence.

## Survey phase — complete

All four surveys returned strong material. Key findings per thread:

- **Presupposition (Q1)**: Van Fraassen's tripartite structure identified — the
  *third* presupposition (that an explanation of the right type exists) is unique
  to why-questions. Bromberger's p-predicament, Tomioka's grammatical stickiness,
  Macagno's burden-reversal all confirmed. No existing work on "loaded why-questions"
  as a specific category.
- **Aristotle (Q2)**: Posterior Analytics II.1 is precise — "when we know the fact
  we seek the reason why." Gelman-Imbens (2013) independently rediscovers the
  ordering principle for causal inference. Gap: no one explicitly argues premature
  why-questioning is a *systematic* methodological error.
- **Cases (Q3)**: Block (1996) on race/IQ is the sharpest logical anatomy. Fine and
  Fausto-Sterling on gender. Ego depletion as replication-crisis case. Culture of
  poverty as policy case. Teo's "epistemological violence" names the harm directly.
- **Pragmatics (Q4)**: Bolden & Robinson's "Janus-faced" why. German warum/wieso
  split shows accusatory function is grammaticalized. MI's clinical prohibition.
  Fricker's framework maps but the connection is unmade in the literature.
