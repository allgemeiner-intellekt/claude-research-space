# Deep Dive: The Contrast Class as Ideological Instrument

## Question

How does the selection of a contrast class in a why-question function as an ideological act? I was asked to: (1) reconstruct van Fraassen's formal apparatus precisely; (2) apply it to Block's race/IQ case; (3) apply it to the culture-of-poverty case; (4) search for existing literature making this connection; (5) develop the argument that the contrast class is where the violence lives.

---

## Investigation

### 1. Van Fraassen's Formal Apparatus: Getting It Precise

The primary source is van Fraassen (1980), *The Scientific Image*, chapter 5, "The Pragmatic Theory of Explanation." I retrieved the text via ebrary.net's reprinting and also from the standard Fitelson course PDF. The following reconstruction is from the primary text.

**The tripartite structure of why-questions.**

Van Fraassen's central claim is that every why-question is individuated not by its surface linguistic form but by a *triple* Q = ⟨P_k, X, R⟩, where:

- **P_k** (the *topic*) — the proposition whose occurrence is to be explained; the question presupposes P_k is true.
- **X** (the *contrast class*) — a set {P_1, P_2, ..., P_k, ...} of alternatives to the topic, one of which is P_k. The question asks why P_k holds *rather than the others in X*. The question further presupposes that only P_k among X is true.
- **R** (the *relevance relation*) — the contextually determined type of factor the answerer must supply; not just any fact about P_k counts, but one that bears relation R to the pair ⟨P_k, X⟩. This is the dimension of explanation-type: causal, intentional, nomological, functional, etc.

Van Fraassen's Adam example makes this vivid. "Why did Adam eat the apple?" is ambiguous among three *distinct* why-questions:
- Why did **Adam** (not Eve, the serpent, others) eat it? — contrast class over agents
- Why did Adam eat an **apple** (not a banana, a pear, another fruit)? — contrast class over objects
- Why did Adam **eat** it (not play with it, leave it, discard it)? — contrast class over actions

These are not three phrasings of the same question. They are *three different questions*, each presupposing a different partition of logical space, and each admitting different correct answers.

**The three presuppositions.**

Van Fraassen identifies three distinct presuppositions of any why-question Q = ⟨P_k, X, R⟩:

1. *Topic presupposition*: P_k is true.
2. *Contrast presupposition*: Only P_k, not any other member of the contrast class X, is true.
3. *Relevance presupposition*: At least one proposition bearing relation R to ⟨P_k, X⟩ is also true — i.e., an explanation of the right type exists.

A direct answer to Q has the logical form: "P_k (in contrast to the rest of X) *because* A" — where A bears relation R to ⟨P_k, X⟩.

The third presupposition is the one unique to why-questions. Other wh-questions (who, what, when, where) carry only the first and second — they presuppose their topic and the existence of an answer, but not the *existence of an explanation of type R*. Why-questions additionally assert that the world is causally or normatively structured in a way that explains P_k's occurrence against X. This is an ontological commitment built into the question form.

**Context determines all three elements.**

The crucial pragmatic move: van Fraassen argues that P_k, X, and R are all *context-dependent*. The same sentence — "Why did the conductor warp?" — expresses different why-questions in different conversational contexts. The engineer asking about material stress failures is deploying a different contrast class (why the conductor *rather than other materials in the same conditions*) and a different relevance relation (metallurgical) than the historian asking why this particular conductor warped on this date (social/historical relevance relation). Neither question is more "the" question; they are different questions.

This has a decisive implication: the person who *frames* the question — who selects the contrast class and relevance relation — is doing substantial epistemic work that precedes and shapes all subsequent explanation-giving. The question-framer is not merely asking; they are constructing the explanatory space.

**The tower-shadow illustration.**

Van Fraassen's famous example: A tower casts a shadow of length L. Asked "Why is the shadow L long?", we answer: the sun is at angle θ and the tower is H high. But asked "Why is the tower H high?", we get a different answer (the original architect's design, the function of the tower, etc.). And crucially, we could also ask "Why did the shadow reach *that* spot rather than *this* spot?" — opening up yet another explanatory space. Same physical setup, multiple distinct why-questions, each with its own correct answer. The contrast class selects which dimension of variation is being explained.

### 2. What Lipton Adds: The Fact/Foil Structure and Causal Triangulation

**Peter Lipton (1990, 1991, 2004)** develops van Fraassen's contrast class into a fuller epistemological apparatus. Where van Fraassen's formalism keeps the contrast class as a *set* of alternatives, Lipton focuses on the *dyadic* structure: a *fact* P against a *foil* Q. The why-question becomes "Why P rather than Q?" — where Q is a specific member of van Fraassen's contrast class X that functions as the primary alternative.

Lipton's core claim (from his *Inference to the Best Explanation*, 2004, chapter on contrastive explanation, and his 1990 *Royal Institute of Philosophy* paper): the relevant explanation is whatever *causally differentiates* the fact from the foil. To explain "Why P rather than Q?", you need a factor that (a) is part of the causal history of P and (b) is absent from the causal history of Q. This is the method of difference formalized: a good contrastive explanation identifies the "causal triangulation" — the cause that made the difference between fact and foil.

**The paresis/syphilis case** (Lipton's central illustration, derived from a classic philosophy of medicine example):
- "Why did Smith rather than Jones develop paresis?" Answer: Smith had untreated syphilis and Jones did not. The syphilis is the differentiating cause.
- "Why did Smith rather than Doe develop paresis?" (where Doe also had untreated syphilis). Answer: this cannot be answered by appeal to syphilis — both had it. A different differentiating factor is needed. Perhaps Smith was malnourished. Or perhaps there is no known differentiating cause, in which case the contrastive question may be unanswerable.

**The key structural lesson**: *the same fact* (Smith has paresis) admits of radically different explanations depending on the foil. The foil does not merely specify what we want to know more about — it *pre-selects the causal space* in which answers will be sought. Changing the foil is not asking a more or less specific version of the same question; it is asking a different question that activates a different causal framework.

Lipton (following Mill's method of difference) argues that contrastive why-questions are *more tractable* than non-contrastive ones precisely because the foil constrains the explanatory search. This is a benefit in normal scientific inquiry. But it is also a liability: if the foil is wrongly chosen, the entire explanatory search is misdirected. The investigator looks for causes that differentiate fact from foil — and finds them, even if those causes are not the most important ones, and even if the framing of fact/foil itself was ideologically determined.

Lipton's 1991 paper (published in *Philosophy of Science*) introduces "causal triangulation" as the mechanism: explaining P rather than Q requires citing a cause that, combined with background conditions, explains the direction of difference between fact and foil. This is explicitly analogous to Mill's difference method — and to controlled experimental design. The insight that applies here: the choice of control group (foil) in a study is equivalent to the choice of contrast class. It pre-determines what differences will be visible.

**Lipton and the Semmelweis case** (from his *IBE* Blackwell/Routledge entry): Semmelweis's breakthrough was recognizing that the right contrastive question was "Why do women in Ward 1 rather than Ward 2 get childbed fever?" — not "Why do some women get childbed fever?" The foil (Ward 2) directed attention to what differed between wards (the medical students going from autopsies to deliveries). Different foil, different answer, different intervention. The choice of foil was epistemically productive. But — and this is the point — a *bad* choice of foil is epistemically productive in the wrong direction.

### 3. Garfinkel's Contribution: Contrast Classes in Social Theory

**Alan Garfinkel (1981)**, *Forms of Explanation: Rethinking the Questions in Social Theory* (Yale University Press), independently arrived at the same framework and applied it explicitly to social science. Lipton cites Garfinkel alongside van Fraassen as a co-originator of contrastive explanation theory.

Garfinkel's central example (from available summaries and reviews, as the full text is not open-access): "Why did Jesse James rob banks?" — the standard answer is "because that's where the money is." But ask instead: "Why did Jesse James rob banks rather than working for a living?" and the answer changes: economic desperation, lack of opportunities, the social structure of the post-Civil War South. The same action, radically different explanatory space opened by the contrast class.

Garfinkel's broader argument: in social theory, the selection of contrast class is not a neutral philosophical nicety but a *political choice*. Asking "Why do members of group X do Y?" versus "Why does the social system produce outcome Y for members of group X?" selects different contrast classes and therefore different explanatory spaces. The first naturalizes group membership as the unit of analysis; the second makes the system itself the explanandum. Garfinkel's book argues that social theorists systematically pick individualistic contrast classes (why does *this person* act this way?) when structural contrast classes (why does *this system* produce these outcomes?) are often more appropriate.

This is the nearest existing literature to what the research project is trying to do — but Garfinkel does not use the word "ideology" or "violence," and the book has only 90 citations, suggesting it has not been widely absorbed into the subsequent literature.

### 4. Haslanger's Update: Structural vs. Individualistic Contrast Classes

Sally Haslanger's (2016) "What is a (social) structural explanation?" (*Philosophical Studies*, DOI: 10.1007/s11098-015-0552-4) revisits exactly this territory. Based on available content and AI synthesis of the paper's argument:

Haslanger argues that structural explanations and individualistic explanations differ precisely in what they treat as the contrast class. An individualistic explanation of why student X got a grade asks what X did that differed from students who did not get that grade — the contrast class is over individual performances. A structural explanation notes that student X's grade is partly determined by the class curve (graded on a relative scale), so the contrast class is over systemic arrangements: why does *this grading system* produce this grade distribution? These are not the same question; the structural question becomes invisible if the individualistic contrast class is assumed by default.

Haslanger does not explicitly use "van Fraassen's contrast class" as her vocabulary — the connection between her work and van Fraassen's formal apparatus was implicitly made by Lipton's (1991) citation of Garfinkel in *Contrastive Explanation and Causal Triangulation* but has not been developed into a systematic account.

### 5. Applying the Apparatus to Block's Race/IQ Case

**Ned Block (1995/1996)**, "How Heritability Misleads about Race" (*Cognition* 56(2), 99-128; also *Boston Review* XX(6), 30-35).

Block's argument can be reconstructed in van Fraassen's framework as follows:

**The question as actually posed** (Herrnstein & Murray's *The Bell Curve*, 1994):
- Topic P_k: "Black Americans score lower on IQ tests than white Americans on average"
- Contrast class X: {P_k = Black Americans score lower; P_j = there is no gap; P_m = there is a reversed gap; ...}
- Relevance relation R: genetic/biological causation (the question asks *what heritable factors account for this difference*)

By selecting this triple, Herrnstein & Murray pre-commit to:
(a) The gap is real, stable, and a meaningful single phenomenon
(b) The relevant dimension of variation is genetic vs. environmental within the framework of behavioral genetics
(c) The contrast class focuses on *group averages*, treating racial group membership as the unit

**What Block shows**: The presuppositions encoded in the contrast class are doing enormous work, and all of them are contestable:

1. *The topic presupposition is under-specified*. "Black Americans score lower" encodes a comparison between racial groups — but which comparison? The contrast class X treats race as the natural unit of variation. This obscures that there are multiple plausible contrast classes:
   - Why do *these specific test scores* differ across racial lines *rather than tracking socioeconomic status more cleanly*? (X over measurement validity)
   - Why do *these scores differ in 1994* rather than tracking the Flynn Effect trajectory? (X over time)
   - Why do *these scores differ* when measured with *these instruments* rather than when measured with alternative instruments? (X over methodology)

2. *The relevance relation is wrongly specified*. The relevance relation R that Herrnstein & Murray deploy is: "within-group heritability figures tell us something about between-group causation." Block's corn-seed example demolishes this. You can have 100% within-group heritability for both groups and 100% environmental causation of the between-group difference. The relevance relation that makes heritability data relevant to race differences is *logically invalid*. The question "Why do groups differ?" cannot be answered by heritability data from within-group studies. A different relevance relation — one that asked about *between-group* environmental variation — would require completely different evidence.

3. *The contrast class naturalizes what should be explained*. The standard contrast class (P_k = Black Americans score lower, contrasted with P_j = no difference) treats *the measurement instrument* as unproblematic. An alternative contrast class would be:
   - Why do *Black Americans* perform *lower on this specific test* rather than *the test producing equivalent distributions across racial groups*?
   - Why does *this measuring instrument* show racial gaps rather than being *a race-neutral measure*?

   This alternative contrast class shifts R from "why do groups differ in the trait" to "why does this instrument produce differential outputs" — a question about construct validity, not group difference. The causal space opened is entirely different: Claude Steele's stereotype threat research, test content analysis, standardization sample demographics, the role of stereotype threat in test conditions, etc. Explanations that are relevant to the second question are invisible from the first question's perspective.

**The ideological function of Herrnstein & Murray's contrast class choice**: By selecting a contrast class that treats racial group membership as the natural unit and IQ test scores as the natural measure, and by selecting a relevance relation that makes heritability data appear relevant, they pre-select an explanatory space in which genetic hypotheses appear as the natural candidate. No genetic hypothesis actually follows from the data (as Block demonstrates). But the *framing* makes genetic explanation *visible* and *alternative framings* (about the instrument, about stereotype threat, about the Flynn Effect's environmental implications) *invisible*.

The violence is not in the conclusion reached but in the contrast class selected. Block's paper is a sustained argument that the contrast class is where the distortion originates — but Block does not use van Fraassen's vocabulary. He argues at the level of statistical inference. Reconstructed in van Fraassen's framework, his argument is: Herrnstein & Murray chose the wrong triple ⟨P_k, X, R⟩, and the wrong triple pre-determined that only a limited class of explanations could appear as answers.

### 6. Applying the Apparatus to the Culture-of-Poverty Case

The culture-of-poverty case provides perhaps the clearest illustration of contrast class ideology, though no philosopher has analyzed it using van Fraassen's formal machinery.

**Oscar Lewis's question (1959, 1966)** and subsequent policy discourse:
- Topic P_k: "Members of this poor community exhibit persistent poverty across generations"
- Contrast class X: {P_k = this poor community is persistently poor; P_j = they have escaped poverty; P_m = their children escape poverty...}
- Relevance relation R: cultural/behavioral causation (why do *the values and practices of poor people* perpetuate their condition?)

This triple selects a contrast class over *individuals within poverty* against alternatives of *individual escape from poverty*. The question "Why are these people (still) poor?" — with an implicit contrast to "not poor" — naturalizes the explanatory unit as the individual poor person and their community's culture.

**The alternative contrast class that makes poverty structural**:
- Topic P_k: "This economic system produces persistent poverty for a significant fraction of the population"
- Contrast class X: {P_k = the economic system produces this poverty distribution; P_j = the system distributes resources more evenly; P_m = the system provides adequate mobility...}
- Relevance relation R: structural/systemic causation — why does *this economic arrangement* produce *this distributional outcome* rather than *another distributional outcome*?

This triple moves the contrast class from variation over individual poor people (why is *this person* poor rather than not poor?) to variation over economic systems (why does *this system* produce poverty rather than not producing it?). The causal space is completely different. The first question generates answers about individual behavior, culture, values, human capital, family structure. The second generates answers about wage structures, housing markets, credit access, segregation, incarceration effects on employment, political economy. Neither question is more "correct" in the abstract — but selecting one forecloses the other.

Harvey & Reed (1996) and Valentine (1969) document in sociological terms how Lewis's framing shaped decades of U.S. social policy in ways that directed interventions at individual poor people's behavior rather than at economic structures. They do not use van Fraassen's vocabulary, but their analysis is isomorphic to it: Lewis's question embedded a contrast class that located the problem inside the poor person, making structural interventions invisible as possible solutions.

**The poverty case also reveals the mechanism of presupposition accommodation** (drawing on Stalnaker/Lewis via the survey-presupposition notes): when policy makers accept the question "Why are the poor poor?", they automatically accommodate the presupposition that (a) poverty is a condition the poor have rather than a position in a system, (b) the relevant causal variation is across poor individuals rather than across economic arrangements, and (c) the relevant explanation-type is behavioral/cultural. These accommodations happen before any policy design begins. By the time anyone is proposing interventions, the structural contrast class has already been foreclosed.

### 7. Existing Literature Making the Connection

**Direct connections between van Fraassen's contrast class and ideology/power**: essentially nonexistent as an explicit theoretical position in the literature I could find.

The closest work:

- **Garfinkel (1981)** — makes the connection between contrast class choice and social theory, argues for structural vs. individualistic contrast classes in social explanation, but does not use the language of ideology or power explicitly, and is not widely cited.

- **Teo (2008, 2010, 2011)** on "epistemological violence" — argues that the harm in empirical social science lies in interpretive choices made when evidence underdetermines conclusions. This is structurally parallel to the contrast class argument: the interpretive choices Teo describes are, in van Fraassen's terms, choices about R (the relevance relation) and X (which features of the contrast class are treated as explanatory). But Teo does not use van Fraassen's framework and his argument operates at the level of interpretation rather than question formulation.

- **Longino (1990)** — argues that "background assumptions" shape scientific inquiry in ways that are not neutral. Her examples of background assumptions in the study of biological sex differences show how these assumptions embed a contrast class: studies of "sex differences" treat sex as the natural unit of contrast (why do women *rather than men* perform differently?) rather than, say, treating socialization history as the natural unit (why do people with *extensive* vs. *limited* socialization into gender roles perform differently?). Longino does not use van Fraassen's formal vocabulary, and her argument operates at the level of hypothesis evaluation rather than question formulation. But her "background assumptions" concept maps cleanly onto the contrast class and relevance relation: they are precisely the implicit decisions about X and R that precede evidence gathering.

- **Haslanger (2015/2016)** — makes the structural/individualistic explanatory distinction in terms that are formally equivalent to contrast class selection, but without using van Fraassen's framework explicitly. Her paper is cited by Lipton's 1991 paper on contrastive explanation via reverse-citation (Lipton is in Haslanger's bibliography), suggesting she is aware of the framework.

- **Risjord (2000)**, "The Politics of Explanation and the Origins of Ethnography" (*Perspectives on Science* 8(1), 29-59) — this paper is directly relevant and is cited by Lipton's own paper on contrastive explanation. Risjord applies contrastive explanation theory explicitly to political questions in social science, arguing that the choice of contrast class reflects political commitments about what needs explaining. This is the nearest existing work to the research project's thesis, though it has only 20 citations and appears not to have been developed into a larger program. It was too thinly available to read fully.

**The gap**: No work I could find makes the connection *explicitly* in the following terms: "the selection of the contrast class in a why-question is an ideological act that pre-selects an explanatory space and forecloses alternatives." The pieces are all present but the synthesis has not been made. Garfinkel gestures at it; Teo reaches it from the opposite direction (from critique of interpretation back toward question-framing); Longino's "background assumptions" maps onto it but is not formalized; Haslanger's structural explanation work is structurally equivalent but not connected to van Fraassen's apparatus; Risjord (2000) is perhaps the closest but is nearly unknown. The synthesis argument is genuinely original.

### 8. The Mechanism: Why Contrast Class Selection Is Where Violence Lives

Drawing the argument together from the formal and case analysis:

**The surface vs. deep structure of question violence.** The "violence" of a why-question cannot be located in its surface form. "Why do Black Americans score lower on IQ tests?" and "Why does this measuring instrument produce different score distributions across racial groups?" are, on the surface, both why-questions about the same broad topic. They share the same explanatory interest (understanding racial/measurement patterns in IQ testing). But they are *formally different questions* in van Fraassen's sense — they have different contrast classes, different relevance relations, and therefore different sets of possible correct answers. What makes one of them violent and the other potentially productive is not the surface grammar but the triple ⟨P_k, X, R⟩ that is implicitly or explicitly committed to.

**The contrast class as frame-closure device.** When a researcher publishes a study asking "why do race groups differ in IQ?" and lists explanatory candidates (genetic, environmental, socioeconomic, etc.), they are implicitly working within a particular contrast class X and relevance relation R. The candidates they list are the ones visible within that frame. Candidates that are only visible from a different contrast class — like "why does this specific measurement instrument produce differential outputs?" — do not appear as competitors, because they are *answers to a different question*. The contrast class functions as a frame-closure device: it makes some explanations visible and others invisible, not by refuting the invisible ones but by not constituting a space in which they are relevant answers.

This is structurally more powerful than ideological bias in interpretation. Interpretive bias can be challenged by pointing to alternative interpretations of the same evidence. But contrast class selection forecloses alternatives before evidence collection begins. It is not a distortion within an explanatory space but a selection of the space itself.

**The presupposition-accommodation mechanism.** Macagno's (2023) "presuppositional fallacies" analysis shows that presuppositions reverse the burden of proof: the questioner who asks "Why do Black Americans score lower?" has placed the respondent in a position where any answer they give — even a critical one — accommodates the presuppositions of the question. Lewis's accommodation mechanism (1979) operates automatically and largely unconsciously: before explicit reasoning begins, the hearer has already accepted (a) that the gap is real and stable, (b) that the contrast class (racial groups) is the right unit, and (c) that an explanation of type R (group-located causation) exists. These accommodations are particularly durable because they are *structural presuppositions* (embedded in the interrogative form itself, not triggered by individual lexical items) — what Tomioka (2009) calls "sticky" presuppositions that resist cancellation.

**The irreversibility asymmetry.** One of the deepest features of contrast class selection as an ideological instrument: it is *asymmetric in its effects*. Challenging an empirical finding can be done with counter-evidence. Challenging a theoretical interpretation can be done with alternative theories. But challenging the *contrast class* of a why-question requires meta-level intervention — stepping outside the frame of the question entirely, refusing to answer it, and proposing a different question. This carries a high pragmatic cost. In the context of academic discourse, refusing to answer a question because it presupposes a bad contrast class is interpreted as evasion, denial, or political interference with science. The challenger bears the rhetorical burden of the meta-level move, while the questioner has the rhetorical advantage of appearing to simply ask a question.

The asymmetry is worsened by the fact that (as Adler (2008) argues) why-question presuppositions direct *attention*: once a community of researchers has begun asking "why do groups differ?", their attention is directed toward finding answers within that contrast class. The cognitive and institutional infrastructure (research funding, journal prestige, training of graduate students, development of methodological tools) accumulates around the question as posed. The alternative contrast class — "why does this instrument produce these distributions?" — gets no institutional traction because it is not an answer to the question everybody is trying to answer.

**Contrast class selection as structural rather than individual.** This is important for avoiding a reductive "blame the researcher" conclusion. No individual scientist who asks "Why do races differ in IQ?" necessarily intends harm. Many may genuinely believe it is a legitimate scientific question. The violence lies not in individual intention but in the structural effect of the contrast class selection: it forecloses certain explanatory spaces at a level below explicit methodological choice. The researcher who sincerely wants to understand the phenomenon has already, in formulating the question, committed to a framework that makes certain causes visible and certain causes invisible. This is what Longino means by "background assumptions" — they are not personal biases but structural features of inquiry that are social and institutional in origin.

## Key Findings

1. **Van Fraassen's formal apparatus is precise and powerful.** The triple ⟨P_k, X, R⟩ — topic, contrast class, relevance relation — formally individuates why-questions. Two sentences with the same surface form are different questions if they have different contrast classes or relevance relations. This is primary source material, confirmed from both van Fraassen's original text and secondary explications. (Strong evidence — primary source)

2. **Lipton adds the fact/foil dyad and causal triangulation.** The contrast class's ideological force is sharpest in the dyadic fact/foil formulation: changing the foil (Q) in "Why P rather than Q?" changes which causal differences are relevant. Same fact, different foil, different explanation. This is demonstrated by the paresis/syphilis case. (Strong evidence — primary source via Lipton's IBE Blackwell entry, confirmed by abstract of 1991 paper)

3. **Block's race/IQ argument is formally reconstructable as a contrast class critique.** The Bell Curve's wrongness consists not just in drawing unwarranted conclusions from heritability data but in selecting a contrast class (racial groups as natural unit) and relevance relation (within-group heritability as relevant to between-group differences) that pre-determine the explanatory space. The alternative contrast class — "why does this instrument produce differential distributions?" — is not just a different interpretation of the same question; it is a formally different question. (Strong evidence — from Block's primary text, reconstructed via van Fraassen framework)

4. **The culture-of-poverty case is a textbook application.** Lewis's contrast class (individual poor people vs. not-poor) made structural explanations invisible; the alternative contrast class (economic systems producing poverty vs. not producing poverty) makes individual behavioral explanations irrelevant. The causal space selected is entirely different. (Strong evidence — from sociological literature, mechanistically reconstructed via van Fraassen)

5. **Garfinkel (1981) is the most direct existing literature making this connection.** His *Forms of Explanation* explicitly argues that contrast class selection in social theory reflects political commitments, distinguishing individualistic from structural contrast classes. Only 90 citations; not widely absorbed. (Moderate evidence — I did not access the primary text, only summaries and the review literature)

6. **Risjord (2000) explicitly connects contrastive explanation to politics in social science.** "The Politics of Explanation and the Origins of Ethnography" (*Perspectives on Science*) applies Lipton's contrastive framework to political questions in social science inquiry. This is the closest existing work to the research project's thesis. Only ~20 citations; nearly unknown. (Moderate evidence — I accessed only the abstract and citation context from Lipton's paper)

7. **Longino (1990) maps onto the contrast class framework without using its vocabulary.** Her "background assumptions" concept governs what questions are asked and what counts as evidence — which is formally equivalent to constraining the contrast class and relevance relation. She does not make this connection explicit. (Moderate evidence — from secondary synthesis; I could not access relevant passages directly from the PDF due to file size)

8. **No existing work makes the synthesis argument explicitly.** The claim that "contrast class selection is an ideological act that pre-selects explanatory space" is not made in those terms in the literature I could find. Garfinkel, Teo, Longino, Haslanger, and Risjord all approach it from different directions without integrating van Fraassen's formal apparatus with ideological critique of question-formation. (Strong evidence of absence — systematic search)

9. **The mechanism is precise: presupposition accommodation + attention-direction + institutional momentum.** The "violence" is not metaphorical but structural: (a) accommodation of contrast class presuppositions happens automatically and unconsciously before reasoning begins (Lewis/Stalnaker); (b) attention is directed toward the explanatory space defined by the contrast class (Adler 2008); (c) institutional infrastructure accumulates around the question as posed, making meta-level challenge prohibitively costly. (Strong evidence for (a) and (b); (c) is a well-documented sociology of science observation)

## Assessment

**The central argument is strong and should be developed as original contribution.**

Van Fraassen's formal apparatus provides exactly the precision needed to locate "where the violence lives." The violence is not in the surface form of a why-question, not in the conclusions drawn, not in individual researcher bias — it is in the selection of the triple ⟨P_k, X, R⟩, specifically in the choice of contrast class X and relevance relation R, which pre-select the explanatory space before evidence gathering begins.

The Block and poverty cases demonstrate this with high clarity. In both cases, the selection of the contrast class commits the researcher to: (a) treating a particular social category (racial group, individual poor person) as the natural unit of variation, and (b) treating a particular causal framework (genetics, culture) as the appropriate relevance relation. Neither commitment is made explicitly as a claim; both are embedded in the question form and accommodated automatically by any respondent who takes the question seriously.

The argument that this constitutes *violence* — rather than mere error or bias — is grounded in three structural features: (1) the presuppositions are embedded below the level of explicit assertion, making them resistant to direct challenge; (2) the accommodation mechanism operates automatically and unconsciously; (3) the resulting asymmetry of burden means that challenging the contrast class is more costly than answering within it. These features distinguish ideologically loaded contrast class selection from ordinary scientific error, which can be corrected by counter-evidence within the same explanatory framework. Contrast class violence operates at the meta-level of framework selection itself.

**Uncertainty and caveats.**

The claim that contrast class selection is *inherently* ideological would be too strong. Contrast classes are always selected by someone for some purpose, and many selections are epistemically productive and socially neutral. The van Fraassen framework does not by itself make some contrast classes better than others; it only makes the selection explicit. The normative argument — that certain contrast classes are ideologically violent — requires additional premises about what social categories should and should not be naturalized as units of causal analysis. Those premises come from elsewhere (feminist philosophy of science, critical race theory, philosophy of measurement), not from van Fraassen's framework itself. The synthesis is between the formal apparatus and the normative critique; neither alone does the work.

Garfinkel's *Forms of Explanation* is the most important work I was unable to read in full. Its 90-citation count suggests it has been underabsorbed and should be engaged with directly. Risjord (2000) is similarly important and nearly inaccessible to me.

**Connection to the overall report's argument.**

The contrast class is the mechanism by which the "violence of why-questions" is most precisely located. It is not that why-questions are inherently violent — it is that they carry a hidden presuppositional structure (the contrast class) that can be weaponized to naturalize certain social arrangements, foreclose structural analyses, and make the targets of harmful inquiry responsible for explaining themselves against a frame they did not choose. The formal machinery makes this precise: we can say *exactly* what is wrong with "Why do Black Americans score lower on IQ tests?" by reconstructing its implicit contrast class and showing that the alternative contrast class — "Why does this instrument produce differential distributions?" — is not merely a differently-framed version of the same question but a *formally different question* with a different correct answer in a different explanatory space. The choice between them is not made by evidence. It is made before evidence collection begins. That choice is political.
