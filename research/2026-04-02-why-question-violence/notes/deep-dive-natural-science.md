# Deep Dive: Natural Science Case of Premature Why-Questioning

## Question

Can the stress-ulcer hypothesis serve as a rigorous natural-science case where asking "why does X cause Y?" before establishing that X actually causes Y distorted inquiry for decades? And are there structurally similar cases in physics/chemistry?

---

## Investigation

### The Presuppositional Structure of the Pre-Marshall Question

Before Marshall and Warren, the dominant research question about peptic ulcers was not a neutral causal inquiry. It was, in effect: **"Why does stress cause ulcers?"** This question carried three embedded presuppositions that went unexamined for roughly fifty years:

1. That stress is the primary causal agent
2. That the mechanism is neuroendocrine/psychosomatic (acid hypersecretion mediated by vagal stimulation and psychological state)
3. That infectious etiology is excluded by definition — not as a hypothesis to be tested, but as a category error ("nothing grows in the acid stomach")

In van Fraassen's framework, a why-question has three components: a **topic** (the fact to be explained), a **contrast class** (what the topic is contrasted against — the implicit "rather than"), and a **relevance relation** (what kind of answer counts). The pre-Marshall question can be reconstructed as:

- **Topic**: Peptic ulcers occur and recur in certain individuals
- **Contrast class**: Why do they occur *rather than not occur*, where the contrast is framed entirely within the psychosomatic register — "why do stressed, emotionally repressed, ambitious individuals develop ulcers rather than healing?" The contrast presupposes stress is operative
- **Relevance relation**: Only psychosomatic and acid-secretion mechanisms are admissible answers; an infectious answer would be *irrelevant* — not false, but outside the space of acceptable explanations

This is van Fraassen's insight at full force: the contrast class does not just shape which answers are preferred; it determines which answers are even visible. Bacterial etiology was invisible to gastroenterology not because the evidence was insufficient but because the question's structure excluded it from the answer space.

Warren himself put it plainly in his Nobel biographical account: he knew "the standard teaching that nothing grows in the stomach." The question "why does stress cause ulcers" presupposed the answer to a prior question — "what causes ulcers?" — and got it wrong.

### The Historical Record: What Was Actually Believed

The psychosomatic framework for peptic ulcer was not a fringe position. It was the central paradigm from the 1930s through the 1980s, expressed with a confidence that now reads as extraordinary. A 1952 review in *JAMA* stated flatly: **"That psychic factors play a prominent role in the causation of ulcer is doubted by no one."** (Source: ME/CFS Science, "The dark psychosomatic history of peptic ulcer Part I," 2022.)

The historical origin of this certainty rested on several interlocking supports, none of which were as strong as they appeared:

**1. The "No Acid, No Ulcer" Axiom (Karl Schwartz, 1910)**
The formula *keine Säure, kein Ulkus* — "no acid, no ulcer" — was the foundational claim of modern gastroenterology for most of the twentieth century. It was empirically well-supported: acid is indeed necessary for ulcer formation. The error was treating a necessary condition as a sufficient explanation. The question became "what causes acid hypersecretion?" and the answer was assumed to be psychological stress.

**2. Franz Alexander's Psychoanalytic Theory (1930s)**
The most influential theory was Franz Alexander's, advanced from the University of Chicago. Alexander argued that ulcer patients had repressed "oral dependency" needs — a wish to be nurtured, expressed through the stomach's constant acid secretion in preparation for food that would never come. This was based on nine cases. The theory was expressed in elaborate quasi-scientific language:

> "The infantile wish to receive, to be taken care of, to be loved, to depend upon someone else, is most ideally gratified in the parasitic situation of the suckling infant... The repressed longing to receive love and help mobilizes the innervations of the stomach."

Note what this theory does epistemically: it takes the pre-existing why-question ("why does stress cause ulcers?") and answers it by elaborating a causal mechanism — neuroendocrine activation of acid secretion via unresolved psychic conflict. The question was never challenged, only answered more elaborately.

**3. Cushing's Clinical Observation and the Vagal Pathway (1932)**
Harvey Cushing's observation of perforated ulcers following brain surgery (Cushing's ulcer) provided apparent anatomical support: the vagus nerve could mediate brain-stomach connections. This became the mechanistic pathway through which "stress" could cause acid hypersecretion. Again, the why-question's answer space was refined and elaborated, not examined for its foundational presupposition.

**4. Experimental Animal Studies**
Rats and monkeys subjected to unpredictable electrical shocks and starvation sometimes developed gastric lesions. These were cited widely as confirmation of the stress-ulcer link. The confound — that the lesions resulted from starvation and physical trauma, not psychological stress — was not adequately considered.

**5. Treatment as Pseudo-Confirmation**
The Sippy diet (hourly milk and cream, bed rest) was the standard treatment and it did provide temporary relief by buffering acid. Psychosomatic theorists explicitly argued this confirmed Alexander's framework: "the effect of instituting frequent feedings of baby-type food with milk or milk and cream every hour or two hours seems to work magic in many patients. We have wondered: Is it really psychotherapy?" (Dr. Marvin Sleisenger, 1977). The treatment appeared to enact the theory — rest, nurturing, milk, regression to infantile care — which is confirmation bias in its purest form.

### The Structure of Resistance

When Warren began finding spiral bacteria in stomach biopsies in 1979, and when Marshall took up the clinical investigation in 1981, they faced not merely skepticism but categorical dismissal. Marshall's biographical account is specific: "I was told that the bacteria were either contaminants or harmless commensals... my results were disputed and disbelieved, not on the basis of science but because they simply could not be true."

Warren's wife, herself a doctor and psychiatrist, "knew the standard teaching that nothing grows in the stomach." She was the only person who stood by the research precisely because she was outside the field and therefore less committed to the existing why-question's presuppositional structure.

The resistance had a clear logical structure: within the framework of the reigning why-question, bacteria *could not be* the answer. The why-question had locked in a contrast class (psychosomatic vs. normal, stressed vs. unstressed) that rendered bacterial etiology not false but *irrelevant*. This is not the same as standard scientific conservatism. Marshall's data was rejected, in his words, "not on the basis of science." The presupposition of the why-question was doing the work, not evidence evaluation.

The most important evidence of the invisible-by-presupposition dynamic is documented in ME/CFS Science's Part III: "Multiple researchers had already found and reported spiral bacteria in the gut and speculated that these might be related to gastritis and peptic ulcer, long before Marshall and Warren did in the 1980s. Their discovery was not due to some new technology that was unavailable in the past. Previous accounts were simply ignored or not followed up on. Researchers following the stress-hypothesis saw those bacteria either as an accidental finding or an opportunistic infection that followed rather than caused the ulcer."

This is the mechanism of why-question violence in its clearest form: prior evidence of the correct answer existed, was observed, and was categorically recoded as irrelevant because it fell outside the contrast class of the reigning why-question.

### Marshall's Self-Experiment as Epistemic Disruption

In 1984, Marshall drank a broth containing cultured H. pylori, developed acute gastritis, had biopsies confirming colonization, and treated himself successfully with bismuth and antibiotics. This is famous as a heroic gesture, but its epistemic significance is less remarked: it was an attempt to force the research community to confront the presupposition directly, not to find a better answer to the old why-question but to show that the old question was wrong.

Even this was not immediately persuasive. The NIH consensus meeting that formally accepted the H. pylori theory did not occur until February 1994 — a decade after the self-experiment, two decades after the initial bacterial observations. The Nobel Prize came in 2005.

The 2005 Nobel Committee press release stated: "in 1982, when this bacterium was discovered by Marshall and Warren, **stress and lifestyle were considered the major causes of peptic ulcer disease**. It is now firmly established that Helicobacter pylori causes more than 90% of duodenal ulcers and up to 80% of gastric ulcers."

This is the official scientific verdict: the presupposed cause — stress — was false as a primary causal agent. The why-question "why does stress cause ulcers?" was asking for the explanation of something that, in its dominant form, was not happening.

### Thagard's Analysis and Its Limits

Paul Thagard analyzed the Marshall-Warren discovery extensively, most fully in *How Scientists Explain Disease* (Princeton, 1999) and in a 2009 paper "Patterns of Medical Discovery." His analysis describes the H. pylori discovery as a "conceptual revolution" involving "reclassification" of peptic ulcer from a metabolic/psychosomatic disease to an infectious one. He identifies the discovery as involving "concept formation" (H. pylori as a new category) and "analogical reasoning" (ulcers are like other infectious diseases).

Thagard notes that "previously, ulcers were viewed as metabolic diseases involving acid imbalance, or even, in older views as being psychosomatic diseases resulting from stress." But his account focuses on the cognitive processes of discovery rather than the distorting effect of the prior question structure. He asks: how did Marshall and Warren form the right hypothesis? The more pointed question for this essay is: why did the wrong hypothesis persist for fifty years despite repeated observations of the correct answer?

The answer that Thagard's framework does not fully capture is the one van Fraassen's provides: the wrong hypothesis persisted because it was embedded in a why-question whose presuppositional structure rendered the correct answer invisible, not merely unpopular.

### After the Revolution: The Residue of the Old Why-Question

The psychosomatic why-question did not simply disappear. After H. pylori was accepted, some researchers — most persistently Susan Levenstein — argued that stress remains an important co-factor, explaining why only some H. pylori-infected individuals develop ulcers. This is not unreasonable as a hypothesis, but as Levenstein's critics note, the evidence for a stress effect, when subjected to rigorous analysis, shows odds ratios below 2, with substantial confounding.

Marshall himself spoke to this dynamic: "You can always find stress in someone's life if you want to. You ask a few questions and eventually it's, 'Yes, I admit, I was worried about something recently.'... the data was very bad. And in fact there was plenty of evidence showing that stress didn't make much difference." He went further: "What else is supposedly caused by stress that we can debunk? A lot of these things that are supposedly caused by stress, you try to track down the reason for that link, and there isn't one, except the fact that we don't have any better cause. Everything that's supposedly caused by stress, I tell people there's a Nobel Prize there if you find out the real cause."

This is Marshall explicitly generalizing the epistemological lesson: "stress causes X" is a holding variable for ignorance — a presupposition that fills the contrast class when no real cause has been found.

### Supporting Cases: Physics and Chemistry

The ulcer case is the strongest for the essay, but three briefer cases from physics and chemistry provide structural parallels.

**1. Phlogiston (Lavoisier revolution, 1780s)**
The phlogiston why-question asked: "Why does burning matter release phlogiston, and why does metal calx absorb phlogiston to become metal?" The question presupposed phlogiston's existence. The Stahl-Becher theory assigned phlogiston as the substance that escaped during combustion. Lavoisier's work revealed that the presupposition was false: burning matter doesn't release anything (in the relevant sense); it combines with oxygen. The why-question "why does combustion release phlogiston?" was asking for the mechanism of a process that did not exist.

Critically, Priestley — who actually discovered oxygen (which he called "dephlogisticated air") — never abandoned the phlogiston framework. He found his discovery could be accommodated within the old why-question: dephlogisticated air was just air that could absorb more phlogiston. He died still defending phlogiston. The why-question's contrast class (combustion is about phlogiston release, not oxygen uptake) made his own discovery uninterpretable to him.

Hasok Chang's paper "The Hidden History of Phlogiston" (2010) shows that phlogiston theory was not merely wrong but was a surprisingly coherent research program that could accommodate anomalies. This is the van Fraassen point again: the contrast class absorbs anomalous evidence by recoding it rather than allowing it to destabilize the presupposition.

**2. Luminiferous Aether (Michelson-Morley, 1887)**
The why-question was: "Why does light propagate at different speeds through the aether depending on direction of motion?" This presupposed the aether's existence. Michelson and Morley designed their interferometer precisely to measure aether wind properties — to answer the why-question more precisely. The null result was, in Michelson's own words, "unexplained." Lorentz and FitzGerald initially proposed *ad hoc* mechanisms to explain why the aether was undetectable (contraction hypotheses) rather than abandoning the presupposition. Einstein's 1905 Special Relativity paper dissolved the question by showing the presupposition was false: there is no privileged medium for light propagation.

What is notable here is that Michelson and Morley were not initially attempting to refute the aether; they were attempting to characterize it. The why-question "why do we observe this aether drift pattern?" was operating even in the experiment that accidentally refuted the presupposition.

**3. Continental Drift Resistance (early 20th century)**
Wegener proposed continental drift in 1912. The prevailing research question in geology was: "Why are the continents stable, and what mechanisms maintain their fixed positions?" The why-question presupposed continental stability. Wegener's hypothesis was dismissed not because geologists evaluated his evidence and found it wanting, but because his hypothesis violated the contrast class of the dominant question: stable continents explained via isostasy, vertical rather than horizontal motion, contraction cooling.

The mechanism objection — "what force could move continents?" — was structurally the same as "what organism could survive stomach acid?" Both are requests for a mechanistic answer that fills the existing why-question's relevance relation, refusing to entertain that the question's presupposition might be false.

### Connecting to Aristotle's Hoti/Dioti

Aristotle's distinction in the *Posterior Analytics* between *hoti* (that something is the case) and *dioti* (why it is the case) maps directly onto this pathology. The *hoti*-question — "does stress cause ulcers?" — was never properly answered before the *dioti*-question — "why does stress cause ulcers?" — became the dominant research framework. The sequence was inverted.

For Aristotle, demonstration proceeds from establishing the *hoti* to explaining the *dioti*. The error in the stress-ulcer case is precisely Aristotelian in structure: a *dioti*-question was asked before the *hoti* was established. The result was not mere wasted effort but active distortion — the why-question's presupposition reshaped evidence collection, research funding, clinical practice, and the categorization of anomalous findings (bacteria were recoded as contaminants rather than causes).

What makes the natural-science case so sharp is that the correction was unambiguous. Unlike cases in social science where the presupposition's falsity is still contested, the H. pylori verdict is not in dispute. H. pylori causes more than 90% of duodenal ulcers. The *hoti* — "stress is the primary cause" — was simply false. The fifty years of why-questioning built on that *hoti* was, in a precise sense, a sustained methodological error.

## Key Findings

- **The 1930s-1980s stress-ulcer paradigm** was explicitly stated in *JAMA* (1952) as fact: "That psychic factors play a prominent role in the causation of ulcer is doubted by no one." **(Strong evidence — primary source citation documented)**

- **Franz Alexander's theory** (1930s, University of Chicago) provided the dominant mechanism: oral dependency needs expressed as acid hypersecretion. Based on 9 cases. **(Strong evidence — direct quotes from theory documented)**

- **"No acid, no ulcer"** (Schwartz 1910) established that stress-question by treating a necessary condition (acid) as the first-order explanatory target, directing research toward psychological causes of acid hypersecretion. **(Strong evidence — widely documented in history of gastroenterology)**

- **Prior observations of gastric bacteria were recoded as irrelevant** by researchers committed to the stress hypothesis — seen as "contaminants or harmless commensals" in Marshall's words. **(Strong evidence — Marshall's biographical account, Nobel Prize 2005; documentary history in ME/CFS Science three-part series)**

- **Marshall's resistance was categorical, not empirical**: his work was rejected "not on the basis of science but because they simply could not be true." **(Strong evidence — Marshall biographical account, Nobel Prize.org)**

- **Warren's wife, a psychiatrist, was one of the only supporters** — her distance from the gastroenterology paradigm preserved her ability to consider the bacterial hypothesis. **(Strong evidence — Warren biographical account, Nobel Prize.org)**

- **The 2005 Nobel Committee press release** explicitly states that stress and lifestyle were considered the major causes in 1982, and that H. pylori now explains 90%+ of duodenal ulcers. **(Strong evidence — primary source)**

- **Thagard (1999, 2009)** analyzes the discovery as a "conceptual revolution" but frames it in terms of cognitive processes of discovery rather than the distorting effect of the prior question structure. His analysis is a resource but does not make the van Fraassen point explicitly. **(Moderate evidence — paraphrase from Thagard's 2009 paper accessed directly)**

- **Phlogiston and aether** are structurally parallel but less precise: in both cases, the why-question's presupposition was false, and researchers accommodated anomalies within the old framework rather than challenging the presupposition. Priestley never abandoned phlogiston; Lorentz never abandoned aether. **(Moderate evidence — well-documented history of science; Hasok Chang's work on phlogiston, Michelson-Morley history)**

- **Continental drift** provides a third parallel where the presupposition of stability ("why are the continents stable?") made Wegener's hypothesis not merely implausible but category-incompatible. **(Moderate evidence — standard history of geology)**

## Assessment

The stress-ulcer case is a strong, well-documented natural-science case of premature why-questioning. Its value for the essay is that it is:

1. **Unambiguous in its verdict**: The Nobel Committee confirmed in 2005 that the reigning causal story — stress — was wrong. The *hoti* was false.

2. **Documented across multiple registers**: Primary sources (Marshall and Warren biographical accounts, Nobel Prize.org), philosophical analysis (Thagard), and historical documentation (the JAMA quotation, the three-part psychosomatic history) converge on the same picture.

3. **Structurally clean**: The van Fraassen decomposition works precisely. The why-question's contrast class (stressed vs. unstressed patients, acid-secretion mechanisms) rendered bacterial etiology invisible by design, not by negligence.

4. **Self-aware in retrospect**: Marshall himself articulated the epistemological lesson: stress is a placeholder for ignorance, a presupposition that fills the contrast class when no real cause has been found.

The main qualification is that the case is not quite as pure as it might appear. Acid is genuinely involved in ulcer formation (the "no acid, no ulcer" axiom is not wrong, just incomplete), and stress may still modulate vulnerability in H. pylori-infected individuals. But this qualification does not weaken the main point — it strengthens it. The why-question "why does stress cause ulcers?" was built on the false presupposition that stress is the primary cause, and this false presupposition organized half a century of inquiry.

For the phlogiston and aether cases, the confidence level is lower because the evidence is secondary (I have not accessed the primary chemistry history directly), but these cases are well-established in the history and philosophy of science literature and their structural features are not in dispute.

The Aristotle connection is strong conceptually. The ulcer case is a modern biomedical example of precisely the error Aristotle was warning against in the *Posterior Analytics*: proceeding to *dioti* before the *hoti* is established. What makes the case particularly useful is that it demonstrates this error in natural science — the domain Aristotle himself was primarily concerned with — not merely in social science where the error might be attributed to political bias.
