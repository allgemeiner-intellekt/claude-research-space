## Key Sources

### 1. Aristotle, *Posterior Analytics* (Book I Ch. 13; Book II Ch. 1-2)
- **Link**: https://faculty.washington.edu/smcohen/433/APoTexts.html (Barnes translation excerpts)
- **Key content**: The foundational source. Post. An. II.1 (89b24) identifies four things we seek: **(1) the fact (to hoti)**, (2) the reason why (to dioti), (3) whether something is, (4) what something is. Crucially, the ordering is explicit: "When we seek whether this or that is the case... we are seeking the fact. Evidence for this: on finding that it is eclipsed we stop; and if from the beginning we know that it is eclipsed, we do not seek whether it is. **When we know the fact we seek the reason why**" (89b26-30). Post. An. I.13 (78a23-78b13) gives the famous planet/twinkling example: a demonstration that the planets are near *via* their not-twinkling "is not of the reason why but of the fact; for it is not because they do not twinkle that they are near, but because they are near they do not twinkle" (78a30). The direction of demonstration matters — only from cause (middle term) to effect gives the *reason why*; the reverse gives only the *fact*. Similarly, the moon being spherical explains its style of increase, not vice versa. Book II.2 (90a7): "in all our searches we seek either if there is a middle term or what the middle term is. For the middle term is the explanation, and in all cases it is the explanation which is being sought."
- **Assessment**: Primary source, high authority. Barnes translation (1994, Oxford) is the standard. The key claim is clear and explicit: seeking *why* is only appropriate after *that* is established. This is a prescriptive methodological norm built into Aristotle's account of scientific inquiry. The passage 89b26-30 is the locus classicus — it is short, direct, and almost algorithmic in character.

### 2. Aristotle, *Metaphysics* Book I and *Posterior Analytics* Book II (knowledge ordering via IEP)
- **Link**: https://iep.utm.edu/aris-log/
- **Key content**: The IEP article on Aristotle's logic clarifies the empiricist scaffolding: "sense perception generates memory, which combines with intuition to produce human experience (empeiria), ultimately yielding art and science." Aristotle's epistemological ascent — perception → memory → experience → first principles — means the *hoti* (experiential knowledge that something is the case) is both temporally and epistemically prior to *dioti* (demonstrative knowledge of why). Scientific demonstration begins from premises that must already be established (true, primary, immediate, better-known than conclusion). The middle term of a syllogism "must embody the cause" — so the cause is what we discover after we have already established the explanandum.
- **Assessment**: IEP is reliable tertiary source for Aristotle's epistemology. Confirms the ordering structure.

### 3. David Gruender, "On Explanation: Aristotelean and Hempelean" (20th World Congress of Philosophy)
- **Link**: https://www.bu.edu/wcp/Papers/Scie/ScieGru2.htm
- **Key content**: This paper directly compares Aristotle's demonstrative science and Hempel's D-N model. Key finding: the two theories share their most important features: (a) both model science on the deductive process, (b) both require premises to be true, (c) both require that the conclusion follow necessarily. Gruender notes Aristotle preferred the Barbara syllogism (AAA-1) as the ideal of scientific knowledge. The D-N model "can be seen as a significant subset of the first-order predicate calculus," a superset of Aristotle's categorical syllogism. Critical difference: Aristotle did not distinguish logical necessity from natural necessity (nomic necessity), while Hempel-Oppenheim explicitly separated them. Gruender cites Wallace (1984) showing Galileo's adherence to Aristotelian deductive standards of explanation. He also notes: "the premises are thought true precisely because we find what they lead us to, the prior requirement of their truth begins to appear otiose or circular" — an important self-critique that applies equally to Aristotle and Hempel.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed conference paper (World Congress of Philosophy). Authoritative synthesis. The explicit parallel between Aristotelian demonstrative syllogism and the D-N model is a key finding for the research project — Hempel's theory is in a real sense a modernization of Aristotle's, both requiring established explananda before asking why.

### 4. Wesley C. Salmon, "Scientific Explanation: Causation and Unification" (Crítica, 1990)
- **Link**: https://iask.hu/wp-content/uploads/2002/07/salmon-scientific-explanation.pdf
- **Key content**: Salmon traces the two grand traditions in philosophy of scientific explanation since Hempel-Oppenheim 1948: (1) the subsumption/nomological approach (Hempel, later Friedman/Kitcher on unification), and (2) the causal approach (Scriven, then Salmon himself). Salmon's own trajectory: from statistical relevance model (1970s) to causal-mechanical model (1984). The causal-mechanical model requires identifying "causal processes and interactions leading up to E." Key quote: "the aim of explanations of this sort is to exhibit the ways in which nature operates; it is an effort to lay bare the mechanisms that underlie the phenomena we observe and wish to explain." Salmon also notes the interesting why/how distinction: why-questions "elicit an appeal to a general law," how-questions "evoke a description of underlying mechanisms." This is an implicit second dimension of the that/why/how structure.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed article from Salmon himself (1990), summarizing his own major contribution to explanation theory. High authority. No explicit engagement with Aristotle's hoti/dioti but the "phenomena we observe and wish to explain" framing assumes the explanandum is already given as an observable fact. The causal-mechanical model presupposes a settled explanandum — it traces backward from an established effect to its causes.

### 5. Bas van Fraassen, *The Scientific Image* (1980) — via multiple secondary sources
- **Link**: Primary: B. van Fraassen, *The Scientific Image*, Oxford UP, 1980. Secondary: https://ebrary.net/284104/philosophy/fraassen_pragmatics_scientific_explanation and https://revistas.pucsp.br/cognitiofilosofia/article/download/30211/21689/0
- **Key content**: Van Fraassen's pragmatic theory of explanation defines a why-question as a triple: **Q = <Pk, X, R>** where Pk is the *topic* (proposition being explained), X is the *contrast-class* (set of alternatives), and R is the *relevance relation*. Crucially, van Fraassen explicitly notes that a why-question has *presuppositions*: **(1) its topic Pk is true; (2) only Pk and not the other members of X is true; (3) there is at least one proposition bearing R to the topic that is true.** This means that for van Fraassen, a why-question is *defective* (has no correct answer) unless the explanandum is established as true. The theory thus formally encodes the Aristotelian principle. Unlike Hempel's context-independent model, van Fraassen's approach emphasizes that "scientific explanation is not a (pure) science but an application of science...to satisfy certain of our desires...in a specific context."
- **Assessment**: Van Fraassen's *Scientific Image* is a landmark in philosophy of science (1980). The pragmatic theory of why-questions is widely cited and debated. The point that why-questions have truth-presuppositions on their topic is philosophically important and directly relevant to the research question. Note that van Fraassen himself is an *anti-realist* constructive empiricist — he uses the presupposition structure not to police inquiry but to show explanation is context-relative, not a special epistemic achievement.

### 6. Ladyman, Douven, Horsten, and van Fraassen, "A Defence of van Fraassen's Critique of Abductive Inference" (*Philosophical Quarterly*, 1997)
- **Link**: https://fitelson.org/epistemology/ldhv_ibe.pdf
- **Key content**: This paper defends van Fraassen's two main objections to IBE: (1) the **"bad lot" argument** — even if the best available hypothesis is the best of the lot we've generated, it could still be "the best of a bad lot" if the true hypothesis wasn't among the candidates; (2) the **Dutch book argument** — any IBE rule either reduces to Bayesian conditionalization or violates probabilistic coherence. The key point for our research: IBE is characterized as "the type of inference in which one derives the conclusion that explains the available evidence best." This means IBE *starts from* observed evidence (an established fact) and infers to the explanation. The presupposition structure is thus preserved in IBE as normally characterized. The "bad lot" argument is the most relevant to the why-question issue: even granting we correctly identify the explanandum (the fact), IBE may not give us the *right* why if our candidate hypotheses are all wrong.
- **Assessment**: Published in a peer-reviewed journal, co-authored by van Fraassen himself. High authority on IBE and van Fraassen's position. The paper addresses IBE and realism, not the ordering question directly, but confirms van Fraassen's view that IBE is epistemically suspect even when the explanandum is known.

### 7. Andrew Gelman and Guido Imbens, "Why Ask Why? Forward Causal Inference and Reverse Causal Questions" (NBER Working Paper No. 19614, 2013)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/papers/w19614
- **Key content**: A directly relevant paper for social science methodology. Gelman and Imbens distinguish between **forward causal questions** ("What is the effect of X on Y?" — estimable via intervention/treatment models) and **reverse causal questions** ("Why did Y happen?" — explanatory/diagnostic). Their argument: asking "why" in social science research is typically not directly answerable via statistical methods and is better reframed as multiple forward causal questions. The title itself echoes Aristotle — "why ask why?" Their blog post (statmodeling.stat.columbia.edu) notes that reverse causal questions are answered not by direct estimation but by "model checking and hypothesis generation." This maps onto Aristotle: the *hoti* (that Y happened and to what degree) must be established before the *dioti* (why Y happened) can be productively approached.
- **Assessment**: NBER working paper (2013), widely cited in economics/social science (136 citations on Semantic Scholar). Gelman and Imbens are leading statisticians/econometricians. The paper doesn't engage with Aristotle explicitly, but its core distinction maps precisely onto the hoti/dioti framework. This is a key bridge between the ancient distinction and modern social science methodology.

### 8. Renato Cesar Cani, "The Pragmatics of Explanation: Remarks on van Fraassen's Theory of Why-Questions" (*Cognitio*, 2016)
- **Link**: https://revistas.pucsp.br/cognitiofilosofia/article/download/30211/21689/0
- **Key content**: Analyzes van Fraassen's response to two classic problems: (1) the *rejection* problem (why some explanations are not acceptable even when logically valid), and (2) the *asymmetry* problem (why "the shadow's length explains the flagpole's height" is unacceptable while the reverse is fine). Van Fraassen resolves both pragmatically: the contrast class and relevance relation are context-dependent, and what counts as a why-question depends on what the questioner already knows. Cani also examines Kitcher and Salmon's objection that van Fraassen's account is "too subjective" — that pragmatics can't do the normative work needed. The asymmetry problem is directly relevant: it shows that "establishing that" correctly (identifying the genuine causally prior factor) is a precondition for the why-question having the right answer.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed journal article in philosophy. Secondary source on van Fraassen, useful for understanding the asymmetry problem. The asymmetry problem is key: it shows that even granting the explanandum, the *direction* of the why-question matters — which also presupposes knowing causal structure.

### 9. Gruender's note on Wallace: Galileo and Aristotelian Explanation
- **Link**: William A. Wallace, *Galileo and His Sources*, Princeton UP, 1984 (cited in Gruender)
- **Key content**: Not directly accessed, but Gruender reports Wallace's finding that Galileo followed Aristotelian deductive standards of explanation. This is historically significant: the scientific revolution did not *abandon* the hoti/dioti distinction but extended and refined it. Galileo's mathematicization of nature preserved the structure — one establishes kinematic facts (hoti) before seeking dynamic explanations (dioti).
- **Assessment**: Secondary reference. Would need direct access to Wallace (1984) for the research project.

### 10. Poggiolesi, "(Conceptual) Explanations in Logic" (*Journal of Logic and Computation*, 2024)
- **Link**: Semantic Scholar paper ID d291e0204e87264e37993c18593cf522d405c839
- **Key content**: This paper opens with a direct Aristotle quote: "it is for the empirical scientist to know the fact and for the mathematical to know the reason why" (*Posterior Analytics*, Oxford UP, 1993, cited in the paper). This is from Post. An. I.13 — the sub-scientific/scientific divide. Mathematical sciences give the reason why; empirical sciences establish the facts. This maps exactly onto Aristotle's disciplinary hierarchy: natural history (hoti) feeds into natural philosophy (dioti).
- **Assessment**: Journal article using Aristotle as motivating epigraph. Confirms the contemporary relevance of the hoti/dioti distinction in formal epistemology.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The hoti/dioti distinction in *Posterior Analytics* is well-documented in ancient philosophy scholarship, with key texts (II.1, 89b24; I.13, 78a23) as the locus classicus. The Barnes translation (1994) is authoritative. The philosophical structure is clear: Aristotle establishes an *epistemological ordering* — seeking the fact (hoti) must precede and ground seeking the reason why (dioti) — and embeds this in his account of demonstrative science.

The **reception in philosophy of science** is substantially indirect. While Hempel's D-N model shares deep structural features with Aristotelian demonstration (both are deductive, both require established true premises, both treat explanation as syllogistic subsumption), the explicit connection is rarely foregrounded in 20th-century philosophy of science literature. Gruender's 1998 paper is one of the few to make the comparison direct. Most philosophy of science since Hempel-Oppenheim (1948) has been conducted *within* the Aristotelian framework without naming it as such.

**Van Fraassen's pragmatic theory** is the most philosophically sophisticated engagement with the why-question structure, and it formally encodes the Aristotelian presupposition: a why-question has its topic as a presupposition (the explanandum must be true). This is philosophically rigorous but van Fraassen deploys it for anti-realist purposes, not to police the *ordering* of inquiry.

**IBE** in its standard formulation (Lipton, Harman) starts from an established evidence base (the hoti) and infers to the best explanation of it (the dioti). The bad-lot objection and the "presupposes privilege" critique (Ladyman et al.) don't challenge the ordering but challenge whether IBE reliably delivers the right dioti even given correct hoti.

### Agreements Across Sources

1. The four-question structure in Aristotle is not disputed: hoti, dioti, whether-it-is, what-it-is (89b24).
2. Aristotle's ordering is explicit: "when we know the fact, we seek the reason why" (89b26-30).
3. Hempel's D-N model and Aristotelian demonstration share the deductive structure and the requirement for true premises.
4. Van Fraassen's pragmatic model formally requires the explanandum to be true (presupposition 1 of a why-question).
5. IBE as standardly characterized starts from evidence (established facts) and infers to explaining hypotheses.

### Conflicts and Tensions

1. **The Gruender critique applies to both Aristotle and Hempel**: if we know premises are true only because they're confirmed by their predicted conclusions, the "true premises" requirement is circular. This complicates the clean ordering — in practice, hoti and dioti may be epistemically entangled rather than neatly sequential.

2. **Salmon vs. van Fraassen**: Salmon (following Kitcher) thinks van Fraassen's contextual/pragmatic approach is too permissive — it makes almost anything an explanation in the right context. This debate is not about the ordering but about whether having established the hoti is *sufficient* for why-questions to be answerable.

3. **The social science gap**: Gelman and Imbens' distinction between forward and reverse causal questions implicitly invokes the hoti/dioti ordering, but they don't use that vocabulary and don't engage with philosophy of science. The mapping is productive but not argued.

4. **IBE and the fact-first issue**: There is a specific risk in IBE that is philosophically under-explored: using IBE to establish the *explanandum* itself (inferring that an event occurred because it would be better explained if it had). This would be asking why before establishing that — a violation of the Aristotelian norm. Van Fraassen's constructive empiricism blocks this move by requiring observable evidence, but in social science applications of IBE, the explanandum may itself be theory-laden or contested.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **Direct engagement between the hoti/dioti distinction and philosophy of social science**: Almost entirely absent. The closest things found are Gelman-Imbens (statistical, non-philosophical) and scattered references in hermeneutics literature (Gadamer, Winch) that engage Aristotle on *phronesis* but not on the epistemological ordering of inquiry.

2. **Explicit arguments that premature why-questioning is a *methodological error***: Not found in any direct source. The philosophical literature establishes that why-questions presuppose their explananda, but no philosopher of science was found explicitly arguing that asking "why" before "that" is a *systematic error* that distorts inquiry. This gap may be productive — the claim exists implicitly in the literature (Aristotle, van Fraassen's presupposition conditions, Gelman-Imbens) but hasn't been assembled into a critique of method.

3. **The IBE-presupposes-explanandum worry**: The specific concern that IBE might be used to establish explananda (rather than explain them) is not discussed in the literature I found. The "bad lot" argument addresses whether IBE gives the right *why*; no source addresses whether IBE can illegitimately create the *that*.

4. **Salmon's asymmetry resolution and Aristotle**: Salmon's work on "why" vs. "how" questions hints at a further sub-structure within the why side of the hoti/dioti divide, but no source ties this back explicitly to Aristotle.

5. **Continental philosophy of science**: Nothing found on whether Gadamer's hermeneutical circle, Heidegger's analysis of questioning, or Habermas on explanation vs. understanding engages specifically with the hoti/dioti ordering. These traditions likely have relevant material under the rubric of Verstehen vs. Erklären.

### Surprises

1. **The directness of Aristotle's text**: Post. An. II.1 (89b24-30) is almost startlingly clear and concise. "When we know the fact we seek the reason why." The principle is stated as a brute observation about how inquiry actually works — descriptive as much as prescriptive.

2. **Van Fraassen formally encodes the Aristotelian presupposition** in his pragmatic theory without noting the Aristotelian provenance. The presupposition structure of why-questions (topic must be true) is precisely Aristotle's ordering principle, but van Fraassen uses it to reach constructive-empiricist conclusions rather than to privilege the ordering of inquiry.

3. **The Hempel-Aristotle parallel is substantial but rarely stated**: Most philosophy of science literature treats Hempel as making a break from speculative philosophy of science, but the structural parallels to Aristotle are deep. Gruender's paper is the exception that names this explicitly.

4. **Gelman-Imbens (2013) is essentially Aristotelian without knowing it**: A landmark paper in social science methodology independently rediscovers the hoti/dioti ordering and argues it matters for empirical research design. That this 2013 NBER paper was reached by searching for "premature explanation why before that methodological error social science" suggests the connection is there to be made but hasn't been articulated explicitly in the literature.

5. **The specific question of IBE creating its own explanandum** is apparently not in the literature — it appears to be a genuine gap that could be the research project's own contribution.
