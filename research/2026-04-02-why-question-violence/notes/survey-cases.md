## Key Sources

### Thomas Teo — "From Speculation to Epistemological Violence in Psychology" (2008)

- **Link**: http://www.yorku.ca/tteo/Teo2008EpiVio.pdf (open access)
- **Semantic Scholar**: paperId ab0005ec2b320d6e23bfe52619b43c97debcff35, 124 citations
- **Key content**: Teo introduces "epistemological violence" (EV) as a specific failure mode in empirical psychology. The argument runs: empirical results typically underdetermine their interpretation. When researchers interpret data about groups in ways that construct the "Other" as inferior or problematic — even when the data equally support neutral or positive interpretations — they commit EV. The violence is epistemic: harm is inflicted through the act of knowledge production itself, not through any separate downstream effect. EV does not require bad intentions; it only requires that the interpreter chooses the unflattering interpretation when alternatives exist. Teo uses race psychology as his central case. He notes that the traditional is/ought separation breaks down here: a "factual" finding that is speculative in its causal interpretation carries normative consequences for those it describes.
- **Assessment**: This is the most philosophically precise source for the "why-question" mechanism. Teo's key move is identifying that the violence occurs *at the interpretive step*, not in the data itself. The why-question functions as an interpretive frame that selects among underdetermined possible conclusions. High-quality peer-reviewed journal article (Theory & Psychology). Widely cited across critical psychology. Some critics (Barbara Held, 2020) argue Teo conflates science with its political misuse — but Held's counter has been replied to (Olson 2020), and the core point about underdetermination stands.

### Thomas Teo — "What Is Epistemological Violence in the Empirical Social Sciences" (2010)

- **Link**: DOI:10.1111/J.1751-9004.2010.00265.X; *Social and Personality Psychology Compass* 4(4), 295–303
- **Semantic Scholar**: paperId e38e86cf690509d780886fd74b79b09b1e23cccf, 277 citations
- **Key content**: The 2010 paper broadens the framework. Teo defines EV as occurring when (a) empirical evidence allows multiple equally defensible interpretations, and (b) the researcher selects the interpretation that disadvantages the subject group. He makes explicit the mechanism: the *framing question* pre-selects which interpretations will count as "findings." If you ask "why do group X score lower on measure Y?", you have already committed to (1) treating the gap as stable and real, (2) locating causation inside or near the group. This is the structural mechanism most relevant to the "violence of why-questions" thesis. The paper discusses several domains: race psychology, cross-cultural comparison, gender differences.
- **Assessment**: More accessible than the 2008 paper; the 2010 version is the one most cited by subsequent critics. Published in a mainstream social psychology journal, which is notable — EV is not a fringe critique. The 277 citations indicate broad uptake. Limitation: Teo works primarily in history and philosophy of psychology; empirical scientists sometimes dismiss the argument as a concern about "framing" rather than method.

### Thomas Teo — "Empirical Race Psychology and the Hermeneutics of Epistemological Violence" (2011)

- **Link**: Semantic Scholar paperId 1a2d8f329465126aed77c6bfc39f730a47facd4e, 68 citations
- **Key content**: Applies EV specifically to race psychology, with historical reconstruction. Shows that the interpretive choices in race psychology — e.g., attributing group differences to "natural" versus socio-historical causes — have been consistently made in the unflattering direction despite the underdetermination. The "hermeneutics" framing emphasizes that meaning-attribution is a methodological act, not a post-hoc addition.
- **Assessment**: Complements the 2010 paper with historical depth. Sociology-classified in the database, but published in a psychology venue.

### Ned Block — "How Heritability Misleads about Race" (1995/1996)

- **Link**: https://web-archive.southampton.ac.uk/cogprints.org/230/1/199712003.html (full text retrieved); originally *Boston Review* XX(6), 30–35; also published in *Cognition* (1995), paperId 81cb7ffa48f2efad15df831d16fe3169c9e88ef6, 159 citations
- **Key content**: Block's essay is a demolition of the causal inference in *The Bell Curve* (Herrnstein & Murray, 1994). His argument is philosophically precise and directly instantiates the "premature why-question" pathology:

  1. *The question presupposes stable, race-linked, heritable IQ gaps.* Herrnstein & Murray frame the issue as: "given high within-group heritability of IQ and a 15-point Black-White gap, how much of the gap is genetic?" Block shows this framing conceals a crucial logical step: high heritability *within* groups licenses no inference about *between*-group differences. Lewontin's corn-seed example (same seeds, different soils, 100% within-group heritability, 100% between-group environmental causation) demonstrates this.
  
  2. *The "genetic" vs. "environmental" dichotomy is confused.* Block distinguishes "genetic determination" (what causes a trait to develop) from "heritability" (what proportion of *variation* in a population is due to genetic variation). The Bell Curve conflates these. IQ is highly heritable within the white population studied, but this does not mean IQ is genetically determined — IQ reacts substantially to environmental change (Flynn Effect: ~3 points per decade across multiple countries; Holland saw 21 points in 30 years).
  
  3. *Indirect heritability.* Even the within-group heritability figure may largely consist of "indirect" genetic effects (genes affecting social treatment, which affects IQ) rather than "direct" IQ-gene effects. Being perceived as Black is a heritable characteristic that brings systematic environmental disadvantages — these show up in heritability statistics but are not genetic in the deterministic sense.
  
  4. The key structural point: by framing the question as "how much of the gap is genetic vs. environmental?" Herrnstein & Murray smuggled in the assumption that the genetic component is non-zero. Block shows this assumption is not supported by the data; zero genetic difference (or even a genetic advantage for the lower-scoring group, outweighed by environmental deprivation) is equally consistent with the observed data.

- **Assessment**: One of the clearest single-author philosophical critiques of a "premature why-question" in science. Block is a distinguished NYU philosopher of mind and language; this essay appeared in *Boston Review* (accessible) and *Cognition* (peer-reviewed). The argument about the heritability/genetic determination distinction is technically unimpeachable. Limitation: Block focuses on the *Bell Curve* specifically; he does not address the broader research program in behavior genetics. But for the "premature why-question" thesis, his corn-seed/Flynn-effect argument is the cleanest demonstration available.

### Cordelia Fine — *Delusions of Gender* (2010) and related work

- **Semantic Scholar**: paperId f4429db6e582f1fb06d006bb2abd71a2139de8ae, 487 citations (book)
- **Link**: Published Norton; also: "Is There Neurosexism in Functional Neuroimaging Investigations of Sex Differences?" *Neuroethics* (2013), paperId cfadb5478abfdb24d1c3cdb90f24cf713de82bf2
- **Key content**: Fine's argument in *Delusions of Gender* is a sustained case that the research program asking "why are women's and men's brains different?" has systematically misread the neuroscience evidence. The specific pathology:

  1. *The question presupposes that observable behavioral and cognitive differences between men and women are both real and neurologically grounded.* Fine documents extensive cases where the presupposed differences are themselves artifacts: small or unstable effect sizes, publication bias, conceptual slippage between "brain difference" and "behavioral consequence."
  
  2. *Neurosexism*: Fine coins this term for the specific practice of interpreting neural data through gendered assumptions. Studies often find a difference and immediately ask "why do women's brains work differently from men's?" rather than first establishing whether the difference is robust, meaningful, or caused by lifelong socialization rather than innate structure.
  
  3. *The research loop*: Beliefs about gender differences influence experimental design, analysis choices, and publication, producing findings that confirm the initial frame. Fine argues this is not mere bias but a structural feature of how the "why do women differ?" question is posed.
  
  4. In her 2021 paper "Fairly Criticized, or Politicized?" (*Cold Spring Harbor Perspectives in Biology*), Fine explicitly addresses the meta-level: when is criticism of sex-difference research fair scientific critique vs. political interference? Her answer distinguishes "framework assumptions" (background commitments that shape what counts as data) from "direct politicization."

- **Assessment**: *Delusions of Gender* is the most widely read feminist science critique in this area. Fine is a cognitive neuroscientist and philosopher at the University of Melbourne. The book has 487 citations in academic literature and substantially influenced the field. Limitation: some researchers (e.g., Geary, 2010; Cahill, 2012) argue Fine selectively emphasizes null results and downplays positive findings. The debate itself is instructive: it shows that the question "are there sex differences in cognition?" resists resolution partly because the answer depends on what one counts as evidence of a "difference." This methodological under-determination is exactly the premature-why-question mechanism.

### Anne Fausto-Sterling — *Myths of Gender* (1985/1987)

- **Semantic Scholar**: paperId 046ae047b1557ca1bc6766350ac62387de0caf8f, 829 citations
- **Key content**: The earlier, foundational critique. Fausto-Sterling examined specific claims in the biological literature about sex differences — in aggression, in cognitive ability, in hormonal effects on behavior — and showed they consistently rested on under-powered studies, unreplicated findings, and selective citation. Her specific argument: the *question* "why do women perform worse at spatial tasks?" was being investigated as if the phenomenon were established, when the evidence for a substantial, generalizable difference was weak. The research program was driven by the question rather than the phenomenon.
- **Assessment**: A foundational text with 829 citations. Published originally in 1985 (revised 1987). Has aged well: the specific studies Fausto-Sterling criticized have largely not replicated. Academic authority is high (Brown University biologist). Limitation: some of the neuroscience Fausto-Sterling critiqued is from a different methodological era; the subsequent decades of fMRI research require separate evaluation (which Fine provides).

### Ego Depletion — The Research Program That Built on a Non-Existent Effect

- **Primary failure documented**: Hagger et al. (2016), pre-registered multi-lab replication, *Perspectives on Psychological Science* — https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1745691616652873 (retrieved via Exa)
- **Related**: Baumeister & Tierney (2011), *Willpower* (popular book); Baumeister (2019), "Self-Control, Ego Depletion, and Social Psychology's Replication Crisis" (18 citations)
- **Key content**: The ego depletion literature is the clearest case of a research program asking "why does self-control exhaust a limited resource?" — a causal why-question — before establishing that the phenomenon itself (reliable performance decrements after self-control exertion) was real and robust. The sequence:

  1. Baumeister et al. (1998) reported that performing a self-control task (resisting cookies) led to faster giving-up on a subsequent difficult task. They explained this as depletion of a shared "resource" — and glucose was later proposed as the biological substrate.
  
  2. Dozens of studies asked *why* ego depletion occurs and what factors modulate it — glucose, beliefs about willpower, type of task, etc.
  
  3. Hagger et al.'s 2016 pre-registered replication across 23 labs found no evidence of the basic effect (overall d = 0.04, not significantly different from zero).
  
  4. A broader pre-registered replication by Lurquin et al. (2016, *PLOS ONE*) similarly found nothing. The "Many Labs 3" project also failed to find ego depletion.
  
  5. The interpretive question "why does depletion occur?" had generated hundreds of papers proposing mechanisms — but the *explanandum* (reliable depletion) was not robustly established to begin with.
  
  6. Baumeister (2019) responds to the replication failures by arguing that the newer studies used weaker manipulations. This may be partly right, but it also illustrates the problem: when the baseline phenomenon is highly variable and manipulation-sensitive, asking causal-mechanistic questions produces answers about the experimental conditions, not about a stable real-world process.

- **Assessment**: Strong case because the timeline is clear: a massive causal literature arose while the basic phenomenon was soft. The pre-registered multi-lab replication is methodologically credible (23 sites, Hagger et al. had 2000+ participants). This case is slightly different from the others in that the original effect is genuinely contested rather than clearly absent — Baumeister claims positive replications exist, and recent work (2024 review) argues for nuanced persistence of the effect under strong manipulations. However, even if a small effect exists, hundreds of studies asked "why?" before anyone established *what* they were explaining.

### Stereotype Threat — Flore & Wicherts Meta-Analysis (2015) on Gender and Math

- **Semantic Scholar**: paperId 0d96ab4b0d14915efccddc79c7dcbea228e9a32c, 324 citations
- **Link**: *Journal of School Psychology* (2015)
- **Key content**: Flore & Wicherts conducted a meta-analysis of stereotype threat effects on girls' math performance. After correcting for publication bias (using the p-curve and trim-and-fill methods), the effect size shrank substantially — from an apparently significant effect to near zero. Their conclusion: the published literature on stereotype threat as an explanation for gender gaps in math performance was substantially contaminated by publication bias, meaning researchers had been explaining a partially-artifact phenomenon. The 2017 cross-temporal meta-analysis (Lewis & Michalak) found that stereotype threat effects have declined over time, raising the question of whether the original effects were cultural moments rather than stable mechanisms.
- **Assessment**: The Flore & Wicherts paper is technically strong (explicit publication-bias correction, 324 citations). This is not a claim that stereotype threat *never* operates — it almost certainly does in some contexts. But the research program assumed a robust, large, reliable effect and spent considerable effort on causal elaboration ("why does threat hurt performance?", "what moderates the effect?") before the basic phenomenon's reliability was established. This is a secondary case but illustrative. The Flore & Wicherts paper is peer-reviewed, quantitative, published in a good venue.

### Helen Longino — *Science as Social Knowledge* (1990)

- **Semantic Scholar**: paperId d6ebcda82b47cba8dea2dc3b8f0337cadb30490c, 799 citations
- **Key content**: Longino provides the philosophical infrastructure that underlies several of the above critiques. Her central claim: "background assumptions" — implicit commitments about which questions are worth asking and what kinds of answers are acceptable — shape scientific inquiry in ways that are not neutral. Applied to the cases above, her framework explains why "why do group X perform worse than group Y?" is not a neutral research question: it embeds assumptions about the stability of the phenomenon, the location of its cause (in the group rather than in the measurement or environment), and what kinds of answers count as explanations. Longino does not use the phrase "premature why-question" but the mechanism she describes is isomorphic.
- **Assessment**: The canonical work in feminist philosophy of science on values in inquiry. 799 citations. Princeton University Press monograph. Limitation: the framework is programmatic rather than case-specific. It needs instantiation in particular cases (which is what Teo, Block, Fine, and Fausto-Sterling provide).

### Culture of Poverty / Oscar Lewis Critiques

- **Key source**: Charles Valentine, *Culture and Poverty: Critique and Counter-Proposals* (1969) — full critique of Lewis's framework; Harvey & Reed, "The Culture of Poverty: An Ideological Analysis" (1996), *Sociological Perspectives* — https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.2307/1389418
- **Key content**: Oscar Lewis (1959, 1966) asked "why do the poor remain poor across generations?" and proposed that poverty creates a subculture whose values and practices perpetuate poverty independently of material conditions. The critique is a textbook case of a why-question presupposing its own answer: by asking why individual poor people behave in ways that perpetuate poverty, Lewis had already framed poverty as a behavioral/cultural problem rather than a structural one. The empirical question of whether distinctively "poor" cultural traits exist and persist independently of economic conditions was not resolved before the explanatory framework was built. Harvey & Reed (1996) trace how the culture-of-poverty framing shaped U.S. social policy in ways that located interventions in changing individual behavior rather than economic structures. Valentine's 1969 critique showed Lewis's observations were methodologically weak: selective ethnography, non-representative samples, and circular reasoning (poor people act "poor" because poverty selects for these behaviors, which then explain poverty).
- **Assessment**: The culture-of-poverty case is a sociological rather than psychological example, and somewhat older, but it is the *paradigm case* in the social sciences of a premature why-question distorting an entire policy discourse. Harvey & Reed's ideological analysis is published in a peer-reviewed sociology journal. Valentine (1969) is a foundational academic critique. Both are well-documented; no serious sociologist of poverty defends the culture-of-poverty thesis in its original form. The contemporary version — Charles Murray's *Losing Ground* (1984) and "Coming Apart" (2012) — recycles the same structure.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The "premature why-question" distortion is well-documented across multiple domains, but there is no single theoretical unification of the pattern under this label. The closest unified framework is Teo's "epistemological violence" — but Teo focuses on interpretive harm rather than on the logical structure of question-presupposition. The Block/Lewontin critique, the Longino framework, and the replication crisis literature each provide pieces of the picture, but they have not been synthesized into a general account of *what it means for a causal question to be premature*.

### Agreements Across Sources

1. **The mechanism is real**: All sources agree that framing a research question causalistically before the explanandum is established creates a distorted research program. This is not a speculative concern — it has produced demonstrably flawed literatures (ego depletion, Bell Curve heritability claims, stereotype threat magnitude claims).

2. **Underdetermination is central**: Multiple sources (Teo, Block, Longino) converge on the point that empirical data typically underdetermine their causal interpretation, and the choice of interpretation is shaped by the presuppositions built into the question.

3. **The distortion is not just "bias"**: Block and Teo both emphasize that the problem is structural, not a matter of individual researchers being biased. The question-form itself commits to a causal model before data collection begins.

### Conflicts and Tensions

- **Is the phenomenon real at all, or just distorted in magnitude?** For ego depletion and stereotype threat, defenders argue the original effect exists but was overstated. For the race-IQ gap, defenders argue the phenomenon is real and the why-question is legitimate. The "premature" critique has more purchase when the phenomenon is substantially artifactual (ego depletion at large effect sizes) than when it is real but modestly sized (stereotype threat in some conditions).

- **Normativity of why-questions**: Teo and Longino argue that asking certain why-questions has harmful consequences independent of their truth value. Block and other analytic philosophers are more circumspect: the question might be legitimate; the inference from data is what fails. This is a genuine tension — it matters for whether the critique is about *question-formation* or *inference-from-data*.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **No philosopher has systematically analyzed "question presupposition" as a distinct logical category in scientific method**. The pragmatics/presupposition literature (Strawson, van Fraassen, Hamblin) has the tools but has not applied them to this specific failure mode. The Exa search for "on the logic of why-questions" found a 1984 Sintonen paper in PSA Proceedings — this should be investigated further, as it may be directly relevant.

2. **The poverty case is underrepresented in philosophy of science**. The culture-of-poverty literature is almost entirely sociological. There is no philosophical analysis of it comparable to Block on race/IQ or Fine on gender/brain.

3. **Connection to history and philosophy of science (HPS) more broadly**: The concept of "research programs" (Lakatos) and "paradigms" (Kuhn) are relevant — a premature why-question may be what generates a degenerating research program. This connection has not been made explicitly in the literature found.

4. **The replication crisis literature rarely uses the "presupposition" framing**. Most replication crisis analyses focus on p-hacking, publication bias, and low statistical power. The upstream question of whether researchers were asking a well-formed causal question in the first place is almost never addressed in that literature.

### Surprises

1. **Block (1996) is more philosophically sophisticated than expected**. The corn-seed example and the indirect-heritability argument are not just critiques of Bell Curve; they are expositions of *why the standard way of dividing "genetic" from "environmental" causation breaks down for between-group comparisons*. This has implications well beyond the IQ debate.

2. **Teo explicitly names "speculation" as unavoidable** — his point is not to eliminate causal inference from psychology but to subject it to ethical scrutiny. This is a more nuanced position than "don't ask why-questions."

3. **The ego depletion case is complicated by a possible real but small effect**. The literature is not a clean case of "zero phenomenon" but rather of a massive research program built on an overestimated phenomenon. The failure mode is quantitative distortion, not complete fabrication. This is arguably *more* typical of how premature why-questioning operates in practice.

4. **Helen Longino's book (1990) has 799 citations** — it is a major work in philosophy of science, not just feminist theory. The fact that mainstream philosophy of science has absorbed her framework about "background assumptions" provides legitimate authority for the critique of question-framing in inquiry.
