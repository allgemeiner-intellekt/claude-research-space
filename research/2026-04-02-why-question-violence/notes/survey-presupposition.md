## Key Sources

### Sylvain Bromberger, "Why-Questions" (1966)
- **Link**: http://www.fitelson.org/290/bromberger.pdf (PDF) and https://philpapers.org/rec/BROW-2
- **Key content**: Bromberger gives the first systematic formal account of why-questions in philosophy of science. His central contributions: (1) the presupposition of a why-question of the form "Why is it the case that P?" is simply the proposition P — i.e., the question presupposes its own topic is true; (2) he introduces the concept of *abnormic laws* (laws stating what happens *unless* some abnormal condition obtains) and *abnormic hypotheses* as the proper form of answers; (3) crucially, he introduces the **p-predicament**: a person is in a p-predicament with respect to "Why P?" when they know P is true, know what a correct answer would look like, but cannot produce one — and cannot even identify what the unknown must be. (The "p" stands for "puzzled/perplexed" but is not merely psychological.) For example, a physicist committed to classical physics who knows about the photoelectric effect is in a p-predicament: the question has a presupposition (the effect occurs), the question is in principle answerable, but the person cannot answer it because the conditions for answer-recognition require going beyond existing theory. This is why questions force us into acknowledging structured ignorance — and why they can be epistemically coercive: accepting the question commits you to accepting that an answer exists and that you lack it.
- **Assessment**: Foundational. Peer-reviewed chapter (in *Beyond the Edge of Certainty*, Colodny ed., 1966). Bromberger's account treats presupposition primarily as the topic being true — it does not yet articulate the *explanatory* presupposition as a separate structure. The p-predicament concept is philosophically important and underutilized in discussions of question violence.

### Bas C. van Fraassen, "The Pragmatic Theory of Explanation" (1980)
- **Link**: http://www.fitelson.org/290/vanfraassen_pte.pdf (reprinted from *The Scientific Image*, Oxford 1980)
- **Key content**: Van Fraassen proposes that explanations are answers to *why-questions*, and why-questions have a tripartite structure: **⟨P_k, X, R⟩** where:
  - **P_k** = the *topic* — the proposition that something is the case (presupposed true)
  - **X** = the *contrast class* — the set of alternatives against which the topic is to be explained (e.g., "Why did *this* conductor warp?" vs. "Why did it warp *now*?")
  - **R** = the *relevance relation* — the contextually determined type of factor the answerer must supply (causal, intentional, nomological, etc.)
  
  A why-question has **three presuppositions**: (1) the topic P_k is true; (2) only P_k, not any other member of the contrast class X, is true; (3) at least one proposition bearing relation R to ⟨P_k, X⟩ actually exists (i.e., there *is* an explanation of the right type). When the first or second presupposition fails, "the why-question does not arise" — it is simply rejected as ill-formed. When the third fails, the question arises but has no correct answer. Context determines all three elements of the triple; the same surface question can be different why-questions in different conversational settings.
  
  The famous tower/shadow example illustrates how context determines which contrast class is relevant: the same physical setup licenses different explanatory answers depending on what the questioner implicitly contrasts. This means that a why-question imports a contextual frame that the answerer may not have consented to.
- **Assessment**: Extremely authoritative (reprinted as a standard text; cited thousands of times). The tripartite structure is the dominant framework in philosophy of science for analyzing why-questions. Van Fraassen's analysis makes explicit that why-questions carry a *third* presupposition — the existence of an explanation — which no other standard question type (who/what/whether/how) carries in the same way.

### Bromberger, "On What We Know We Don't Know" (1992, book)
- **Link**: https://web.stanford.edu/group/cslipublications/cslipublications/bromberger-corpus/On-What-We-Know-We-Dont-Know.pdf
- **Key content**: Develops the p-predicament further. Someone is in a p-predicament with respect to "Why P?" when: they know P is true; they know what type of answer is needed; they believe a correct answer exists; but they cannot produce it, and cannot identify what is missing. The concept is not merely epistemic — it has a formal structure tied to the presuppositions of why-questions. Crucially: the p-predicament is only possible because why-questions presuppose the existence of an explanation *and* presuppose that you know the form a correct answer would take. Without these presuppositions, you would not be puzzled — you would simply not understand the question.
- **Assessment**: Book-length treatment by the founding figure; authoritative. The p-predicament is a conceptually rich complement to van Fraassen's account: van Fraassen explains the structure of why-questions, Bromberger explains the epistemic situation they create.

### SEP, "Presupposition" (Beaver & Geurts, Fall 2025 edition)
- **Link**: https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2025/entries/presupposition/
- **Key content**: Comprehensive treatment. Key points relevant to why-questions:
  - **Frege-Strawson lineage**: Frege (1892) held that presuppositions are definedness conditions — a sentence lacking a presupposed referent fails to express a proposition. Strawson (1950) radicalized this: presupposition failure causes truth-value *gaps*, not falsehood. His test: a presupposition survives negation of the main claim. "The King of France is bald" and "The King of France is not bald" both presuppose there is a King of France.
  - **Semantic vs. pragmatic presupposition**: Stalnaker (1972, 1974) reframed presupposition as a property of speaker states (what a speaker takes for granted in the common ground) rather than of sentences. This matters greatly for why-questions: the presupposition that an explanation *exists* is pragmatic — it reflects what the questioner takes for granted about the world's causal structure.
  - **Accommodation**: Lewis (1979) — hearers adjust their knowledge state to satisfy speaker presuppositions when they're not already in common ground. This happens *automatically* and often *unconsciously*. When someone asks "Why did the Roman Empire fall?" the hearer accommodates: (a) the fall occurred, (b) there is an explanation. Both accommodations happen before the hearer even begins to answer.
  - **Projection**: presuppositions project out of embeddings, through negation, conditionals, questions. "Is it true that John knows P?" still presupposes P is true.
  - **Cancellation**: presuppositions can be cancelled by explicit denial ("I don't know if there is a King of France, but...") or by contextual mismatch — but cancellation requires *explicit* effort.
- **Assessment**: The authoritative reference. The accommodation point is directly relevant to the "violence" of why-questions: the hearer who begins to answer has already unconsciously accommodated all three of a why-question's presuppositions.

### SEP, "Questions" (Cross & Roelofsen, current edition)
- **Link**: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/questions/
- **Key content**: Establishes the formal difference between question types. Three major accounts:
  - **Hamblin semantics**: a question denotes the set of its possible answers (propositions). Under this account, why-questions are formally unruly — the set of possible answers is not bounded by any simple semantic operation.
  - **Partition semantics** (Groenendijk & Stokhof): a question meaning is a partition of possible worlds by their true answers. Why-questions resist clean partition treatment because what counts as the "right" answer is context-sensitive.
  - **Inquisitive semantics** (Ciardelli, Groenendijk, Roelofsen): question meanings are downward-closed sets of propositions. Again, why-questions sit awkwardly — they are studied separately from elementary questions (who/what/whether) precisely because they require philosophical accounts of explanation, not just logical ones.
  - Key formal distinction: the presupposition of elementary questions is the proposition that *some* answer is true (existential presupposition). The presupposition of why-questions includes this *plus* the specifically explanatory presupposition — that there exists a reason/cause/explanation of the right contextual type.
- **Assessment**: Authoritative SEP entry. The key insight for this survey: why-questions are *not* tractable by standard formal semantics of questions — they require a separate philosophical theory (van Fraassen, Bromberger, Hintikka). This intractability is not incidental; it reflects the deeper structure of why-questions.

### Satoshi Tomioka, "Why questions, presuppositions, and intervention effects" (2009)
- **Link**: https://udel.edu/~stomioka/papers/tomioka_JEAL_why.pdf; DOI: 10.1007/S10831-009-9053-0; *Journal of East Asian Linguistics* 18:253-271
- **Key content**: Tomioka offers a linguistic account of why-questions that identifies their *peculiar presuppositional property*. He argues that why-questions presuppose not just that the topic is true, but that *there exists a specific reason* for it — an assertion-type presupposition that differs structurally from the existential presuppositions of other wh-questions. Key finding: this presupposition explains *intervention effects* (degraded grammaticality when certain quantified elements intervene between "why" and its clause). The presupposition of why-questions affects *information structure* differently from other wh-words: because why presupposes a reason exists, it cannot be subordinated to or quantified over by intervening elements the way "what" or "who" can. This "sticky" quality — the presupposition resists being embedded or quantified over — is a formal linguistic manifestation of why-question distinctiveness.
- **Assessment**: Well-regarded linguistics paper (49 citations). Works primarily in Japanese/Korean contexts but the presupposition analysis generalizes. Provides formal linguistic support for the intuition that why-question presuppositions are "harder to cancel" — the stickiness is not merely rhetorical but grammatically evidenced.

### Fabrizio Macagno, "Presuppositional Fallacies" (2023)
- **Link**: *Argumentation* (Springer), DOI: 10.1007/s10503-023-09625-6; open access PDF available
- **Key content**: Develops a dialectical-pragmatic framework for understanding presuppositions as mechanisms that can be used manipulatively. Key concepts:
  - Presuppositions function as *dark side commitments* — they are attributed to "a collective voice representing what is commonly shared," so the individual presupposer can disclaim personal responsibility for them while still encoding them.
  - Presuppositions *reverse the burden of proof*: instead of being required to establish a claim, the presupposer forces the interlocutor to *challenge* what was never overtly asserted. The more implicit a presupposition, the harder it is to challenge without seeming to disrupt the conversational flow.
  - There are degrees of presuppositional implicitness: structural presuppositions (embedded in the grammatical form of a sentence/question) are harder to retract or challenge than lexical presuppositions (triggered by particular words like "stop," "know").
  - Why-questions carry *structural presuppositions* built into their interrogative form — the questioner is not asserting "there is an explanation" but the grammatical structure of the question presupposes it. This makes these presuppositions extremely difficult to challenge without refusing to engage with the question at all.
- **Assessment**: Recent, peer-reviewed (2023), directly relevant to the "violence" framing. The burden-of-proof reversal mechanism is precisely what makes loaded why-questions effective: the responder who answers has already conceded the presuppositions; the responder who challenges the presupposition bears the communicative cost of appearing to refuse engagement.

### Marie Duží & Martina Cíhalová, "Questions, Answers, and Presuppositions" (2015)
- **Link**: http://www.scielo.org.mx/scielo.php?pid=S1405-55462015000400647&script=sci_arttext; DOI: 10.13053/CyS-19-4-2327
- **Key content**: Uses Transparent Intensional Logic (TIL) to formally define presuppositions of different question types. Key finding on why-questions specifically: they treat "the whys and how comes" as a distinct class of presupposition trigger. The paper defines the presupposition of a question as what is entailed by every possible answer, but notes that for why-questions, *negative answers are structurally ambiguous* — you can answer "No, P didn't happen" (rejecting the topic) or "There is no explanation" (rejecting the explanatory presupposition). This ambiguity is diagnostic of why-questions' double presuppositional load. The paper also addresses how negative answers to why-questions are *not unambiguous*, unlike negative answers to yes/no questions.
- **Assessment**: Technical paper in computational logic (27 citations), but the philosophical analysis of why-questions as a special class is directly relevant. Confirms formally that why-questions carry multiple distinct presuppositional layers.

### Peter Lipton, "Contrastive Explanation" (1990) and "Inference to the Best Explanation" (1993)
- **Links**: *Royal Institute of Philosophy Supplement* (1990), 321 citations; book (1993), 962 citations
- **Key content**: Lipton develops van Fraassen's contrast class insight into a full theory of contrastive explanation: "Why P rather than Q?" (the *fact* P against the *foil* Q). The contrastive structure reveals that why-questions *select* a contrast class — and different selections of contrast class yield different explananda. Asking "Why did Sally rather than Jane develop cancer?" selects a different explanation than "Why did Sally develop cancer rather than being healthy?" The presupposition embedded in the contrast class determines what counts as a relevant explanation. Lipton argues that contrastive why-questions are more tractable than monadic ones precisely because the contrast class makes explicit what is otherwise implicit.
- **Assessment**: Highly influential in philosophy of science. The contrastive account exposes how the presuppositional frame of a why-question (via contrast class selection) can pre-determine what kind of explanation is sought — and thus which causal factors become invisible.

### Jonathan Adler, "Presupposition, Attention, and Why-Questions" (2008)
- **Link**: Chapter 36 in *Reasoning: Studies of Human Inference and Its Foundations* (Cambridge); https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2008-10943-036
- **Key content**: Adler argues that why-questions direct *attention* in ways that are epistemically consequential. The presupposition of a why-question channels the answerer's attention toward finding an explanation within a particular causal framework — and away from questioning whether the framework applies. This is the pragmatic mechanism of why-question violence: accepting the question (even to answer it critically) involves orienting one's attention toward the presupposed explanatory domain. Adler argues this makes why-question presuppositions *harder to cancel* than those of other question types: once attention is directed toward explanation-seeking, the metacognitive step of stepping back to challenge the presupposition is much more difficult. The harder the presupposition is to *notice*, the harder it is to challenge.
- **Assessment**: Book chapter (1 citation in Semantic Scholar, but widely cited in broader argumentation literature). Directly relevant and important. The attention mechanism offers a cognitive account of why even a sophisticated interlocutor may fail to challenge a loaded why-question's presuppositions.

### Ryan Cox, "How why-interrogatives work" (2019)
- **Link**: *Synthese*, DOI: 10.1007/s11229-019-02364-w
- **Key content**: Proposes that why-interrogatives are not just questions about causes but about *reasons* in an expanded sense — and that the presupposition of a why-question is that a *reason* (not just a cause) exists. This distinguishes why-questions from "how"-questions, which seek process descriptions without presupposing that reasons in the normative/teleological sense exist. Cox argues that why-questions carry a presupposition of explanatory *relevance* that is much stronger than the mere existential presupposition of other wh-questions.
- **Assessment**: Recent Synthese paper; not yet widely cited but appears to offer a useful contemporary synthesis.

### William Jaworski, "The logic of how-questions" (2007)
- **Link**: *Synthese*, DOI: 10.1007/s11229-007-9269-3
- **Key content**: Argues that how-questions presuppose only that a *process* or *mechanism* exists by which the explanandum occurred — not that a *reason* or *cause* in the stronger sense exists. "How did the Roman Empire fall?" presupposes only that there was some process; it does not presuppose that the fall was intelligible or explanatorily tractable in the same way "why did it fall?" does. How-questions are more easily answered by description; why-questions require justification.
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant for comparison with why-questions. Establishes the presuppositional distinction between how and why at the level of their request for explanation type.

### David Beaver, Craige Roberts, Mandy Simons, Judith Tonhauser, "Questions Under Discussion: Where Information Structure Meets Projective Content" (2017)
- **Link**: *Discourse & Dialogue* (Annual Review of Linguistics volume); DOI: 10.1146/annurev-linguistics-011516-033952; 84 citations
- **Key content**: The QUD (Question Under Discussion) framework provides a general account of how presuppositions project and when they can be challenged. Key insight: presuppositions project when their content is *not at-issue* — i.e., when it is not what the current discourse is treating as the question to be resolved. Why-questions structure a discourse so that the *existence of an explanation* is never at-issue (it is presupposed in the background), while the *identity of the explanation* is at-issue. This means: in any discourse framed by a why-question, it is *structurally impossible* to bring the existence-of-explanation presupposition into the foreground through a simple answer — you can only reject the question entirely. This is the formal mechanism of why why-question presuppositions are so hard to challenge within an ongoing discourse.
- **Assessment**: Highly regarded (84 citations), cutting-edge framework for thinking about presupposition in discourse. Directly applicable.

### Hintikka & Halonen, "Semantics and Pragmatics for Why-Questions" (1999)
- **Link**: In *Inquiry as Inquiry: A Logic of Scientific Discovery*, Springer, pp. 183-204; DOI: 10.1007/978-94-015-9313-7_9
- **Key content**: Hintikka approaches why-questions through his interrogative model of inquiry, treating them as requests for an *ultimate presupposition* — a set of premises that, together with background knowledge, would logically entail the explanandum. This puts why-questions at the core of scientific inquiry but also shows their special status: unlike what- or how-questions, why-questions demand a *complete* explanation rather than just a partial one. The presupposition here is that a *logically sufficient reason* exists.
- **Assessment**: By a major figure in formal epistemology. Complements van Fraassen and Bromberger.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The formal structure of presupposition in why-questions is well-studied across three domains — philosophy of science, philosophy of language/semantics, and argumentation theory — but these literatures rarely speak to each other.

**Philosophy of science** (Bromberger, van Fraassen, Hempel, Salmon, Lipton): the most developed account. There is consensus that why-questions carry a *distinctive tripartite presupposition structure* — topic (P is true), exclusivity (only P among the contrast class), and explanatory relevance (an explanation of type R exists). The third presupposition is unique to why-questions and has no parallel in other question types. Van Fraassen's framework is the canonical statement.

**Formal semantics of questions** (Hamblin, Groenendijk & Stokhof, inquisitive semantics): why-questions resist standard formal treatment and are studied separately from elementary questions. The existential presupposition of elementary questions — that some answer is true — is a pale shadow of why-questions' presupposition, which additionally asserts the existence of an *explanation* (not just any answer). Standard semantics largely sidesteps why-questions.

**Pragmatics and argumentation** (Stalnaker, Lewis, Macagno, Adler): the most relevant for the "violence" framing. Presupposition accommodation — the automatic and largely unconscious adjustment of the hearer's context to satisfy the speaker's presuppositions — is the mechanism by which loaded why-questions work. Macagno's burden-of-proof reversal and Adler's attention-direction mechanism together explain *why* challenging a why-question's presupposition is so costly: it requires refusing a form of engagement rather than making a counter-assertion.

### Agreements

- Why-questions presuppose at minimum that their topic is true (Bromberger, van Fraassen, all parties agree)
- Why-questions carry an additional presupposition: that an explanation exists (van Fraassen's third presupposition)
- This additional presupposition is not shared by what-, who-, when-, whether-, or how-questions in the same structural way
- The presuppositions of why-questions are relatively sticky — they resist cancellation and accommodation is easier than challenge (Tomioka, Macagno, Adler, SEP on presupposition)
- The contrast class (van Fraassen) is a hidden structural element that frames which explanations count as relevant, often without the answerer's explicit consent

### Conflicts and Tensions

- **Van Fraassen vs. Bromberger on what the presupposition is**: For Bromberger, the presupposition is primarily the topic being true (plus the formal conditions for a correct answer). For van Fraassen, there are three distinct presuppositions, including the context-sensitive relevance-relation presupposition. The van Fraassen account is richer but more context-dependent.
- **Semantic vs. pragmatic presupposition**: Whether why-question presuppositions are semantic (part of the linguistic meaning of "why") or pragmatic (arising from context and speaker commitments) is contested. The pragmatic account is more flexible but makes it harder to say definitively that why-questions *always* carry a particular presupposition.
- **Cancellability**: Some accounts treat presuppositions as defeasible (Gazdar's cancellation model) while others treat them as projection-resistant (Macagno's structural presuppositions). For why-questions, the formal evidence (Tomioka's intervention effects) suggests the explanatory presupposition is more resistant to cancellation than simple existential presuppositions.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **Loaded why-questions as a specific fallacy type**: Macagno (2023) addresses presuppositional fallacies in general but does not focus specifically on why-questions. "Why are women worse at math?" as a paradigm case of a why-question with a false and contestable presupposition is not systematically treated in the formal literature. This is the most significant gap for the research project's purposes.

2. **The contrast class as a locus of violence**: Van Fraassen identifies the contrast class as a presuppositional element, but the full rhetorical consequences of implicit contrast class selection are under-explored. When you ask "Why did the crime rate rise in Black neighborhoods?" rather than "Why did the crime rate rise in all neighborhoods?", the contrast class selection smuggles in a racialized framing. This mechanism is not studied in the philosophy of language literature.

3. **Why-questions vs. other question types**: A direct systematic comparison of presuppositional load across question types is lacking. Jaworski's (2007) paper on how-questions and Adler's (2008) chapter are the closest, but there is no comprehensive formal account.

4. **The QUD framework applied to why-questions specifically**: The Beaver et al. (2017) QUD framework is not explicitly applied to why-questions' distinctive presuppositions. This is a productive connection that seems not to have been made in the literature.

5. **Self-answering why-questions**: Questions like "Why did John do X?" often presuppose that John *did* X intentionally, or that there is an intention-based explanation. This teleological presupposition (exposed by Joo, Yousif, and Keil 2021) differs from the causal presupposition. The interaction between teleological and causal presuppositions in why-questions is under-theorized.

### Surprises

1. **Van Fraassen's third presupposition** — that at least one explanation of the right type exists — is strikingly strong. It means that every sincere why-question asserts the existence of an explanation. This is not a tentative epistemic commitment but a structural feature of the question form itself. The questioner who asks "Why are women worse at math?" is not merely wondering — they are presupposing that an explanation of women's mathematical inferiority exists (within whatever R is contextually salient: biological, psychological, sociological).

2. **The intractability of why-questions in formal semantics** is significant. Partition semantics and Hamblin semantics are well-developed for other wh-questions. The fact that why-questions require their own philosophical apparatus (van Fraassen's pragmatic theory, Bromberger's abnormic laws) suggests something genuinely distinctive about their logical structure.

3. **Tomioka's intervention effects evidence** provides *grammatical* evidence that why-question presuppositions are sticky in a way that goes beyond mere conversational pragmatics. The presupposition is grammatically encoded in a way that resists the usual quantificational operations. This is unexpected and strengthens the claim that why-questions are not just rhetorically but formally distinctive.

4. **The p-predicament as cognitive entrapment**: Bromberger's concept is rarely discussed outside philosophy of science, but it describes a very specific epistemic situation created by why-questions: the questioner who accepts the presuppositions is forced into a position of acknowledged structured ignorance — they know what they would need to know, and know they don't know it. This is a form of epistemic violence that is distinct from the false presupposition case.

5. **Accommodation is automatic**: The Lewis/Stalnaker account of accommodation makes clear that hearer adjustment to presuppositions is largely *unconscious*. This means that the violence of a loaded why-question operates below the level of explicit reasoning — it is not a fallacy that a careful reasoner can simply spot and reject.

**Gaps**: No directly focused academic work found on "loaded why-questions" as a specific subtype. The literature on loaded questions (complex question fallacy) is largely informal/popular or treats the phenomenon categorically without the specificity of why-questions. The intersection of van Fraassen's pragmatic theory with critical discourse analysis is essentially unexplored in academic literature.
