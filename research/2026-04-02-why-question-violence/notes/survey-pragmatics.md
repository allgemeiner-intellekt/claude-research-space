# Survey: How does the pragmatics of "why" function as a rhetorical and social act?

**Research question (Q4 from log.md)**: What does asking "why" *do* to the conversational space? Beyond formal logic, "why" questions have a performative dimension — accusatory, presupposition-imposing, potentially violent.

---

## Summary

The landscape here is surprisingly rich and cross-disciplinary. Conversation analysis (CA) has directly studied why-questions as account solicitations with built-in disaffiliative potential. Critical discourse analysis (CDA) has touched the issue obliquely through presupposition and power, but rarely focuses on "why" per se. The clearest and most directly relevant scholarly cluster is in CA, especially Bolden & Robinson (2011), Koshik (2003), and Egbert & Vöge (2008). Epistemic injustice literature (Fricker, Teo) offers a powerful frame for why "why"-type questions about groups do harm. Levinas provides phenomenological grounding for why comprehension itself can be violent. Motivational interviewing and educational research offer practical evidence that the "why" question creates defensive, accountability-laden conversational space. The weakest link in the existing literature is the precise connection between CDA's treatment of presupposition and the specific pragmatics of why-interrogatives — this gap is worth a deep dive.

## Key Sources

### 1. Bolden & Robinson, "Soliciting Accounts With Why-Interrogatives in Conversation" (2011)
- **Link**: DOI: 10.1111/J.1460-2466.2010.01528.X | PDF: https://gbolden.comminfo.rutgers.edu/RussianCA/publications/Bolden_Robinson11.pdf
- **Key content**: The core CA paper on why-interrogatives as account solicitations. Argues that why-questions are "Janus-faced": they simultaneously index an epistemic gap (the questioner doesn't know why) *and* claim epistemic access to the accountable event (the questioner knows *that* something happened, and it was accountable). The result: why-questions carry a built-in challenging stance and are associated with disaffiliative acts such as criticizing and blaming. The canonical example, "Why did you do that?", is not informationally neutral — it encodes that the prior act requires justification. Published in *Journal of Communication*, 149 citations.
- **Assessment**: This is the most directly relevant empirical paper for Q4. Peer-reviewed, high-citation, methodologically rigorous (conversation analysis of natural interaction). The "Janus-faced" framing is precise and useful: it captures the double move of why-questions (information-seeking surface + epistemic challenge beneath).

### 2. Koshik, "Wh-Questions Used as Challenges" (2003)
- **Link**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/14614456030050010301 | DOI: 10.1177/14614456030050010301
- **Key content**: A CA study demonstrating that wh-questions (including "why") can function not as information requests at all, but as challenges to a prior utterance. Specifically, these questions convey a strong *negative epistemic stance* — the questioner is asserting that there is no adequate account available, and thus no grounds for the prior claim or action. The question form disguises what is actually a challenge or rejection. The argument: by requesting an account, the questioner implies none will be satisfactory. Published in *Discourse Studies*; widely cited.
- **Assessment**: Directly relevant. Shows how why-questions can be used to challenge the *legitimacy* of what someone has done or said — not just to seek information. This explains why "why" feels aggressive: it is often doing the work of saying "there is no good reason for what you did/said."

### 3. Egbert & Vöge, "Wh-interrogative formats used for questioning and beyond: German *warum* (why) and *wieso* (why) and English *why*" (2008)
- **Link**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/1461445607085583 | DOI: 10.1177/1461445607085583
- **Key content**: A contrastive CA study showing that two German near-synonyms for "why" — *warum* and *wieso* — function very differently interactionally: *wieso* is affiliative (genuine information request), while *warum* is complaint-implicative and disaffiliative (points to something wrong). English "why" is ambiguous between these two functions — unlike German, it allows the speaker to be unclear about whether they are requesting or challenging. Crucially, in business meetings, complaint-implicative *warum* is correlated with leadership status: superiors use it, subordinates don't. This maps complaint/challenge permission onto hierarchy. Published in *Discourse Studies*.
- **Assessment**: Highly relevant — especially the hierarchy finding. It suggests that who is *permitted* to ask the challenging "why" is socially structured. The German/English contrast also illuminates why English "why" is so flexible and therefore so potentially dangerous: its ambiguity gives the questioner deniability ("I was just asking!") while the recipient still receives the challenging force.

### 4. Schegloff, "On Some Questions and Ambiguities in Conversation" (1984) — cited in Egbert & Vöge
- **Link**: In J.M. Atkinson and J. Heritage (eds), *Structures of Social Action: Studies in Conversation Analysis*, pp. 28–52
- **Key content**: Schegloff's foundational CA analysis of how questions work in conversation, including the ambiguity of why-questions between information-seeking and challenging. Also relevant: his separate paper "The Limits of Questioning: Negative Interrogatives and Hostile Question Content" (2002, *Journal of Pragmatics*): https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0378216602000723
- **Assessment**: Canonical foundational source. The 2002 paper on negative interrogatives and hostile question content directly addresses when questions become aggressive — treating the interrogative form as a vehicle for hostility.

### 5. Clayman & Heritage, *The News Interview* (2002) and related work
- **Link**: https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/news-interview (book); also "Questioning Presidents" (2002) PDF: https://clayman.scholar.ss.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/15/2019/07/Clayman-and-Heritage-2002-Questioning-Presidents.pdf
- **Key content**: Systematic analysis of how journalists use questioning strategies — including "why" questions — to hold politicians accountable. "Why" is a key tool of adversarial questioning: it demands a justification, placing the burden of proof on the interviewee. Clayman's "hardballs and softballs" paper (Benjamins) shows how the adversarialness of questions can be modulated. The "why" question is among the hardest of hardballs. Published with Cambridge UP.
- **Assessment**: Authoritative academic work. Establishes the journalistic/institutional use of "why" as an accountability mechanism. Relevant because it shows "why" is not just accidental in its coercive force — in institutional settings it is deliberately deployed as pressure.

### 6. Brown & Levinson, *Politeness: Some Universals in Language Usage* (1987)
- **Link**: Cambridge University Press (textbook/monograph, 3581+ citations in Fricker entry alone)
- **Key content**: The foundational politeness theory framework. Questions are identified as face-threatening acts (FTAs) against positive face (they imply the questioner doesn't know what the hearer thinks/has done) and sometimes against negative face (they impose an obligation to respond). "Why" questions have a particularly high FTA load because they combine the information-requesting burden with an implied challenge to the adequacy of the addressee's conduct.
- **Assessment**: This is canonical theory — any pragmatics work must cite it. The framework doesn't focus specifically on "why," but it provides the vocabulary (face, FTA, redressive action) that all subsequent work uses.

### 7. Fricker, *Epistemic Injustice: Power and the Ethics of Knowing* (2007)
- **Link**: Oxford University Press (monograph) — DOI in Semantic Scholar: paperId f7e21d707f096cd46e3fe33464a3ca2db741c075 — 3581 citations
- **Key content**: Fricker identifies two forms of epistemic injustice: *testimonial injustice* (deflated credibility given to a speaker because of their identity) and *hermeneutical injustice* (gaps in collective interpretive resources that disadvantage certain speakers from making sense of their experiences). The why-question connection: asking "why do you people do X?" combines testimonial injustice (treating the person as a spokesperson for their group) with hermeneutical injustice (imposing a conceptual framework that presupposes the problematic phenomenon rather than the subject's own experience). While Fricker doesn't focus on "why" questions specifically, the framework maps cleanly onto the harm.
- **Assessment**: Canonical, high-citation philosophical monograph. The extension to why-questions is mine/the research team's — Fricker doesn't make it explicit. Worth noting as a productive gap.

### 8. Teo, "Empirical Race Psychology and the Hermeneutics of Epistemological Violence" (2011)
- **Link**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10746-011-9179-8 | *Human Studies* (Springer)
- **Key content**: Teo argues that certain research questions in empirical psychology constitute "epistemological violence" — harm caused not by explicit racism but by the *questions* researchers choose to ask, which embed harmful presuppositions. Specifically: asking "why do racial groups differ in X?" without first establishing that the measurement is valid, that the categories are meaningful, and that the difference is real, produces interpretations that reinforce racial stereotypes. The violence lies in the hermeneutic deficit: an impoverished interpretive framework applied to the data. Teo connects to Gadamer and hermeneutics to argue that the problem is interpretive, not merely methodological.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed journal article in *Human Studies* (phenomenology/human science focus). Directly relevant — probably the most pointed academic formulation of the specific idea that "why" questions about groups can constitute violence. Connects presupposition (the question assumes the phenomenon is real) with epistemic harm.

### 9. Brodsky & Lichtenstein, "Don't Ask Questions: A Psychotherapeutic Strategy for Treatment of Involuntary Clients" (1999)
- **Link**: https://psychiatryonline.org/doi/10.1176/appi.psychotherapy.1999.53.2.215 | *American Journal of Psychotherapy*, 53(2), pp. 215–224
- **Key content**: Argues that asking questions — especially "why" questions — is detrimental to therapy with unwilling clients. The paper explicitly identifies "why" as a particularly poor therapeutic intervention because it initiates a "futile search for causation" and creates defensive responses. It recommends using statements rather than questions with reluctant clients. This is a clinical operationalization of the intuition that "why" questions feel like accusations.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed clinical paper. The claim is narrow (involuntary clients) but the reasoning generalizes: "why" questions create a context of accountability that can shut down rather than open up dialogue. Relevant as evidence from a clinical/applied domain.

### 10. Resnicow & McMaster, "Motivational Interviewing: Moving from Why to How with Autonomy Support" (2012)
- **Link**: https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3330017/
- **Key content**: Explicitly frames the shift from "why" to "how" questions as a therapeutic principle. MI training explicitly avoids asking clients "why" they engage in problematic behavior, because such questions invite defensiveness and feel judgmental. Instead, MI practitioners ask open-ended questions that are not causal attributions. The paper grounds this in self-determination theory: "why" questions threaten autonomy by implying that the client must justify their behavior.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed; published in *International Journal of Behavioral Nutrition and Physical Activity*. This is evidence that the coercive force of "why" has been recognized in applied clinical work strongly enough to generate a prohibition.

### 11. Melhuish, Strickland, Han & Sorto, "Why Ask Why? An Analysis of Teachers' Why-Questions in Elementary and Middle Grade Mathematics Classrooms" (2024)
- **Link**: https://doi.org/10.1007/s10857-024-09644-4 | *Journal of Mathematics Teacher Education* (Springer)
- **Key content**: An analysis of 61 mathematics lessons (grades 4–8). Finds that "why" questions from teachers are not uniformly productive — they vary enormously in the kind of response they elicit, from recalled fact to rich justification. Crucially, the same linguistic form ("why") can operate as rote-recall drill or as genuine inquiry prompt depending on contextual features. This complicates the assumption that "why" = higher-order thinking. The paper also notes that why-questions have often been treated as automatic markers of good teaching, but this is an oversimplification.
- **Assessment**: Very recent peer-reviewed paper. The finding that "why" questions can actually reduce student activity (when structured to have a fixed "right answer") is relevant to the question of whether why-questions open or close inquiry. The pedagogical literature is less focused on the *coercive* dimension than on the epistemic one, but both point in the same direction.

### 12. Carlsen, "Questioning in Classrooms: A Sociolinguistic Perspective" (1991 / cited 2024)
- **Link**: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.3102/00346543061002157 | *Review of Educational Research*
- **Key content**: A sociolinguistic analysis of how teachers' questions function in classrooms. Notes that high-status questioners (teachers) can use questions as control mechanisms. Questions in classrooms — particularly demand-for-justification questions — reinforce power asymmetries. The IRE (Initiation-Response-Evaluation) sequence is itself a structure of accountability: every student answer is subject to evaluation.
- **Assessment**: Academic, peer-reviewed. The IRE framework (Mehan 1979; Sinclair & Coulthard 1975) establishes that questions in institutional settings are not exchanges of information but rather rituals of accountability and evaluation. "Why" questions within IRE sequences are particularly high-stakes because they demand reasoning, not just recall.

### 13. Altez, "Banal and Implied Forms of Violence in Levinas' Phenomenological Ethics" (2007)
- **Link**: https://www.kritike.org/journal/issue_1/altez_june2007.pdf | *KRITIKE* 1(1): 52–70
- **Key content**: Analyzes Levinas's account of violence as not only physical but also conceptual and relational. For Levinas, the totalizing movement of comprehension — reducing the Other to what can be grasped within one's own conceptual system — is already a form of violence. The "face" (le visage) overflows any concept; to ask "what are you?" or "why are you this way?" in a mode that presupposes an already-fixed answer is to commit violence against the Other's irreducible alterity. Levinas writes against "ontological imperialism" — the colonization of the Other by the categories of the Same.
- **Assessment**: A secondary source (philosophical journal, 2007). The Levinas connection is real but requires care: Levinas does not discuss "why" questions specifically — the connection is at the level of the "totalizing gaze" of comprehension. However, the journal article does engage directly with the concept of "implied violence" in Levinas, which is exactly the register the research question uses.

### 14. Teo, "What Is Epistemological Violence in the Empirical Social Sciences?" (2010)
- **Link**: https://compass.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1751-9004.2010.00265.x | *Social and Personality Psychology Compass*
- **Key content**: A companion piece to the 2011 paper. Defines epistemological violence more broadly as harm caused by scientific practices — including the choice of research questions — that embed and reproduce harmful assumptions about marginalized groups. The connection to "why" questions: any "why does group X have property Y?" question that bypasses the establishment of the phenomenon smuggles in an explanatory framework before the descriptive work has been done.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed review article. Broader and somewhat more programmatic than the 2011 paper, but provides the theoretical foundation.

### 15. Walton, "The Fallacy of Many Questions: On the Notions of Complexity, Loadedness and Unfair Entrapment in Interrogative Theory" (1999)
- **Link**: DOI: 10.1023/A:1007727929716 | *Argumentation* 13: 379–383
- **Key content**: Argumentation theory analysis of "complex questions" (loaded questions) as a formal fallacy. The "many questions" fallacy involves a question that presupposes an unanswered or contested claim. "Why did you stop beating your wife?" is the classic example. Walton shows that the fallacious character of such questions lies in *unfair entrapment*: any answer the respondent gives concedes the presupposition. Relevant to why-questions: "why does group X do Y?" entraps the respondent in a presupposition about Y.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed academic paper in argumentation/informal logic. Provides the formal machinery for understanding how "why" questions can be weapons of entrapment rather than genuine inquiry.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of knowledge

The pragmatic "violence" of "why" questions is well-attested across multiple disciplines, though no single field has theorized it comprehensively. The strongest work comes from conversation analysis (CA), where the disaffiliative and accountability-imposing force of why-interrogatives has been documented with precision. The weakest coverage is on the specific harm of why-questions about social groups — that territory is addressed more broadly under "epistemic violence" (Teo, Fricker) than under pragmatics specifically.

### Agreements across sources

All relevant sources agree on three things:
1. "Why" questions are not informationally neutral — they carry a stance (the questioner has already determined that the event requires explanation/justification).
2. "Why" questions create asymmetric accountability: the questioned party must justify themselves to the questioner.
3. This accountability structure can be weaponized — in therapy, law, politics, and everyday life, "why" is used to entrap rather than to inquire.

### Conflicts and tensions

One tension: CA work (Koshik, Bolden & Robinson) focuses on naturally-occurring conversation and shows context-sensitivity — "why" can be genuinely affiliative in some uses. The "why" questions that are clearly disaffiliative are those that are *complaint-implicative*: they follow something the questioner objects to. Pure information-seeking "why" (Egbert & Vöge's *wieso*) is not inherently coercive. This matters because it means the *violence* of "why" is not intrinsic to the word but to the interactional frame. The research question is sharper than a claim that all why-questions are violent.

Another tension: The phenomenological/Levinasian analysis operates at a level of abstraction quite distant from the CA microanalysis. Connecting them requires care. Levinas's "violence of comprehension" is about totalizing thought, not specifically interrogative acts. The bridge has to be built, not assumed.

### Gaps and under-explored areas

**Major gap**: No literature directly connects the presuppositional structure of "why" questions (as analyzed in formal linguistics/philosophy of language — the Q1 territory) with their social function as instruments of epistemic harm. The philosophy-of-language work (Stalnaker, van Fraassen, the chapter by Adler in the Cambridge *Reasoning* volume) treats presupposition as a semantic/pragmatic property without asking about power. The CDA work treats power but rarely parses "why" specifically versus other question types.

**Under-explored**: The *taxonomic* why-question ("why does group X do/have Y?") versus the *action-directed* why-question ("why did you do that?"). Both are accusatory but in structurally different ways. The taxonomic form is perhaps more dangerous because it naturalizes a group-level fact before explaining it — but this specific form is undertheorized.

**Under-explored**: The question of whether why-questions can *also* open up inquiry — the "5 whys" in lean manufacturing, Socratic "why" in pedagogy. The literature skews toward pathological uses. A full account needs to explain when "why" liberates versus when it forecloses.

**Under-explored**: Legal interrogation. Cross-examination "why" questions as coercive speech acts deserve specific treatment.

### Surprises

1. The finding from Egbert & Vöge that complain-implicative "why" is a *leadership prerogative* in business meetings — superiors use it, subordinates don't — is striking. It means the right to demand an account is itself a form of power.

2. Motivational interviewing's explicit prohibition of "why" questions is an unusual case where a clinical field has encoded, in professional training, the interactional harm that linguists have described theoretically.

3. The Melhuish et al. (2024) mathematics education finding that why-questions in classrooms are not uniformly more demanding than other question types — that their function depends heavily on context — complicates any simple narrative. "Why" is not automatically a higher-order inquiry tool; it can be a recall drill with an interrogative face.

4. The connection between Fricker's *hermeneutical injustice* and the taxonomic "why" question is surprisingly direct but has apparently not been made explicitly in the literature — a productive gap.
