# Survey: Anton Korinek — Intellectual Profile and Research Agenda

Surveyed: 2026-04-07. Sources: korinek.com, econtai.org CV (Aug 2025 update), Semantic Scholar, NBER Reporter Dec 2024, LessWrong transcript of Korinek lecture (Jan 2026), IMF Finance & Development Dec 2023, MIT Economics (Acemoglu), EA Forum critique of Acemoglu, TIME Magazine profile (Apr 2024).

---

## Summary

Korinek is a legitimate academic economist (Columbia PhD 2007, Stiglitz student, h-index 31 per Semantic Scholar) who pivoted from macroprudential/international finance toward AI economics around 2017–2018 and has since built the most visible single-person platform in that space within the economics profession. His work spans three distinct modes: (1) theoretical macro models of automation and growth, (2) practical AI toolkits for economists (the JEL series), and (3) policy/advocacy writing. Modes 1 and 2 have genuine intellectual content; mode 3 is largely agenda-setting. He is institutionally at UVA Economics + Darden (primary appointment since 2021, tenured), runs the EconTAI initiative he founded Sep 2025, and holds advisory/affiliate positions at Brookings, NBER, CEPR, GovAI (former), Anthropic Economic Advisory Council (2025–), and the Stanford Digital Economy Lab. He is actively advising PhD students at UVA. His main intellectual tension is with Acemoglu, who represents the dominant skeptical school in labor economics; they share the same basic modeling tradition but differ dramatically on priors about AI capability progress.

### 2. NBER Reporter: "The Economics of Transformative AI" (Dec 2024)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/reporter/2024number4/economics-transformative-ai
- **Key content**: Self-authored research summary. Lays out his two-pronged agenda: (1) analyzing transformative AI's economic implications, (2) using AI to enhance economic research. Explicitly organizes his work around three parallels to the Malthusian-to-Industrial transition: land was the bottleneck, then labor became the bottleneck, now AI may make labor reproducible and thus no longer the bottleneck. States the Korinek-Suh simulation: business-as-usual vs. AGI-in-20-years vs. AGI-in-5-years scenarios show wages initially rising then "plummeting" as automation crosses a threshold.
- **Assessment**: Authoritative first-person synthesis of his own agenda. Useful for understanding how he frames his work internally. No original analysis here — it is a research program description.

### 3. LessWrong transcript of Korinek lecture (Jan 2026)
- **Link**: https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/epFKhn24trRP2cs3k/the-economics-of-transformative-ai
- **Key content**: Lightly edited lecture transcript, described by the LessWrong editors as "the best introductory resource on the topic." Korinek explicitly contrasts himself with Acemoglu: "If we ask the most cited economist in the world, Daron Acemoglu, he published a paper last year on 'The Simple Macroeconomics of AI' in which he predicted that AI will affect growth by raising it by 0.07% a year. So he does not believe that this is going to be very transformative. He thinks AI is essentially BS. But that means there is more work for us to be done." — This is his clearest statement of the intellectual stakes. The lecture also covers the three-channel model (tech progress, automation, capital accumulation), the wage-collapse threshold simulation, the tax base problem (labor income tax eroding), and the "the economy doesn't need us" uncomfortable conclusion.
- **Assessment**: This is a public intellectual performance more than a research paper, but it is extremely clear and honest about uncertainties. Remarkably, it was received positively in the LessWrong/EA community (64 upvotes, no substantive objections in comments). The transcript reveals how much of his work is conceptual scaffolding and qualitative framing vs. formal results.

### 4. "Scenarios for the Transition to AGI" (Korinek & Suh, NBER WP 32255, Mar 2024)
- **Link**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.12107 (open access) / https://www.nber.org/papers/w32255
- **Key content** (from abstract + lecture transcript): Models output and wages under different technological scenarios culminating in AGI, defined as AI that can perform ALL human tasks. Key modeling assumption: human work decomposes into "atomistic tasks" of varying complexity. Technological progress automates increasingly complex tasks over time. Central result depends on whether human task complexity has a "sufficiently thick infinite tail" (humans always find new work, wages rise forever) or is bounded (full automation eventually reached, wages collapse). Before that bound, wages can still collapse if automation outpaces capital accumulation, making labor "too abundant." The three scenarios: (I) business as usual — AI boosts productivity but humans move into new tasks; (II) baseline AGI in ~20 years — labor gradually devalued; (III) aggressive AGI in ~5 years. In AGI scenarios, output potentially grows 10x but wages plummet. Key subtlety: "automating productivity growth" (R&D automation) leads to broad gains for all factors; but "irreproducible scarce factors" (land, energy, rare earths) may exacerbate wage decline. Citation count: 41 (as of Apr 2026), published less than 2 years ago.
- **Assessment**: This is Korinek's most formally serious AI paper. The model is in the Acemoglu-Restrepo task-based tradition — it is not a departure from mainstream macro methodology. The novelty is in the specific scenario analysis of what happens as automation approaches 100% of tasks, which the Acemoglu school has largely refused to model because it treats AGI-level automation as outside the 10-year planning horizon. Donghyun Suh (co-author) was Korinek's own PhD student (graduated 2024, so this was likely his dissertation work). The 41 citations are moderate — it is being cited but is not yet a field-defining paper. Labor economists treat it as interesting but speculative; longtermist/EA circles treat it as important foundation.

### 5. "Generative AI for Economic Research: Use Cases and Implications for Economists" (JEL 2023, with rolling updates)
- **Link**: https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/jel.20231736 (lead article JEL 61(4), Dec 2023); companion site genaiforecon.org
- **Key content**: 168 citations per Semantic Scholar, published as the lead article in the Journal of Economic Literature — a very high-profile slot. Catalogs dozens of LLM use cases across ideation, writing, background research, data analysis, coding, mathematical derivations. Classifies each as experimental to highly useful. Does not present new empirical results or theory. The paper is a practitioner guide written by someone who had clearly spent a lot of time using these tools as of early-to-mid 2023 (GPT-4 era). Has been updated four times: "LLMs Level Up" (Jun 2024), "LLMs Learn to Collaborate and Reason" (Dec 2024), "AI Agents for Economic Research" (Aug 2025). The 2024 updates cover reasoning improvements and multi-agent collaborative workspaces; the 2025 update covers agent-based workflows.
- **Assessment**: The novelty was primarily in timing and context-setting — being the first prominent economist to systematically demonstrate LLM workflows for research, in the field's top survey journal. It aged reasonably well in structure (the categories are still valid), but many of the specific examples were overtaken by rapid progress. The updates have successfully kept pace. This is not a theoretical contribution — it is a practitioner guide that served a bridging function between AI capabilities and the econ profession. High citation count reflects its use as a teaching/reference document rather than as an intellectual foundation. Korinek explicitly notes the paper is accompanied by CNN coverage — he is fully aware of his public-intellectual function here. SURPRISING FLAG: The paper is being perpetually updated in a living-document style unusual for JEL. This is either innovative or a sign that the intellectual content is hard to pin down formally.

### 6. "Artificial Intelligence and Its Implications for Income Distribution and Unemployment" (Korinek & Stiglitz, NBER WP 24174, 2017; published in Economics of AI NBER volume 2019)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w24174/w24174.pdf
- **Key content**: 310 citations. The foundational Korinek-Stiglitz AI paper. Provides a systematic overview of implications of "worker-replacing" technological progress for inequality. Distinguishes types of AI progress by which workers they complement vs. replace. Argues that without redistribution, worker-replacing AI can generate explosive growth and dramatically increase inequality. Policy: robot taxation, profit-sharing, pre-distribution through asset ownership. Method is standard macro with heterogeneous agents.
- **Assessment**: This was agenda-setting at the time. It was in the Agrawal-Gans-Goldfarb NBER volume that launched the economics-of-AI subfield. The citations are strong. However, the framework is not novel — it is applying standard factor-biased technological change theory (Acemoglu-style) to the AI context. Korinek and Stiglitz were doing exactly what Acemoglu was doing at the same time, but with more alarm about the distributional consequences. Stiglitz co-authorship is a double-edged sword: it gave the paper visibility and institutional weight but also signals that this work lives in a heterodox-adjacent space (Stiglitz is not mainstream labor econ).

### 7. "Steering Technological Progress" (Korinek & Stiglitz, updated Apr 2025; NBER WP 34994, Mar 2026)
- **Link**: https://www.ineteconomics.org/research/research-papers/steering-technological-progress
- **Key content**: 10 citations (very recent). Analyzes how to guide innovative efforts to increase labor demand and create better-paying jobs. Develops a theoretical framework identifying what makes an innovation "worker-friendly": technological complementarity to labor, factor share of labor in producing the goods, relative income of affected workers. Applications: robot taxation, factor-augmenting progress, task automation. Key finding: steering becomes more desirable when safety nets are weak; if labor value falls enough, policy should shift from steering to redistribution. Cited by The Economist's Free Exchange.
- **Assessment**: This is Korinek's most theoretically ambitious recent paper. The framework goes beyond the standard automation-vs-complementarity dichotomy by trying to give criteria for when steering is efficient vs. futile. It is genuinely normative economic theory. However, it is a Stiglitz collaboration again, which means it will be seen skeptically by the mainstream of the profession. The 10 citations reflect its recency.

### 8. "Economic Growth under Transformative AI" (Korinek & Trammell, NBER WP 31815, Oct 2023; requested by Annual Review of Economics)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/papers/w31815
- **Key content**: Invited for the Annual Review of Economics — a clear sign of the field's legitimacy. Co-authored with Philip Trammell (Oxford GPI — a longtermist economist). Surveys the range of possible output growth, wage, and labor share outcomes under scenarios where AI is "truly transformative." The paper is a typology rather than a single model — it covers Malthusian analogies, semi-endogenous vs. fully endogenous growth, the role of irreproducible factors, and the concept of an "intelligence explosion."
- **Assessment**: Being co-authored with Trammell from the Global Priorities Institute (Oxford) signals an explicit connection to the longtermist intellectual ecosystem. This is neither hidden nor apologized for — it is a feature of Korinek's positioning. The paper is conceptually rich and the Annual Review invitation is a strong institutional signal. However, it is a theoretical survey paper, not an empirical or formal contribution.

### 9. "Concentrating Intelligence: Scaling and Market Structure in Artificial Intelligence" (Korinek & Vipra, Economic Policy 40(121), Jan 2025)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/papers/w33139
- **Key content**: Published in Economic Policy (CEPR journal). Examines market concentration in AI, potential for market tipping, risks from vertical integration. Uses the industrial organization literature on natural monopolies and network effects. Jai Vipra is a governance/policy researcher (not a mainstream economist). The paper identifies AI training compute as having very large fixed costs and falling marginal costs — a classic natural monopoly structure — and argues this creates tipping dynamics where a few incumbents pull ahead permanently.
- **Assessment**: This is Korinek's foray into IO economics of AI. It is applied and policy-relevant. The vertical integration analysis is timely (given OpenAI, Google, Meta consolidation). Not a deeply original theoretical contribution but a useful mapping of existing IO theory onto the AI industry. Citation count not available (recently published).

### 10. Acemoglu, "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI" (Economic Policy, Aug 2024)
- **Link**: https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/2024-10/The%20Simple%20Macroeconomics%20of%20AI.pdf
- **Key content**: Acemoglu's entry in the same genre as Korinek. Models AI's GDP effect: estimates ~1.1-1.6% cumulative GDP boost over 10 years (0.05% annual TFP gain). Based on: 20% of tasks exposed (Eloundou et al.), 23% of those profitably automatable within 10 years (MIT FutureTech), 27% average cost savings from experiments. Explicitly excludes: deepening automation, new tasks/products, science acceleration. Acemoglu's self-defense: "I tried to write the paper in a very transparent way... people can disagree by saying the things I excluded are a big deal."
- **Assessment**: This is the methodological polar opposite of Korinek. Acemoglu grounds every number empirically in current GPT-3.5/4 studies and refuses to extrapolate. Korinek explicitly argues this is the wrong approach — AGI scenarios must be modeled even if they seem implausible today, because the consequences are large enough to warrant stress-testing institutions. The EA Forum critique ("Contra Acemoglu on AI" by Maxwell Tabarrok, Jun 2024) makes the strongest case against Acemoglu: he excludes exactly the channels most likely to matter (deepening automation, new tasks, science acceleration) without adequate justification. Notably, Korinek uses Acemoglu as a foil in his LessWrong lecture, calling him "the most cited economist in the world" who "thinks AI is essentially BS" — a respectful but clear signal of disagreement. Neither has published a direct formal rebuttal of the other.

### 11. TIME Magazine profile of Korinek (Apr 2024)
- **Link**: https://time.com/6966882/economist-ai-transformation-society-anton-korinek
- **Key content**: Profiles Korinek as "The Economist Breaking Ranks to Sound the Alarm on AI." Framing: mainstream economists are optimists (Acemoglu's 0.07% GDP gain vs. transformative scenarios). Korinek is presented as a credentialed insider who takes AGI seriously. Named to TIME100 AI 2025.
- **Assessment**: This is a public-intellectual profile, not a research assessment. But it is a data point on his reception: TIME treats him as the "alarmed economist" vs. Acemoglu's "skeptical economist." This framing serves Korinek's policy agenda but may also limit him academically — being the "AI alarmist economist" is a brand that can crowd out appreciation of his formal contributions.

### 12. Senate Testimony, AI Insight Forum on Workforce (Nov 2023)
- **Link**: https://www.brookings.edu/articles/anton-korineks-statement-to-the-u-s-senate-ai-insight-forum-on-workforce/
- **Key content**: Testimony to the US Senate (organized by Senate Majority Leader Schumer). Argues for scenario planning including "transformative scenarios in which AI can automate most work within 5 to 20 years." Recommends two policies: (1) steering AI progress toward labor-complementary forms; (2) reforming income allocation institutions for potential post-labor scenarios.
- **Assessment**: This is advocacy work, not research. It shows his policy reach. He was invited to the Senate when Acemoglu was not — this is partly because Korinek's position (take AGI seriously) is more politically legible than Acemoglu's (it's probably fine but we shouldn't rush). Policy influence: meaningful.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge on Korinek's Research Agenda

The AI-economics field is genuinely young (~2017 onward) and fractured between two camps:

**Camp 1 (Acemoglu/Autor)**: Ground all analysis in current empirical evidence on task exposure, wage effects from robots, etc. 10-year horizon. Conclusion: AI impact is real but modest; more concerning is what kind of AI we build than whether AI advances. Labor economists dominate this camp. Acemoglu's "Simple Macroeconomics" is their flagship.

**Camp 2 (Korinek/Brynjolfsson/Agrawal-Gans-Goldfarb)**: Argue we must model high-impact/low-probability scenarios including near-AGI. Historical analogies (Malthusian transition) justify taking transformative scenarios seriously. Growth economists and management scholars dominate this camp.

Korinek occupies an interesting hybrid position: he uses standard macro methodology (task models, factor accumulation) but applies it to scenarios that Camp 1 regards as science fiction for policy purposes.

### What Is Genuinely Innovative vs. What Is Advocacy

**Genuinely innovative (or at minimum, load-bearing formal work):**
- The Korinek-Suh (2024) paper is the clearest example of formal modeling of AGI transition dynamics within a mainstream macro framework. The insight that wages can collapse *before* full AGI if automation outpaces capital accumulation is non-obvious and formally derived.
- The Korinek-Trammell (2023) paper on growth scenarios is a useful typological contribution, invited by a top review journal — this signals peer recognition.
- The Korinek-Stiglitz "Steering" paper (2025) tries to give formal criteria for when technology steering is efficient vs. futile — this is a theoretical contribution, though the proof is in whether it influences subsequent work.
- The "Concentrating Intelligence" paper (2025) applies IO theory to AI market structure — useful applied work, though not deeply original theory.
- The Pecuniary Externalities paper (RES 2018, with Davila) and the Macroprudential Regulation paper (RES 2020, with Jeanne) are his best formal economics work, full stop — but they predate the AI pivot.

**Primarily advocacy/agenda-setting/bridging:**
- The JEL "Generative AI" series: valuable bridge function, not a research contribution in the traditional sense. It is a practitioner guide. The living-document model means it tracks progress rather than making a durable contribution.
- The IMF Finance & Development article (Dec 2023): explicitly advocacy for scenario planning as a policy approach. No original formal content.
- Senate testimony: pure advocacy.
- The "Economic Policy Challenges for the Age of AI" (NBER WP 32980, 2024): a conceptual overview, not a formal model. Being included in an MIT Press volume is a prestige signal, not an intellectual one.
- The Oxford Handbook editorship: institutional position-building, not research.

**Somewhere in between:**
- The "AI's Economic Peril to Democracy" paper (Journal of Democracy, with Bell): applies inequality dynamics to democratic stability — genuine interdisciplinary insight, but not a formal economic contribution. The Journal of Democracy is a political science venue.
- The "Aligned with Whom?" paper (with Balwit, Oxford Handbook of AI Governance): conceptually interesting distinction between direct and social AI alignment, but very short and not formal.

### Agreements and Conflicts Between Sources

- Korinek and Acemoglu agree on the basic task-based framework. They disagree on priors about AI capability trajectory and what scenarios deserve policy preparation.
- Korinek explicitly frames Acemoglu as "wrong" in his lecture (diplomatically: "he thinks AI is essentially BS"). Acemoglu has not publicly responded directly to Korinek's scenario work.
- The LessWrong/EA community is receptive to Korinek's analysis — the Jan 2026 transcript is called "the best introductory resource on the topic." This is a significant departure from mainstream econ's view.
- The Anthropic advisory council appointment (2025) is the clearest signal of where Korinek sits institutionally: he is trusted by the leading AI safety lab but also by NBER and Brookings. He bridges.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

- **No empirical work**: All of Korinek's AI papers are theoretical or practitioner-oriented. He has not published an empirical paper on AI's actual labor market effects (unlike Acemoglu/Autor who have robot exposure papers, etc.). This is a notable gap. His AI research agenda is almost entirely either macro theory or qualitative framing.
- **Timing assumptions are a black box**: The 5-year vs. 20-year AGI scenarios are presented with ">10% probability" but no formal probability model or calibration. This is the weakest link in the Korinek-Suh paper — the scenarios are interesting but their probability structure is asserted, not derived.
- **The Stiglitz connection**: Multiple high-profile papers co-authored with Joseph Stiglitz (2019, 2021, 2025). Stiglitz is a Nobel laureate but is seen as heterodox and politically engaged. These papers get press coverage and reach policymakers, but mainstream labor economists may discount them. This is a reputational trade-off Korinek has explicitly made.
- **The GovAI-to-Anthropic trajectory**: Korinek was "Economics of AI Lead" at GovAI (Oxford) 2021–2024, and is now on Anthropic's Economic Advisory Council (2025). GovAI/Anthropic are AI safety organizations. This is not a conflict per se, but it means his institutional environment reinforces transformative-AI views rather than challenging them. He is not embedded in a labor economics department environment where colleagues would push back on his priors.

### Surprises

1. **He is a genuine bridge between longtermism and mainstream economics** — more than almost anyone else. The Trammell co-authorship (GPI, Oxford) and the LessWrong reception are not things typical mainstream economists seek or achieve. He seems to navigate this deliberately.

2. **The JEL paper's perpetual update model is strange and potentially precedent-setting.** JEL publications are normally durable contributions. Korinek has turned his into a living practitioner document. This is either innovative or a sign that the field is moving too fast for fixed contributions.

3. **He explicitly uses Acemoglu as a foil in public settings** — not with hostility but with something like scholarly exasperation. The LessWrong quote is striking. This suggests the Acemoglu vs. Korinek divide is the defining intellectual tension in the field right now.

4. **His pre-AI work is genuinely strong**: The Davila-Korinek RES paper on pecuniary externalities is a major contribution. He was a serious macro-finance economist before the AI pivot. He is not a dilettante who wandered into AI from outside economics.

5. **EconTAI has two PhD student RAs, not an army**: The initiative is genuinely young (Sep 2025). It is not yet a department-sized enterprise. Korinek is personally at the center of it. This matters for prospective PhD students: you would be working with Korinek himself, not a large established group.

## Practical Assessment for a PhD Applicant (MSc Economics)

**Is UVA under Korinek a viable place for an LLM-economics PhD?**

Yes, with important caveats:

- Korinek actively advises PhD students on AI-economics topics. Donghyun Suh (2024) is the clearest existence proof — a Korinek-supervised dissertation that produced a significant NBER working paper.
- EconTAI is brand new (Sep 2025), has FLI grant funding, and has explicit PhD RA positions.
- UVA Economics is ranked ~25-30 nationally — not top-5, but legitimate. Placement from AI-economics dissertations is untested since the subfield is new everywhere.
- Halperin and Lockwood are associated faculty at EconTAI — both UVA econ — giving a small but real research environment.
- Korinek has the Brookings/NBER/Anthropic network, which would help with placements in policy and industry roles.

**Risks:**
- He is very visible as a public intellectual. Time constraints on PhD advising may be real.
- The AI-economics field has no established placement track record. A dissertation on AGI scenarios or AI tax policy is a career bet on the field maturing.
- The Stiglitz affiliation and longtermist adjacency may limit placement at certain top labor economics departments. Fine for policy, finance industry, or non-traditional placement.

**The Acemoglu alternative**: If you want the empirical labor-economics side of AI economics, MIT (Acemoglu/Autor/Johnson) is the obvious alternative. The intellectual stance is more cautious, the methodology is more empirical, and the placement track record is better. The tradeoff is that Acemoglu's work may age poorly if AI capabilities develop faster than he models.

**Bottom line on Korinek's agenda**: He is occupying a genuine intellectual space that nobody else in mainstream economics is occupying — modeling AGI-scale scenarios with rigorous macro methods rather than either dismissing them (Acemoglu) or only discussing them qualitatively (longtermists). The JEL series is important for his visibility but not his deepest contribution. The Korinek-Suh paper and the Trammell-Korinek paper are his most substantive formal work. Whether this agenda matters depends almost entirely on whether AGI-scale AI arrives in any meaningful policy timeframe. If Acemoglu is right (modest AI, long horizon), Korinek's agenda will look prematurely alarmed. If capabilities keep scaling as they have, Korinek will look prescient.
