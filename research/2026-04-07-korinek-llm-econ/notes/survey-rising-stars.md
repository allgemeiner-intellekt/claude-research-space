# Survey: Rising-Star Economists at the LLM x Economics Frontier

## Summary

The field is smaller than it looks from the outside. Most of the people doing
genuinely novel LLM-economics work cluster at MIT Sloan/IDE, Chicago Booth,
Yale, Wharton, Stanford DEL, and a handful of business school econ departments
(NYU Stern, BU Questrom). The "obvious giants" (Brynjolfsson, Acemoglu, Autor)
are all full professors building out labs — they will supervise students but
won't give them much time. The real action for a prospective PhD student is the
cohort of assistant/associate professors who hit the job market 2014–2022 and
are now actively building research groups around LLMs specifically.

Key signal: several of the most interesting people are on the boundary between
econ and information systems / organizational behavior. A student should not
restrict the search to "economics departments" — MIT Sloan, Wharton OID, Booth,
and Toronto Rotman all have tenure-track faculty who supervise econ-style PhD
theses and publish in the same journals.

A strong unexpected finding: **Lindsey Raymond** (PhD MIT, postdoc at Microsoft
Research) is joining MIT in 2026 as an assistant professor with a joint
appointment in Economics, EECS, and the Schwarzman College — arguably the
single most exciting emerging advisor in this space. Also: **Lukas Althoff**
(Stanford, PhD 2021) is already publishing a general-equilibrium model of AI's
task-level effects at QJE quality, with a new GitHub repo on AI labor markets.
Neither was the most obvious name on the starting list.

**Verdict for prospective student**: Very high priority. Early assistant professor with a hot agenda, a clear empirical advantage (Scandinavian data), and papers in top journals. ## Landscape Assessment

### State of knowledge (April 2026)

The empirical LLM-econ literature has two main branches:
1. **Exposure analysis** (what fraction of tasks/jobs are theoretically automatable): GPTs are GPTs (Eloundou et al.), Lukas Althoff's structural model, Humlum's surveys.
2. **Causal impact studies** (what actually happens when workers use AI tools): Noy & Zhang (*Science* 2023), Brynjolfsson/Li/Raymond call-center study, Humlum & Vestergaard's Danish data work.

The gap between branch 1 predictions (massive exposure) and branch 2 findings (moderate, concentrated gains; little aggregate employment effect so far) is the central tension in the field.

### Agreements

- LLMs disproportionately affect high-skill, high-wage, cognitive-task-intensive work (the opposite of prior automation waves that hit routine/manual work).
- Productivity gains within occupations are real but concentrated among lower-skill workers within those occupations (closing internal skill gaps, per Brynjolfsson et al. and Noy & Zhang).
- Aggregate labor market effects are small so far — but this may reflect slow diffusion rather than small eventual impact.

### Conflicts

- Humlum's headline finding ("small labor market effects") vs. the enthusiasm in exposure analyses. Humlum's own retitling suggests he acknowledges the gap: the paper is now "Still Waters, Rapid Currents" — small surface effects, large underlying currents.
- How much does the "homo silicus" / LLM simulation approach (Horton et al.) actually replicate human behavior in economics experiments? Still an open empirical question.

### Gaps and under-explored areas

- **LLMs as research inputs** (economists using LLMs to do economics research): almost no rigorous study of this.
- **Firm-level adoption dynamics**: most studies look at individual workers, not organizations.
- **Long-run equilibrium effects**: all studies are short-run; reallocation takes years.
- **Non-US, non-Danish contexts**: Humlum has a Danish data monopoly; US firm-level data is much harder to access.

### Surprises

1. **Lindsey Raymond** joining MIT in 2026 with a *joint appointment across Economics, EECS, and Schwarzman* is the most important institutional development. This structure is explicitly designed to produce LLM-econ researchers who can work across both fields — and she will be taking PhD students immediately.
2. **Lukas Althoff** is primarily known as a labor/economic history economist but has a new GE model of AI task effects with a GitHub repository — he is moving into the space aggressively and is at Stanford.
3. **Sarah Eichmeyer** (listed in the starting prompt) is the wrong person: she is at Bocconi, does political economy and health economics, and has no LLM-specific work.
4. **Zanele Munyikwa** (MIT Sloan PhD 2024, co-authored LLM hiring paper with Horton) went to industry (Revelio Labs) rather than academia — a reminder that many of the most talented junior researchers in this space are getting pulled into industry.
