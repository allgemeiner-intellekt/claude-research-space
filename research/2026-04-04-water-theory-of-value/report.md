# Is There a Water Theory of Value?

In 2011, the statistician Cosma Shalizi dropped a remark — almost offhand, buried in a review of Farjoun and Machover's *Laws of Chaos* — that has quietly haunted the labour theory of value ever since. If the labour theory of value is supported by the correlation between labour inputs and prices, Shalizi observed, then water or electricity or any other "universal input" should show the same correlation. The labour theory of value, in this reading, is not wrong so much as unspecial: it tells you that a thing which goes into everything correlates with the price of everything, which is true but trivially true, and equally true of water. Nobody has convincingly responded. Nobody has even run the test. But the question is far older and far deeper than Shalizi seems to have known. For three centuries, every major school of value theory has picked a favoured substance — land, labour, energy, utility — and every one has faced a structurally identical challenge: why *that* substance and not another? The history of these attempts, and the reasons they fail or survive, turns out to be one of the most illuminating threads in political economy.

The most surprising finding: the question has a mathematical answer that is recent and largely unknown outside specialist circles. Mariolis, Tsoulfidis, and Torres-González have shown, using spectral decomposition of real input-output matrices, that prices in modern economies are approximately determined by a rank-1 system — the dominant eigenvector of the production matrix accounts for almost all price variation. Any input whose sectoral distribution aligns with this eigenvector will correlate with prices. Labour correlates best not because of any metaphysical property but because it is the most evenly distributed input across sectors: its coefficient vector is statistically proportional to the Perron-Frobenius eigenvector. Energy carriers, being concentrated in specific industries, are not. This is the empirical resolution of Shalizi's challenge — but it dissolves the labour theory in the act of defending it, because it explains the correlation without needing value theory at all.

## Three centuries of picking a substance

The impulse to ground economic value in a single physical or social substance is as old as political economy itself. Each school's choice reveals its deepest commitments about what is real in an economy.

The **Physiocrats** chose land. Quesnay — a court physician who modelled the economy on blood circulation — argued that only agriculture yields a genuine surplus, the *produit net*. Artisans and merchants merely reshape matter; only farmers, aided by sun and soil, create new substance. This was not metaphor: the Physiocrats literally believed that nature's contribution to agriculture was the sole source of net physical creation. Their political conclusion followed directly: the single tax (*impôt unique*) should fall on land rent, the only genuine surplus. The obvious objection — that manufacturing also creates useful things — was met with the distinction between *creation* and *transformation*. Industry transforms without multiplying; agriculture multiplies. Turgot softened this by admitting that capital in any sector could generate returns, and his modified Physiocracy, channelled through Adam Smith, became the classical labour theory.

**Smith and Ricardo** chose labour. Smith's argument was partly empirical ("labour alone is the ultimate and real standard by which the value of all commodities can at all times and places be estimated") and partly about permanence: gold and silver fluctuate with discoveries, but the "toil and trouble" of labour is constant. He immediately ran into the water-diamond paradox — water is essential but cheap, diamonds are frivolous but expensive — and his unsatisfying resolution (distinguishing use-value from exchange-value) left the theory incomplete. Ricardo tried to make labour rigorous as both the source and the measure of value, but discovered that the same labour-input good would have different prices depending on capital intensity and the prevailing profit rate. His search for an "invariable measure of value" — a commodity whose price would be unaffected by distributional changes — failed. He admitted in the third edition of his *Principles* (1821) that he had never solved it. The problem was structural: labour time as a measure of value is internally consistent only if capital does not generate independent returns, which is precisely what needs explaining [1].

The **energy theorists** chose thermodynamic work. This lineage begins with Serhiy Podolinsky, a Ukrainian physician and socialist who published "Socialism and the Unity of Physical Forces" in 1880, attempting to reconcile Marx with Carnot. His argument: human labour, directed by intelligence, accumulates solar energy in useful forms — crops, infrastructure, domestic animals — producing a net thermodynamic surplus many times the caloric input. Labour creates real value in a biophysical sense. Marx was reportedly sympathetic but ultimately sceptical; Engels was dismissive. Their objection, carefully reconstructed by Burkett and Foster [2], was not scientific ignorance but a principled category distinction: thermodynamic surplus is not social surplus value. Energy is a physical fact; value is a social relation. Conflating them is a category error. Podolinsky died young at 40 after a mental breakdown, and his work was forgotten until ecological economists rediscovered it in the 1970s.

Frederick Soddy, Nobel laureate in chemistry, revived the energy argument in *Wealth, Virtual Wealth and Debt* (1926). His core distinction: real wealth is physical goods governed by thermodynamics; virtual wealth is money and debt governed by compound interest. The mismatch between exponentially growing debt and thermodynamically constrained real wealth is the root of crisis. Soddy was dismissed as a crank — the *Times Literary Supplement* lamented a respected chemist ruining his reputation — yet his specific policy proposals (abandon the gold standard, floating exchange rates, counter-cyclical fiscal policy) are now mainstream practice. His energy economics remain heterodox; his monetary economics became orthodoxy. The split is instructive: the establishment accepted the parts it could absorb into existing frameworks and rejected what challenged them [3].

Howard Odum pushed furthest. His concept of *emergy* — embodied solar energy, measured in solar emjoules — attempted to make energy a universal accounting unit. Not all energy is equal: one joule of sunlight is worth far less than one joule of electricity, which is worth far less than one joule of neural activity. Odum's "transformity" ratios capture these quality differences. His most radical claim was that market prices are *inversely* related to real wealth: when a resource becomes scarce, its market price rises while its emergy stock declines. Markets, in this view, systematically misprice the physical world [4].

And then Robert Costanza did the empirical work. Using 92-sector US input-output tables with labour and government services included in the energy accounting, he found R-squared values of 0.85 to 0.98 between embodied energy and dollar value of sectoral output [5]. These are real numbers, published in *Science* in 1980, with over 650 citations. They are comparable in magnitude to the labour-value correlations that Cockshott and Cottrell report (R-squared of 0.95–0.98 across various countries) [6]. This is the empirical fact that gives Shalizi's challenge its teeth: energy-price correlations exist, they are strong, and they were found independently by a different research tradition using different methods.

## The pattern

Step back and the pattern is clear. Each school identifies a substance — land, labour, energy — and observes that this substance enters production broadly enough to correlate with prices. Each faces the same objection: other substances also enter production broadly. Each responds with a version of the same move: *our* substance is special because of some unique property that the others lack. The Physiocrats said nature uniquely multiplies physical matter. The classical economists said labour uniquely enters all production as a conscious, purposive activity. The energy theorists said thermodynamic work uniquely underlies all physical transformation. The marginalists, watching this parade, abandoned the quest entirely: value, they said, is not *in* things at all but in the mind of the chooser.

Shalizi's contribution is to see this pattern from the outside and name it. The correlation between a universal input and prices is a structural feature of input-output economies, not evidence for any particular theory. But is he right?

## The eigenvector answer

The deepest mathematical treatment of this question comes from work that few participants in the debate seem aware of. Mariolis and Tsoulfidis, working in the tradition of Sraffa and Leontief, have shown across a decade of research that real input-output matrices exhibit dramatically low effective rank [7]. The eigenvalue spectrum of the vertically integrated input matrix H decays exponentially: in economy after economy, across the World Input-Output Database, only one to three eigenvalues have non-negligible magnitude. The rest cluster near zero.

The consequence is stark. Prices, expressed as a function of the profit rate through the Sraffa price equation, behave as if determined by a one- or two-dimensional system. The dominant Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue and its associated eigenvector account for nearly all price variation. In such a system, any input vector whose distribution across sectors is close to the dominant eigenvector will have its vertically integrated content correlate tightly with prices. This is not a conjecture; it follows directly from the spectral decomposition of the price system.

Torres-González, building on this framework, identified what he calls a "new stylized fact" about productive structures: the labour coefficient vector is statistically proportional to the Perron-Frobenius eigenvector of the input coefficient matrix, verified across the entire WIOD database and across US data from 1977 to 2012 [8]. The mechanism is intuitively clear. The Perron-Frobenius eigenvector has strictly positive components and represents the "average" distribution of production activity — what all sectors would converge to under repeated rounds of production. Labour's distribution closely tracks this average because labour enters *every* sector directly. Energy carriers — coal, oil, electricity — enter some sectors heavily and others negligibly. Their concentrated distributions produce large projections onto subdominant eigenvectors, which is precisely why they correlate worse with prices.

Cockshott and Cottrell's 1997 empirical comparison confirms this at the level of brute numbers. Using UK input-output data for 100 sectors, they computed vertically integrated content for labour, electricity, oil, and iron and steel, then correlated each with prices. Labour achieved R-squared of 0.955. The best alternative, electricity, managed 0.682. Oil and iron did worse. The coefficient of variation of the price-to-content ratio — a size-normalized measure of how well the "theory" works — was 0.198 for labour, 3.69 for electricity, 11.41 for oil [9]. When labour was included in a regression alongside the alternatives, electricity and iron added no independent information. Only oil retained marginal significance, attributable to North Sea rent rather than any energy theory.

So Shalizi is formally right: the correlation does not uniquely identify labour. But empirically, labour is not just better than the alternatives — it is qualitatively better. The gap between an R-squared of 0.955 and 0.682 is the gap between a good theory and a poor one. And the gap between a CV of 0.198 and 11.41 — a 57-fold difference — is not explainable by the size effects that Kliman and Nitzan-Bichler invoke to dismiss all price-value correlations [10]. The eigenvector framework explains *why* labour wins (diffuse distribution) without needing to invoke value theory. This is either a vindication of the labour theory or its absorption into linear algebra, depending on your philosophical commitments.

## The philosophical response: abstract labour and its limits

There is an entirely different response to Shalizi, one that the empiricists never make and that Shalizi never engages. It comes from the value-form school of Marxism — Rubin, Backhaus, Arthur, Heinrich, Postone — and it begins by denying the premise. Marx's argument for labour was *not* that labour is a universal input.

The argument, as reconstructed most clearly by I.I. Rubin in his 1927 lecture on abstract labour, runs as follows [11]. There are three things that get conflated under the label "abstract labour":

1. **Physiologically equal labour** — the expenditure of human energy in any form of work. This exists in all societies, has nothing to do with capitalism, and is what the empiricists measure.
2. **Socially equalised labour** — labour that is commensurated through some social mechanism (planning, tradition, markets). This exists in any economy with a division of labour.
3. **Abstract labour** — the specific social form that labour takes in commodity production, where privately performed labour achieves social validity *only through* market exchange.

The Cockshott-Cottrell empirical programme measures (1). Shalizi's challenge hits (1). But Marx's theory is about (3). Abstract labour is not "labour with its particular qualities stripped away" — a mental abstraction — but the real social process through which capitalist commodity exchange constitutes all labour as generically interchangeable. It is not a property of the production process observable from outside; it is a social relation constituted by exchange.

This is a genuinely different claim. Water, energy, and iron enter production as physical inputs. Labour-power enters production as a *commodity* — a thing that is bought and sold on the market — whose use-value is the capacity to produce. And labour-power has a unique property that no other commodity shares: when consumed in production, it generates more value than it costs to reproduce. This is the theory of surplus value. You can buy a worker's labour-power for a wage that represents, say, six hours of socially necessary labour time, and then work them for ten hours. The four extra hours are surplus value — the source of profit. You cannot do this with water or electricity. The ratio of energy output to energy input is fixed by thermodynamics; you cannot buy electricity "cheap" and extract "surplus electricity" by extending the working day. The surplus-value structure of capitalism requires a commodity whose use-value consists of productive activity itself, and that commodity is labour-power.

The value-form school, especially in Postone's version, goes further [12]. Labour under capitalism is not merely an input among inputs. It is the *medium of social synthesis* — the mechanism through which individuals are connected to the social totality. In pre-capitalist societies, social relations are constituted through explicit domination (feudal hierarchy, slavery, kinship). Under capitalism, social relations are mediated through things — commodities and money — which means they are mediated through abstract labour. To ask "why not water?" is to miss that the theory is not about which input best predicts prices; it is about what organises capitalist society as a specific historical form.

This response is philosophically powerful. It is also empirically inert. The value-form school explicitly refuses to make predictions about price-labour correlations: the theory operates at the level of social ontology, not price determination. Whether prices correlate with labour inputs is, for Heinrich or Postone, a question about the *forms of appearance* of value, not about value itself [13]. This creates an awkward situation. The empiricists (Cockshott) can be hit by Shalizi but can point to data. The philosophers (value-form school) are immune to Shalizi but cannot point to data. Neither camp talks to the other in any sustained way. Cockshott explicitly claims that "the theories [of Marx and Ricardo] are substantially identical in their empirical predictions" [14] — a concession that the value-form school would find baffling, since their entire project is to show how deeply Marx's theory differs from Ricardo's.

## Real abstraction and its discontents

The concept of "real abstraction" — developed most systematically by Alfred Sohn-Rethel — is often invoked as the value-form school's trump card against the water challenge. The idea: commodity exchange is not merely a mental operation but a practical act that enacts an abstraction in reality. When two commodities are exchanged as equivalents, their qualitative differences are practically annulled. This abstraction is "real" because it happens in the market, not in the economist's head [15].

But here is the problem: real abstraction, in Sohn-Rethel's formulation, applies to *all* commodities, not specifically to labour. When electricity is traded on futures markets — kilowatt-hours from coal, solar, nuclear, and wind rendered perfectly interchangeable at the grid connection point — this is real abstraction in precisely Sohn-Rethel's sense. When the CME launched water futures in 2020, pricing California water rights as a fungible financial instrument, that too was a real abstraction of water. If the concept of real abstraction is supposed to explain why labour is special, it fails on its own terms: it explains the form of commodity exchange in general, not the substance of value in particular.

The value-form school's actual answer to "why labour and not water?" is not real abstraction alone — it is the surplus-value argument described above. Real abstraction explains the *form* (all commodities undergo practical abstraction in exchange). The *substance* claim (that abstract labour specifically is the basis of value) requires the additional argument that only labour-power produces more value than it costs. This is a coherent position, but it means the concept of real abstraction is doing less work than its proponents sometimes suggest. It describes the social form of capitalism; it does not, by itself, single out labour.

John Milios has identified a further problem [16]: Sohn-Rethel extends real abstraction to all commodity exchange throughout history, from ancient Greece onward. But if real abstraction is supposed to be specifically *capitalist* — constituting abstract labour as the substance of value — then it cannot predate capitalism. Sohn-Rethel's over-generalisation deprives the concept of its historical specificity. The fix is to restrict real abstraction to specifically capitalist production relations — wage labour, labour-power as a commodity — but this means the concept already presupposes the very theory it is supposed to ground.

## What the three levels tell us

Shalizi's challenge operates at three levels, and the answer is different at each.

At the **mathematical** level, Shalizi is correct. In a Leontief system with low effective rank, any sufficiently diffuse input will correlate with prices. The correlation does not uniquely identify labour as the substance of value. This is now well-established through the Mariolis-Tsoulfidis spectral decomposition, even if few participants in the debate have absorbed it.

At the **empirical** level, labour wins — but for a reason that undercuts the theory. Labour correlates best because it is the most evenly distributed input across sectors, making its coefficient vector align with the dominant eigenvector. This is a fact about industrial structure, not about value. A sufficiently homogeneous alternative input — something used in exactly equal proportions across all sectors — would correlate just as well. Water, as it happens, does not: water use is highly concentrated in agriculture, utilities, and some manufacturing, and negligible in finance, education, and professional services. Shalizi's hypothetical water theory would likely fail empirically, not because water lacks some metaphysical value-creating property, but because water's sectoral distribution is lumpy. The same would be true of energy carriers. Only labour is diffuse enough to approximate the eigenvector.

At the **philosophical** level, the value-form school offers an answer that Shalizi never engages and probably did not know existed. Labour is not special because it is a universal input. Labour is special because under capitalism, it is constituted as the medium through which social relations are reproduced — the form of social synthesis specific to commodity production. Water mediates nothing socially; labour mediates everything. This is a claim about the ontology of capitalist societies, not about price determination, and it is immune to Shalizi's statistical critique. Whether it is *satisfying* depends on what you want a theory of value to do.

The deepest problem is that these three levels do not communicate. The empiricists measure physiological labour and defend correlations. The philosophers theorise abstract labour and refuse to make predictions. The statistician identifies a structural feature of input-output systems and frames it as a reductio. Each is right about something. None is right about everything.

What the history of substance theories teaches is that the impulse to find a single ground of value — one substance that explains prices, profits, and exploitation — may itself be the error. The Physiocrats chose land and were wrong. The classicals chose labour and were partially right but could not solve the measure problem. The energy theorists chose thermodynamic work and got comparable correlations but no theoretical victory. The marginalists abandoned the quest and built an internally consistent but empirically vacuous alternative. Each school was driven by a legitimate insight: land really does produce biological surplus; labour really does enter all production; energy really does underlie all physical transformation; subjective preferences really do influence prices. The mistake is not in seeing these truths but in claiming they exhaust the explanation.

Shalizi's water challenge is, in the end, a reductio of a particular style of argument — the style that says "X correlates with prices, therefore X is the substance of value." That style of argument cannot distinguish between labour and energy, and probably cannot distinguish between either and any sufficiently diffuse input. The response is not to find a better correlation but to ask a better question. The question the value-form school asks — not "what predicts prices?" but "what kind of social relation is value?" — is the better question. Its weakness is that it has no empirical programme. The question the eigenvector literature asks — "why does this particular input's distribution match the productive structure of the economy?" — is also a better question. Its weakness is that it has no philosophical ambition.

A synthesis would look like this: the eigenvector framework explains *why* labour-value correlations hold (industrial structure, not metaphysics); the value-form tradition explains *what it means* that capitalist societies organise production through abstract labour (social domination, not natural law); and Shalizi's challenge reminds everyone that correlation is not explanation. The water theory of value does not work — not because water is unimportant, but because water is distributed too unevenly across sectors to approximate the eigenvector. Labour works — not because it creates value in any physical sense, but because the way capitalism organises production makes labour the statistical signature of production itself.

## Open questions

- **Run the Shalizi test.** Nobody has computed vertically integrated water content via the Leontief inverse and compared its price correlation to labour's. This would be straightforward with any national input-output table that includes a water supply sector. The eigenvector framework predicts water will correlate poorly (concentrated sectoral distribution), but the prediction has not been checked.
- **Multiple regression.** No study has simultaneously regressed prices on labour content, energy content, and capital content in the same model. Cockshott's 1997 "placebo" tests compare each input separately; the obvious next step is to check whether labour's coefficient remains significant and dominant after controlling for energy. Collinearity between inputs may make this difficult, but that itself would be informative.
- **The bridge between eigenvectors and social theory.** The Torres-González finding that labour aligns with the Perron-Frobenius eigenvector is presented as a stylized fact. Is there a deeper economic explanation for *why* capitalist production structures exhibit this alignment? Is it contingent on industrial structure, or does it follow from something about how labour markets work?
- **Can value-form theory be made empirically tractable?** The philosophical response to Shalizi is powerful but refuses empirical engagement. Is there any observable implication of the claim that "abstract labour is the medium of social synthesis" — something that would distinguish a world where this is true from one where it is not?
- **Costanza's R-squared under scrutiny.** His 0.85-0.98 range includes labour and government services in the energy accounting. If these are excluded, does the energy-price correlation drop to levels comparable to Cockshott's electricity-only result (R-squared 0.68)? This would resolve whether energy-price correlations are independent of labour-price correlations or parasitic on them.
