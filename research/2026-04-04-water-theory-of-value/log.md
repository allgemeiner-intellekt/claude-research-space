# Research Log — "Water Theory of Value"

## Scoping

**Origin**: Open question from the classical-econophysics report. Shalizi's
challenge to Cockshott/Cottrell/Farjoun-Machover: if labour-value correlations
support the labour theory of value because labour is a "universal input," then
water, energy, or any other universal input should correlate equally well with
prices. The user wants this explored *theoretically and historically*, not
empirically.

**Why this is more interesting than it first appears**: Shalizi frames this as
a statistical critique, but it's really a question that has been asked — in
different forms — for three centuries. Every major school of value theory chose
a specific "substance" as the basis of value (land, labour, energy, utility)
and had to defend why *that* substance and not another. The debate has a rich
history that Shalizi may not have been aware of, and the responses from the
Marxian tradition are more sophisticated than "labour is universal."

### Questions

1. **The substance-of-value tradition**: What is the history of theories that
   ground economic value in a single physical or social substance? Physiocrats
   chose land, classical economists chose labour, ecological economists chose
   energy, Podolinsky and Soddy tried thermodynamic work. What was each
   school's argument for *why their substance and not another*?

2. **Marx's argument for labour's special status**: The Cockshott-Cottrell
   empirical programme inherits from Marx, but Marx's argument for labour was
   *not* that it's a universal input — it was about abstract labour as the
   social substance of value under commodity production. What exactly is this
   argument, how does it differ from a mere "universal input" claim, and does
   it survive Shalizi's challenge?

3. **The energy theory of value**: This is the most developed rival "substance"
   theory. Frederick Soddy (1926), Howard Odum (emergy), Robert Costanza,
   and the ecological economics tradition all argued that energy (or exergy/
   emergy) is the true basis of value. What were their arguments? How were
   they received? Do energy-price correlations exist, and if so, does this
   vindicate Shalizi or complicate him?

4. **The formal structure of the universal-input argument**: Under what
   conditions does correlation between an input and prices tell you something
   about value theory vs. being a statistical tautology? Is there a way to
   discriminate between competing "substance" theories empirically, or is
   Shalizi right that the correlations are inherently underdetermining?

5. **Has anyone responded to Shalizi directly?** Have Cockshott, Cottrell, or
   other defenders of the labour theory of value addressed the water/energy
   challenge specifically? What was the response?

## Survey phase

Dispatched 5 survey agents in parallel (one per question). All returned.

Key findings:
- Substance-of-value tradition is three centuries deep: physiocrats (land),
  classicals (labour), thermodynamicists (energy), marginalists (utility).
  Each faced the identical objection: why your substance and not another?
- Marx's argument was NOT "labour is universal input" — it was about abstract
  labour as social form specific to commodity production. Value-form school
  (Rubin, Arthur, Heinrich, Postone) develops this most rigorously.
- Energy theory of value is real and developed. Costanza (1980) got R² = 0.85-0.98
  for energy-price correlations — comparable to labour-value correlations.
- Mariolis-Tsoulfidis: I-O matrices have low effective rank. Any sufficiently
  diffuse input correlates with prices. Labour wins because its distribution
  aligns with the Perron-Frobenius eigenvector.
- Nobody has directly responded to Shalizi. Cockshott's 1997 paper testing
  alternative value bases predates the challenge by 14 years. No one has run
  the water test.

## Deep dive phase

**Eigenvector**: Torres-González (2022) identifies a "new stylized fact" —
labour coefficient vector is proportional to PF eigenvector across WIOD
database. Structural reason: labour enters every sector (diffuse), while
energy carriers are concentrated in few sectors. Cockshott-Cottrell 1997:
R² = 0.955 (labour) vs 0.682 (electricity); CV = 0.198 vs 3.69-11.41.
Parys (2018) confirms formal equivalence of labour/energy value theories
in Sraffa-Leontief framework but empirical superiority of labour.

**Abstract water**: Real abstraction (Sohn-Rethel) applies to ALL commodities,
not just labour. Electricity markets trade energy as perfectly homogeneous
commodity. The value-form school's actual argument for labour's uniqueness
rests on surplus value: only labour-power produces more value than it costs.
Water and electricity cannot generate surplus. This is the non-trivial
response — but it requires the full apparatus of Marxian value theory, not
just the concept of real abstraction.
