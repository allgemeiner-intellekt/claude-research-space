# Survey: Marx's Argument for Labour's Special Status as Substance of Value

**Question:** What exactly is Marx's argument for labour's special status as the substance of value, and how does it differ from a mere "universal input" claim?

**Context:** Cosma Shalizi (2012) argues in a Crooked Timber footnote that the empirical labour-value correlations found by Cockshott and Cottrell don't specifically support the LTV because any "universal input" — water, electricity — would show similar correlations. The question is whether Marx's argument was ever meant to rest on the "universal input" premise, or whether it was an entirely different kind of claim.

---

## Summary

Marx's argument for labour as the substance of value is not an empirical claim about which input best predicts prices. It is a social-ontological claim: that abstract labour is the specific form taken by human productive activity under commodity-producing (capitalist) social relations. The substance of value and its form are inseparable; abstract labour is not a pre-existing thing measured by the market, but a social category that is *constituted* by commodity exchange. Shalizi's "universal input" critique hits a genuine target — but that target is the empiricist tradition (Cockshott/Cottrell) that borrows the positivist framing of Ricardo, not the deeper argument in Capital. The value-form school (Rubin, Backhaus, Arthur, Heinrich, Postone) has developed the social-ontological reading most rigorously, and this reading is largely immune to the Shalizi challenge — though it purchases immunity at the cost of making the theory less empirically tractable.

There is a genuine tension in the secondary literature: Cockshott explicitly says Marx and Ricardo are "substantially identical" in their empirical predictions, while value-form theorists argue this misses Marx's specific philosophical move entirely. Both sides are right about something. Sorting out which argument is doing the work is the central problem.

## Key Sources

### 1. Cosma Shalizi, "In Soviet Union, Optimization Problem Solves You"
- **Link:** https://crookedtimber.org/2012/05/30/in-soviet-union-optimization-problem-solves-you/
- **Key content:** The "water theory of value" argument appears in footnote 4, where Shalizi discusses Kantorovich's attempt to show that his "objectively determined values" are compatible with the LTV by proving proportionality between shadow prices and required labour inputs. Shalizi notes that the same argument would show proportionality between shadow prices and *any* required input — water, electricity, or anything else — since the ordering of inputs is arbitrary. He therefore calls the proportionality result "ideological cover" rather than a genuine argument for labour's special status. The broader essay is about the computational complexity of socialist planning, but this footnote contains the sharpest philosophical challenge: empirical correlation between prices and labour content proves nothing specifically about labour.
- **Assessment:** This is a blog post, not a peer-reviewed paper. But the argument is philosophically precise and has not been adequately answered by the empiricist Marxist tradition. Shalizi is a statistician and complexity theorist, not a Marx scholar, so he is engaging with the Cockshott/Cottrell line rather than the value-form literature. High credibility for what he is actually arguing; but the scope of his critique is narrower than it first appears (it targets empiricist LTV, not Marx's own argument).

### 2. Karl Marx, *Capital* Vol. 1, Chapter 1, Section 1 (1867)
- **Link:** https://history.hanover.edu/texts/marx/KAPI1-1.html (text); https://www.mit.edu/~allanmc/marx.capital.1.pdf (PDF)
- **Key content:** The logical structure of Marx's derivation proceeds as follows:
  1. The commodity has a dual character: use-value (qualitative, specific) and exchange-value (quantitative, relational).
  2. Exchange-value presents itself as a proportion in which commodities trade — say, 1 coat = 20 yards of linen. If these are equal, they must share some "common element."
  3. This common element cannot be any physical or natural property, since those are precisely what makes commodities *different*. "If we make abstraction from [use-value], we make abstraction at the same time from the material elements and shapes that make the product a use-value; we see in it no longer a table, a house, yarn."
  4. What remains is that "human labour-power has been expended in their production." Commodities are "crystals of this social substance" — and as such, they are values.
  5. The *magnitude* of value is determined by "the labour-time socially necessary... the labour-time required to produce an article under the normal conditions of production, and with the average degree of skill and intensity prevalent at the time."
  
  The argument is NOT: "labour is a universal input, so it determines value." It IS: "exchange value has no natural property; what commodities share as exchangeable things is that they are products of *abstract human labour*, which is human productive activity stripped of its concrete specificity." The social substance is identified by a logical elimination procedure, not by empirical observation of correlations.
  
  Critically: Marx later specifies (Section 2) that what he calls "labour" is not concrete labour (weaving, spinning) but *abstract labour* — the "twofold character of labour" which he describes as "the pivot on which a clear comprehension of Political Economy turns."
- **Assessment:** Primary source. The logical structure is clear, though contested. The key philosophical move — stripping away physical properties — is exactly what the value-form school defends as distinctly non-Ricardian.

### 3. I.I. Rubin, "Abstract Labour and Value in Marx's System" (1927)
- **Link:** https://www.marxists.org/archive/rubin/abstract-labour.htm (also at https://isr.press/Rubin_Abstract_Labor/index.html)
- **Key content:** Rubin's 1927 lecture is perhaps the clearest statement of why abstract labour is a social, not physiological, category. Key arguments:
  - **The physiological interpretation is inadequate:** Treating abstract labour as the mere expenditure of human energy (calories, muscular effort) exists in *all* societies. If this were what Marx meant, value would be a transhistorical category — but Marx insists it is specific to commodity production. "The expenditure of physiological energy as such is not abstract labour and does not create value."
  - **The crucial distinction between commensuration and abstraction:** In *any* society based on division of labour, labour must be commensurated — some mechanism equates different kinds of work. But the *form* of this commensuration differs radically. In a planned community, labour is first socially coordinated, then produces goods. In commodity production, the reverse: privately performed labour becomes social *only through* market exchange. "Labour only becomes social in commodity production when it assumes the characteristic of socially equated labour." Abstract labour is labour that has undergone this specific social process — it is not the common physiological substrate, but labour as it appears *to* commodity exchange.
  - **The form is inseparable from the substance:** Because abstract labour is constituted by the relations of commodity exchange, it cannot be identified by inspecting the production process alone. It is a relational, social category. "The transformation of private labour into social labour can only be carried out through the transformation of concrete labour into abstract labour."
  - **Why this matters against the "universal input" claim:** Water, electricity, or iron are inputs — but they are not *labour*. The claim isn't that labour is a universal input. The claim is that in a commodity-producing society, the social validation of labour (through market exchange) is what constitutes value. Other inputs don't undergo this social transformation — they contribute to use-value but their "social substance" is not constituted by the same mechanism.
- **Assessment:** Peer-reviewed (published in journal *Capital and Class*, 1978 English translation). Rubin was a major Marxist economist, writing before his arrest in the Soviet Union. This lecture is widely considered a foundational text for understanding abstract labour correctly. Highly credible. Represents the "social-form" school before the West German Neue Marx-Lektüre developed similar ideas independently.

### 4. I.I. Rubin, *Essays on Marx's Theory of Value*, Chapter 14: "Abstract Labor" (1928)
- **Link:** https://www.marxists.org/archive/rubin/value/ch14.htm
- **Key content:** Chapter 14 builds on the lecture with more detailed analysis. Key passages:
  - Rubin identifies three distinct categories that get conflated: (a) physiologically equal labour (universal human energy), (b) socially equalized labour (any organised economy), and (c) abstract labour (commodity economy specifically). "Every abstract labour is social and socially equalized labour, but not every socially equalized labour can be considered abstract labour." This three-way distinction is the key to seeing why Shalizi's challenge, though devastating to the empiricist position, misses Marx's own claim.
  - Abstract labour has quantitative determination through labour-time — but this is a *social* magnitude. The hours count only as socially necessary hours, which is determined through the competitive process of market exchange.
  - "Value is a social property (or a social form) of a product of labour, just as abstract labour is a 'social substance' which lies at the basis of this value." Value does not contain a single atom of matter; it has "a purely social reality."
- **Assessment:** Same as above. Essential text for the Rubin school interpretation.

### 5. Patrick Murray, "Marx's 'Truly Social' Labour Theory of Value: Part I, Abstract Labour in Marxian Value Theory" (2000)
- **Link:** DOI: 10.1163/156920600100414551 (Historical Materialism 6, pp. 27–66)
- **Key content:** Murray argues that what makes Marx's labour theory distinctly "social" is the *form-determination* of abstract labour. Commodity exchange doesn't merely measure pre-existing abstract labour; it *constitutes* labour as abstract. This leads to Murray's concept of "practically abstract labour" — labour that is actually made abstract (stripped of its concrete specificities) by the real social processes of commodity production and exchange. The point is that abstraction is not a mental operation of the economist or a logician, but a real social process. A weaver, producing linen under capitalist conditions, is already performing abstract labour — not because we mentally discard the weaving, but because the capitalist system treats weaving, spinning, and carpentry as qualitatively identical (interchangeable sources of surplus value). This is what makes it specific to capitalism, not universal.
- **Assessment:** Peer-reviewed, in Historical Materialism. Murray is a leading scholar in this tradition. High credibility. 42 citations. This paper is crucial for understanding why Marx's argument is not the same as Ricardo's.

### 6. Chris Arthur, "The Spectral Ontology of Value" (2001)
- **Link:** https://www.radicalphilosophy.com/article/the-spectral-ontology-of-value
- **Key content:** Arthur represents the most philosophically adventurous wing of the value-form school ("New Dialectic"), explicitly influenced by Hegel. His key claims:
  - Labour does not naturally produce value; value is not "congealed labour" in a simple substance-to-form relationship. Rather, "the form of exchange establishes the necessary social synthesis in the first place before labours expended may be commensurated in it."
  - The concept of "real abstraction" (from Alfred Sohn-Rethel): abstraction happens in the real world of exchange, not merely in our minds. When commodities are exchanged, their use-values are genuinely suspended, and they confront each other as abstract values. This is an ontological claim, not an epistemological one.
  - "Only the very fact of being exchanged unites the commodities generically." Use-value is abstracted from in the actual market — not by us, but by the market itself.
  - The implication for the Shalizi challenge: Arthur's position would say that *water cannot be abstract water*. Water has a use-value that is present in every exchange, precisely as water. Labour is special because in commodity society, human productive activity is systematically treated as abstract (interchangeable) by the social form itself. No other input undergoes this systematic abstraction.
- **Assessment:** Published in Radical Philosophy, a peer-reviewed journal. Arthur is a highly respected Marxist philosopher. This is the strongest philosophical version of the value-form argument for labour's uniqueness. Its weakness: Arthur sometimes gets accused (by Carchedi and others) of making value a product of circulation alone, losing contact with production.

### 7. Michael Heinrich / Neue Marx-Lektüre
- **Link:** Wikipedia overview: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neue_Marx-Lekture; Heinrich's book: *An Introduction to the Three Volumes of Karl Marx's Capital* (2012)
- **Key content:** The Neue Marx-Lektüre (Backhaus, Reichelt, Heinrich) makes several related claims:
  - Abstract labour is "not the physiological expenditure of human energy" but a social form unique to capitalism — a "relation of social validation" where private labours achieve social reality only when validated through market exchange.
  - This leads to what Heinrich calls the "monetary theory of value": value is not a pre-monetary substance that money merely represents. Rather, value is constituted in its monetary form; there is no value "behind" or "before" money. Money is not a representation of labour-time but the form in which abstract labour achieves objective social reality.
  - This view entails that socially necessary labour-time is *not directly measurable* — the value of a commodity is not fixed at the moment of production but is only determined retroactively through exchange. This directly contradicts the Cockshott/Cottrell program.
  - The paper by Rauhala (2021, Historical Materialism) shows that this "monetary interpretation" was actually articulated in early 1960s East Germany, not just in the West German academic context, and that it concluded: "the substance of value is not a measurable quantity of labour time but, rather, a social relation."
- **Assessment:** Highly influential in the contemporary academic Marxist literature. Heinrich's book is a standard reference. The Neue Marx-Lektüre represents a genuine paradigm shift within Marxist theory. The "monetary theory of value" interpretation is controversial — Lietz & Schwarz (2023, Historical Materialism) argue it misreads key manuscripts — but the basic social-form argument is well-supported.

### 8. Cockshott, "Did Marx Have a Labour Theory of Value?" (2019)
- **Link:** https://paulcockshott.wordpress.com/2018/04/05/did-marx-have-a-labour-theory-of-value/ and published in *World Review of Political Economy*
- **Key content:** Cockshott explicitly claims that "the theories [of Marx and Ricardo] are therefore substantially identical in the empirical predictions they make." He defends this by listing four points of agreement on how labour determines value (direct labour, indirect labour, actual hours not wages, profit-rate equalisation modifies values). He presents the empirical correlations as evidence. Notably, he does not engage with the Shalizi challenge that these correlations could support a "water theory of value" equally well. Cockshott's position represents the most exposed flank: by reducing Marx to Ricardo, he removes precisely the philosophical distinctiveness that makes Marx's argument non-trivially different from a "universal input" claim.
- **Assessment:** Blog post + journal article. Cockshott is a computer scientist, not a philosopher of economics. His position is internally consistent but philosophically thin, and it effectively concedes the Shalizi point by claiming empirical equivalence with Ricardo's framework. This is the tradition Shalizi is actually critiquing.

### 9. Cockshott, Cottrell & Valle Baeza, "The Empirics of the Labour Theory of Value: Reply to Nitzan and Bichler" (2014)
- **Link:** https://bnarchives.yorku.ca/496/2/cockshott_cottrell_valle_baeza_2014_the_empirics_of_the_ltv_reply_to_nitzan_and_bichler.pdf (*Investigación Económica*, Vol. LXXIII, No. 287)
- **Key content:** This paper defends the empirical LTV against Nitzan/Bichler's claim that the correlations are spurious (driven by industry size) and that the studies measure prices against prices rather than true labour values. Cockshott et al. respond by arguing that (a) socially necessary labour-time is measurable statistically via clock hours aggregated across industries, and (b) the size effect is demonstrably not driving the correlations. The paper is technically competent but does not engage with the deeper "universal input" challenge.
- **Assessment:** Peer-reviewed. The empirical defense against the Nitzan/Bichler objection is reasonably convincing. But the paper's framing — treating SNLT as directly measurable from wage bills and input-output data — is exactly what the Neue Marx-Lektüre school denies is legitimate.

### 10. Moishe Postone, *Time, Labor, and Social Domination* (1993)
- **Link:** https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/time-labor-and-social-domination/B2429FE0E1B401CC2F775DC2683C2025 (Cambridge University Press)
- **Key content:** Postone's major argument is that labour in capitalism functions as a form of *social mediation* — a mechanism through which social relations are constituted and through which individuals are related to one another. This is historically specific to capitalism:
  - In pre-capitalist societies, social relations are constituted through explicit domination (feudal hierarchy, slavery). Under capitalism, social relations are mediated through *things* — commodities and money — which in turn means they are mediated through abstract labour.
  - Labour is not simply one input among many. It is the form through which *social synthesis* (the integration of individuals into a social whole) occurs in capitalism. Water and electricity are inputs to production, but they do not constitute social relations.
  - The "treadmill effect": as labour-saving technology is introduced, individual firms gain advantages, but once generalised, the amount of value produced per hour returns to baseline. This creates a structural drive toward endless accumulation without any increase in material wealth. This dynamic is specific to labour as the substance of value, not to any other input.
- **Assessment:** Highly influential monograph, published by Cambridge University Press. Postone represents a distinctive interpretation ("non-traditional Marxism") that specifically argues the working class cannot liberate itself through labour but must abolish labour as a category. Controversial within Marxism but philosophically rigorous.

### 11. David Harvey, "Marx's Refusal of the Labour Theory of Value" (2018)
- **Link:** https://davidharvey.org/2018/03/marxs-refusal-of-the-labour-theory-of-value-by-david-harvey/
- **Key content:** Harvey argues Marx explicitly rejected Ricardo's version of the LTV. Where Ricardo sought a static principle mapping labour content to prices, Marx developed a dynamic analysis of value as constituted through the totality of circulation and accumulation. Harvey introduces the "value theory of labour" — not a theory that labour determines value, but a theory of what value's rule does to labour. This reframes the question: Marx isn't explaining prices (as Ricardo was), he's critiquing a social form of domination. Harvey is partially corroborating the value-form school's reading, though from a less formal philosophical standpoint.
- **Assessment:** Blog post by a major Marxist geographer. Harvey is influential but not a technical philosopher. His position here is contested — Cockshott (2019) explicitly argues against it, saying Marx definitely had an LTV. The debate between Harvey and Cockshott maps onto the deeper dispute between value-form and empiricist readings.

### 12. "Labour-value: living labour as an alternative to physiological and value-form interpretations" — Patrick Galba de Paula (2022)
- **Link:** *Critique*, 2022
- **Key content:** This recent paper attempts a third position between the physiological (Cockshott-style) and value-form (Heinrich-style) interpretations. It argues that what determines value is "living labour, i.e. the socially necessary labour for the reproduction of a commodity at the time of its exchange," and that abstract labour is "the result of the reduction of the labour process to a moment of capital accumulation, thus the labour characteristic of the capitalist era." The paper synthesises elements of both schools while trying to preserve the quantitative determination of value in labour-time.
- **Assessment:** Peer-reviewed, recently published. Represents an emerging synthesis position that may be worth deeper investigation. Low citation count so far, but the interpretive framework is careful.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The question of what makes labour "special" in Marx's theory divides into two largely non-communicating camps with very different methodological assumptions:

**The empiricist camp (Cockshott, Cottrell, Shaikh, Moseley on some issues):** Labour is the substance of value because it is the universal input that best predicts prices across sectors, and this is confirmed by econometric studies. This tradition reads Marx and Ricardo as essentially continuous. It is this tradition that is most vulnerable to Shalizi's "universal input" challenge.

**The social-form camp (Rubin, Backhaus, Arthur, Heinrich, Postone, Murray):** Labour is the substance of value because commodity-producing society constitutes a specific social form in which abstract labour — human productive activity stripped of its concrete character through the social mechanism of commodity exchange — serves as the *medium of social synthesis*. Value is not a natural property of commodities that we discover; it is a social form that commodities acquire through their role in market exchange. Abstract labour is what Marx calls "socially necessary labour" not merely because it reaches the social average of efficiency, but because it has been *socially validated* through exchange. This camp's argument is largely immune to Shalizi's challenge: water cannot be "abstract water" as a medium of social synthesis; only labour, as the specifically human activity that commodity-producing societies treat as generically interchangeable, can play this role.

### Agreements

- All camps agree that Marx's distinction between concrete and abstract labour is central to his value theory.
- All agree that abstract labour is associated specifically with commodity-producing society (though they disagree on what exactly makes it specific).
- All agree that Ricardo's theory is different from Marx's — though they disagree on how important the difference is.

### Conflicts

The major conflict is between the two camps above, with several sub-disputes:

1. **Is abstract labour determined in production or in exchange?** The traditional reading (Cockshott, Moseley, Lietz/Schwarz) says abstract labour is a characteristic of the labour process itself — the expenditure of human labour-power — that precedes exchange. The Neue Marx-Lektüre (especially Heinrich) says abstract labour is only validated through exchange: labour performed but not sold is just "wasted." This dispute has direct implications for whether Cockshott's empirical program makes sense.

2. **Can socially necessary labour-time be directly measured?** Cockshott: yes, by aggregating clock hours and using input-output data. Neue Marx-Lektüre: no, because value is a social relation, not a measurable quantity of time. This makes the entire empirical program suspect from the value-form perspective.

3. **Is Marx's argument continuous with Ricardo's?** Cockshott says yes; Harvey, Arthur, Postone say no; Murray and Rubin say no, but that Marx was building on Ricardo's insight while transforming the theoretical framework entirely.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **The Shalizi challenge has not been directly addressed in the academic Marxist literature.** The "universal input" challenge is clearly formulated in his blog post, but I found no peer-reviewed response specifically engaging with it. This is a genuine gap.

2. **The bridge between value-form theory and empirical research is almost completely absent.** If value is a social relation rather than a measurable quantity, what would empirical research on value even look like? The value-form school has not offered a satisfactory answer.

3. **The question of whether other "universal inputs" could in principle generate equivalent empirical correlations has not been directly tested.** Shalizi asserts this; it seems verifiable. It would be worth checking whether water content or energy content of commodities show similar correlations to prices as labour content does. Some energy-theory-of-value work exists (Costanza 1980 and others in ecological economics), which might bear on this.

4. **Rubin's three-way distinction (physiological / socially equalized / abstract) is underutilized.** It is the cleanest conceptual tool for sorting the camps, but it rarely appears in contemporary literature.

### Surprises

1. **Shalizi's exact argument is in a footnote** about Soviet planning, not in a paper on the labour theory of value. The "water theory of value" quip is almost offhand. This suggests the challenge has not been formulated with full awareness of the value-form literature.

2. **The empiricist Marxists (Cockshott et al.) explicitly concede that Marx and Ricardo make the same empirical predictions.** This is the reverse of what I expected — the defenders of the empirical LTV are voluntarily giving up the claim that Marx's theory is distinct from Ricardo's. If true, Shalizi's challenge bites directly.

3. **Backhaus died in 2026** — the Historical Materialism obituary was retrieved in the search results, suggesting he died very recently. His work on the value-form began in the late 1960s and directly influenced Heinrich, Arthur, and the whole Neue Marx-Lektüre.

4. **There is a genuine internal debate within the value-form school** about whether abstract labour is created in production (Murray's "practically abstract labour") or only constituted in exchange (Heinrich's strict version). This matters for whether Marx's argument has any traction against the "universal input" challenge — because if abstract labour is only confirmed in exchange, then indeed any input validated through exchange would seem to be equally "abstract."

5. **The Cockshott-Harvey debate** (Harvey argues Marx refused the LTV; Cockshott argues Marx definitely had one) is a proxy for a much deeper dispute about what kind of theory Marx was doing — is it an empirical-predictive theory of prices, or a critical-social theory of how human activity is constituted as a specific social form under capitalism?
