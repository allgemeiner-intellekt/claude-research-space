# Survey: Energy Theory of Value and the Shalizi Challenge

**Question**: What is the energy theory of value, and how does it relate to Shalizi's "water theory of value" challenge?

---

## Summary

A substantial body of heterodox and ecological economics — running from Frederick Soddy (1926) through Georgescu-Roegen (1971), Howard Odum (1971–1996), Robert Costanza (1980, 1984), and Robert Ayres and Benjamin Warr (2005) — argues that energy is the fundamental substrate of economic value. This tradition has produced both theoretical frameworks and empirical correlation studies. Costanza (1980) reported R² = 0.85–0.98 between embodied energy and dollar value across US input-output sectors; Ayres and Warr (2005) showed that a 3-factor production function using "useful work" (exergy converted to productive output) explains US GDP growth from 1900–2000 without resort to unexplained TFP residuals.

These results are empirically real and non-trivial. They directly instantiate Shalizi's challenge: Shalizi (2012), in a footnote to his critique of Cockshott et al.'s LTV empirics, pointed out that Kantorovich's mathematical argument for proportionality between optimal-plan values and labour inputs would apply with equal force to *any* input — water, electricity, energy — because the labelling of the "first component" of the input vector is arbitrary. He suggested that if labour-value correlations support the LTV, then equally good energy-value correlations would support an equally valid "water theory" or "electricity theory of value."

Energy theorists have not squarely engaged with this challenge. Their typical response is to argue that energy is *physically special* — it is the only conserved quantity that underlies all transformation, the only input that cannot itself be produced from other inputs at the global scale — but this is a theoretical claim that does not immediately resolve the statistical degeneracy Shalizi identified. The deeper problem, raised by Pirgmaier (2020/2021) and others, is that the whole framing of identifying a single "substance" as the determinant of exchange value may be category error.

## Key Sources

### 1. Frederick Soddy — *Wealth, Virtual Wealth and Debt* (1926); *The Role of Money* (1934)

- **Link**: Semantic Scholar paperId `7aeba93c3ed6a898f6de42e921f5885b27851063` (Wealth, Virtual Wealth and Debt, 1927); `9ae8b6547921fe6c751ffd4db8ec6200c306e109`
- **Key content**: Soddy argued that real "wealth" is governed by thermodynamic laws — it is "the humanly useful forms of matter and energy," subject to the constraint that free energy (available work) is irreversibly degraded. Money and debt, by contrast, follow compound-interest mathematics and can grow indefinitely, creating a structural mismatch with physical wealth. Soddy did not construct a formal "energy theory of value" in the sense of claiming energy is the measure of exchange value; his argument was more ontological: that the classical economists (including Marx) were right to ground value in something physically real, and that thermodynamics identifies that something more rigorously than labour time.
- **Reception**: Dismissed as a "crank" by mainstream economists despite his Nobel Prize in chemistry (1921). *Times Literary Supplement* called it "a respected chemist ruin[ing] his reputation." Economist Frank Knight took him seriously on monetary theory but rejected the thermodynamic foundations. His 100% reserve banking proposal was later rediscovered by the Chicago School. Herman Daly (1980) published "The Economic Thought of Frederick Soddy" in *History of Political Economy*, rehabilitating him as a precursor to ecological economics.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed in the sense that his *Nature* pieces were published there, but his economics books were largely outside normal academic channels. Highly credible as science of thermodynamics; heterodox as economics. His argument is more a metaphysical grounding than an empirical value theory — he does not claim energy *predicts* prices.

### 2. Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen — *The Entropy Law and the Economic Process* (1971)

- **Link**: Harvard University Press, 1971. DOI reference via Semantic Scholar.
- **Key content**: Georgescu-Roegen introduced the concept of entropy into economics in a systematic way, arguing that production is irreversible matter-energy transformation and that low-entropy (available) energy is the true scarce resource. The economic process degrades free energy and matter into heat and waste — an arrow of time that neoclassical economics ignores by treating production as a reversible flow. He did not formally propose that prices should be proportional to energy content, but his framework established the conceptual basis for ecological economics' treatment of energy as the binding constraint.
- **Key distinction**: Georgescu-Roegen carefully separated "useful work (exergy)" from mere energy quantity, anticipating later Ayres-Warr distinctions. His target was not Marx but mainstream neoclassical economics.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed scholarly monograph, highly cited (700+ cites). Represents the foundational academic statement of thermodynamic limits in economics. Not itself an energy theory of value but the intellectual parent of several.

### 3. Robert Costanza — "Embodied Energy and Economic Valuation" (1980, *Science*)

- **Link**: DOI: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.210.4476.1219; PubMed ID: 17810761; Semantic Scholar paperId `b27dc1efb0a935cd01fb922e0b4bf203e064c9a5`
- **Key content**: Costanza extended Leontief input-output analysis to include the energy required to produce labor and government services (earlier analyses had omitted these). He found "a strong relation between embodied energy and dollar value for a 92-sector US economy." Published in *Science* (1980) with 659 citations as of 2024.
- **Key statistics**: Costanza's later synthesis ("Value Theory and Energy," in *Encyclopedia of Ecological Economics*, 2004) reports R² = 0.85–0.98 between embodied energy and dollar value across sectors when fuel quality corrections are made. The 1984 follow-up with Herendeen ("Embodied Energy and Economic Value in the United States Economy: 1963, 1967 and 1972," *Resources and Energy*) extended the analysis to three input-output tables. Also co-authored "Energy and the US Economy: A Biophysical Perspective" in *Science* (1984) with Cleveland, Hall, and Kaufmann (DOI: 10.1126/science.225.4665.890; 827 citations).
- **Costanza's theoretical claim**: Markets, if operating efficiently, generate prices "proportional to energy" because markets function as "efficient energy allocation devices." He argued that energy-price proportionality is what *should* be true if markets are efficient — with deviations being market failures.
- **Debt to Shalizi's challenge**: Costanza acknowledges that fuel quality corrections are needed — not all energy inputs are equal. This is an implicit admission that "energy" as a simple quantity does not predict prices without additional theory about what kind of energy and how concentrated. He does not address whether water or mass would correlate as well.
- **Assessment**: High-quality empirical work published in *Science* and peer-reviewed journals. Costanza is a credentialed ecologist and economist (founder of *Ecological Economics* journal). The correlation results are real, but the theoretical interpretation (that correlation *validates* the energy theory) is contested.

### 4. Howard Odum — Emergy Theory (1971–1996)

- **Link**: Key paper: "Self-Organization, Transformity and Information," *Science* (1988). DOI not available from search; Semantic Scholar paperId `20f4228eb31e83abe439cb728b5bb1be2e3355af` (1242 citations). Book: *Environmental Accounting: Emergy and Environmental Decision Making*, Wiley, 1996.
- **Key content**: Odum coined "emergy" (embodied energy, spelled with an 'm') to measure the total amount of one type of energy (usually solar energy equivalent, measured in "solar emjoules") required to produce a product or service, including all indirect inputs. He introduced "transformity" — the emergy per unit energy at different levels of the energy hierarchy. Higher transformity energy (e.g., electricity, information, concentrated fossil fuels) requires more lower-quality solar energy to produce.
- **Odum vs. Costanza debate**: The Pirgmaier (2021) ecological economics article notes a debate in the 1990s between Odum and Costanza about whether emergy-price proportionality holds. Odum was more skeptical than Costanza about treating markets as efficient energy allocators — his view was that money *should* track emergy but that actual markets systematically fail to do so, making emergy a corrective measure of *real wealth* rather than a predictor of market prices. The Frontiers in Energy Research 2014 paper found nominal GDP best explained by a power function of total emergy use (exponent 2.8).
- **The "not just any energy" distinction**: Odum's hierarchy distinguishes solar energy, fossil fuels, electricity, information — each with different transformity. This is an attempt to solve the quality problem that simple energy theories face. But transformity values are contested and hard to calculate independently of economic data.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, highly cited (1242 cites for 1988 *Science* paper). Represents the most sophisticated "substance" theory of value from the energy side. But transformity calculations are not independent of price data, raising circularity concerns similar to those raised against the LTV.

### 5. Robert Ayres and Benjamin Warr — "Accounting for Growth: The Role of Physical Work" (2005) and related work

- **Link**: *Structural Change and Economic Dynamics* 16, 181-209. DOI: 10.1016/J.STRUECO.2003.10.003; Semantic Scholar paperId `2f6612526d8daa469e9c8c419a5bf37d9d0d0b18` (399 citations). Also: Warr, Schandl, and Ayres (2008) "Long-Term Trends in Resource Exergy Consumption and Useful Work Supplies in the UK, 1900 to 2000" (58 citations).
- **Key content**: Ayres and Warr's REXS (Resource Exergy Services) model introduces "useful work" — the actual mechanical, thermal, and electrical work performed by end-use devices, as distinct from primary energy inputs. They estimate time-series of useful work efficiency for the US (1900–2000) and UK (1900–2000) and insert this as a third factor of production alongside capital and labour. The main finding is that a 3-factor production function using useful work explains historical GDP trajectories *without* requiring an exogenous TFP (total factor productivity) residual.
- **What this means for the Shalizi challenge**: Ayres and Warr are careful not to claim that useful work *determines value* in the sense of an energy theory of value. Their argument is weaker: that useful work is a *necessary input* whose contribution mainstream economics misattributes to unexplained TFP. But the framework could be read as supporting an energy-based value theory since useful work correlates so strongly with GDP.
- **Assessment**: Published in respectable journals, 399 citations. Represents the strongest *empirical* case that energy inputs (specifically, the economically useful subset) drive economic growth. The theoretical gap between "useful work drives growth" and "useful work determines value" is significant and Ayres generally avoids claiming the latter.

### 6. Costanza, Cleveland, Hall, Kaufmann — "Energy and the US Economy: A Biophysical Perspective" (1984, *Science*)

- **Link**: DOI: 10.1126/science.225.4665.890; Semantic Scholar paperId `9cea23b6da773d2e0b73a031538fce18aa82ee0d` (827 citations)
- **Key content**: Presented a biophysical framework for the US economy across time-series and cross-sectional data. Also introduced the concept of energy quality — different fuels have different "economic effectiveness per heat unit" — which explains why energy/GNP ratios changed over time even as energy use grew. Kaufmann (1992) extended this with a regression model predicting the energy:GNP ratio (R* = 0.96) based on fuel mix.
- **Implication**: This is the empirical bedrock of biophysical economics. The 0.96 R² figure refers to *explaining variance in the energy:GNP ratio* (i.e., accounting for quality changes in the energy mix), not the cross-sectional correlation between embodied energy and prices.

### 7. Pirgmaier (2021) — "The Value of Value Theory for Ecological Economics"

- **Link**: *Ecological Economics* 179, 106790 (2021). DOI: 10.1016/j.ecolecon.2020.106790. PMC: PMC7445139.
- **Key content**: A critical survey that explicitly identifies what she calls the "energy theory of value" implicit in much ecological economics. She argues it suffers the same flaw as classical and neoclassical value theories — it theorizes exchange value in "transhistorical terms." Since all societies produce goods using energy, this cannot explain why exchange value dominates in capitalist societies specifically. She cites the 1990s Costanza-Odum debate. She argues energy theories treat markets as "efficient energy allocation devices" — a claim that requires additional theoretical justification.
- **Key passage**: "A historical explanation of the dominance of exchange value matters for understanding why planetary boundaries are reached."
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed in *Ecological Economics*. Represents a sophisticated internal critique from within ecological economics. Does not engage with the Shalizi challenge directly but raises a structurally similar point about theoretical underdetermination.

### 8. Guarino, Martini, Bagliani — "Energy Theory of Value and Input-Output Analysis" (2023)

- **Link**: *Economia & Lavoro* 2/2023. DOI: 10.7384/109533
- **Key content**: Recent Italian paper that reconstructs the ETV using a modified Leontief input-output model (including labour and government in the transaction matrix), applied to Italian 2015 data. Found correlation between embedded energy and economic value "demonstrates the interdependence between energy inputs and economic outputs." Frames this within the "World-Ecology" conceptual framework (Jason Moore). Argues this "seriously compromises the possibility of real decoupling."
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed journal paper. Represents the most up-to-date explicit attempt to formalize and test ETV. Low citation count (recent). Consistent with earlier Costanza findings but provides no direct comparison to labour-value correlations.

### 9. Benjamin Leiva — "Why Are Prices Proportional to Embodied Energies?" (2018/2019)

- **Link**: *BioPhysical Economics and Resource Quality* 4, 1-16. DOI: 10.1007/s41247-019-0062-y. ArXiv: 1811.12502.
- **Key content**: Provides a formal microeconomic model for why prices would be proportional to embodied energy, based on (1) goods are material rearrangements and (2) humans can only rearrange matter with energy transfers. Under perfect competition, nominal prices arise as social manifestations of goods' marginal energy transfers. The proportionality between prices and average embodied energy follows from the mathematical relationship between marginal and average energy requirements.
- **Assessment**: Published in a specialist ecological economics journal, low citations (1). Represents the strongest attempt to give a proper theoretical foundation for energy-price proportionality rather than treating it purely as an empirical finding. The model's assumptions (perfect competition, goods as pure material rearrangements) are strong.

### 10. Parys (2018) — "Labour Values and Energy Values: Some Developments on the Common Substance of Value since 1867"

- **Link**: *European Journal of the History of Economic Thought* 25(5), 1052-1080 (2018). DOI: 10.1080/09672567.2018.1523939.
- **Key content**: Historical and analytical comparison of labour-value and energy-value theories. Discusses "percentage formulas for price-value deviations" for both. Traces the parallel development of both approaches since Marx. Access-restricted; full contents not retrieved.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed academic journal. Directly addresses the question of how labour-value and energy-value theories compare. The most directly relevant academic source on the explicit parallelism.

### 11. Shalizi (2012) — "In Soviet Union, Optimization Problem Solves You"

- **Link**: Originally at `cscs.umich.edu/~crshalizi/weblog/918.html` (now 404); cross-posted at Crooked Timber: https://crookedtimber.org/2012/05/30/in-soviet-union-optimization-problem-solves-you/
- **Key content**: In footnote 4, Shalizi analyses Kantorovich's attempt to reconcile linear programming "objectively determined values" with Marxist LTV. He notes that Kantorovich's mathematical proof establishes proportionality between OD-values and "the required consumption of the *first component* of the vector of inputs" — but input ordering is arbitrary. Any input could be the "first component." Therefore, "the first component could be any input to the production process, and the same argument would go through, leading to many parallel 'theories of value.'" He implies that proponents could equally argue for a "water theory of value" or "electricity theory of value" with identical logical structure.
- **Assessment**: Blog post by a prominent statistician (Carnegie Mellon professor), not peer-reviewed. But the argument is a clear mathematical point about the structure of Kantorovich's theorem and is logically valid. The "water theory" framing became a common reference in LTV debates.

### 12. Cockshott (2020) — "Defending the Labour Theory of Value Again" (blog)

- **Link**: https://paulcockshott.wordpress.com/2020/05/07/defending-the-labour-theory-of-value-again/
- **Key content**: Cockshott acknowledges the energy challenge implicitly by arguing labour is special because it "enters preponderantly into every branch of production" due to its polymorphic character. He tells commenters concerned with energy/thermodynamic arguments to test theories using input-output tables, suggesting empirical testing will show labour outperforms energy as a predictor. He does not engage with Shalizi's water/electricity argument directly.
- **Assessment**: Blog post. Cockshott is a computer scientist and Marxist economist (University of Glasgow), prolific in the LTV empirics literature. His response to the energy challenge amounts to: "test it and you'll see labour wins."

## Landscape Assessment

### State of knowledge

The energy theory of value exists as a real, developed research tradition with:
1. A founding philosophical argument (Soddy, Georgescu-Roegen)
2. Empirical correlation studies (Costanza 1980, 1984; Costanza et al. 1984)
3. Macro-econometric models (Ayres-Warr 2005, 2008, 2012)
4. A formal microeconomic foundation attempt (Leiva 2018)
5. An emergy/transformity extension addressing quality (Odum 1988, 1996)

The correlation statistics are real. Costanza's embodied-energy to price correlations (R² 0.85–0.98 across 92 US sectors) are comparable in magnitude to the labour-value correlations reported by Cockshott et al. (r ≈ 0.95–0.97 in various countries). Neither set of correlations, by itself, establishes a theoretical claim.

### Agreements across sources

- Energy is a universal input to all production
- Embodied energy correlates substantially with sectoral price levels
- Useful work (exergy actually converted) correlates strongly with aggregate GDP growth
- Simple energy quantities fail to predict prices accurately without quality adjustments (both Costanza's fuel-quality corrections and Odum's transformity approach acknowledge this)

### Conflicts and tensions

- **Costanza vs. Odum**: Costanza treated efficient markets as implied energy allocators (prices should track embodied energy). Odum was skeptical — he thought *actual* markets fail to price energy correctly, and emergy analysis is a corrective normative tool, not a positive predictor of prices.
- **ETV vs. LTV**: The Parys (2018) paper directly compares the two. Both face the same empirical challenge: their correlation statistics are of similar magnitude, which neither vindicates nor refutes the other.
- **Theoretical claim vs. empirical correlation**: Leiva (2018) tries to derive *theoretically* why energy-price proportionality holds; Costanza simply observed it; Odum argued it *should* hold normatively if markets were rational. These are distinct claims that energy theorists conflate.
- **The universal-input problem**: No energy theorist directly rebuts Shalizi's challenge that water or mass inputs might correlate equally. Cockshott's implicit response (labour dominates empirically) applies symmetrically to energy — it just pushes the debate to: whose correlation is stronger? This is an empirical question that appears not to have been systematically tested with a direct head-to-head comparison.

### Gaps and under-explored areas

1. **Direct head-to-head comparison of energy vs. labour correlations across the same sectors and countries.** Costanza reports R² 0.85–0.98 for embodied energy; Cockshott et al. report r ≈ 0.95–0.97 for labour values. But these are from different datasets, different countries, different years. A unified study comparing both (plus water, capital, mass) to prices in the *same* sectors does not appear to exist in the accessible literature.

2. **Whether Shalizi's challenge has been addressed by ETV proponents.** Energy theorists typically argue energy is physically special (cannot be substituted at the global scale, unlike water which can be replenished). But the *statistical* challenge — that any sufficient-coverage input will produce high sectoral correlations because of input-output structure — is not directly addressed.

3. **The circularity problem for transformity/emergy.** Odum's transformity values are in part computed from economic data, creating a circularity analogous to the one Nitzan and Bichler raised against LTV empirics (that labour values are inferred from prices, making the correlation partially tautological).

4. **Stallinga (2020)** — A paper in *Modern Economy* (SCIRP journal) titled "On the Energy Theory of Value: Economy and Policies" could not be retrieved in full. It may contain additional empirical material.

5. **The "useful work" vs. "embodied energy" distinction as a response to Shalizi.** Ayres-Warr's useful-work measure is arguably less "universal" than raw energy — most products do not make it into the useful-work category. If useful work correlates better with GDP than raw energy does, this might suggest the correlation is about economic productivity rather than a universal physical substrate.

### Surprises

1. **The magnitude of the correlations is genuinely impressive.** R² = 0.85–0.98 (Costanza) is not trivially explained by size effects alone — Costanza explicitly corrected for this by examining per-unit ratios. These numbers are as strong as reported labour-value correlations and cannot be dismissed as noise.

2. **The Costanza-Odum split** is theoretically significant: the founder of ecological economics (Costanza) and the founder of emergy analysis (Odum) disagreed about whether markets actually *do* price energy efficiently, even while both believed energy was fundamental to real wealth. This suggests energy theorists themselves are uncertain about the mechanism.

3. **No one appears to have done the obvious test**: run both embodied labour and embodied energy in the same regression on sectoral prices and see which dominates, or whether they are collinear. This test would directly address Shalizi's point.

4. **Shalizi's "water theory" framing was made in a footnote**, in the context of Kantorovich and Soviet planning mathematics, not in a systematic methodological critique of ETV. The argument is logically airtight but was not directed at Costanza's or Odum's programs specifically.
