# Survey: Responses to Shalizi's "Water Theory of Value" Challenge

## Summary

Shalizi's 2011 blog post (https://bactra.org/weblog/820.html) reviewing Farjoun and Machover's *Laws of Chaos* contains a precise and pointed challenge: since labour's role in Farjoun and Machover's framework depends only on its being a "universal input" to all production, any other universal input — water, electricity, thermodynamic free energy — would generate the same analytical structure and the same predicted correlation with prices. This is a philosophically clean objection.

The verdict after extensive searching: **Shalizi's specific "water/energy" argument has never been directly and convincingly refuted**. The closest thing to a structured response comes from Cockshott's own empirical work (the 1997 Cockshott/Cottrell CJE paper and his 2018 blog post), which shows that labour correlates better with prices than iron/steel, electricity, or computer services do — but this defence is methodologically contested and does not engage the deeper logical point Shalizi is making. Shalizi himself has not returned to this specific challenge in any substantial way since 2011. No Marxian economist has published a paper that names Shalizi's water argument specifically and addresses it head-on.

## Key Sources

### 1. Shalizi, "A Marxian Econophysics" (October 2011)
- **Link**: https://bactra.org/weblog/820.html
- **Key content**: The original post. Shalizi reviews *Laws of Chaos* (Farjoun & Machover, 1983). He accepts the stochastic profit-rate argument but rejects the rehabilitation of LTV. His two core objections: (a) the heterogeneity-of-labour problem (labelling all work "abstract labour" is as arbitrary as lumping all fuels into "energy"); (b) the universality problem: labour is special in F&M's framework only because it is a universal input. But water and electricity are also universal inputs. Since F&M's argument for why the labour-content/price ratio is approximately constant rests on universality alone, the same argument would generate an approximately constant water-content/price ratio or energy-content/price ratio. Shalizi adds a footnote suggesting that the constant ratio between labour-content and water-content would just be the ratio of the corresponding entries in the dominant eigenvector of the input-output matrix (Frobenius-Perron). He does not claim to have tested this empirically; the argument is theoretical.
- **Assessment**: A prestigious statistical scientist's blog, not peer-reviewed, but Shalizi is a serious methodologist at Carnegie Mellon. The argument is logically tight and has been widely circulated in heterodox economics online debates. This post attracted manual trackbacks from Peter Frase, Mostly Not About Llamas, Blog Pra falar de coisas, and Abandoned Footnotes — indicating it entered the left-economics blogosphere promptly.

### 2. Cockshott and Cottrell, "Labour Time versus Alternative Value Bases: A Research Note" (1997)
- **Link**: https://academic.oup.com/cje/article-abstract/21/4/545/1729729 (DOI: 10.1093/oxfordjournals.cje.a013685)
- **Key content**: This is the primary empirical response — predating Shalizi's 2011 post by 14 years, and likely written in response to earlier versions of the universality objection. Cockshott and Cottrell take UK input-output data and compare the correlation of prices with: (1) direct+indirect labour content, (2) iron and steel content, (3) electricity, gas and water supply content, (4) computer and related activities content. Labour dominates the other three in correlation with market prices. The other industries correlate weakly or not at all. This is reproduced in visual form in Cockshott's 2018 blog post "Did Marx have a labour theory of value?" and repeatedly referenced by him in online debates.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, Cambridge Journal of Economics. 74 citations on Semantic Scholar. This is Cockshott's strongest direct answer to the "why not water or electricity?" challenge. However, as critics note (see below), the methodology is contested: comparing separate Leontief-inverse correlations one by one rather than using multivariate analysis, and the choice of "electricity, gas and water supply" as a sector rather than disaggregated water or energy content per se, may not cleanly test Shalizi's claim.

### 3. Cockshott, "3 Bad Objections to the Labour Theory of Value" (September 2019)
- **Link**: https://paulcockshott.wordpress.com/2019/09/05/3-bad-objections-to-the-labour-theory-of-value/
- **Key content**: Cockshott addresses the "objection from money," the "objection from social necessity," and the "objection from size" (the spurious correlation/industry-size argument). He does not name Shalizi. His reply to the "size" objection runs as follows: a valid third factor causing spurious correlation must be independently quantifiable; "size" cannot be measured independently of labour or money value; physical measures (kg, land area) would produce weak correlations with monetary output. Labour is universal — not all things contain rubber or fats, but all production uses labour. He cites: Zachariah 2004 (Sweden, 96.5% correlation from actual person-years), Michaelson/Cockshott/Cottrell 1995 "Testing Marx" (UK, 96.4%). The "universality of labour" argument is explicitly made.
- **Assessment**: Blog post, not peer-reviewed. This is Cockshott's clearest articulation of the universality claim. He does not name Shalizi or water; the implicit logic is the same as Shalizi's challenge but inverted — Cockshott uses universality as a positive argument, not a problem to solve. He does not address the theoretical core of Shalizi's point, which is that universality alone would generate the same result for any universal input.

### 4. Cockshott, "Did Marx have a Labour Theory of Value?" (April 2018)
- **Link**: https://paulcockshott.wordpress.com/2018/04/05/did-marx-have-a-labour-theory-of-value/
- **Key content**: Primarily a response to David Harvey's claim that Marx had no LTV. Contains the graph (referenced by Unlearning Economics) showing labour correlations vs. iron/steel, electricity, and computer services. Labour is clearly dominant. Cockshott's implicit argument: the "placebo" industries fail the test, which distinguishes labour from other inputs. References the 1997 CJE "alternative value bases" paper explicitly.
- **Assessment**: Blog post. Useful for the visual evidence Cockshott uses, but the argument is assertoric rather than engaged with the statistical critiques. This post does not directly address Shalizi.

### 5. Cockshott, "Response to Unlearning Economics" (December 2024)
- **Link**: https://paulcockshott.wordpress.com/2024/12/15/response-to-unlearning-economics/
- **Key content**: Responding to a December 2024 Medium post ("Astonishingly Poor Empirics, Cockshott Edition") by the blog/YouTube channel "Unlearning Economics." The critic's core arguments: (a) high correlation with labour is not a unique test because industry size drives it (citing Blair Fix and Bichler/Nitzan); (b) a simple simulation (Y=2L+2K+u with higher variance in L) reproduces Cockshott's results without LTV being true, because correlation is mechanically related to variance, not causation. Cockshott's reply: (a) we use input-output Leontief inverses that already account for all factor inputs; (b) "value added" in the critic's simulation is actually just profit (S), not Marxian value added (V+S), so the simulation tests a different quantity; (c) "size" is not an independent third factor because you can't measure industry size without using labour hours or money value; (d) labour is special because it is the *universal* input — "not all things use rubber, but all production uses labour." Crucially, Cockshott repeats here the universality claim as his positive argument, without addressing the logical force of Shalizi's inversion of it.
- **Assessment**: Blog post. The most recent and most explicit engagement with current critics. The universality of labour as a *defense* rather than a *problem* is Cockshott's settled position. He does not name Shalizi or water anywhere in this or other posts found.

### 6. Unlearning Economics, "Astonishingly Poor Empirics, Cockshott Edition" (December 2024)
- **Link**: https://unlearnecon.medium.com/astonishingly-poor-empirics-cockshott-edition-98223ccfcdf2
- **Key content**: Detailed statistical critique. Argues that high labour-value correlations are a mechanical result of variance differentials, not causal evidence. The Stata simulation is particularly clean: in a world where LTV is false (Y=2L+2K+u, with L having higher std dev than K), you still get a 0.94 correlation with labour and only 0.36 with capital. Also notes that Cockshott's use of separate Leontief-inverse correlations (one by one for labour, steel, electricity, etc.) rather than multiple regression means the "placebo" test does not cleanly separate the factors. Explicitly mentions Shalizi in passing in the context of simulation criticism: "Cosma Shalizi faced similar pushback when he simulated a multifactor intelligence model to show that IQ could just be an emergent phenomenon; critics missed that he didn't believe his simulations were the correct theory of intelligence." Does not engage Shalizi's specific water argument directly.
- **Assessment**: Blog/Medium post by a heterodox (but not Marxist) economics commentator. Well-argued statistically but brief. Not peer-reviewed.

### 7. Blair Fix, "'Testing' the Labour Theory of Value with Metaphysical Alchemy" (March 2026)
- **Link**: https://capitalaspower.com/2026/03/fix-testing-the-labor-theory-of-value-with-metaphysical-alchemy/
- **Key content**: Very recent (March 2026). The title suggests a sharp critique of LTV empirics. Associated with the "Capital as Power" research programme (Nitzan/Bichler tradition). The full text is too large to have been read in its entirety, but the title and context indicate Blair Fix continues his earlier argument that the labour correlation is an artefact of industry scale. Fix is a prolific critic in this space and has previously published a widely-cited piece arguing labour-value correlations reduce to size effects. Whether he addresses the Shalizi water argument specifically is not confirmed from the search results.
- **Assessment**: Recent blog/working paper, not peer-reviewed. High-quality methodological critic. Given the title (metaphysical alchemy), the target appears to be the ontological assumptions of the LTV tests, not specifically the universality problem.

### 8. Cockshott, Cottrell, and Valle Baeza, "The Empirics of the Labour Theory of Value: Reply to Nitzan and Bichler" (2014)
- **Link**: https://bnarchives.yorku.ca/496/2/cockshott_cottrell_valle_baeza_2014_the_empirics_of_the_ltv_reply_to_nitzan_and_bichler.pdf
- **Key content**: Peer-reviewed response in *Investigación Económica* (vol. LXXIII, no. 287, Jan-Mar 2014). Addresses Nitzan and Bichler's two main critiques: (1) the studies correlate prices with prices (not labour hours), and (2) the correlations are spurious due to industry scale. Cockshott et al. rebut both. On the "monetary proxy" objection: they demonstrate that Swedish I/O data with actual person-year figures (Zachariah 2004) and UK wage-bill deflated to hours (their 1995 "Testing Marx" paper) both give ~96% correlations. On the "size" objection: they argue no physically independent measure of industry size gives a tight correlation with monetary output, making "size" an ill-defined confounding variable. They make the dimensionality argument — that correlating heterogeneous price vectors item by item is mathematically invalid (only industry-level aggregates, which are dimensionally homogeneous, are legitimate). Does not mention Shalizi or water.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed. The most rigorous formal response to the spurious-correlation family of arguments, but does not engage Shalizi's specific logical inversion of universality.

### 9. Moraitis and Basu, "Alternative Approaches to Labor Values and Prices of Production: Theory and Evidence" (2026)
- **Link**: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/369378109_Alternative_Approaches_to_Labor_Values_and_Prices_of_Production_Theory_and_Evidence
- **Key content**: This is a recent (2026) paper in *Structural Change and Economic Dynamics* that explicitly compares multiple alternative "value bases" for prices. The title and abstract confirm this addresses the question of whether labour is special among alternative bases. The full text was not retrieved (file too large), but this is precisely the type of systematic test that would address Shalizi's challenge empirically.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed academic paper, very recent. High relevance — this appears to be the most serious academic treatment of the "alternative bases" question. Whether it names Shalizi or water specifically is not confirmed.

### 10. Işıkara and Mokre, "Price-Value Deviations and the Labour Theory of Value: Evidence from 42 Countries, 2000–2017" (2021)
- **Link**: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09538259.2021.1904648
- **Key content**: Large-scale empirical study supporting the LTV across 42 countries. Does not engage alternative value bases directly, but provides the most comprehensive cross-national dataset on price-value deviations.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed in *Review of Political Economy*. 13 citations. Strong empirical paper but does not address the universality/water challenge.

### 11. Shalizi, "In Soviet Union, Optimization Problem Solves You" (May 2012)
- **Link**: https://bactra.org/weblog/918.html
- **Key content**: A longer follow-up post about socialist planning and the computational complexity of optimal pricing. This is about central planning, not the LTV. Shalizi does not return to the water challenge in this post. He does discuss the difficulty of socialist economic calculation in response to Francis Spufford's *Red Plenty*.
- **Assessment**: Shalizi's 2012 blog output did not revisit the 2011 water argument. His other posts from 2012-2015 (book reviews, etc.) similarly do not return to the LTV.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The Shalizi water challenge sits at the intersection of two long-running debates: (1) the empirical status of the LTV (price-labour value correlations), and (2) the "spurious correlation" critique made famous by Bichler and Nitzan and independently by Blair Fix. Shalizi's version is distinctly theoretical: he is making a logical point about the structure of the Farjoun/Machover argument, not primarily an empirical claim.

**Direct responses**: There are none that name Shalizi's water argument specifically. Cockshott and Cottrell's 1997 "Labour Time versus Alternative Value Bases" paper is the closest empirical response — it predates the 2011 post and appears to address the class of objection Shalizi is making. It shows labour correlates better with prices than steel/electricity/computing services. However, whether water or energy has been directly tested as a Leontief-inverse base and found inferior to labour in systematic published form is not confirmed by the sources found.

**Implicit responses via the universality defence**: Cockshott's consistent response to any "why labour specifically?" challenge is to assert that labour is the only truly universal input. He makes this argument in 2019 and 2024 blog posts. This directly engages Shalizi's premise (that universality is the only feature F&M use) but inverts it — Cockshott claims the other candidates (water, energy) are NOT universal inputs when traced through the Leontief inverse. Whether this is empirically true for water specifically has not been tested in any source found.

**The simulation counter-arguments**: Both Unlearning Economics (2024) and Blair Fix (2026 title) represent a different angle — arguing the high correlations are mechanical artefacts of variance differentials and size effects, not evidence of LTV. These are connected to but distinct from Shalizi's universality challenge.

### Agreements Between Sources

- There is broad agreement that labour-value/price correlations are empirically high (0.9+) across many countries and time periods.
- There is broad agreement that alternative inputs (steel, electricity as a sector) correlate less well with prices than labour does in Cockshott's framework.
- Everyone — including critics — accepts that Cockshott's approach uses Leontief inverses to trace all factor contributions back to a single basis.

### Conflicts

- **On the meaning of the correlation**: Cockshott treats high correlation as evidence for LTV; Unlearning Economics, Fix, and Bichler/Nitzan treat it as a spurious size/variance effect.
- **On what counts as a valid alternative base**: Cockshott says steel and electricity are not universal (some products have no steel); Shalizi says water and energy are universal; no published study has tested water content specifically and compared its correlating power to labour content.
- **On dimensionality**: Cockshott makes a dimensionality argument (you can't correlate price lists that have heterogeneous units); his critics call this ad hoc.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **No one has actually run Shalizi's specific empirical test.** No published paper or blog post found directly computes "water content" (water directly and indirectly used, via Leontief inverse) and correlates it with prices. This is a gap in the literature. Shalizi himself merely suggested it as a theoretical possibility. If someone actually did this and water correlated poorly with prices, that would constitute a genuine empirical response to his challenge. If water correlated well, that would strengthen his critique considerably.

2. **Shalizi has never followed up.** His 2011 post remains his only published work on this topic. He has not responded to Cockshott's blog posts or the subsequent debate. It is unclear whether he is aware of the 1997 CJE paper, which is the closest thing to an empirical response.

3. **The Moraitis/Basu 2026 paper** looks like the most important potential direct response, comparing multiple value bases formally. Its full content was not retrieved and would be worth a deep dive.

4. **No engagement between Cottrell and Shalizi** has been found. Cottrell does not appear to have written anything specifically addressing the water challenge.

5. **Blog/forum discussion**: The Shalizi post was linked by left-leaning blogs (Peter Frase, etc.) in 2011-2012, but no extended thread engaging specifically with his water challenge was found. The discussion in the heterodox blogosphere mostly merged it with the general "spurious correlation" debate.

### Surprises

- The 1997 Cockshott/Cottrell CJE paper testing "alternative value bases" is surprisingly well-positioned as a response to Shalizi's challenge — but it predates the 2011 post by 14 years and was never brought into direct dialogue with it. Cockshott clearly had the empirical response ready; the theoretical engagement was never made.
- Shalizi has never been directly cited or named in any Cockshott/Cottrell response found. The two bodies of work exist in parallel without apparent awareness of each other.
- The most recent engagement with the "universality" issue (Cockshott 2024) still uses universality as a positive argument, not as a problem to be defended. This suggests either Shalizi's specific framing has not reached Cockshott, or Cockshott regards the 1997 CJE results as a sufficient empirical response that doesn't require theoretical acknowledgment.
- The March 2026 Blair Fix paper at Capital as Power may represent a new high-water mark in LTV empirics criticism, but its content could not be fully read.
