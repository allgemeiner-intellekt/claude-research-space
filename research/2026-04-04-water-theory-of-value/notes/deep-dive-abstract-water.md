# Deep Dive: Real Abstraction and the Water/Electricity Challenge

## Question

Does the value-form school's concept of "real abstraction" successfully rebut the challenge that other homogeneous commodities — water, electricity — could in principle ground an alternative theory of value? Is "real abstraction" a coherent, non-circular concept that does genuine explanatory work?

---

## Investigation

### 1. What is "Real Abstraction"?

The term "real abstraction" (reale Abstraktion) has a genealogy that runs through several overlapping but not identical uses. Understanding the intellectual history is necessary before assessing whether it answers the water/electricity challenge.

**Simmel's precedent**: Georg Simmel used the term before Sohn-Rethel in *The Philosophy of Money* (1900), writing that "not only the study of the economy but the economy itself is constituted by a real abstraction from the comprehensive reality of valuations" (cited in Reichelt, "Marx's Critique of Economic Categories"). For Simmel, money converts qualitatively incommensurable items into quantitatively commensurable commodities — a real process, not a mental one. This is importantly prior to any claim about labour's unique status.

**Sohn-Rethel's formalization**: Alfred Sohn-Rethel in *Intellectual and Manual Labour* (1970, English translation 1978) defines real abstraction as arising "in exchange from the reciprocal relationship between two commodity-owners and it applies only to this interrelationship." The key thesis: commodity exchange is not merely a mental operation — it enacts an abstraction in reality. When two commodities are exchanged as equivalents, their qualitative differences are practically annulled. The commodities are treated as if they share a common "substance" of value, regardless of their physical properties. This equivalence-setting occurs in practice, not just in theory.

Sohn-Rethel's further claim — the one that generated the most philosophical interest — is that this practical, real abstraction is the historical origin of the conceptual abstractions of Greek philosophy and natural science. The pure categories of thought (identity, quantity, substance, causality) are not invented by philosophers but are "ideal reflections" of the real abstraction enacted in the exchange process. This is Sohn-Rethel's materialist epistemology.

Crucially: in Sohn-Rethel's framework, the real abstraction applies to **commodity exchange as such** — not to labour specifically. His "exchange abstraction" brackets the use-values of commodities during exchange. Any commodity, including water or electricity, undergoes this bracketing when exchanged as a commodity.

**Adorno's formulation** (cited in The Charnel-House, 2014): "The abstraction in question here is really the specific form of the exchange process itself, the underlying social fact through which socialization first comes about." In exchange, "the parties must leave aside a certain aspect of the commodities… they are reduced to a universal unit." This universal unit is, as Adorno notes, "the average necessary amount of social labor time."

So for Adorno and Sohn-Rethel, the real abstraction is the exchange process itself, but what is abstracted *to* is abstract labour time. This is where the claim about labour's specificity enters.

**Rubin's (1927) contribution**: Rubin's *Essays on Marx's Theory of Value* makes the key argument about abstract labour as a social, not physiological, category. Rubin critiques the "physiological interpretation" of abstract labour (reading it as sheer expenditure of human energy) and argues for the "social interpretation": abstract labour is the equalization of all forms of labour in a many-sided exchange. It is labour that is "social" only in the specific form in which it becomes social in a commodity economy — through the indirect equalization of all private labours by exchange.

Key passage from Rubin (Chapter 14, via marxists.org): "Abstract labour is the variety of social labour or socially equalized labour in general... The physiological homogeneity of human labour is a biological presupposition, and not a cause of the development of the social division of labour." And: "if abstract labour is an expenditure of human energy in physiological form, then value also has a reified-material character. Or value is a social phenomenon, and then abstract labour must also be understood as a social phenomenon connected with a determined social form of production."

Rubin's argument is that abstract labour is not labour minus its particular qualities (a mental abstraction from concrete labour); it is a specific *social form* that labour takes in commodity production — the form of being a unit of the total social labour distributed through exchange. This is a real property, not an ideal one, but it is specifically social, not physical.

**Value-form school synthesis** (Arthur, Heinrich, Postone, Backhaus): The contemporary value-form school inherits both Sohn-Rethel's practical/real conception of abstraction and Rubin's social interpretation of abstract labour. The synthesis goes roughly:

1. Commodity exchange is a practical act that enacts equivalence — a "real abstraction."
2. What commodities are equated *as* is value, which is the objectification of abstract labour.
3. Abstract labour is not a physiological property of labour but a specifically capitalist social form: the form that private labour takes when it must be validated through exchange.
4. Labour is specifically singled out because it is the only "commodity" whose use-value is itself the capacity to produce value — labour-power, when consumed in production, creates more value than it costs. This is the source of surplus value.

From John Milios's critical review of Sohn-Rethel (2020): "Only under this precondition [that all active agents are commodity owners, including the seller of labour-power] all products of labor... become commodities, that is goods produced for-exchange-and-for-profit bearing a value which is expressed in monetary units; only under this precondition useful-concrete labor exists also as abstract labor; we may therefore speak about a real abstraction."

**Postone's version**: In *Time, Labor, and Social Domination* (1993), Moishe Postone argues that abstract labour is not just a neutral input equalized by exchange, but a form of social domination specific to capitalism. Labour is unique because it serves as the medium of social synthesis under capitalism — the way in which individuals are connected to the social totality. Capital accumulation drives the progressive reduction of necessary labour time while maintaining labour as the form through which social membership is constituted. This creates the peculiar dynamic of capitalist modernity: productivity increases without reduction of working time.

For Postone, the specificity of labour in capitalism is not that it's "harder to replace" or "more fundamental" as an input, but that it is constituted as the medium through which the social totality reproduces itself. Water and electricity are inputs; labour is the form of social mediation itself.

### 2. The Electricity Challenge

Electricity markets represent the strongest empirical challenge to the uniqueness argument. A kilowatt-hour (kWh) of electricity is genuinely homogeneous — a kWh from coal, nuclear, solar, or wind is physically identical at the point of consumption and is traded as such. Electricity markets explicitly price electricity as a homogeneous commodity. The CME Group launched water futures in December 2020 (the Nasdaq Veles California Water Index, NQH2O), and electricity futures markets have existed for decades.

Is this not "real abstraction"? When coal-fired electricity and solar electricity are traded as equivalent kWh on the grid, is not a "practical abstraction" occurring — the ignoring of source, production method, environmental impact?

**The value-form theorist's response** would likely proceed along these lines:

First, Sohn-Rethel's exchange abstraction applies to *all* commodities, including electricity and water. In this sense, yes, electricity undergoes the real abstraction of exchange. But this is not the same as saying electricity *grounds* value. The exchange abstraction explains the social form of value, not its substance or magnitude.

Second, the critical distinction is between a commodity that is *traded* as homogeneous versus a commodity that *produces* more value than it costs to reproduce. Electricity is traded as homogeneous because kWh are physically identical at the point of consumption. But the value-form argument is not that things which are traded as homogeneous thereby create value. The argument is that value's substance is abstract labour, constituted through the specific social relation in which labour-power is sold as a commodity.

Third, and most importantly: electricity cannot produce surplus value in the Marxian sense. An electricity generator cannot purchase electricity for less than its value and then use that electricity to produce more electricity than went in. But capital *can* purchase labour-power (pay workers wages that represent less value than the workers produce). This asymmetry — the capacity of labour-power to produce more value than it costs — is the key structural feature.

From Foster and Burkett's *Value Isn't Everything* (Monthly Review, 2018): "The expenditure of physiological energy as such is not abstract labour and does not create value... not an atom of matter enters into the objectivity of commodities as values." The same logic applies to electricity: the physical energy expended does not, as such, constitute value in the Marxian sense.

But the objection bites harder than this answer admits. The value-form school's own insistence on the *social constitution* of abstract labour (not its physiological substrate) creates a potential problem: if abstract labour is constituted by exchange (by the practical equivalence-setting that occurs in the market), and if electricity is now exchanged as a perfectly fungible commodity in sophisticated futures markets — doesn't that mean electricity is *also* undergoing real abstraction in the relevant sense?

The value-form school's best answer to this must draw on the distinction between:
(a) The *form* of real abstraction (which applies to all commodities in exchange), and
(b) The specific claim that *labour alone* is the substance of value.

The form argument (a) is Sohn-Rethel's exchange abstraction. The substance argument (b) requires a further claim: that abstract labour is the unique source of value because labour-power is uniquely capable of producing surplus. If electricity could produce surplus, you could have an "electricity theory of value" in Marxian terms. But electricity cannot produce surplus value because the ratio of output to input energy is fixed by thermodynamics — you cannot buy electricity "cheap" and get more electricity out than went in, except by exploiting workers who produce electricity. The surplus arises in production from the purchase of labour-power below the value it creates.

This is the non-trivial argument. It is not merely that labour is traded as homogeneous (all three — labour, electricity, water — can be traded as homogeneous). It is that only labour, when sold as a commodity and consumed in production, produces more value than it costs.

### 3. The Water Challenge Specifically

The CME water futures (NQH2O, launched December 2020) reflect water rights prices in California — specifically, water delivered at specified locations. As CME explains, the futures "allow traders to manage water price risk." Water futures are financial instruments that represent claims on water rights, not homogeneous physical water — the contract settles against the Nasdaq Veles California Water Index, which reflects actual water market prices.

This is already more complicated than the electricity case. The NQH2O is an index of water prices across California water markets. Different water sources and delivery locations are not perfectly homogeneous — water at different points in the delivery network has different costs of transport, treatment, and reliability. The index abstracts over these differences to produce a single price.

More importantly: water at its point of delivery is in many ways more heterogeneous than electricity at the grid connection point. A kWh is a kWh by physical definition. Water can vary in quality, mineral content, salinity, etc. Water rights are also not fungible in the same way — California water law distinguishes riparian rights, appropriative rights, groundwater rights, etc.

The "water theory of value" challenge — as articulated by Shalizi (bactra.org, "Reading Capital") — is not primarily about water futures markets. Shalizi's challenge is: if labor theory of value just says "the thing that inputs into everything underlies value," why couldn't you substitute water or energy? The answer has to be that abstract labour is doing more theoretical work than just "universal input."

The value-form school's answer is precisely this: abstract labour is not a "universal input" at all. It is a specific social form — the form taken by human activity when it is organized as commodity production, when the products of privately-performed labour must be socially validated through exchange. Water is not a "form of social activity" that gets constituted as homogeneous through exchange. Water is an object of nature that capital appropriates; it is not the medium through which capitalist social relations reproduce themselves.

From Milios (2020): "The capitalist organization of production... is underpinned by the direct (institutional) independence of each individual producer (capitalist) from all the others. Nevertheless, all these individual productive procedures are linked indirectly between themselves through the mechanism of the market... This procedure imposes an increasing social (capitalistic) uniformity on all individual productive activities precisely through generalized commodity exchange and competition between individual capitalist production processes."

In other words: abstract labour is not "labour with its particular qualities stripped away." It is the form through which the *social division of labour* is organized under capitalism. Water, electricity, raw materials — these are inputs to that process, but they are not themselves the medium of social synthesis. They do not bear the social-form characteristic of being the substance of value.

### 4. Sohn-Rethel on Exchange and Use

Sohn-Rethel's specific argument about the practical nature of the exchange abstraction involves a key claim: during the moment of exchange, commodities must be treated as if their use were suspended. You cannot both exchange a commodity and use it simultaneously. This "formal ban on use" during exchange is where the real abstraction occurs — the commodity's use-value is practically negated in the act of exchange, leaving only its exchange-value as the relevant property.

This applies to all commodities. When electricity is traded as a forward contract, its actual physical delivery is deferred, and during the trading act, the traders deal only with abstract units of kWh-equivalent value. The physical substance is bracketed.

Sohn-Rethel's further, more contentious claim is that this exchange abstraction is the basis for the abstract categories of natural science — quantity, identity, causality. Natural science thinks in abstract, context-free terms because society has already enacted abstract, context-free equivalences in the exchange process. The physicist's unit of energy is an ideal reflection of the commodity market's unit of equivalent value.

This is where the electricity challenge is most interesting: if electricity is now traded as a perfectly abstract unit (kWh), and if Sohn-Rethel's epistemological thesis holds, then we would expect "energy thinking" — treating all phenomena as ultimately reducible to energy — to be a particularly natural form of scientific ideology in a society where energy is itself abstracted in markets. And indeed, there have been physicalist and energetic theories of value (from Podolinsky in the 19th century through the "energy theory of value" literature). The value-form school would regard these as ideological reflections of the real abstraction of energy markets — not as correct theories of value, but as socially produced intellectual forms.

The key point for our purposes: Sohn-Rethel's exchange abstraction is the *form* of real abstraction; it applies to all commodities. It does not by itself establish that labour is the substance of value. That requires the additional argument about surplus value and the unique property of labour-power as a commodity that produces more than it costs.

### 5. Critics of Real Abstraction

**The circularity objection**: The strongest objection to the value-form school's use of real abstraction is that the argument is circular. The claim is: exchange constitutes labour as abstract because in exchange, all commodities are equated as expressions of the same substance (value = abstract labour). But how do we know that abstract labour is what the exchange equates them as, rather than something else (energy, utility, etc.)? The answer tends to be: because value is the social form of abstract labour, and the exchange abstraction is the practical enactment of this. But this presupposes what it tries to show.

From Milios's critique of Sohn-Rethel (2020): he notes that Sohn-Rethel's generalization of real abstraction to "all ages of commodity production from ancient Greece onward" is historically inaccurate — the concept of value requires specifically capitalist production relations (wage labour, labour-power as a commodity). Sohn-Rethel conflates any commodity exchange with the specifically capitalist exchange that generates value. This is a genuine error, and Milios is right that Sohn-Rethel's historical over-extension undermines his framework.

**The "value-form without substance" problem**: The value-form school, especially in its monetary/circulationist version (Heinrich's "monetary theory of value"), faces the challenge that if value is constituted only in exchange, then the exchange of any homogeneous commodity could ground a theory of value. Heinrich argues that value is not determined in production but emerges through social validation in the market. Critics like Lietz and Schwarz (Historical Materialism, 2023) argue this "fails to explain value-formation by circulation and production" and makes the capitalist production process "no longer the unity of the labour process and the valorisation process."

**The unfalsifiability objection**: Sabatino's "Humbug Labor Values" (Capital as Power, 2026) demonstrates that Shaikh's empirical tests of the labour theory of value are methodologically circular — labour values are imputed from prices (the very thing they're supposed to explain), and random data produces equally "high correlations." This undermines the empirical side of the labour theory of value. The value-form school would say: that's fine, we're not making empirical predictions about price-labour correlations; we're making ontological claims about the social form of capitalism. But this concession raises its own questions.

**The "operating on a different level" problem**: The value-form school is explicit that real abstraction is not an empirical claim about price-labour correlations; it is a claim about the social ontology of capitalism. The value-form argument operates at the level of what capitalist social relations *are*, not at the level of what prices *do*. From the value-form perspective, the labour theory of value is not a theory that predicts relative prices; it is an account of the social form of exploitation under capitalism.

This move is philosophically coherent but creates a gap. If the theory doesn't predict price-labour correlations, it becomes unclear how it could be empirically tested or refuted. The value-form school's answer is that critique is not about empirical prediction but about revealing the hidden social relations behind the categories of political economy. Whether this is satisfying depends on what one thinks social theory should do.

**The "N. Pepperell / Rough Theory" objection**: A sophisticated critique comes from the "rough theory" blog (N. Pepperell, 2007), which argues that most interpretations of "real abstraction" set it up either as an illusion (to be unmasked) or as an essence (the real underlying the apparent) — and that both framings are problematic. A better understanding would treat real abstractions as "socially-constituted positivities — actively constructed with their own determinate qualitative characteristics generated (unintentionally) in collective practice — representing neither illusion nor essence, but rather alienated potentials." This critique cuts against simplistic deployment of real abstraction as a trump card.

**Milios's limitation on Sohn-Rethel**: The most focused academic critique is Milios's (2020): "Sohn-Rethel's notion of real abstraction constitutes an important contribution to Marxist theory of value and the value form. However, its generalization to cover 'ages of commodity production from their beginnings in ancient Greece...' deprives it of its hermeneutic accuracy." The concept should be confined to specifically capitalist commodity production — not extended to all markets everywhere.

### 6. Does Real Abstraction Do the Work It Needs to Do?

The question is: does real abstraction, as developed by the value-form school, actually *explain* why labour-value correlations exist (to whatever extent they do), or is it operating at a different level entirely?

The answer is: it operates at a different level. The value-form argument is not that "because labour is really abstracted, prices correlate with labour inputs." The value-form argument is that:

1. Capitalism is a specific historical form of social organization in which the social synthesis is mediated by abstract labour (the exchange of commodities as values).
2. The categories of political economy (price, profit, rent, wages) are the necessary forms of appearance of this specific social relation.
3. Understanding these categories requires grasping the real abstraction that underlies them — the practical equivalence-setting of exchange, which constitutes labour as abstract and social.
4. The "real" in real abstraction means that this abstraction is not merely conceptual — it is enacted in the material practice of exchange, with real consequences.

Whether labour-value correlations exist empirically is, for the value-form school, a separate question from whether the theory of real abstraction is correct as an account of capitalist social relations. The Milios/Lietz-Schwarz debate about whether abstract labour is constituted in production or exchange is a debate about this social ontology, not about empirical price-labour correlations.

The electricity/water challenge is most threatening not at the empirical level (do electricity-value correlations exist?) but at the conceptual level: if the real abstraction argument applies to all homogeneous commodities traded as equivalents, why does it single out labour? The value-form school's answer must be:

(a) Real abstraction in Sohn-Rethel's sense applies to all commodities (the exchange abstraction). This is correct.

(b) But the *substance* of value — what commodities are equated *as* — is abstract labour, because only labour-power, when consumed in production, creates more value than it costs to reproduce. Water and electricity are inputs that are transferred into the value of products; only labour creates *new* value.

(c) The real abstraction argument is therefore not "exchange abstracts labour"; it is "in specifically capitalist exchange, the social validation of labour-expenditures occurs through the market, constituting those expenditures as abstract and homogeneous." The social ontology of capitalism is one in which human productive activity (labour) is the form through which the social totality reproduces itself — not because labour is more "universal" as a physical input, but because labour-power is uniquely capable of generating surplus value.

The weak point: this argument presupposes the labour theory of value (that labour, not energy or other inputs, is the source of surplus) rather than deriving it from the concept of real abstraction. The concept of real abstraction describes the social form of capitalism; the claim that abstract labour is the substance of value requires a separate, and contested, argument.

## Key Findings

1. **Real abstraction in Sohn-Rethel's sense applies to all commodities, not just labour.** The exchange abstraction — the practical bracketing of use-values during exchange — occurs whenever commodities are exchanged as equivalents. Electricity and water, when traded in futures markets, undergo this abstraction in Sohn-Rethel's sense. (Strong evidence: Charnel-House summary of Sohn-Rethel; Milios 2020)

2. **Rubin's argument distinguishes physiological from social labour.** Abstract labour is not "labour minus its particular qualities" (a physiological residue) but the social form taken by labour in commodity production — the form in which private labour becomes social through exchange. This is specifically about *human productive activity*, not physical energy inputs. (Strong evidence: Rubin, *Essays*, Chapter 14; Chapter 8)

3. **The value-form school's argument for labour's uniqueness rests on surplus value.** Labour-power is unique because when sold as a commodity and consumed in production, it generates more value than it costs to reproduce. This is why labour is the substance of value, not because it undergoes a "more real" abstraction than other commodities. Energy and water cannot produce surplus value in this sense. (Moderate evidence: Foster/Burkett; Milios; Rubin)

4. **The electricity challenge is genuine at the formal level.** Electricity markets do abstract over source, production method, and environmental footprint — kWh from coal and solar are indistinguishable in exchange. This is formally similar to what happens to labour in Sohn-Rethel's exchange abstraction. Value-form theorists would say this shows that real abstraction applies to all commodities; but the commodity that grounds value (abstract labour) is specifically that whose use-value produces surplus. (Moderate evidence: commodity.com electricity markets; CME water futures documentation)

5. **CME water futures do not demonstrate "real abstraction" of water in the value-founding sense.** Water futures (NQH2O) are financial instruments pricing water rights in California. They do not make all water interchangeable (water varies in quality, location, legal status). Even if they did, this would only show that water can be traded as a homogeneous commodity — not that water is the substance of value. (Strong evidence: CME Group documentation)

6. **The strongest critique of real abstraction is that the concept is under-theorized in how it specifically singles out labour.** The exchange abstraction applies to all commodities; the claim that it singles out *labour* as the substance of value requires the surplus-value argument as an additional premise. The concept of real abstraction does not, by itself, do the work of grounding the labour theory of value. (Strong evidence: Milios's critique; Lietz-Schwarz; logical analysis)

7. **Value-form theory operates at social-ontological level, not empirical prediction.** The claim is about what capitalist social relations *are* (mediated by abstract labour), not about whether prices correlate with labour inputs. This makes the theory harder to falsify but also more coherent as a theory of social form. Sabatino's "humbug" critique attacks empirical LTV, not value-form theory as social ontology. (Strong evidence: Sabatino; Pepperell's analysis)

8. **Milios's critique of Sohn-Rethel is sound**: extending real abstraction to all historical commodity exchange (ancient Greece onwards) deprives the concept of its historical specificity. Real abstraction in the value-constituting sense requires specifically capitalist production relations — wage labour, labour-power as commodity. Without this, the concept floats free. (Strong evidence: Milios 2020)

---

## Assessment

The value-form school's use of "real abstraction" to answer the water/electricity challenge is partially successful but ultimately requires supplementation by the surplus-value argument to do its full work.

**What real abstraction succeeds at**: The concept correctly identifies that the equivalence-setting of commodity exchange is a practical, real act (not merely a mental one). It correctly identifies that under capitalism, human labour is constituted as abstract, homogeneous, and commensurable through the market process — this constitution is "real" because it occurs in social practice, not just in economic thought. The concept also correctly identifies that pre-capitalist societies, even those with commodity exchange, did not organize social reproduction through the law of value, and so did not have abstract labour in the value-creating sense.

**What real abstraction does not succeed at**: The concept does not, by itself, explain why labour specifically (and not energy, water, or other homogeneous inputs) is singled out as the substance of value. In Sohn-Rethel's framework, the exchange abstraction applies to all commodities; labour is not treated differently at the level of the exchange abstraction itself. The claim that abstract labour is specifically the substance of value requires the additional argument: that only labour-power, as a commodity whose use-value is productive activity, can generate surplus value.

**The water/electricity challenge answered, but not by real abstraction alone**: The answer to Shalizi's challenge (why not a water theory of value?) is not "because water doesn't undergo real abstraction." The correct answer is more structural: a water theory of value would require that water-power be uniquely capable of producing more value than it costs to reproduce — that you could buy water "cheap" in production and get more value out than went in. This is impossible because water's contribution to production is fixed by its physical properties; unlike labour, water cannot be made to work "extra" by extending the working day or intensifying labour. The surplus-value structure of capitalism requires a "commodity" whose use-value consists of productive activity — which is what labour-power is.

**The real abstraction concept is more useful for social ontology than for grounding value theory**: The value-form school is correct that real abstraction is a social-ontological concept — it describes the form of social relations under capitalism, not a physical property of labour. But this means the concept does not do the empirical work of predicting price-labour correlations, and it does not, by itself, entail that labour is the substance of value. For that, you need the full apparatus of labour-power as a commodity, the distinction between labour and labour-power, and the theory of surplus value.

**Where uncertainty remains**: The most unresolved issue is whether the value-form school's refusal to make empirical predictions (treating value theory as social ontology rather than price theory) is a strength or a retreat. The empirical critique (Sabatino's humbug argument, Shalizi's challenge) bites at the level of price-labour correlations. The value-form school's response is that this misunderstands the theory's level of abstraction — but this creates a theory that is philosophically sophisticated and empirically inert. Whether this is acceptable depends on one's view of what a critical theory of capitalism should accomplish.
