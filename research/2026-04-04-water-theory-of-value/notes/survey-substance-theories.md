# Survey: History of Single-Substance Theories of Economic Value

## Summary

The history of economic thought contains a recurring impulse: to ground the
"real" foundation of value in one privileged physical or social substance that
underlies the shifting surface of prices. This impulse runs from the
Physiocrats (land/nature, 1750s-70s) through the classical labour theorists
(Smith, Ricardo, Marx, 1770s-1880s), through the thermodynamic turn
(Podolinsky, Soddy, Odum, 1880s-1990s), and into contemporary ecological
and entropy economics. Each school identifies a different candidate substance
and faces a structurally identical objection: if multiple inputs contribute to
production, why is your chosen one the "source" rather than just another
input? The answers reveal the deep philosophical commitments underneath each
theory. The thermodynamic lineage is currently the most active area of new
research.

## Key Sources

### The History of Economic Thought Website: The Physiocrats
- **Link**: https://www.hetwebsite.net/het/schools/physioc.htm
- **Key content**: Comprehensive overview of the Physiocratic school. The
  cornerstone was Quesnay's axiom that *only agriculture yielded a surplus*
  — the *produit net* (net product). Manufacturing and commerce, in the
  Physiocratic view, consumed as much value in inputs as they created in
  output, and therefore produced no net addition to national wealth. Their
  logic: land (through agriculture) is the only sector that returns more
  than was put in, because Nature itself contributes to the transformation
  (seeds → harvest). Artisans merely reshape matter; only farmers, aided by
  the sun and soil, actually *create* new substance. The school classified
  society into (1) the "productive" class (farmers), (2) the "sterile" class
  (artisans, merchants), and (3) "proprietors" who extracted the net product
  as rent. Their political conclusion: the single tax (*impôt unique*) should
  fall on land rent, the sole source of genuine surplus. Adam Smith killed
  them with faint praise, writing that the Physiocratic system "never has done,
  and probably never will do any harm in any part of the world." The Turgotian
  wing modified the doctrine by admitting industry could also produce a net
  product — this softening was the road to the classical labour theory.
- **Assessment**: The HET website is a well-regarded secondary source for
  history of economic thought, maintained by José Luis Pereira Fonseca. It is
  not peer-reviewed but is widely cited as a reference by academic economists.
  The treatment of Physiocracy is thorough. Perspective: neutral-historical.

### Quesnay's *Tableau Économique* and Physiocratic Doctrine (primary)
- **Link**: Referenced in HET website above; original 1759 text by François
  Quesnay
- **Key content**: The *Tableau* models the circular flow of income between
  the three classes. Crucially, Quesnay's *produit net* is not just an
  accounting surplus — it is a claim about *ontology*: only nature creates
  new matter from old. The argument is implicitly physiological (Quesnay was
  a court physician and drew the analogy with blood circulation and bodily
  homeostasis). The "sterile" designation for industry does not mean industry
  is useless — it means industry transforms without net creation. The
  philosophical move is: value = net creation of physical substance, and only
  nature (mediated by agriculture) does this. The Physiocrats' answer to the
  obvious objection that artisans add value: artisans merely *conserve* value
  in a different form; they don't multiply it. The HET notes the Physiocrats
  owed their "land theory of value" to Richard Cantillon before them.
- **Assessment**: Primary source (18th century). Must be read with awareness
  that "surplus" here is a physical-metabolic concept before it became a
  purely monetary/distributional one.

### Turgot's *Reflections on the Formation and Distribution of Wealth* (1766)
- **Link**: https://www.econlib.org/library/Essays/trgRfl.html
- **Key content**: Turgot bridges Physiocracy and classical political economy.
  He modified the Physiocratic position by recognizing that capital (advances
  made by entrepreneurs) also generates returns. Where Quesnay's model was
  purely agricultural, Turgot's is proto-industrial. The critical move: value
  of any output is grounded in the *labour* of those who produced it, but land
  retains special status as the original source of raw materials that labour
  transforms. This transitional position prepared the ground for Smith's
  fuller labour theory.
- **Assessment**: Classic primary text. Widely cited.

### Steven Horwitz, "Adam Smith on the Labor Theory of Value" — Econlib
- **Link**: https://www.econlib.org/econlog/adam-smith-on-the-labor-theory-of-value/
- **Key content**: Clear secondary exposition of Smith's position in *Wealth
  of Nations* (1776). Smith argued that "Labour... is alone the ultimate and
  real standard by which the value of all commodities can at all times and
  places be estimated and compared." Smith's argument for labour specifically:
  (a) labour is the common element in all production — every commodity
  embodies some labour, even if land and capital also contribute; (b) labour
  has a kind of permanence that gold and silver do not (their value fluctuates
  with discoveries); (c) labour is the measure by which we *command* other
  commodities in exchange. Smith immediately ran into the water-diamond
  paradox: water (high use-value) is cheap; diamonds (low use-value) are
  expensive. His resolution — distinguishing *use value* from *exchange value*
  — was unsatisfying and left his theory incomplete. The article situates
  Smith against the marginalist revolution (Jevons, Menger, Walras, 1870s)
  which reversed the causal arrow: value flows *from* consumer subjective
  assessment of outputs *back to* inputs, not the other way.
- **Assessment**: Secondary source, economics-of-ideas perspective, from a
  libertarian/marginalist standpoint (Econlib/Liberty Fund). Useful for clear
  exposition but explicitly critical of LTV. Needs to be balanced with
  defenders.

### StackExchange Economics: The Invariable Measure of Value Problem
- **Link**: https://economics.stackexchange.com/questions/37921/what-was-the-invariable-measure-of-value-problem-and-how-did-sraffa-solve-it
- **Key content**: Clear exposition of Ricardo's core problem. Ricardo wanted
  labour to be both the *source* of value and the *measure* of value. But he
  recognised that the same labour-input good would have different prices
  depending on how capital-intensive its production was and what the
  prevailing rate of profit was. The "invariable measure of value" problem:
  find a commodity whose price is invariant to changes in *both* the
  distribution of income (wages vs. profits) *and* production technique,
  so that changes in relative prices can be attributed to real changes in
  values rather than to distributional shifts. Ricardo tried corn, then gold,
  but neither worked. He admitted in his third edition of *Principles* (1821)
  that he had never solved it. The problem is that *labour time* as a measure
  of value is internally consistent only if capital does not generate
  independent returns — i.e. if you assume away the very thing you want to
  explain (profit). Piero Sraffa (1960) constructed a partial solution using
  a "standard commodity" — a composite good whose price is invariant to
  distributional changes — but this is purely mathematical, not a physical
  substance argument.
- **Assessment**: StackExchange is not peer-reviewed but this answer cites
  Kurose & Yoshihara (2014) which is a genuine academic paper. Useful for
  the intellectual-history summary.

### Tsoulfidis, "Ricardo's Labor Theory of Value Is Alive and Well in Contemporary Capitalism"
- **Link**: *World Review of Political Economy*, Vol. 12 No. 4 (ScienceOpen
  PDF)
- **Key content**: Defends Ricardo's LTV empirically using input-output data
  from the US and China. Argues that price deviations from labour values are
  small and stable, suggesting labour values do "gravitate" towards prices
  as Ricardo claimed. The theoretical argument: labour-value deviations from
  prices arise from differences in capital intensity (the "organic composition
  of capital" in Marx's terminology) and turnover times, but these are second-
  order corrections to a first-order labour-value determination.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed academic paper in a heterodox political
  economy journal. Defends the LTV from an empirical standpoint. Represents
  the "surplus approach" / Sraffian-Marxist school.

### Wikipedia: Serhiy Podolynsky
- **Link**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serhiy_Podolynsky
- **Key content**: Podolynsky (1850-1891) was a Ukrainian physician and
  socialist who published "Socialism and the Unity of Physical Forces" (La
  Revue Socialiste, 1880) and the German version in *Die Neue Zeit*
  (1883). He set out to reconcile socialist thought with the second law of
  thermodynamics, synthesising Marx, Darwin, and Carnot. His core argument:
  human labour, directed by intelligence, can *accumulate* solar energy in
  useful forms (crops, animals, infrastructure) rather than merely degrading
  it. Therefore labour creates *real* value in a thermodynamic sense — value
  measured as net surplus of embodied solar energy. He calculated that one
  "unit" of human work (in food calories) could, when applied to agriculture,
  produce a net *gain* in solar energy storage many times over. This was his
  attempt to give the labour theory of value a biophysical foundation.

  Marx's response: reportedly sympathetic but ultimately sceptical. According
  to Foster and Burkett (see below), Marx found Podolinsky's framework
  physically interesting but thought he had confused the *physical* and
  *social* dimensions of value. Value for Marx was not a physical quantity
  but a social relation — the form taken by abstract labour in capitalist
  commodity exchange. Thermodynamic surplus ≠ surplus value.

  Engels's response: more dismissive. He called Podolinsky a "deviationist"
  and said he had made a "category error" by trying to reduce social relations
  to natural-scientific facts. Engels was particularly skeptical that energy
  accounting could capture the social determination of what counts as "labour"
  under capitalism.

  Podolynsky died young (aged 40) after a mental breakdown, and his work was
  largely forgotten until ecological economists rediscovered it in the 1970s.
- **Assessment**: Wikipedia article for biographical facts; quality varies.
  The core scholarly source on Marx/Engels and Podolinsky is Burkett & Foster
  (2004, 2006) — see below.

### Burkett & Foster, "Metabolism, Energy, and Entropy in Marx's Critique of Political Economy: Beyond the Podolinsky Myth" (2006)
- **Link**: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11186-006-6781-2
  (*Theory and Society*, Vol. 35)
- **Key content**: The key revisionist article on this debate. The "Podolinsky
  myth" refers to a narrative common in ecological Marxism (especially from
  Martinez-Alier's earlier work) that Marx and Engels rejected Podolinsky's
  ecological contribution out of scientific incompetence or ideological
  blindness, and that this created a fatal anti-ecological flaw in Marxism.
  Burkett and Foster contest this. Their argument: Marx and Engels did not
  reject an energetics-based supplement to value theory because they were
  ignorant of thermodynamics; rather, they understood and used an
  "open-system metabolic-energetic model" that was consistent with ecological
  economics principles. They rejected Podolinsky's *specific conflation* of
  thermodynamic energy surplus with social surplus value — a category error.
  Marx's critique of political economy was about the *social form* taken by
  labour under capitalism, not the physical quantity of energy transformed.
  The paper argues this makes Marx *more* ecologically sophisticated than
  Podolinsky, not less.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, Theory and Society (Springer). Authors are
  respected ecological Marxist scholars. Paul Burkett (Indiana State) and
  John Bellamy Foster (Oregon, Monthly Review). This is the definitive
  secondary source on the Podolinsky-Marx-Engels episode. Represents the
  "metabolic rift" / ecological Marxism perspective.

### Blair Fix, "Frederick Soddy's Debt Dynamics" — Capital As Power blog
- **Link**: https://capitalaspower.com/2021/09/frederick-soddys-debt-dynamics/
- **Key content**: Clear secondary exposition of Soddy's framework. Soddy
  (1877-1956) won the Nobel Prize for Chemistry (1921) for work on radioactive
  decay, then turned to economics. His core distinction: *real wealth* (physical
  goods and resources, governed by thermodynamics) vs. *virtual wealth* (money
  and debt, governed by mathematics, able to grow exponentially). The mismatch
  between exponentially growing debt and thermodynamically constrained real
  wealth is, for Soddy, the root of most economic crises. His "energy as basis
  of wealth" argument: all economic activity is ultimately the transformation of
  energy; exhaust your energy base and the economy collapses regardless of
  monetary policy. The article uses Soddy's own cow-loan example to illustrate
  the exponential debt dynamic. Soddy's policy proposals (abandon gold standard,
  floating exchange rates, counter-cyclical fiscal policy, 100% reserve banking)
  were dismissed as crank economics in his lifetime but are now largely
  mainstream practice — except the 100% reserve banking critique, which remains
  heterodox.
- **Assessment**: A blog post, not peer-reviewed, but by an ecological
  economist (Blair Fix) and drawing on solid primary sources (Soddy's own
  books). The Soddy scholarship it cites — especially Eric Zencey's NYT op-ed
  and Soddy's own four books (1921-1934) — is well-established.

### Economic Reform Australia, "Frederick Soddy's Contribution to Economic Thought"
- **Link**: https://era.org.au/frederick-soddys-contribution-to-economic-thought/
- **Key content**: Extracts from Soddy's *Wealth, Virtual Wealth and Debt*
  (1926). Soddy's argument in his own words: real wealth is positive, money-
  debt is negative (a "claim" on wealth, not wealth itself). Economics has
  confused the two. His target: the fractional reserve banking system, which
  creates "virtual wealth" (debt money) far in excess of the real physical
  substrate it supposedly represents. The energy foundation: "Society is now
  dominated and administered not by and for those who create real wealth and
  health, but by and for those who create want and unreal debt." Soddy proposed
  that all money should be issued by the state directly, with full asset backing
  — eliminating the capacity of commercial banks to create money through
  lending.
- **Assessment**: Advocacy website (Economic Reform Australia), so perspective
  is reformist/heterodox. The Soddy extracts are authentic. Should be
  supplemented with Merricks (1996) *The World Made New* for scholarly biography.

### T.J. Trenn, "The Central Role of Energy in Soddy's Holistic and Critical Approach" (1979)
- **Link**: *British Journal for the History of Science* (Semantic Scholar
  record: paperId c2665ce3cbf2f17d9c4fd697a508c1a1078936ad)
- **Key content**: Scholarly analysis placing Soddy's economics in the context
  of his scientific career. Trenn argues Soddy's energy economics was not a
  late-career eccentricity but flowed logically from his work on radioactivity
  — he had directly observed how the transformation of matter releases
  enormous energy, and he generalised this insight to the economy.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, British Journal for the History of Science.
  12 citations (Semantic Scholar). Represents the history-of-science approach
  to Soddy.

### Rex Weyler, "Real Wealth: Howard T. Odum's Energy Economics"
- **Link**: https://www.rexweyler.ca/ecologue/2017/10/1/real-wealth-howard-t-odums-energy-economics
- **Key content**: Secondary but well-informed account of Howard T. Odum's
  energy economics. Odum (1924-2002) was an ecologist at the University of
  Florida who developed the concept of *emergy* (embodied energy) as a
  universal accounting unit. The key idea: not all energy is equal — energy
  quality increases as energy is transformed through ecological hierarchies.
  One joule of sunlight is worth far less than one joule of coal, which is
  worth far less than one joule of nerve impulse in a complex organism. Odum
  coined "emergy" to mean the total solar energy required (directly and
  indirectly) to produce any good or service. Economic value, he argued,
  should be measured in solar emjoules (sej), not dollars.

  Odum's critique of standard economics: "Money is only paid to people and
  never to the environment for its work... Therefore, money and market values
  cannot be used to evaluate the real wealth from the environment." The
  famous inversion: "Market values are *inverse* to real wealth... When the
  resources are scarce, obtaining costs are higher... and the market puts a
  high value on the product." This inversion of the usual price-value
  relationship is one of Odum's most radical claims — scarcity makes market
  prices *rise*, but from an emergy standpoint it means we are *depleting*
  real wealth.

  His *Maximum Power Principle*: self-organizing systems evolve to maximize
  energy throughput, not efficiency. This gives a thermodynamic basis for
  understanding economic growth, collapse, and pulsation.
- **Assessment**: Secondary, a Greenpeace co-founder's blog. Not peer-
  reviewed, but accurately represents Odum's major works (*Environment, Power
  and Society*, 1971; *Environmental Accounting: Emergy and Environmental
  Decision Making*, 1996). Odum's own work is the authoritative source.

### Costanza & Neill, "Energy Intensities, Interdependence, and Value in Ecological Systems" (1984)
- **Link**: https://www.robertcostanza.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/1984_J_CostanzaNeill.pdf
  (*Journal of Theoretical Biology*, 106, 41-57)
- **Key content**: An important early paper establishing the formal machinery
  for "embodied energy" as a value measure. Costanza and Neill use linear
  programming (a generalisation of Leontief input-output analysis) to calculate
  "energy intensities" — the total direct and indirect solar energy required to
  produce each component of an ecosystem. Applied to the Silver Springs,
  Florida, ecosystem data. The paper explicitly invokes Georgescu-Roegen
  (1971) on the second law and argues that *second-law* energy accounting
  (which accounts for entropy degradation, not just first-law conservation)
  is the appropriate framework. They note: "It is low-entropy matter and energy
  that are 'useful' and therefore necessary for economic value." The paper
  showed that embodied energy intensities correlate with economic values,
  offering empirical support for an energy theory of value.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, *Journal of Theoretical Biology*. Costanza is
  a major figure in ecological economics (University of Vermont / Australian
  National University). This paper is a primary source for the ecological
  economics energy-value tradition, cited in that community's canonical texts.

### Burkett & Foster, "Ecological Economics and Classical Marxism: The 'Podolinsky Business' Reconsidered" (2004)
- **Link**: DOI: 10.1177/1086026603262091 (*Organization & Environment*, 17(1),
  32-60)
- **Key content**: More accessible companion to the 2006 paper above. Traces
  the "Podolinsky business" — Engels's dismissal of Podolinsky — through the
  primary texts. Engels's two letters (to Marx, 1882) describe Podolinsky's
  work as confusing physical and social science. The paper also traces how
  Juan Martinez-Alier (*Ecological Economics: Energy, Environment and Society*,
  1987) revived Podolinsky as a founding father of biophysical economics,
  creating the myth that Marx and Engels had missed an ecological opportunity.
  Burkett and Foster contest this but also affirm that the Podolinsky episode
  *does* represent a genuine fork in the road: one path leads to an energy-
  theory of value (Soddy, Odum, Costanza), the other stays with the social-
  relational theory of value (Marx). These are not reconcilable; they answer
  different questions.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, Organisation & Environment. Important for the
  intellectual genealogy connecting Podolinsky to 20th-century ecological
  economics.

### Chen & Galbraith, "An Entropy Theory of Value: With Reflections on the Arrow-Debreu Model" (2022)
- **Link**: https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4137452
  (also published: *Review of Evolutionary Political Economy*, 2023)
- **Key content**: A recent attempt to formalise an "entropy theory of value"
  as an alternative to marginalist utility theory and labour theory alike.
  The argument: value arises from the effort required to maintain
  *thermodynamic disequilibrium* — the degree to which a good or system
  represents concentrated, organised, low-entropy structure. Galbraith (LBJ
  School, Texas) and Chen (UNBC) present a mathematical framework and argue
  it describes monopoly and oligopoly better than Arrow-Debreu equilibrium
  theory. The key physical claim: producing and maintaining organised
  structures requires energy expenditure against the tendency toward entropy,
  and this is the real basis of economic value.
- **Assessment**: Working paper (SSRN) turned peer-reviewed article
  (*Review of Evolutionary Political Economy*). James K. Galbraith is a
  well-known heterodox economist. Jing Chen is less prominent. The paper
  has modest citations (3 on SSRN). Represents a contemporary attempt to
  formalise the thermodynamic value tradition.

### Miriam Silverman, "Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen and Ecological Economics" (2022)
- **Link**: https://pdxscholar.library.pdx.edu/econ_workingpapers/54/
  (*Portland State University Economics Working Papers*, No. 54)
- **Key content**: Good secondary overview of Georgescu-Roegen's contribution.
  His 1971 *The Entropy Law and the Economic Process* is the foundational text
  for entropy-based economic thinking. Key claim: the economic process is
  fundamentally a *dissipative* process — it takes low-entropy inputs (fossil
  fuels, minerals) and converts them to high-entropy outputs (heat, waste).
  Standard economics ignores this irreversibility, treating resources as
  perfectly substitutable and the economy as a closed circular flow. Georgescu-
  Roegen introduced the *fourth law of thermodynamics* (matter entropy, not
  just energy entropy — controversial and disputed by many physicists), arguing
  that even recycling cannot recover all dispersed matter. His policy
  conclusion: degrowth is thermodynamically necessary.
- **Assessment**: Portland State working paper (not peer-reviewed). But
  accurately represents Georgescu-Roegen's actual positions. Georgescu-Roegen's
  original 1971 book (*Harvard University Press*) is the definitive primary
  source.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The history of substance theories of value is extremely well documented in the
history of economic thought literature. The Physiocrats are a standard chapter
in every HET textbook. Smith, Ricardo, and Marx are among the most studied
economists in history. The thermodynamic turn (Podolinsky to Odum) is less
thoroughly documented in mainstream economics but has its own rich secondary
literature in ecological economics and environmental sociology.

There is good coverage of:
- The internal logical problems of each theory (especially the Ricardo measure
  problem)
- The Physiocratic-to-classical transition via Turgot
- The Marx/Engels response to Podolinsky (especially Burkett & Foster)
- Odum's emergy framework in ecological economics circles
- Soddy's general arguments and policy proposals

There is weaker coverage of:
- Quantitative empirical tests of energy-value correlations beyond Costanza
  (1980) and the Tsoulfidis-style labour-value empirics
- The detailed reception history of Soddy in economics (he was dismissed, then
  partially vindicated — the exact pathway matters)
- The connection between Odum's emergy accounting and its policy use (it
  remains mostly academic, not adopted by any government)
- More recent information-theoretic or complexity-theoretic approaches

### Agreements and Conflicts

**Agreements across traditions:**
- All substance theories agree that the price system does not self-evidently
  reveal the "real" foundations of value
- All agree that physical constraints matter and that standard economics
  underweights them
- There is convergence between the energy theories and aspects of ecological
  economics on the irreversibility of the economic process

**Key conflicts:**
1. *Land vs. labour*: The Physiocrats vs. classical political economy. The
   classical economists' answer: labour is present in *all* production, even
   agricultural, so it is the more general substrate. Agriculture is not
   special; it is just one sector where labour operates on natural materials.

2. *Labour (social) vs. energy (physical)*: Marx/Engels vs. Podolinsky/Soddy/
   Odum. The Marxian answer: value is a *social* category specific to
   capitalist commodity production; it cannot be reduced to physical energy
   flows. Energy is a physical fact; value is a social relation. Conflating
   them is a category error.

3. *Energy (first law) vs. emergy / exergy (second law)*: Within the
   thermodynamic tradition, there is genuine technical debate about which
   energy concept is the right basis for value — raw calories (first law),
   available work / exergy (second law), or Odum's emergy (which adds
   an energy hierarchy on top of exergy). Costanza & Neill (1984) argue
   explicitly for second-law accounting over first-law.

4. *Substance theory vs. marginalism*: All the above are opposed by the
   dominant neoclassical/marginalist tradition (post-1870s), which rejects any
   substance theory. The marginalist answer: value is not *in* things; it is a
   subjective assessment by agents at the margin. No physical property of goods
   determines value; only the beliefs and preferences of choosers do.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **The Cantillon-Physiocrat connection**: The HET source mentions that
   Quesnay inherited his "land theory of value" from Richard Cantillon.
   Cantillon's role as originator deserves more attention.

2. **Turgot as the hinge**: The transition from Physiocracy to classical
   labour theory via Turgot is underappreciated. The HET source says "The
   modified Turgotian system, when channeled into the hands of Adam Smith,
   would yield up the 'labour theory of value' of the Classical School" — but
   the exact mechanism needs more examination.

3. **The Podolinsky reception in detail**: Wikipedia says Marx was "reportedly
   sympathetic" while Engels was dismissive. The Burkett-Foster papers argue
   this is more complex. The actual texts of Marx's letters and notebooks on
   Podolinsky are primary sources that deserve direct examination.

4. **Emergy accounting as applied policy**: Odum's framework has theoretical
   advocates but almost no uptake in actual policy. Why? The gap between the
   theory and its reception is under-documented.

5. **Contemporary information-theoretic value theories**: Chen & Galbraith
   (2022-23) are attempting to formalise this but citation counts are very
   low. This may represent an emerging (or marginal) strand. The connection
   to complexity economics (W. Brian Arthur, Santa Fe Institute) and to
   "assembly theory" in chemistry deserves investigation.

6. **Alf Hornborg's critique of energy theories of value**: Hornborg (Lund
   University) has argued that energy-theory-of-value approaches themselves
   commit a variant of the naturalistic fallacy — they assume that because
   energy underlies everything physically, it underlies value socially. His
   "fictive energy" concept (money as "fictive energy") tries to navigate
   between the social-relational and biophysical positions. This is a
   significant critical perspective that needs more attention.

### Surprises

1. **Soddy's rehabilitation**: Soddy's policy proposals (floating exchange
   rates, counter-cyclical fiscal policy, reserve requirements) are now
   mainstream, yet he is still remembered as a "crank." This disjunction is
   striking. His energy economics are still heterodox; his monetary proposals
   are now orthodoxy. The reason for this split reception deserves attention.

2. **The Turgotian pivot**: The clearest single statement of how substance
   theories evolve is in the HET description of the Turgotian wing modifying
   Physiocracy to admit industrial net product — which is then generalised to
   all labour by Smith. This suggests substance theories are not just replaced
   but *transformed* by incremental generalisation.

3. **Costanza & Neill (1984) found correlation**: That a 1984 paper was able
   to demonstrate statistical correlation between embodied energy intensities
   and economic values in an actual ecosystem is a stronger empirical result
   than is widely appreciated. The methodology is contestable but the
   correlation was real. This is rarely foregrounded in critiques of energy
   theories of value.

4. **Podolinsky's Ukrainian nationalism**: He is a significant figure in
   Ukrainian intellectual history (his *Steam Engine* pamphlet influenced the
   Ukrainian national movement), not just a footnote in the history of Marxism.
   This context — a colonised people trying to assert their productivity through
   solar energy accounting — adds a political dimension to his energy theory.
