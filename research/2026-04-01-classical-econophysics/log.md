### Key findings across surveys

1. **The "classical" double meaning is real and productive**: classical political economy (Smith/Ricardo/Marx) + classical statistical mechanics (Boltzmann/Maxwell). The book occupies this intersection deliberately.

2. **Three distinct research programmes unified in one book**:
   - Cockshott-Cottrell: empirical labour-value correlations (r² = 90-98% across countries)
   - Yakovenko: Boltzmann-Gibbs income distributions (exponential bulk + Pareto tail)
   - Wright: agent-based unification of six empirical distributions from one model

3. **The Wright model is the most ambitious contribution**: a single agent-based model reproducing power-law firm size, Laplace growth rates, lognormal-Pareto income, gamma profit rates, exponential recession duration, and Goodwin business cycles — all from the assumption that the law of value operates.

4. **Reception is thin but serious**: only one formal book review (Wells 2010, sympathetic). Shalizi's blog critique of the Farjoun-Machover tradition is the most substantive critical piece. The labour-value correlations are contested (Shaikh/Mohun size-effect critique). Mainstream economics is silent.

5. **Unresolved tension**: money conservation (required by Yakovenko's model) vs Chartalist money theory (used in the book's credit chapters). Credit creation breaks conservation.

6. **The information-theory chapters may be the most original material**: connecting Shannon entropy, Kolmogorov complexity, and thermodynamic entropy to value theory. Under-discussed in reviews.
