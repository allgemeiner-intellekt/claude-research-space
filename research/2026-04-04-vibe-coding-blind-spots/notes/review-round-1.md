# Review -- Round 1

## Overall Assessment

The surveys have produced a rich, well-sourced landscape of failure modes, security evidence, and conceptual frameworks. There is clearly enough material to write a strong article. However, the material is currently organized around *what goes wrong* rather than *how to think differently*. The article's promise is teaching -- giving the reader mental models and concrete heuristics they can actually use. To deliver on that, we need to close two specific gaps: (1) concrete, walkthrough-level examples that show a vibe coder what a dangerous pattern looks like in their own context (small personal tools, not enterprise SaaS), and (2) a tighter synthesis of which primitives cluster together into a teachable framework versus which are isolated security tips.

The strongest thematic backbone is already visible: **the distinction between code that computes and code that acts on the world** (state, side effects, idempotency, destructive operations). This is the thread that connects the double-email Google Apps Script, the S3 deletion, the double-charge checkout, and the silent database write failure. It is also the thread that is most *teachable* to a non-technical person, because it maps onto intuitions they already have (sending a letter vs. reading a letter; you can re-read safely but you cannot un-send).

A secondary cluster -- **the invisible defaults problem** (permissive CORS, public Firebase rules, hardcoded secrets, missing auth) -- is well-documented but risks reading as a checklist of security gotchas rather than a mental model. It becomes teachable only if framed as a single concept: "AI resolves ambiguity by choosing the most permissive option, because permissive options produce fewer errors during development."

## Per-Question Evaluation

### Q1: What do experienced developers report as the most common failure modes?
- **Coverage**: Excellent. The survey-failure-modes note is the strongest of the four -- well-sourced across academic (Tambon et al., Cotroneo et al.), empirical (Columbia DAPLab, Tenzai, GitClear), and incident-level (Tea app, Moltbook, Base44, SaaStr/Replit) evidence. The consolidated taxonomy (Categories A-E) is analytically sharp.
- **Strengths**: The finding that context-dependent vulnerabilities (auth, business logic) are the real danger -- not the generic ones (SQLi, XSS) that models have learned to avoid -- is counterintuitive and well-supported. The "silent failures over crashes" framing is the single most important insight for the article.
- **Gaps**: The taxonomy is organized by failure type, not by the underlying concept a vibe coder would need to learn. For the article, we need to reverse-engineer: which 2-3 concepts, if understood, would have prevented the largest cluster of failures? This is a synthesis task for the orchestrator, not a gap requiring more research.

### Q2: What concepts have the highest danger-to-invisibility ratio?
- **Coverage**: Very strong. Eight specific concepts ranked and explained with evidence. The Veracode "larger models are no more secure" finding is a genuinely important anchoring point.
- **Strengths**: The authorization vs. authentication distinction, the secrets management problem, and the silent failure / error handling philosophy are all well-developed with concrete examples. The Carnegie Mellon stat (61% functionally correct, 10.5% secure) is devastating and memorable.
- **Gaps**: Two things are thin. First, the concepts are listed in parallel without a hierarchy -- the article needs to argue that some of these are *more fundamental* than others (e.g., understanding side effects is more fundamental than understanding CORS configuration, because the former is a thinking tool and the latter is a specific fact). Second, the "small personal tools" calibration is weak -- most examples are from startups or SaaS products. A personal budgeting script that writes to a JSON file has a very different risk profile from an app with 10K users, and the article needs to be honest about which risks apply at which scale.

### Q3: How do state/side effects/idempotency manifest in small personal tools?
- **Coverage**: Strong and specific. The survey found excellent concrete examples at the right scale: Google Apps Script double-triggers, the JSON power-outage corruption, cron jobs running twice, the "blind append" anti-pattern. The light switch vs. counter analogy for idempotency is perfect for a teaching article.
- **Strengths**: The "minimum viable understanding" bullet list at the end of this survey is essentially a draft of the article's core teaching. The three questions heuristic ("what happens if this runs twice? what happens if it crashes halfway? is this reading or writing?") is exactly the kind of tool a non-technical person can actually use. The write-then-rename and marker-file patterns are concrete and learnable.
- **Gaps**: The survey covers the problem well but doesn't have a worked example that walks through a realistic vibe-coded script showing where the danger is. The article would benefit enormously from a single, detailed "before and after" scenario -- a script a vibe coder might actually build, with the specific line where things go wrong and the specific question they should have asked. This is the most important gap for teachability.

### Q4: What mental models separate dangerous from safe vibe coding?
- **Coverage**: Broad and philosophically rich, but somewhat diffuse. The survey covers CS education research (notional machines, computational thinking), practitioner wisdom (Infinite Intern, Zero Trust posture), and decision theory (Type 1 vs. Type 2 decisions). The five-model synthesis at the end (threat model, notional machine, adversarial test reflex, reversibility scanner, cost/resource model) is a good draft framework.
- **Strengths**: The "Infinite Intern" mental model is vivid and immediately usable. The insight that the most important mental model is *epistemic* (assume the AI is wrong in ways you cannot see) rather than technical is powerful and should anchor the article. The peer-reviewed Namoun et al. finding that non-programmers think in terms of outcomes and visual results -- not data flow or execution order -- is the key empirical grounding for why vibe coding fails.
- **Gaps**: The five-model framework is too many models for a teaching article aimed at non-technical readers. The article needs to collapse these into 2-3 that are genuinely distinct and memorable. I suspect the right move is: (1) the "reading vs. writing" distinction (side effects), (2) the "what could go wrong" reflex (adversarial/unhappy path thinking), and (3) the "AI chose the easiest path, not the safest" awareness (permissive defaults). Everything else -- idempotency, reversibility, cost models -- can be taught as consequences of these three. But this synthesis is the orchestrator's job.

## Deep-Dive Assignments

### Assignment: Concrete walkthrough scenarios for the teaching article

- **Question to answer**: What does a realistic "vibe-coded personal tool gone wrong" scenario look like, step by step? We need 2-3 detailed scenarios that a non-technical reader would recognize as something they might build, showing exactly where the blind spot is, what goes wrong, and what question they should have asked. These should be at the scale of "personal tool" -- not SaaS, not enterprise.
- **Why it matters**: The article's entire value proposition is teachability. We have plenty of statistics and taxonomies. What we lack is the narrative scaffolding that makes a non-technical reader say "oh, that is exactly what I built last week." The scenarios need to be specific enough to be visceral (not "a script that writes to a file" but "a script that renames your photos by date, and what happens when you run it on a folder that is already partially renamed").
- **Where to start**: The survey-state-small-tools note has the best raw material -- the Google Apps Script double-trigger, the JSON corruption on power loss, the cron job overlap, the blind append. Build scenarios around these. Also: the survey-danger-invisibility note's Supabase service key in frontend bundle example is perfect for a "you built a small app and shared it with friends" scenario. The S3 deletion story from Reddit could be adapted to a smaller-scale "AI helper deletes your files" scenario.

### Assignment: The "AI chooses permissive defaults" pattern -- mechanism and examples

- **Question to answer**: Why does AI-generated code systematically choose the most permissive, least secure configuration when resolving ambiguity? What is the precise mechanism (training data distribution? RLHF optimization target? error-minimization during generation?), and what are the 3-5 most common specific instances of this pattern that a vibe coder building small tools would encounter?
- **Why it matters**: This could be the single most powerful teaching concept in the article -- a single explanation that accounts for hardcoded secrets, open CORS, permissive Firebase rules, disabled auth checks, and suppressed error messages. If the reader understands this one pattern ("the AI always takes the path of least resistance, and least resistance means least security"), they have a framework for catching a whole class of problems. But we need the mechanism explained precisely enough to be credible, not just asserted.
- **Where to start**: The DataHogo piece (structural roots of AI security failures) and the Reya Vir/Columbia piece ("to an AI, a security wall is just a bug preventing the code from running") both describe this mechanism. The Veracode finding that model size does not improve security suggests this is structural, not a capability gap. Trace this thread: is it training data bias (insecure code is overrepresented in open source)? Is it RLHF rewarding "code that works" over "code that is safe"? Is it the optimization target of next-token prediction (the most likely next token after an error is a fix, not a security check)?

### Assignment: What experienced developers do automatically that vibe coders cannot

- **Question to answer**: When an experienced developer reviews AI-generated code, what specific checks do they perform -- not in theory, but in practice? What is the actual cognitive process? We need 3-5 specific "things an expert does in 10 seconds that a non-expert cannot do at all," described concretely enough that a non-technical reader understands what they are missing.
- **Why it matters**: The article needs to make the invisible visible. The Addy Osmani "70% problem" and the meta-analysis finding that AI benefits seniors more than juniors both point to expert knowledge as the key differentiator -- but neither source describes what that expert knowledge actually looks like in action. If the article can show, concretely, "here is what an experienced developer sees when they look at this code that you cannot see," it creates the motivation for the reader to learn the concepts.
- **Where to start**: The notthecode.com "AI Code Review: Senior Dev's Guide" is the closest source. The Columbia DAPLab failure patterns could be reverse-engineered: for each failure pattern, what would an expert have caught and how? The Gross & Kelleher (2010) study on how non-programmers read code provides the empirical baseline for what non-experts actually do (landmark-mapping at 41% success rate).

## Verdict

**CONTINUE** -- with the three deep-dive assignments above.

The surveys have established a strong empirical and conceptual foundation. What is missing is the bridge from "here is what goes wrong" to "here is how to think differently" -- the teaching layer. The three assignments target this directly: concrete scenarios the reader can identify with, a unifying explanatory pattern (AI defaults to permissive), and a concrete picture of what expert review looks like (so the reader knows what they are missing).

After these deep dives, the orchestrator should have enough to write a genuinely teaching article rather than a risk catalog.
