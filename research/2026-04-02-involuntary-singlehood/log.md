# Research Log — Involuntary Singlehood

## Scoping

**User's prompt**: "Are there more people being single (intend of not) than before?"

The demographic trend itself is well-documented and not worth a report: yes, singlehood rates are rising across virtually all developed nations. The interesting research territory is *why* and *what kind of singlehood* — specifically, the structural forces that produce involuntary singlehood even among people who actively want partnership.

### Narrowed questions

1. **What is the "mating market" failure mode?** Economics uses matching theory (Gale-Shapley, etc.) to model marriage markets. What specific market failures — information asymmetry, search costs, preference misalignment, geographic sorting — does the empirical literature identify as driving rising involuntary singlehood? Is this a demand problem, a supply problem, or a coordination problem?

2. **How has the economic structure of partnership changed?** Marriage historically was an economic production unit (pooled labor, land, childcare). As individual economic self-sufficiency rose (especially for women), the *economic* rationale for partnership weakened. But did this create a new equilibrium where partnership is optional — or a trap where people *want* partnership for non-economic reasons but the institutions that facilitated matching were built on the old economic logic and have now decayed?

3. **What does dating technology actually do to matching outcomes?** The popular narrative is "apps ruined dating." The empirical picture is more complex. What do the data say about how dating apps affect: (a) assortative mating, (b) relationship formation rates, (c) the distribution of attention (Gini coefficient of matches), and (d) the gap between stated and revealed preferences? Is there a measurable technology-driven coordination failure?

4. **Is there a gendered asymmetry in involuntary singlehood, and what explains it?** Survey data suggest men report involuntary singlehood at higher rates than women. Is this a measurement artifact (different definitions of "involuntary"), a real structural asymmetry (e.g., women's rising economic independence + hypergamy producing a mismatch), or something else? What do cross-cultural comparisons reveal?

5. **What are the second-order social consequences of mass involuntary singlehood?** Beyond individual wellbeing: what happens to political systems, social trust, housing markets, and intergenerational wealth transfer when a large and growing fraction of the population is partnerless against their preference? Is there historical precedent (e.g., "bare branches" in China, post-war gender imbalances)?

### Rationale

Questions 1-3 form the analytical core: market structure, institutional decay, and technological disruption. Question 4 addresses the most politically charged dimension — I want to see what the data actually say rather than what the culture war claims. Question 5 zooms out to consequences, connecting individual experience to macro social dynamics.

I'm deliberately avoiding: self-help framing, "what's wrong with young people" moralizing, and the incel subculture specifically (which is well-covered elsewhere and would pull the research toward a narrow population).

## Survey Phase

Dispatched 5 survey agents in parallel, one per question. All returned with substantial findings:

- **Mating market failures**: Primarily a supply-side failure (non-college male economic deterioration) cascading into coordination failure. Dallas Fed falsified search-cost reduction as a driver. Autor/Dorn/Hanson provide causal identification via trade shocks. Goldman/Chambers/Winkelmann show college women's marriage rates are stable; the collapse is among non-college women.
- **Economic structure**: Three-phase institutional arc (production unit → companionate → capstone). Rosenfeld HCMST data shows traditional meeting infrastructure declined since 1940s, not 1990s. Internet displaced friend networks that had already replaced community institutions.
- **Dating technology**: Apps increased sexual activity but not committed relationships (Buyukeren et al. 2026). Mobile-era apps may reduce marriage rates (Ershov et al. 2026). No peer-reviewed Gini for match distributions. Dallas Fed says apps changed nothing about sorting patterns.
- **Gendered asymmetry**: Pew headline gap (29pp) shrinks to ~10pp with better measures. But subjective gap (men experience singlehood as more unwanted) is robustly real cross-nationally. Structural explanation is class-based, not gender-generic.
- **Social consequences**: South Korea is the best-documented live experiment. Strongest evidence on political radicalization, housing market effects. Wealth concentration and social trust threads are thinner.

## Review Round 1

Verdict: CONTINUE. Three deep-dive assignments:
1. Dallas Fed paradox — how can apps change nothing about who matches while reducing whether people match?
2. South Korea — trace the full causal chain from economic precarity to political polarization
3. Class stratification — what exactly happened to non-college men?

## Deep Dive Round 1

All three delivered substantial payoffs:

- **Dallas Fed paradox resolved**: Not a paradox — papers measure different things (sorting structure vs. formation rate). Primary mechanism: evaluation mode shift (Finkel et al. 2012). Profile-browsing induces assessment mindset anti-committal to relationship formation. Information degradation from desktop to mobile era is secondary. Simple choice overload is NOT supported.
- **South Korea**: Marriage rate fell 60% (9.3 to 3.7 per 1000) in 32 years. Gender voting gap 25-37pp among under-30s. Kim (2023) paradox: grievance-based antifeminism suppresses men's own marriage interest. $280B in pronatalist spending failed. Hwang (2023) confirms marriage collapse is primary driver of 0.72 TFR.
- **Class stratification**: Pool of economically viable AND available non-college men fell from 72.9% to 35.3% ("double depletion"). Kearney & Wilson fracking evidence: positive labor shocks raise fertility but NOT marriage — ratchet effect. Kalmijn: egalitarian welfare states widen, not narrow, the educational marriage gradient for men.

## Review Round 2

Verdict: SATISFIED. Three tensions to resolve in writing:
1. Recovery asymmetry (ratchet effect — negative shocks reduce marriage, positive shocks don't restore it)
2. Scandinavian paradox (egalitarian welfare states widen the gradient via criteria shift)
3. Kim paradox (antifeminist grievance politics accelerates the crisis it claims to address)
