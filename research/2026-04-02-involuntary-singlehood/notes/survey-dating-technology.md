## Key Sources

### Rosenfeld, Thomas & Hausen (2019) — "Disintermediating your Friends"
- **Link**: https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1908630116 (PNAS)
- **Key content**: Nationally representative 2017 HCMST survey. Online meeting became the #1 way heterosexual US couples meet, surpassing friends around 2013. By 2017 it dominated. Traditional intermediaries (family, church, neighborhood) have declined since WWII; friends have declined since ~1995. As couples meet online, third-party mediation has declined — people increasingly meeting strangers without social vetting.
- **Assessment**: Highly credible — PNAS, 262 citations, nationally representative survey with longitudinal tracking. The key sociological fact about the structural shift in how people meet.

### Rosenfeld (2025) — "Singleness and the Pandemic Dating Recession"
- **Link**: Journal of Family Issues, 2025
- **Key content**: HCMST data 2017–2022. US adult singleness rose from 18.9% (2017) to 24.3% (2022), implying 10+ million more single adults. The pandemic disrupted *relationship formation*, not just marriage. Young adults most affected. Vaccines did not reverse the dating recession. The rise in singlehood is structurally understudied because official statistics focus on marriage, missing casual relationships.
- **Assessment**: Authoritative (Rosenfeld is the main data keeper on HCMST). Important for establishing that the current moment is genuinely unusual.

### Bruch & Newman (2018) — "Aspirational Pursuit of Mates in Online Dating Markets"
- **Link**: https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aap9815 (Science Advances)
- **Key content**: Platform data from a popular free US dating service (likely OKCupid) across 4 cities. Key findings: (1) A pronounced desirability hierarchy emerges across all cities, correlated with demographics. (2) Both men AND women pursue partners ~25% more desirable than themselves on average. (3) Response probability drops sharply as the desirability gap widens. (4) Strategic messaging can modestly improve outcomes, but the effects are small. The hierarchy is remarkably consistent across cities.
- **Assessment**: Very strong — peer-reviewed Science Advances, actual platform data, clear methodology. 99 citations. This is the best direct empirical evidence of aspirational pursuit and its limits. Does NOT measure Gini directly but documents the hierarchy structure.

### Hitsch, Hortacsu & Ariely (2010) — "Matching and Sorting in Online Dating"
- **Link**: Quantitative Marketing and Economics (AER-related publication)
- **Key content**: Foundational study on online dating preferences using real platform data. Found that observed matching is consistent with a stable matching equilibrium (Gale-Shapley). People have consistent, ranked preferences. Established that online dating preferences show strong racial, income, and height sorting similar to offline. Also found that men's preferences are more appearance-focused while women's preferences incorporate multiple attributes.
- **Assessment**: 690 citations — one of the most-cited studies in this space. Published in 2010, so pre-smartphone-app era (website-based dating). Establishes that revealed preferences do drive sorting rather than purely stated ones.

### Buyukeren, Makarin & Xiong (2026) — "The Impact of Dating Apps on Young Adults: Evidence From Tinder"
- **Link**: SSRN/AEA Applied Economics (forthcoming) — DOI: 10.2139/ssrn.4240140
- **Key content**: Uses quasi-experimental identification via Tinder's initial Greek-organization marketing strategy for college campuses. Key findings: (1) Tinder's launch caused a **sharp, persistent increase in sexual activity**, but **little impact on long-term relationship formation**. (2) Dating outcome inequality especially among men **rose**. (3) Sexual assault rates and STDs rose alongside. (4) Mental health did not worsen on average; women may have improved slightly. This is the strongest causal estimate of Tinder specifically.
- **Assessment**: Strong identification strategy (quasi-experiment using rollout timing), recently published or forthcoming in AEA journal. 2 citations currently (very new). Most direct causal evidence available.

### Jung & Lusher (2026) — "Dating Apps and Marriage Rates"
- **Link**: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.econlet.2026.112873 (Economics Letters)
- **Key content**: Uses Google Trends search intensity for "Tinder" as proxy for exposure; CPS microdata, event-study and DID designs for adults 21–35. Findings: **no effect on overall marriage rates or timing**. However, Tinder exposure associated with **higher divorce/separation rates**, especially at younger ages. Marriage effects emerge in male-heavy and thin dating markets — suggesting apps ease matching frictions in underserved markets but don't increase aggregate marriage formation.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, clean empirical design. The no-effect-on-marriage finding is important and somewhat surprising given the narrative.

### Ershov, Fong & Yildirim (2026) — "What Happens When Dating Goes Online?" (NBER WP 34757)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/papers/w34757
- **Key content**: Analyzed data from hundreds of dating websites/apps across US, with county-level marriage and divorce data 2002–2023. Key finding: **desktop era (2002–2013)**: 1% increase in online dating usage → 0.5% **rise in divorce rates**. **Mobile era (2017–2023)**: 1% increase in usage → 0.4% **decline in marriage rates** and 0.33% drop in divorce rates (the decline in divorces seen as a mechanical result of fewer marriages). Identified "low-quality information" and "choice overload" as mechanisms. Mobile apps emphasize photos/brief bios vs. rich questionnaires of desktop era.
- **Assessment**: NBER working paper (not yet peer-reviewed), but authored by credible economists (UCL, Wharton, etc.). Covers a long time horizon. The split finding — desktop raised divorces, mobile reduced marriages — is striking and plausible mechanically.

### Potarca (2020) — "The Demography of Swiping Right"
- **Link**: https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0243733 (PLoS ONE)
- **Key content**: Swiss survey 2018, couples who met through dating apps vs. offline. Key findings: (1) No difference in relationship/life satisfaction. (2) Dating app couples have **stronger cohabitation intentions** and women have stronger fertility intentions. (3) Dating apps **allow more educationally diverse and geographically distant couples** — counter to the homophily narrative. (4) Mobile dating had taken over as the main online context in Switzerland by 2018.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, representative Swiss data, 39 citations. Contradicts the "apps reduce relationship quality" narrative directly. Geographic scope (Switzerland) limits generalizability.

### SwipeStats.io (2025) — Tinder Statistics from 7,079 Profiles
- **Link**: https://www.swipestats.io/blog/tinder-statistics
- **Key content**: User-uploaded actual Tinder data exports (2020–2025). 294M swipes, 3.1M matches analyzed. Key numbers: (1) Women get 8.4x higher average match rates than men (44.4% vs 5.3%). Median male match rate: **2.04%**. (2) Top 10% of men get 12.5%+ match rate; bottom 10% get under 0.3%. (3) Inequality is growing: average male match rate up 21% YoY but median down 28% — "winners winning bigger, everyone else falling behind." (4) 43% of male matches result in 0–1 messages. (5) Only 15% become real conversations. (6) Men who never hit the daily swipe limit achieve 20.87% match rate (sample n=81, treat with caution). Women's match rates **increase with age** until 40–44.
- **Assessment**: Largest real dataset on Tinder behavior available publicly. Self-selection bias (users who track stats differ from typical users; 88% male). Not peer-reviewed. But consistent directionally with academic findings. The gender gap and inequality trend are the most important numbers here.

### Worst Online Dater / Hinge Engineer Aviv Goldgeier (2015/2017) — Gini Analysis
- **Link**: Popularized via Quartz article (Aug 2017); discussed at paulvanderlaken.com and senseoffairness.blog
- **Key content**: (1) "Worst Online Dater" estimated Tinder Gini coefficient for male attractiveness at **0.58** — worse than income inequality in all but the most unequal countries (South Africa ~0.63). Bottom 80% of men competing for bottom 22% of women by likes. (2) Hinge engineer Goldgeier: top 1% of men receive 16% of all likes; top 10% receive ~60%; bottom 50% receive under 5%. Hinge Gini: **0.73 for men, 0.63 for women**.
- **Assessment**: Neither is peer-reviewed. "Worst Online Dater" study had ~27 participants obtained via catfishing — methodologically weak. Hinge data came from an internal engineer's analysis, not published. However, the SwipeStats data (7,079 profiles) broadly supports the scale of gender gap. The Gini numbers are widely cited in popular discourse but should be treated as indicative rather than definitive.

### Bruch, Feinberg & Lee (2016) — "Extracting Multiscale Structure from Millions of OKCupid Users"
- Note: Found via reference trail rather than direct search. Published in Science Advances. Established that OKCupid users exhibit a shared ranking of desirability, with gender-specific hierarchies.

### Thomas (2019) — "Online Exogamy Reconsidered"
- **Link**: Social Forces, 2019
- **Key content**: Used 2009 and 2017 HCMST nationally representative surveys. Couples who met online are **more likely to be interracial, interreligious, and to cross college degree boundaries**, but also **more similar in age**. No more/less likely to cross political boundaries. Population-level: only a small share of recent changes in couple diversity can be directly attributed to online meeting. But potential for more if online expands.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, nationally representative data, 49 citations. Directly addresses assortative mating. The finding that online is less assortative on race/religion but *more* assortative on age is a nuanced result.

### Ortega & Hergovich (2017) — "The Strength of Absent Ties: Social Integration via Online Dating"
- **Link**: arXiv:1709.10478 (preprint/working paper, 48 citations)
- **Key content**: Theoretical modeling using random graphs and matching theory. When dating introduces "absent ties" (strangers), social integration (including interracial marriage) can increase sharply even if the number of online-met partners is small. Consistent with the observed increase in interracial marriages in the US over two decades.
- **Assessment**: Primarily theoretical, not empirical. But provides a mechanism for why online dating might break down racial homophily — by expanding the pool to include strangers outside one's social network.

### Zhao, Sidari, Lee & Zietsch (2024) — "Speed-dating and simulation data explain the discrepancy between stated and revealed preferences"
- **Link**: European Journal of Personality, 2024
- **Key content**: High-powered speed-dating study (n=1,145). Found that **4 out of 9 traits** showed some correspondence between stated and revealed preferences. Simulations show that when attractiveness depends on multiple traits, stated preferences for individual traits can only be minimally related to revealed preferences — this is partly a **statistical artifact** of multi-trait evaluation rather than evidence of radical self-deception. Multiple-trait aggregation methods (Euclidean distance, pattern metric) showed association but likely reflect artefacts.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, well-powered, recent. Important methodological contribution: the stated/revealed gap may be partly a measurement problem, not purely a behavioral gap. Limits inference from earlier studies that found stark discordances.

### Fisman, Iyengar, Kamenica & Simonson (2004/2006) — Speed Dating Race Preferences
- **Link**: Columbia speed-dating studies
- **Key content**: Classic speed-dating experiments showing racial preferences in revealed behavior. People selected partners of same race at higher rates. Stated preferences regarding race showed similar-race bias among some groups. Women showed stronger preferences in general; men's stated-to-revealed gap was larger on some attributes.
- **Assessment**: Influential early work (4–21 citations in different versions). Methodologically clean (randomized speed-dating). But speed-dating is not the same context as swipe-based apps, and findings on stated/revealed consistency have since been complicated by Zhao et al. 2024.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The empirical picture is substantially more complex than either "apps ruined dating" or "apps are net positive." Here is what the data shows with reasonable confidence:

**On relationship formation rates:** The two best causal studies point in the same direction but with different mechanisms. Buyukeren et al. (2026) find Tinder increased sexual activity sharply while not improving long-term relationship formation. Jung & Lusher (2026) find no aggregate effect on marriage rates. Ershov et al. (2026) find mobile apps actually *reduced* marriage rates by ~0.4% per 1% increase in usage. Rosenfeld's HCMST data confirm a pandemic dating recession but pre-pandemic trends did not clearly show apps suppressing relationships. **Best interpretation**: apps made casual sexual encounters more available but did not improve — and may slightly worsen — the rate of committed relationship formation, especially for mobile/swipe-era apps.

**On assortative mating:** Surprisingly, online dating may be *less* assortative than offline on race and religion, and somewhat more educationally diverse (Thomas 2019, Potarca 2020, Ortega & Hergovich 2017). The theoretical mechanism is clear: apps introduce strangers outside your social network, breaking social-network-based homophily. But the Hitsch/Hortacsu/Ariely tradition (2010) shows that preference-based sorting still operates strongly; the question is whether the pool expansion effect dominates. Current evidence slightly favors modest heterogamy gains on race/religion. Educational homogamy appears roughly similar online vs. offline, or slightly more heterogamous online.

**On attention inequality (Gini coefficient of matches):** This is where the data is most striking but methodologically weakest. The non-peer-reviewed figures (Gini 0.58 for Tinder men, 0.73 for Hinge men) are based on small samples or internal engineer analyses. However, SwipeStats.io with 7,079 profiles provides strong support for the general finding: the **gender asymmetry** is massive (men 5.3% vs. women 44.4% average match rates; median man gets 2.04%). The **intra-gender inequality** among men is real and growing by year-over-year data. Bruch & Newman (2018) document a consistent desirability hierarchy. No peer-reviewed paper has computed a formal Gini coefficient on app data, but the distribution is highly concentrated.

**On stated vs. revealed preferences:** Early studies found large gaps; more recent work (Zhao et al. 2024) suggests this partly reflects measurement artifacts of multi-trait evaluation. However, the Hitsch/Hortacsu/Ariely tradition shows that what people *do* on dating platforms (messaging, responding) is consistent with coherent revealed preferences, even if stated preferences are noisy. People genuinely prefer higher-desirability partners and rationally calibrate messaging strategy accordingly (Bruch & Newman 2018). The "gap" is less about self-deception and more about the failure of stated single-attribute preferences to capture how people aggregate across multiple dimensions.

**On coordination failure:** The most interesting framing is whether apps create a coordination failure — where individually rational behavior leads to collectively poor outcomes. Several mechanisms are plausible: (1) **Choice overload**: more options → lower commitment to any option → higher "exit" rate when problems emerge (Ershov et al. mechanism); (2) **Attention concentration**: winner-take-all dynamics concentrate attention on top-tier users, leaving most men with very low match rates; (3) **Platform incentive misalignment**: Rahwan et al. (Abeliuk et al. 2019) formalize this — platforms maximizing engagement may not optimize for user welfare, but find the "price of anarchy" is bounded and competition among platforms helps; (4) **Reduced social vetting**: by disintermediating friends, apps remove a quality filter that historically improved match quality (Rosenfeld 2019).

### Agreements Across Sources

- Online meeting is now dominant in the US (Rosenfeld 2019)
- There is a massive structural gender asymmetry in app engagement and outcomes
- Apps have not demonstrably improved aggregate relationship formation rates
- Dating apps are associated with increased sexual activity without proportional relationship gain
- Online meeting is associated with marginally more diverse couples on race/religion dimensions

### Conflicts and Tensions

- **Marriage rates**: Ershov et al. (2026) find a negative causal effect; Jung & Lusher (2026) find no effect; Buyukeren et al. (2026) find no effect on long-term relationships but strong effects on sex. These studies use different identification strategies, time periods, and outcome measures — they don't fully contradict each other but the synthesis is unclear.
- **Assortative mating**: Some studies find online dating reduces educational homophily (Potarca 2020, Thomas 2019); others find it doesn't change it much (Arosio 2022/2023). Italian data shows near-zero change; Swiss and US data show some heterogamy gains.
- **Relationship quality**: Several well-designed studies (Potarca 2020, Cacioppo et al. though not confirmed here) find no difference in satisfaction between online- and offline-met couples. But Ershov et al. and Buyukeren et al. suggest compatibility issues may surface later (higher divorce/separation).

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **No peer-reviewed Gini calculation for swipe-app data.** The most-cited numbers (0.58, 0.73) are from non-academic sources. This is a genuine gap.
2. **Long-run relationship quality** for app-met couples is still understudied. Most data looks at short-to-medium horizons.
3. **Algorithm effects** are largely a black box. No published study tests whether algorithmic changes (e.g., ELO-style desirability scoring, switching to "machine learning" recommendation) affect outcomes differently from user behavior alone.
4. **Whether attention inequality has *worsened over time*** is suggested by SwipeStats trends but not rigorously established.
5. **Heterosexual women's experience** is less studied than men's (most internet discourse focuses on male frustration). Women face different issues: harassment, filtering quality, choice overload from a large pool.
6. **Non-Western and non-English-speaking markets**: most strong studies are US/Europe.
7. **Mechanism identification for the stated/revealed gap** at scale: Zhao et al. 2024 is promising but speed-dating is not app data.

### Surprises

1. **Apps may have *reduced* marriage rates in the mobile era** — while they clearly increased sexual activity and meeting rates, the net effect on committed relationship formation may be negative. This directly contradicts the intuition that more matchmaking = more matches.
2. **Dating apps may have made couples *more* racially/religiously diverse** — the common narrative that apps reinforce existing social hierarchies is at least partially wrong on racial homophily.
3. **Women's match rates *increase* with age on Tinder** (until 40–44), while men's peak in early 20s and decline. This contradicts the common assumption that women face increasing difficulty with age on apps.
4. **The desktop era raised divorce rates; the mobile era reduced marriages** — a striking generational shift in mechanism. Desktop sites (eHarmony, Match.com) with rich profiles may have *improved* match quality but increased dating pool, leading to better alternatives after marriage. Mobile apps produce fewer but lower-quality committed relationships.
5. **Relationship quality does not differ** between online- and offline-met couples in most well-designed studies — the narrative that "app relationships are worse" lacks empirical support at the relationship level, even if aggregate rates are affected.
