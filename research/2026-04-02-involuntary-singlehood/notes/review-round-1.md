# Review — Round 1

## Overall Assessment

The five survey notes are impressively thorough. Each covers its question with genuine depth, cites strong academic sources, and identifies contradictions honestly rather than papering over them. The material is more than sufficient for a shallow report -- but not yet sufficient for the kind of essay that takes positions and builds arguments. The surveys have surfaced excellent raw material; what is missing is resolution of several key tensions that would allow the final report to make claims rather than just catalog findings. Three specific threads, if traced deeper, would substantially elevate the report.

## Per-Question Evaluation

### Q1: Mating market failure modes (demand/supply/coordination)

- **Coverage**: Strong. The survey identifies the supply-side failure (male economic deterioration among non-college men) as the dominant mechanism, with coordination failures compounding it. The Rossi & Xiao decomposition for China and the Dallas Fed paper on sorting are excellent anchors.
- **Strengths**: The sequential framing -- supply failure reduces demand, which weakens norms, which shifts the equilibrium -- is analytically sharp and well-supported. The Goldman et al. and Autor et al. findings provide the empirical backbone. The East Asian comparison (Korea, China) adds cross-national depth.
- **Gaps**: The coordination-failure / multiple-equilibria dimension is flagged but underdeveloped. Lundberg & Pollak gesture at it; Cox describes the symptoms; but nobody has modeled whether the shift from "marriage is universal" to "marriage is optional" is self-reinforcing. This is conceptually important for the report's argument -- if the equilibrium is self-reinforcing, policy interventions face a much harder problem. The survey notes this gap but does not attempt to trace it further.

### Q2: How the economic structure of partnership changed

- **Coverage**: Excellent. The Becker-to-Cherlin-to-Rosenfeld arc is well-constructed. The Rosenfeld HCMST data on how couples meet is the single most important empirical finding across all the surveys -- the disintermediation story is powerful and well-sourced.
- **Strengths**: The three-phase periodization (production unit marriage -> companionate -> individualized/capstone) is clear and historically grounded. The Rosenfeld finding that traditional meeting institutions began declining in the 1940s, not the 1990s, is a genuinely surprising insight that should anchor the report. The Autor et al. finding that male earnings shocks still causally affect marriage rates even in 2014 is important -- it shows the old economic logic persists even as the institutional framework decays.
- **Gaps**: The survey identifies a critical gap it cannot fill: the counterfactual question of how many people who *want* partners fail to find them due to infrastructure collapse vs. preference change vs. economic mismatch. This is probably unanswerable with current data, so I would not assign it as a deep dive. However, the "intermediary quality difference" (vetted community members vs. strangers on platforms) is flagged but underdeveloped. This connects directly to Q3 and deserves more attention.

### Q3: What dating technology actually does to matching outcomes

- **Coverage**: Strong, with good use of recent causal studies. The Buyukeren et al. (2026), Jung & Lusher (2026), and Ershov et al. (2026) papers provide the best available causal evidence. The SwipeStats data and Gini analysis are informative even if methodologically imperfect.
- **Strengths**: The core finding -- apps increased sexual activity but did not improve (and may have slightly worsened) committed relationship formation -- is well-supported by multiple studies using different identification strategies. The distinction between desktop-era effects (raised divorce) and mobile-era effects (reduced marriage) from Ershov et al. is striking and novel. The Potarca finding that app-met couples are no worse in relationship quality is an important corrective.
- **Gaps**: The platform incentive misalignment story (apps profit from engagement, not from successful matches) is identified as important but has almost no academic evidence behind it. This is a genuine gap in the literature, not a gap in the survey. The algorithm black-box problem is similarly identified but unfillable. What *is* fillable: the Dallas Fed finding that online dating did NOT change matching patterns (2008-2021) deserves to be reconciled more carefully with the Ershov et al. finding that mobile apps reduced marriage rates. If apps didn't change *who* matches with *whom* but did reduce *how many* people match at all, that is a specific and important claim about mechanism. This tension is not resolved in the current notes.

### Q4: Gendered asymmetry in involuntary singlehood

- **Coverage**: Very strong. This is probably the best-executed survey of the five. The careful decomposition of the Pew headline figure (63% vs. 34%) into its components -- age-gap effects, commitment-threshold differences, LGB identification gaps -- is exactly the kind of critical analysis the report needs. The Wolfinger, Kofke-Egger, and Pew 2025 ACS data provide the corrective.
- **Strengths**: The three-layer finding is well-supported: (1) the measurement gap is real but smaller than headlines suggest (~10pp not 29pp); (2) the subjective gap (men experience singlehood as more unwanted) is robustly real cross-nationally; (3) the structural explanation is primarily about economic class, not gender per se. The Cuthbert feminist critique adds an important dimension. The cross-national evidence (Sweden, South Korea, Germany) prevents the analysis from being US-centric.
- **Gaps**: The survey identifies but does not resolve the puzzle that Sweden (high gender equality) shows the same male singlehood asymmetry as South Korea (low gender equality). If the asymmetry persists across very different gender-norm regimes, this either points to something biological/psychological or to a shared economic structure (labor market precarity for non-college men is a feature of all advanced economies). This deserves deeper investigation because it bears on whether the asymmetry is tractable through policy.

### Q5: Second-order social consequences of mass singlehood

- **Coverage**: Good breadth, weaker depth than the other four surveys. The survey correctly identifies that this literature is fragmented across six disciplines that rarely cite each other. The South Korea political radicalization evidence is strong; the housing and fiscal evidence is solid but narrow; the wealth concentration and social trust threads are thinly sourced.
- **Strengths**: The South Korea case is well-documented with multiple peer-reviewed sources. The bare-branches literature provides historical precedent. The Finnish urban fertility trap (Pettay et al.) is a genuinely surprising finding. The identification of feedback loops (singlehood -> resentment -> withdrawal from partnership norms, per Kim 2023) is analytically important.
- **Gaps**: Two significant gaps. First, the bare-branches literature deals with sex-ratio imbalances, not the preference-mismatch-driven singlehood that characterizes the West. The survey acknowledges this but does not adequately bridge the gap. Are the mechanisms actually the same (low-status unpartnered men as destabilizing force) or fundamentally different (sex-ratio-driven surplus men vs. economically-marginalized men in sex-ratio-neutral populations)? Second, the wealth concentration / "heirs are missing" thread is entirely sourced from journalism. There may be academic demographic modeling on this topic that was not found.

## Deep-Dive Assignments

### Assignment 1: The Dallas Fed paradox -- why didn't dating apps change matching?

- **Question to answer**: The Dallas Fed (Cheremukhin et al. 2024) finds that online dating produced "negligible change" in preferences, mate choice, and sorting patterns from 2008-2021. Meanwhile, Ershov et al. (2026) find mobile apps causally reduced marriage rates. How can apps change nothing about *who* matches with *whom* while simultaneously reducing *whether* people match at all? What is the specific mechanism? Is it choice overload, platform incentive misalignment, the shift from rich-profile desktop sites to photo-based mobile apps, or something else? Can we find primary evidence (the Dallas Fed paper itself, the Ershov NBER paper) that speaks to this?
- **Why it matters**: This is the central puzzle for the technology question (Q3). If the report can articulate a clear mechanism for why search-cost reduction failed to improve matching outcomes, it would be one of the essay's most original contributions. The naive expectation (more options = better matching) is clearly wrong, and explaining *why* would give the report genuine analytical teeth.
- **Where to start**: Read the Dallas Fed working paper (https://s3.amazonaws.com/real.stlouisfed.org/wp/2023/2023-023.pdf) more carefully, especially the section on 2008-2021 changes. Read the Ershov et al. NBER paper (w34757) for their proposed mechanisms. Look for Fong's field experiment on market thickness and selectivity. Search for "choice overload" + "dating" in academic literature; also "platform design incentives dating app marriage."

### Assignment 2: South Korea as the leading indicator -- the full mechanism from economic precarity to political polarization

- **Question to answer**: South Korea has the world's lowest fertility rate, the fastest marriage decline, and the largest gender voting gap in the OECD. Trace the full causal chain: What specific economic conditions (youth unemployment, housing costs, precarious employment) drove the marriage collapse? How did this translate into gendered political radicalization (young men rightward, young women leftward)? What role did online communities (Ilbe, Megalia) play as organizational infrastructure? Is this a leading indicator for other advanced economies, or is it specific to Korean cultural conditions (Confucian family norms, compressed modernization)?
- **Why it matters**: South Korea is the most fully realized example of what mass involuntary singlehood does to a society's politics. If the report can tell this story well -- with specifics, not generalities -- it becomes the concrete case that anchors the otherwise abstract "second-order consequences" question. It also allows the report to make a forward-looking argument about whether other countries face similar risks.
- **Where to start**: The Social Politics 2024 paper, Kim 2023 (Socius), and Park 2022 are already identified. Go deeper: look for the specific voting data by age and gender in recent Korean elections (2022 presidential, 2024 parliamentary). Look for Yoo 2026 (Demographic Research) on Korean marriage decline mechanics. Search for "South Korea gender war" or "South Korea anti-feminism young men" in academic databases. Also look for comparative work: has anyone explicitly compared Korea's trajectory to Japan's or to European patterns?

### Assignment 3: The class stratification of the matching crisis -- what exactly happened to non-college men?

- **Question to answer**: Multiple surveys converge on the claim that the marriage/partnership crisis is concentrated in non-college men, not distributed evenly across the male population. College-educated women's marriage rates are stable; they are "marrying down" in education to economically successful non-college men, cream-skimming the top of that pool. What happened to the *bottom* of the non-college male pool? Is this purely about earnings decline, or also about geographic isolation, social skill deficits, health deterioration (deaths of despair), or incarceration? What does the actual earnings distribution look like -- is there a bimodal split within non-college men? And critically: the Chambers et al. finding that metro areas with lower male joblessness and incarceration have 50%+ smaller class gaps in marriage rates -- can this be traced more carefully? It suggests a very specific policy lever.
- **Why it matters**: This is the thread that connects Q1 (market failure), Q2 (economic structure), and Q4 (gendered asymmetry). If the report can characterize with precision *which* men are failing in the mating market and *why*, it moves beyond the vague "men are struggling" narrative into something analytically useful. The Chambers et al. metro-area variation finding is potentially the most policy-relevant result in the entire research.
- **Where to start**: The Goldman/Chambers/Winkelmann SSRN paper (5086363) is the primary source -- read more carefully for the metro-area analysis. Autor, Dorn & Hanson (2019) for the trade-shock mechanism. Binder & Bound (2019) for the labor force participation feedback loop. Case & Deaton on deaths of despair among non-college men. Search for "non-college men earnings distribution bimodal" or "working class men marriage prospects earnings" in economic literature. Also look for the Wilson "marriageable men" hypothesis (1987) and subsequent tests -- this is the original formulation and may have been tested more recently.

## Verdict

**CONTINUE** -- with the three deep-dive assignments above.

The surveys have built a strong foundation. The three assignments target the specific tensions and gaps that, if resolved, would allow the final report to make three distinctive claims: (1) a precise mechanism for why dating technology failed to solve the matching problem despite reducing search costs; (2) a concrete case study (South Korea) of what happens when mass singlehood hits politics; and (3) a class-specific rather than gender-generic account of who is failing in the mating market and why. These three threads, well-executed, would make the difference between a competent literature review and an essay with genuine analytical force.
