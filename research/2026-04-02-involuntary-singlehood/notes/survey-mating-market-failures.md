## Key Findings

### 1. The Scale of the Problem: Empirical Baselines

Marriage rates in the United States have fallen steeply. Lyman Stone (2024, Institute for Family Studies) projects that roughly **one-third of Americans who turn 45 in 2050 will never have married** — the highest rate in recorded U.S. history. In 1980, about 90% of 35-year-old men had ever married; today that figure is approximately 60%. The decline accelerated through the 2000s and 2010s. Similar trends appear in South Korea, Japan, Germany, and urban China, suggesting a broad structural phenomenon, not an American idiosyncrasy.

The American Survey Center (Cox 2025 newsletter "How Bad is America's Romantic Recession?") documents the experiential dimension: most singles hate dating apps, a growing share have withdrawn from dating entirely, and many — like the case study profiled — report their singlehood as involuntary and distressing. The Pew Research Center found record numbers of 40-year-olds who have never married.

### 2. Is This a Demand Problem, a Supply Problem, or a Coordination Problem?

The empirical literature offers a nuanced answer: **primarily a supply-side failure concentrated in a specific demographic, with coordination failures compounding it at the margins.**

#### 2a. The Supply Failure: Male Marriageability Collapse Among the Non-College-Educated

The single most empirically robust finding is that the marriage market failure is heavily stratified by education. Goldman, Chambers & Winkelmann ("Bachelors Without Bachelor's," 2025, working paper covered in Cornell Chronicle) analyze 50 years of U.S. census data and find:

- Marriage rates for **college-educated women have remained stable above 70%** throughout the entire period.
- Marriage rates for **non-college-educated women have plummeted from 78.7% to 52.4%** over 50 years.
- The cause is not a shortage of non-college men (there are more of them relative to non-college women) but the **economic deterioration of non-college men**: average real earnings of non-college men who do not marry college-educated women have *declined* by $10,000 in inflation-adjusted terms over 50 years.

This is a supply-side problem: the pool of "marriageable" men (by economic-viability criteria women use) has contracted dramatically for working-class women.

**Autor, Dorn & Hanson (2017, AER Insights)** provide clean causal identification: using trade-shock variation from Chinese import competition, they show that manufacturing employment shocks that specifically reduce male earnings and employment (relative to female) causally reduce marriage rates and increase unwed motherhood in exposed local labor markets. The mechanism runs through Becker's household specialization model — as men's relative earnings advantage erodes, the gains from specialization-driven marriage decline.

**Ruggles (2022, Demographic Research)** finds that economic composition historically explained most of the racial marriage gap, though its role has diminished since the late 20th century as the breadwinner model weakened.

**Binder & Bound (2019, JEP)** examine the broader decline in less-educated men's labor force participation and argue it cannot be explained by standard neoclassical models alone — feedback loops between labor market decline and family structure (fewer men forming stable families reduces their incentive to work) compound the supply-side deterioration.

#### 2b. The Preference Mismatch / Demand-Side Complication

There is a genuine demand-side component, but it interacts with the supply problem rather than acting independently.

**Lundberg, Pollak & Stearns (2016, JEP, 281 citations)** argue that marriage's function has shifted: college graduates use marriage as a **commitment device for intensive joint investment in children** — an insurance/coordination mechanism. For less-educated couples, such investments are less feasible, so the value of formal marriage commitment is lower. This creates a divergence in demand for marriage across educational groups.

**Grow & Van Bavel (2015, PLoS ONE, 107 citations)** show via agent-based simulation that the **reversal of gender inequality in education** — women now outnumber men in higher education — creates structural mating market mismatches without any change in preferences. Simply changing the population composition in a model where both sexes prefer similar-education partners generates the observed rise in unmarried educated women. This is a structural coordination failure, not a preference failure.

**Rossi & Xiao (2025, CEPR DP20696)** decompose China's marriage decline (probability of marrying halved from 1999 to 2019) into:
- 50% explained by **population structure change** (more educated people, especially women) causing **mismatch between highly-educated women and less-educated men**
- 13% explained by **marriage squeeze** (female-to-male ratio deterioration due to sex-selective birth historically)
- Remainder by decline in **marital surplus** (the intrinsic value of marriage itself)

This is probably the most careful decomposition in the literature. It suggests that preference shifts (declining surplus from marriage) are real but secondary to structural demographic mismatches.

### 3. Geographic Sorting as a Market-Thickening / Thinning Mechanism

**Wen & Mao (2025, SSRN working paper)** model the interaction of geographic sorting and assortative marriage in the U.S. Between 1980 and 2000, college graduates increasingly concentrated in high-skill cities, and this geographic segregation compounded educational homogamy. Their key welfare finding: after accounting for marriage, the **college well-being gap grew substantially more than the college wage gap** — marriage market access is a major but under-counted inequality dimension.

**Cheremukhin, Restrepo-Echavarria & Tutino (2024, Dallas Fed Working Paper)** do a comprehensive search-model analysis of U.S. marriage market sorting 1960–2020. Their key empirical findings:
- **Online dating did NOT reduce search costs enough to meaningfully change sorting patterns** (2008–2021 period shows "negligible change" in preferences, mate choice, and sorting despite rise of apps)
- **Reduced search costs and growing spatial segregation are NOT the primary drivers** of long-term sorting trends
- The real driver is **enhanced workforce participation and college attainment among women** — a changed supply-side composition of the market
- Assortativeness by skill and education accounts for about half of increased household income inequality

This is a significant finding: it falsifies two common candidate explanations (search costs and geographic sorting as independent drivers) and points back to labor market and educational structural change.

### 4. Search Costs and Information Asymmetry: Ambiguous Effects

The story on search costs and information asymmetry is more complicated than expected.

**Hitsch, Hortacsu & Ariely (2010, AER, 690 citations)**, the foundational online dating study, estimate mate preferences using actual message-sending and reply data. Key findings:
- Revealed preferences (what people actually pursue) match Gale-Shapley stable matching predictions well
- Both men and women show strong preference for own-race partners and partners with higher income/education
- There is large variance in desirability — a small number of profiles receive the vast majority of messages, implying extreme concentration

**Fong (2020, SSRN)** runs a field experiment on two-sided market thickness in an online dating platform. She finds that when users are told there are more potential matches, they become **more selective** (paradoxically reducing match rates). When told they have more competition, they become less selective. More choice does not straightforwardly improve outcomes — there are negative general-equilibrium effects from greater market size.

**Jung et al. (2021, Information Systems Research)** find gender-asymmetric effects in online dating design: giving men more choice raises engagement but giving women more choice raises actual match rates — suggesting men and women use dating platforms differently.

The "one-way mirror" studies (Bapna et al. 2013–2016) found that information asymmetries (who can see you without you seeing them) significantly affect matching outcomes, suggesting information design matters.

The Dallas Fed paper's finding that search cost reduction did not change actual sorting is a puzzle: apps should have helped, but didn't, because **people's capacity to process and evaluate information hasn't improved despite technological advancement**. More candidates + same cognitive capacity = same or worse matching efficiency.

### 5. Assortative Mating: The Homogamy Debate

There is genuine scientific disagreement here.

**Hirschl, Schwartz & Boschetti (2024, Demography, 13 citations)** extend the Schwartz-Mare findings to 1940–2020 and find that the rise in educational homogamy **stalled around 1990 and began reversing in the 2000s** — contrary to popular wisdom. The reversal is driven primarily by women increasingly marrying men with less education. The college degree remains the strongest barrier to intermarriage.

**Gihleb & Lang (2016, 48 citations)** go further, arguing that statistical evidence for increased assortative mating is based on inappropriate techniques; they find neither educational homogamy nor correlation between spouses' potential earnings has dramatically changed.

**However**, the Dallas Fed (2024) still finds long-term (1960–2020) increased assortativeness by income, education, and skills, even if the pace has moderated.

The key reconciliation: **aggregate homogamy statistics mask divergent class-based dynamics**. College-educated women are increasingly "marrying down" educationally (which reduces measured homogamy) but selecting non-college men who are economically strong — essentially cream-skimming the top of the non-college male pool. This leaves the bottom of the non-college male pool without partners, which is the primary driver of aggregate marriage decline.

### 6. The Korea/Japan/China Case: Preference Conflict as Independent Driver

East Asian cases suggest an additional failure mode: **preference conflict between genders about relationship roles**, especially around women's careers and domestic labor expectations.

**Raymo & Park (2020, Demography, 48 citations)** study Korea's marriage decline and find that changing domestic marriage market composition — specifically the educational reversal with women now outnumbering men in college — created a structural mismatch in a context where traditional hypergamous norms persist (women expected to marry up educationally). The result: highly-educated Korean women face a shortage of equally-educated men, while less-educated men face a shortage of less-educated women who have moved into higher education.

The Rossi & Xiao (2025) finding for China — that mismatch from educational expansion explains 50% of marriage decline — is mirrored in Korea. Both cases also feature a cultural dimension: women increasingly rejecting the terms of traditional marriage (domestic burden, career sacrifice) even where suitable partners exist. This is a demand-side failure, but it's driven by preference conflict about **division of surplus within marriage** rather than mere search frictions.

### 7. Coordination Problems: Multiple Equilibria and Norm Shifts

The literature hints at but doesn't fully model a coordination-failure dimension: societies may be shifting from a "marriage is universal" equilibrium to a "marriage is optional" equilibrium, and individuals rationally update their own marriage likelihood based on others' behavior.

**Lundberg & Pollak (2016)** gesture toward this in their commitment-device framework: as marriage becomes less universal among less-educated groups, the social infrastructure supporting it weakens, norms shift, and the coordination equilibrium deteriorates. This is consistent with the Cox (2025) finding that social pressure to marry has largely evaporated for young adults — removing a force that helped coordinate toward the marriage equilibrium.

The "romantic recession" frame (Cox's term) implies a coordination failure: everyone might prefer to be in a relationship, but the transaction costs of finding and maintaining one have risen relative to the alternatives, and the cultural infrastructure (institutions, norms, physical venues) that formerly reduced those costs has eroded.

## Surprises, Contradictions, and Gaps

**Surprises:**

1. The Dallas Fed finding that online dating didn't change matching patterns at all (2008–2021) is striking. Apps were expected to be a major search-cost reducer, but the evidence suggests they haven't improved matching efficiency — possibly because of how the platforms are designed (maximizing engagement, not marriages).

2. The Goldman et al. finding that college-educated women's marriage rates have been stable is counterintuitive given the "college men shortage" narrative. The real collapse is among non-college women, not college women.

3. Rossi & Xiao's decomposition showing only 13% of Chinese marriage decline comes from the sex-ratio squeeze (a huge policy-driven imbalance) — demographic supply constraints matter much less than expected relative to mismatch and preference factors.

**Contradictions:**

1. Assortative mating trends are genuinely contested. Hirschl et al. say homogamy peaked around 1990 and is declining; the Dallas Fed says assortativeness by income/education has risen over the full 1960–2020 period. These may be compatible if we distinguish educational homogamy (the measure Schwartz/Hirschl use) from economic/skill similarity (the Dallas Fed measure), but the papers don't engage directly.

2. The literature is divided on whether declining marriage is primarily a supply problem (bad men for working-class women) or a demand problem (women preferring singlehood). Probably both are true at different education levels.

**Gaps:**

1. No clean empirical decomposition for the U.S. analogous to Rossi & Xiao's China analysis. That would tell us how much of declining U.S. marriage is structural (demographic composition changes) vs. preference/surplus decline vs. search/matching frictions.

2. The literature focuses heavily on heterosexual marriage markets and first marriages. Non-marital partnership formation (cohabitation), remarriage, and same-sex markets are mostly treated separately. If involuntary singlehood is the outcome of concern, broader relationship formation data is needed.

3. Very little empirical work directly tests the **coordination equilibrium hypothesis** — whether norm shifts are self-reinforcing and whether there are multiple stable equilibria. The Lundberg-Pollak framework implies this but there's no direct empirical test.

4. The link between dating app **design** (platform incentives to maximize engagement rather than facilitate relationships) and market failure is widely discussed but poorly measured. Apps have incentives misaligned with users' stated goals (finding a partner). This is a genuine information asymmetry / moral hazard problem but the academic literature barely touches it.

5. Most studies focus on the U.S. Comparative cross-national evidence, especially from societies with different welfare states and gender norms (Nordic countries vs. East Asia vs. Mediterranean) could illuminate which structural factors are most important.

## Assessment of What I Found

The literature is richer than expected. There is a well-developed theoretical framework (Becker, Gale-Shapley, search models) and increasingly strong empirical work, especially in the 2017–2025 period. The best papers (Autor et al., Goldman et al., Rossi & Xiao, Dallas Fed) provide causal or structural decomposition evidence, not just correlations.

The strongest finding is that **this is primarily a supply-side failure driven by male economic deterioration at the working-class level** — a demand-supply mismatch that is structural and intensifying. The preference-mismatch and coordination-failure components are real but secondary in the U.S. context; they may be more central in East Asian contexts where gender-role conflict is more acute.

The search-cost / information-asymmetry frame is mostly a red herring for explaining aggregate trends, though it remains relevant for understanding individual-level matching inefficiencies and platform design problems.

The framing of "demand problem vs. supply problem vs. coordination problem" is useful but the real answer is: **supply failure (marriageable men) → reduces demand for marriage (women in affected class rationally update) → weakens coordinating norms → equilibrium shift toward singlehood**. These are sequential mechanisms, not independent alternatives.

- **Link**: https://news.cornell.edu/stories/2025/02/struggling-men-hurt-noncollege-womens-marriage-prospects (working paper summary; Cornell Chronicle)
- **Key content**: Marriage rates for college-educated women stable above 70% for 50 years. Non-college women's marriage rates collapsed from 78.7% to 52.4%. ### Autor, Dorn & Hanson — "When Work Disappears" (2017)
- **Link**: DOI: 10.1257/aeri.20180010
- **Key content**: Causal identification using trade shocks shows male earnings shocks reduce marriage rates, increase non-marital births, and increase single-parent households. Consistent with Becker specialization model and with sociological accounts of male social decline.
- **Assessment**: Highly credible — AER Insights, strong IV identification, 451 citations. This is the strongest causal evidence linking male economic deterioration to marriage decline.

### Cheremukhin, Restrepo-Echavarria & Tutino — "Marriage Market Sorting in the U.S." (2024)
- **Link**: https://s3.amazonaws.com/real.stlouisfed.org/wp/2023/2023-023.pdf ; DOI 10.20955/wp.2023.023
- **Key content**: Falsifies "reduced search costs" and "spatial segregation" as primary drivers of long-term sorting changes. Primary driver is women's educational and workforce gains. Online dating (2008–2021) changed nothing detectable in mate choice or sorting patterns. Assortativeness by skill/education accounts for ~50% of household income inequality growth.
- **Assessment**: Fed working paper, structural search model, strong data. Very significant for falsifying the search-cost hypothesis.

### Rossi & Xiao — "Left Over or Opting Out? Squeeze, Mismatch and Surplus in Chinese Marriage Markets" (2025)
- **Link**: https://cepr.org/publications/dp20696
- **Key content**: Decomposes Chinese marriage decline (probability halved 1999–2019): 50% from educational expansion/mismatch (educated women, less-educated men), 13% from sex-ratio squeeze, remainder from declining marital surplus. Best structural decomposition of the failure modes.
- **Assessment**: CEPR discussion paper, Choo-Siow structural model, census data. China-specific but methodology is generalizable.

### Hirschl, Schwartz & Boschetti — "Eight Decades of Educational Assortative Mating" (2024)
- **Link**: DOI: 10.1215/00703370-11558914
- **Key content**: Educational homogamy rose through 1990 then began reversing. Women increasingly marry men with less education. College degree remains the key dividing line. If not for women marrying down, homogamy would have continued rising.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed in Demography (top field journal), 80-year longitudinal census analysis, 13 citations already. Complicates the "increasing assortative mating" narrative.

### Lundberg, Pollak & Stearns — "Family Inequality" (2016)
- **Link**: DOI: 10.1257/jep.30.2.79
- **Key content**: Marriage functions as commitment device for child investment among college graduates; less valuable for less-educated where such investments less feasible. Explains educational divergence in marriage as rational institutional choice response.
- **Assessment**: JEP (accessible, high-quality), 281 citations, leading family economists. Essential theoretical framework.

### Grow & Van Bavel — "Assortative Mating and the Reversal of Gender Inequality in Education" (2015)
- **Link**: PLoS ONE, 107 citations
- **Key content**: Agent-based model shows that observed changes in assortative mating and unmarried educated women can be reproduced purely through changing population composition (educational reversal), without any preference changes. Coordination failure from structural demographics.
- **Assessment**: PLoS ONE peer-reviewed, widely cited (107 citations for a simulation study in sociology). Good structural argument for why the market fails without needing to invoke preference changes.

### Stone — "1-in-3: A Record Share of Young Adults Will Never Marry" (2024)
- **Link**: https://ifstudies.org/blog/1-in-3-a-record-share-of-young-adults-will-never-marry
- **Key content**: Demographic projections based on cohort data. At current trends, ~33% of those turning 45 in 2050 will never marry. Only 60% of 35-year-old men are ever-married today vs. 90% in 1980.
- **Assessment**: Institute for Family Studies (pro-marriage thinktank, ideological priors should be noted). Statistical methodology is reasonable — extrapolating observed cohort trajectories. The numbers are directionally consistent with academic sources.

### Cox — "How Bad is America's Romantic Recession?" (2025)
- **Link**: https://www.americansurveycenter.org/newsletter/how-bad-is-americas-romantic-recession/

### Raymo & Park — "Marriage Decline in Korea" (2020)
- **Link**: DOI: 10.1007/s13524-019-00844-9 (Demography)
- **Key content**: Korean marriage decline driven by changed educational composition of domestic marriage market (women outnumber men in college, against hypergamous norms) and growth in international marriages as Korean men seek overseas brides.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed in Demography (48 citations). Cross-national evidence that educational reversal + norm mismatch drives marriage decline.

### Hitsch, Hortacsu & Ariely — "Matching and Sorting in Online Dating" (2010)
- **Link**: DOI: 10.1257/AER.100.1.130
- **Key content**: Using online dating data, estimates mate preferences and shows Gale-Shapley algorithm predicts actual matches reasonably well. Strong sorting by race, income, education. Highly unequal desirability distribution (few profiles get most messages).
- **Assessment**: AER, 690 citations, foundational paper. Shows revealed preferences in dating markets and that Gale-Shapley-type matching can characterize outcomes — but also that concentration of attention creates matching inefficiency.
