# Survey: Is There a Gendered Asymmetry in Involuntary Singlehood, and What Explains It?

## What I Searched / Read

Searches conducted:
- Academic: gender differences in singlehood, involuntary celibacy/incel sociology, male sexlessness trends, hypergamy and educational attainment, reversed gender gap in education and mating, assortative mating income, cross-national singlehood rates, singlehood satisfaction gender.
- Survey data: Pew Research Center 2023 Valentine's Day report on single Americans, Pew 2025 unpartnered adults update, GSS data analysis, Korean/Japanese demographic data.
- Cross-cultural: South Korea marriage decline, Japan singlehood, World Values Survey 59-country multilevel analysis.
- Specific papers: Twenge JAMA 2020 (US sexual inactivity), Bozick 2021 (sex recession men), van Bavel et al. 2018 Annual Review of Sociology, Chambers/Goldman/Winkelmann 2025 SSRN working paper, Hoan & MacDonald 2024 (singles' wellbeing), Wahring et al. 2024 (singlehood transition gender), Wemrell 2024 Sweden survey, Cuthbert 2023 UK women's celibacy, Kowal & Adamczyk 2025 PLOS One 59-country analysis, Wolfinger 2023 IFS analysis of Pew data.

## Key Sources

### Pew Research Center (2023): "For Valentine's Day, 5 Facts About Single Americans"
- **Link**: https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2023/02/08/for-valentines-day-5-facts-about-single-americans/
- **Key content**: Survey of 6,034 US adults (July 2022). Reports 63% of men 18-29 describe themselves as single, vs. 34% of women the same age — a 29-point gap. Among those not in relationships, men were substantially more likely to want one: ~half of single men actively sought a relationship vs. 35% of single women. Single men became 11 percentage points less likely to seek romance from 2019 to 2022; women's interest was stable.
- **Assessment**: The most-cited source for the claimed asymmetry. Major methodological limitation: the 30-point singlehood gap appears to be inflated by definitional differences in what counts as being "in a relationship" (see Wolfinger below). The survey is nationally representative via random-address sampling. This is survey self-report, not census data. Strong source for the trend in *desire for a relationship* by gender; weaker as evidence of actual behavioral singlehood gap.

### Wolfinger, Nicholas (2023): "There's No Huge Gender Gap in Being Single Among Young Adults"
- **Link**: https://ifstudies.org/blog/number-3-in-2023-theres-no-huge-gender-gap-in-being-single-among-young-adults
- **Key content**: Compares Pew 2023 data against GSS POSSLQ variable (tracking cohabiting/partnered status). GSS shows a much smaller gender gap — roughly 10 percentage points in most years, not 30. Key explanations offered: (1) men date slightly younger women on average (2-year gap), which inflates apparent singlehood in age-matched samples; (2) women are more likely to identify relationships as "committed" at lower levels of involvement; (3) rising LGB identification among Gen Z women (19.5% of Gen Z women identify LGB vs. ~6% of men), with bisexual women potentially counted as "partnered" with each other. When sexual activity in past year is used instead of relationship status, gender differences shrink significantly.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed professor at University of Utah; published in Institute for Family Studies blog (conservative-leaning but empirically serious; IFS sometimes has ideological framing but the statistical critique here is methodologically sound). Critical corrective to the Pew headline figure. Credible but the source has an ideological lean.

### Pew Research Center (2025): "Share of US Adults Living Without a Romantic Partner Has Ticked Down"
- **Link**: https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2025/01/08/share-of-us-adults-living-without-a-romantic-partner-has-ticked-down-in-recent-years/
- **Key content**: Uses Census/ACS cohabitation data (more objective than survey self-report). Overall 42% of adults unpartnered in 2023 (down from 44% in 2019). Gender/age crossover: men under 40 are MORE likely than same-age women to be unpartnered; women over 40 are MORE likely than same-age men. Among 65+: 51% of women unpartnered vs. 29% of men. Gender overall: women 44% unpartnered, men 40% unpartnered — women are *more* likely to be unpartnered in aggregate.
- **Assessment**: Richard Fry, Pew senior researcher. Uses Census Bureau ACS data (large sample, 3M+ addresses). This is more objective than the February 2023 survey. The overall numbers cut against the "men are uniquely disadvantaged in singlehood" narrative — women are modestly more unpartnered overall. The young adult crossover (men more single under 40, women more single over 40) is the real structural finding.

### Kofke-Egger, Heather (2023): "Men, Women, and Singlehood"
- **Link**: https://databehindthedata.substack.com/p/men-women-and-singlehood
- **Key content**: Statistical decomposition of the Pew 2023 gap. Estimates that: age-gap effects (men commit to relationships ~2 years later) account for ~36% of the difference at most; LGB identification differences among Gen Z women account for up to ~46% at most. The most robust explanation for remaining gap: differential commitment thresholds — men and women may disagree on when a relationship qualifies as "committed." Men in ACS data: 17.2% of 18-29-year-olds married; she estimates ~20% in unmarried relationships. Women: 23.8% married + ~42% in unmarried relationships. She considers the figure implausible: "it would mean half of all sex young men have is outside of relationships."
- **Assessment**: Independent data journalist, methodologically careful decomposition. Substack blog (not peer-reviewed), but reasoning is grounded in well-sourced Census and survey data. Key contribution: quantifies the specific candidate explanations.

### Hoan & MacDonald (2024): "Sisters Are Doin' It for Themselves: Gender Differences in Singles' Well-Being"
- **Link**: DOI: 10.1177/19485506241287960 (Social Psychological and Personality Science)
- **Key content**: N=5,941 US singles (50% men; mean age 31.74). Single women on average report: higher satisfaction with relationship status, higher life satisfaction, higher sexual satisfaction, and lower desire for a partner. These findings hold overall; some variation by age and ethnicity. Single men more actively desire partnership.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, respectable social psychology journal. Directly addresses the question of whether singlehood is differentially "involuntary" for men vs. women at the level of desire. Strong evidence that the gender gap in *experienced* singlehood (unhappiness with being single) is real, not just a measurement artifact — single men are genuinely more distressed by their single status. N is large, well-designed.

### Wahring et al. (2024): "Men and Women Transitioning to Singlehood in Young Adulthood and Midlife"
- **Link**: Psychology and Aging (APA), DOI implied
- **Key content**: Longitudinal German pairfam data (N=1,530 participants who experienced relationship dissolution). Men relative to women: (a) less likely to initiate separation, (b) less satisfied with singlehood, (c) wished for a partner more. No gender differences found in *changes* in wellbeing or loneliness post-separation itself. Gender differences did not vary by age group.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed longitudinal data; sophisticated propensity-score matching. Complements Hoan & MacDonald: men's greater dissatisfaction with singlehood is robust across dissolution contexts and age groups.

### Wemrell, Maria (2024): "Involuntary Singlehood, Gender Equality and Mental Health in Contemporary Sweden"
- **Link**: https://doi.org/10.1093/eurpub/ckae144.2203 (European Journal of Public Health, conference abstract)
- **Key content**: Swedish survey (n=5,000 general population) investigating prevalence and correlates of involuntary singlehood and celibacy by gender, age, and SES. Preliminary results: involuntary singlehood among men is identified as a "public health issue." Examines correlations with mental health, attitudes toward gender equality, and violence against women. Sweden context: growing polarization between young men and women on gender equality views; young men more likely to endorse gender-conservative values.
- **Assessment**: Conference abstract only (European Journal of Public Health supplement) — preliminary results, no full data published as of the search. Important because: (1) Sweden is a high-equality country, so the asymmetry is not merely due to underdevelopment; (2) explores the population-level prevalence directly rather than inferring from other data; (3) links to mental health outcomes. Limited credibility as conference abstract with no peer-reviewed findings yet.

### Bozick, Robert (2021): "Is There Really a Sex Recession? Period and Cohort Effects on Sexual Inactivity Among American Men, 2006–2019"
- **Link**: American Journal of Men's Health, DOI: 10.1177/15579883211057710
- **Key content**: 14 years of National Survey of Family Growth data. Finding: no evidence of a *population-wide* sex recession among men. Rates of sexual inactivity have been constant across time. However, men born 2000–2004 show significantly higher inactivity than previous birth cohorts at the same age. Unemployed men and men living with parents are more likely to be sexually inactive.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, gold open access. Counters the popular "sex recession" narrative as a uniform trend; reframes it as a cohort-specific effect among the youngest men, tied to economic precarity. Directly relevant to the gender asymmetry question: it is not a broad male decline but a specific young male, economically marginalized subset.

### Twenge, Jean (2020): "Possible Reasons US Adults Are Not Having Sex as Much as They Used To"
- **Link**: https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamanetworkopen/fullarticle/2767063
- **Key content**: JAMA Network Open commentary. Finds that US men 18-24 saw inactivity rise from 18.9% (2000-2002) to 30.9% (2016-2018). More pronounced increases in men than women among young cohorts. Proposed causes: delayed adulthood, employment decline among men, smartphone/digital media displacing social interaction. Pornography use does not explain it (correlates with more sex, not less).
- **Assessment**: Jean Twenge is a credible psychologist (San Diego State), high-profile JAMA outlet. Commentary/observational rather than primary causal analysis. Important for establishing the magnitude of male sexual inactivity increase and the cohort-age specificity.

### Van Bavel, Schwartz, Esteve (2018): "The Reversal of the Gender Gap in Education and Its Consequences for Family Life"
- **Link**: Annual Review of Sociology, DOI: 10.1146/annurev-soc-073117-041215
- **Key content**: Comprehensive review. The gender education gap has reversed in most Western and many non-Western countries — women now outnumber men in higher education. Implications: (1) couples in which wives have more education than husbands now outnumber the reverse; (2) aversion to female status-dominant relationships is declining (though not gone); (3) marriages with more-educated wives are no longer systematically more unstable; (4) breadwinner-homemaker model persists in residual form. Low-educated men increasingly single because women (including educated women) prefer economically stable partners, not merely similarly-educated ones.
- **Assessment**: Annual Review of Sociology — top-tier review journal. 204 citations. This is the authoritative academic summary of the educational reversal's consequences. Crucial context for the mismatch hypothesis.

### Chambers, Goldman, Winkelmann (2025): "Bachelors Without Bachelor's: Gender Gaps in Education and Declining Marriage Rates"
- **Link**: https://ssrn.com/abstract=5086363 (Yale/Cornell/Harvard working paper, SSRN)
- **Key content**: Key findings: (1) College-educated women maintain stable marriage rates by increasingly "marrying down" in education — to non-college men with good economic prospects. (2) Marriage rates have declined sharply for women *without* a college degree, not for women with one. (3) The crisis is concentrated in non-college men whose economic outcomes have declined. (4) In metro areas where men have lower joblessness and incarceration, class gaps in marriage rates are 50%+ smaller. Conclusion: "concerns about declining marriage rates among college-educated women are misplaced; the real challenge lies in addressing the struggles of working-class men and women."
- **Assessment**: Working paper (not yet peer-reviewed), but from high-prestige institutions; 1,316 downloads, strong methodology using historical trends and cross-area comparison. Important for: (a) undermining the simplistic "hypergamy produces male singlehood across education" story; (b) isolating non-college men (not men broadly) as the group with dramatically worsened marriage prospects.

### PMC6241077 (2018): "The Reversed Gender Gap in Education and Assortative Mating"
- **Link**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6241077/
- **Key content**: Finds that as the education gap reversed, highly educated women adapted by partnering "downward" rather than staying single. Low-educated men, however, increasingly remained single rather than partnering "upward." Education is now a bonus on the mating market for women as well as men, creating an asymmetric effect: educated women's partnership rates were maintained or improved; low-educated men's partnership rates declined.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed. Directly addresses the asymmetry question at the mechanism level. Complements van Bavel et al. review and Chambers et al.

### Cuthbert, Karen (2023): "Neither 'Incel' nor 'Volcel': Relational Accounts of UK Women's Sexual Abstinence"
- **Link**: Women's Studies International Forum, DOI: 10.1016/j.wsif.2023.102835
- **Key content**: Qualitative study of UK women's sexual abstinence. Argues the incel/volcel binary is male-centric and obscures women's experience. For women, abstinence was often a "necessity" due to heteropatriarchal context (fear of violence, exploitation) rather than purely choice or failure. Women's celibacy exists but is under-theorized. Challenges the concept that the involuntary/voluntary distinction is cleanly applicable — for women, "consent" and "safety" complicate the category.
- **Assessment**: Feminist sociology perspective. Peer-reviewed, qualitative. Important for: challenging the assumption that men's singlehood is simply more distressing/involuntary — women's celibacy may reflect structural safety constraints rather than genuine preference. Suggests the gender asymmetry in *reported* involuntariness may partly reflect different social safety calculations.

### Kowal & Adamczyk (2025): "Sociodemographic and Cultural Factors Are Related to Singlehood Rates: A Multilevel Analysis Across 59 Countries"
- **Link**: PLOS One, DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0335416
- **Key content**: 71,169 individuals, 59 countries, World Values Survey data. Being male, younger, urban, more highly educated, lower income, and unemployed all independently predict singlehood. Individualistic national cultures show higher singlehood. Lower "flexibility" (monumentalist cultures, emphasizing self-regard) also associated with higher singlehood. Male gender is a significant independent predictor of being single, cross-nationally.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, open access, PLOS One. Large cross-national scope. Confirms gender asymmetry is not US-specific. Limitation: uses WVS data which may have cross-cultural measurement inconsistencies (similar definitional ambiguity problem as Pew). Does not distinguish voluntary from involuntary singlehood.

### Yoo, Sam Hyun (2026): "Tracking the Decline of First Marriage in South Korea"
- **Link**: Demographic Research, 54(3): 71–86 (via N-IUSSP summary, March 2026)
- **Key content**: South Korea: first marriage probability for women fell from ~95% (early 1990s) to ~67% by early 2020s. Men's rates fell even more and are more volatile. Male marriage is more sensitive to economic uncertainty and timing shocks. Men who don't meet stable employment standards face sharply reduced marriage prospects. International marriage had previously offset some of this (older Korean men marrying foreign women) but that channel has narrowed.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed demographic journal. Recent data. South Korea is the extreme case of the global trend. Consistently shows men's marriage rates declining *more steeply* than women's in East Asia.

### Statistics Korea (2024): Population and Housing Census Data
- **Link**: https://www.chosun.com/english/national-en/2024/10/31/L6UAF3AKBRCEDHFEJU4OB6M4RA/
- **Key content**: Among South Koreans 20+: 34.2% of men are never-married vs. 24.9% of women — a 9.3 percentage point gap. Among 30-somethings: 59.7% of men vs. 42.2% of women unmarried. First time over 50% of 30-somethings are unmarried (51.3% total).
- **Assessment**: Official administrative/census data (not survey self-report). Objective measure of legal marital status. Similar gender gap (men more unmarried) to US figures, even in a culturally very different context.

### Seoul Economic Daily / Korea Population Survey (2026)
- **Link**: https://en.sedaily.com/society/2026/02/01/61-percent-of-single-men-48-percent-of-single-women-open-to
- **Key content**: Survey of 2,050 Koreans 20-44. 60.8% of single men willing to marry vs. 47.6% of single women. Men's top reason for not marrying: financial burden. Women's top reason: "difficulty finding a partner who meets expectations." Women also report lower child-desired numbers (0.91 expected children vs. 1.54 for single men).
- **Assessment**: News report of population welfare survey; not peer-reviewed. But directly operationalizes the question: in South Korea, men want to marry more than women do, and women are more constrained by partner expectations. This mirrors US Pew data.

### Bouchet-Valat, Milan (2015): "Fewer Singles Among Highly Educated Women: A Gender Reversal of Hypergamy Across Cohorts in France"
- **Link**: Population, 70(4): 665–688 (INED/Sciences Po)
- **Key content**: Historical cohort data from France. Across 20th century cohorts: hypergamy (women marrying up in education) declined and reversed. Highly educated women are now *less* likely to be single than less-educated women — a complete reversal of the old pattern. Low-educated men are increasingly single.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed demography journal, top researcher at INED. 2015 but important historical baseline for European context.

### Krämer et al. (2026): "Life Satisfaction, Loneliness, and Depressivity in Consistently Single Young Adults in Germany and the UK"
- **Link**: Journal of Personality and Social Psychology (APA)
- **Key content**: 17,390 participants, 110,261 yearly observations ages 16-29 (German pairfam and UK BHPS-derived data). Who stays single longer: lower wellbeing, male gender, higher education, living alone or with parents. Consistent singles experience steeper life satisfaction decline and loneliness increase over emerging adulthood. Effects become more pronounced in late 20s. Evidence for gender moderation was "absent or inconsistent" — meaning the wellbeing costs of prolonged singlehood are similar for men and women, even though men disproportionately stay single.
- **Assessment**: APA flagship journal; longitudinal; large N across two countries. Important nuance: men are more likely to be consistently single, but once in that condition, their wellbeing trajectory is not dramatically worse than women's on the within-person level.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The headline claim — that men report involuntary singlehood at substantially higher rates than women — is real but more complicated than it first appears. The evidence points to at least three interacting phenomena that need to be disentangled:

**1. A real but partially inflated measurement gap in young adults.** The Pew 2023 figure of 63% vs. 34% is genuinely striking but probably overstates the true behavioral gap. More objective Census/ACS data (Pew 2025) and GSS analysis (Wolfinger 2023) show a smaller gender difference of around 10 percentage points in young adults, not 30. The remaining gap is partly real and partly an artifact of: (a) men and women having different commitment thresholds for calling themselves "in a relationship"; (b) age-gap effects in heterosexual dating (men tend to date younger women, so age-matched samples overcount male singlehood); (c) rising LGB identification among Gen Z women, which removes women from the male-partnerable pool without equivalent effects on men.

**2. A genuine asymmetry in how singlehood is experienced.** Even after correcting for measurement artifacts, men are consistently more likely to desire a partner, are less satisfied with singlehood, and are more likely to report singlehood as unwanted. Hoan & MacDonald 2024, Wahring et al. 2024, and Pew 2023 (desire data) all converge on this. This is not just a US phenomenon — it appears in Korean survey data (men 61% open to marriage vs. women 48%), in German longitudinal data, and in cross-national WVS data (Kowal & Adamczyk 2025).

**3. A structural mismatch explanation with a class dimension.** The strongest structural story is not "hypergamy broadly produces male singlehood" but rather: the education/income reversal and women's rising economic independence create a mismatch concentrated in non-college men. College-educated women maintain stable marriage rates by "marrying down" in education (but not income). The crisis hits non-college men whose economic outcomes have deteriorated. This is the Chambers/Goldman/Winkelmann (2025) finding and the van Bavel review conclusion. The hypergamy mechanism is real — women consistently prioritize partner economic resources more than men do (Fisman et al. 2006 speed-dating experiment; Turkish survey 2025; Japanese dating data) — but its practical effect on singlehood rates is shaped primarily by the economic collapse of the non-college male labor market, not by women's standards per se.

### Where Sources Agree

- Men under ~40 are more likely to be unpartnered than same-age women in most high-income countries.
- Single men desire partnership more than single women.
- Women are more likely to be satisfied with or choose singlehood; for some women, singlehood represents relative freedom/safety, not failure.
- The education gap in education has reversed in most Western countries, with women outnumbering men in higher education.
- Low-educated men face the sharpest declines in marriage/partnership prospects.
- In East Asia (especially South Korea and Japan), men's marriage rates are declining more steeply than women's.
- The "sex recession" is more cohort-specific (youngest adults) than generation-wide (Bozick 2021).

### Where Sources Conflict or Are Unclear

- **Magnitude of the gender gap**: Pew 2023 says 29 percentage points; GSS and ACS data suggest 10 percentage points. The true number probably lies between them, depending on how you define "in a relationship."
- **Whether hypergamy is increasing or decreasing**: Cross-cultural evidence is mixed. In France (Bouchet-Valat 2015) and much of Western Europe, hypergamy has reversed — educated women increasingly partner down in education. But surveys of mate preferences (Turkey, Indonesia, Japan) still show women prioritizing partner income/status more than men. The revealed preference (actual marriage patterns) and stated preferences may diverge.
- **Sweden vs. high-patriarchy contexts**: In Scandinavia, higher gender equality does *not* eliminate the male singlehood asymmetry (Wemrell 2024 Sweden data), suggesting this is not simply a product of traditional gender norms. But the Korea data shows the asymmetry is also large in traditional contexts. Cross-national comparisons (Kowal 2025) find the male singlehood effect is consistent across individualistic and collectivistic cultures, though moderated by cultural context.
- **Whether the wellbeing cost is gendered**: Krämer et al. 2026 found that men and women *who remain single* experience similar wellbeing trajectories (the within-person trajectory is similar), even though men are disproportionately selected into consistent singlehood. This complicates the "singlehood is worse for men" story — men may be more distressed by singlehood, but once controlling for selection, the trajectory effect is similar.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **Direct measurement of "involuntary" singlehood in population samples.** Most studies measure singlehood (unpartnered status) or desire for a partner, not specifically "involuntary" singlehood as a construct. The Wemrell Sweden study is one of the few population-level attempts to directly assess involuntary singlehood, and results were only available as a conference abstract.

2. **The definition problem is under-studied.** Cuthbert 2023 raises but does not fully resolve the question of whether "involuntary" applies symmetrically to men and women — women's sexual abstinence often involves avoiding unsafe situations, which is "voluntary" in a technical sense but constrained by structural violence. No study has directly compared men's and women's attributions for their own singlehood (did they frame it as choice, circumstance, or failure?) in representative samples.

3. **The sexual orientation correction.** The LGB-identification gender gap (women ~3x more likely to identify bisexual in Gen Z) is a plausible partial explanation for the young-adult singlehood gap, but no study has precisely quantified its contribution to the overall gap in unpartnered rates. The 46% upper-bound estimate from Kofke-Egger is a rough calculation, not a formal analysis.

4. **Longitudinal tracking of the involuntary-to-voluntary transition.** Some men who start as involuntarily single may shift to preferring singlehood over time (and vice versa). No longitudinal data tracks this transition in representative samples.

5. **Race and class interactions.** The US data shows Black adults (61% unpartnered) face much higher rates of singlehood than white adults (38%), driven largely by structural factors. The gender asymmetry within racial groups is under-studied.

6. **The "male standards" question is rarely asked.** Research almost uniformly examines women's elevated standards for partners. Men's standards (especially in the context of online dating, where men report high rejection rates) are rarely empirically examined with equivalent rigor. Are men's stated standards vs. revealed partner preferences contributing to their singlehood? Some incel research touches this but is qualitative and non-representative.

### Surprises

1. **The overall singlehood rate is higher for women than men** when measured by ACS data across all ages (44% vs. 40%). The "men are more single" narrative is specific to young adulthood; it reverses sharply after 40, where women's widowhood and lower remarriage rates dominate.

2. **College-educated women's marriage rates are not declining** in the US (Chambers et al. 2025). The singlehood crisis is concentrated in the non-college population on both sides — primarily non-college men's collapsed economic prospects drive it, affecting non-college women's marriage rates as a knock-on effect.

3. **Higher education predicts singlehood for both genders** (Kowal 2025), not just for women. This cuts against the simple "highly educated women can't find partners" narrative. More educated people are more likely to be single cross-nationally, suggesting educational expansion itself promotes singlehood (via later formation, higher standards, greater economic independence).

4. **Sweden's high gender equality does not reduce the male singlehood asymmetry** (Wemrell 2024). If the gap were purely about traditional gender norms (women's dependence on men), it should be smaller in Scandinavia. Its persistence in egalitarian societies suggests something more fundamental — perhaps reflecting genuine differences in relationship motivation or mating psychology across genders, rather than structural coercion.

5. **The cohort finding in Bozick 2021** is striking: there is no secular trend in male sexual inactivity in the US when properly modeled, but the specific 2000-2004 birth cohort (Gen Z early) shows markedly higher inactivity. This is not a generation-wide male problem but a very specific cohort of young men whose economic precarity (unemployment, living with parents) is the key predictor.

## Summary Assessment

The gender asymmetry in involuntary singlehood is real, but the evidence does not support a single clean explanation. The most defensible position after this survey:

- **The measurement gap is real but smaller than headline figures suggest.** The 29-point Pew gap shrinks to roughly 10 points when more objective measures are used and age/LGB effects are controlled.
- **The subjective gap (men experiencing singlehood as more unwanted) is robustly real.** This holds cross-nationally and across age groups in Western countries and East Asia.
- **The structural explanation is primarily about economic class, not gender per se.** Non-college men's deteriorating economic position (not women's hypergamy broadly) is the driver of widening singlehood gaps in the US.
- **Hypergamy persists as a partner preference pattern** (women consistently prioritize partner economic resources more) but its practical consequences for singlehood depend on labor market conditions for men. In high-inequality economies with weak male non-college labor markets, it becomes acute.
- **Cross-cultural comparisons challenge simple structural accounts**: both high-equality countries (Sweden, Japan) and traditional countries (South Korea, Turkey) show the same asymmetry, suggesting some cross-cultural regularity in male-female differences in relationship motivation/desire, not just structural mismatch.
- **The feminist critique (Cuthbert 2023)** is an important corrective: women's singlehood and celibacy are under-theorized, and the category "involuntary" imposes a male-centric framework on a diverse range of women's experiences including safety-motivated abstinence.
