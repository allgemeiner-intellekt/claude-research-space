## Per-Question Evaluation

### Q1: Mating market failure modes (demand/supply/coordination)

- **Coverage**: Excellent after the deep dives. The supply-side story (non-college male economic deterioration) is now supported by precise magnitudes from Autor et al. (14% of marriage decline attributable to trade shocks alone) and Chambers et al. (51.4% reduction in the education marriage gap in high-employment areas). The coordination-failure dimension remains theoretically flagged but empirically unresolved -- this is a gap in the literature, not in the research.
- **Strengths**: The "double depletion" mechanism from the class stratification deep dive is the single most analytically sharp finding in the entire body of notes. Non-college women face simultaneous losses from (a) absolute male economic decline and (b) selective upward poaching of the best non-college men by college-educated women. The pool of viable-and-available non-college men fell from 72.9% to 35.3%. This is a market structure argument with real numbers.
- **Gaps**: The coordination equilibrium question (is the shift from "marriage is universal" to "marriage is optional" self-reinforcing?) remains unresolved. Kearney & Wilson's fracking evidence -- positive labor shocks raise fertility but NOT marriage -- is the closest empirical evidence for irreversibility, and it is suggestive but limited to one episode. This is worth noting in the report as an open question but does not need further investigation.

### Q2: How the economic structure of partnership changed

- **Coverage**: Strong from the initial survey. The Becker-to-Cherlin-to-Rosenfeld arc is complete. The Rosenfeld HCMST data on institutional decline of meeting venues is a powerful empirical anchor.
- **Strengths**: The three-phase periodization (production unit, companionate, capstone) is historically grounded and well-sourced. The finding that traditional meeting institutions began declining in the 1940s is genuinely surprising and well-documented.
- **Gaps**: No significant gaps remaining. The intermediary quality difference (vetted community members vs. strangers) is addressed indirectly through the Dallas Fed deep dive's treatment of information degradation. This is sufficient for the report.

### Q3: What dating technology actually does to matching outcomes

- **Coverage**: Excellent after the Dallas Fed paradox deep dive. The resolution -- apps changed nothing about who matches with whom but reduced how many people match at all -- is convincingly argued with the evaluation-mode mechanism from Finkel et al. (2012) providing the psychological grounding.
- **Strengths**: The deep dive's hierarchy of mechanisms (evaluation mode shift > information degradation > platform incentive misalignment > outside option effect > choice overload) is well-calibrated to the evidence. The finding that simple choice overload is NOT well-supported in dating contexts (Hu & Markowitz 2026) is a valuable corrective to popular narratives. The desktop-vs-mobile era distinction from Ershov et al. is analytically productive.
- **Gaps**: No peer-reviewed Gini coefficient exists for dating app match distributions. The SwipeStats data (7,079 profiles, 294M swipes) provides the strongest available numbers but is self-selected and non-academic. This is a limitation to acknowledge, not a gap to fill.

### Q4: Gendered asymmetry in involuntary singlehood

- **Coverage**: Very strong from the initial survey. The three-layer finding (measurement gap smaller than headlines; subjective gap real; structural explanation is class-based) is well-supported.
- **Strengths**: The careful decomposition of the Pew headline figure is exactly the kind of critical analysis the report needs. The cross-national evidence (Sweden, South Korea, Germany) prevents US-centrism. The Cuthbert feminist critique adds important nuance.
- **Gaps**: The Sweden puzzle (high equality, persistent male singlehood asymmetry) remains unresolved. The Kalmijn deep dive partially addresses this -- egalitarian welfare states may actually widen the educational gradient in partnership for men by shifting the relevant criteria from income to cultural/domestic traits. This is an important finding that should be discussed in the report. No further investigation needed.

### Q5: Second-order social consequences of mass singlehood

- **Coverage**: Now very strong on the South Korea case and adequate on the other threads. The deep dive on South Korea is the strongest single piece of research in the entire set of notes.
- **Strengths**: The South Korea causal chain is documented with remarkable precision: economic insecurity (Fleckenstein, Matsuda) -> marriage collapse (Statistics Korea, Hwang) -> gendered political polarization (Kim 2023, Lee 2024, Mo & You 2025) -> feedback loop through women's withdrawal (Lee/Song/Park 2025). Kim's finding that grievance-based antifeminism actually suppresses male marriage interest is the essay's most counterintuitive insight. The $280 billion policy failure is a powerful anchor for the consequences discussion.
- **Gaps**: The wealth concentration / "heirs are missing" thread remains thinly sourced (journalism only). The social trust / civic participation thread is implied but not directly researched. The Western political radicalization comparison (is the Korean pattern appearing in the US/Europe?) is gestured at but not empirically established. None of these gaps is severe enough to warrant another round of investigation. The South Korea case is strong enough to anchor the consequences section on its own, and the report can note these other threads as emerging rather than established.

## Tensions Worth Resolving in the Report (Not Via Further Research)

Three tensions emerged across the notes that the orchestrator should address directly in writing, rather than through additional investigation:

1. **The recovery asymmetry**: Negative labor shocks reduce marriage (Autor et al., strong causal evidence), but positive labor shocks do not restore it (Kearney & Wilson, fracking boom). This suggests a ratchet effect -- the cultural/normative shift is not easily reversible. The report should engage with this directly rather than leaving it as an open question.

2. **The Scandinavian paradox**: Kalmijn (2013) shows that egalitarian welfare states widen, not narrow, the educational gradient in male partnership. This complicates any simple policy narrative about "fixing" the marriage crisis through redistribution. The mechanism (criteria shift from economic to cultural/domestic) is important and should be woven into the argument rather than just mentioned.

3. **The Kim paradox in South Korea**: Grievance-based antifeminism suppresses men's own marriage interest. The political mobilization of male singlehood grievance accelerates the crisis it claims to address. This is the most counterintuitive finding in the entire research and deserves prominent placement in the essay.

These are claims backed by evidence, not just survey findings stitched together.
