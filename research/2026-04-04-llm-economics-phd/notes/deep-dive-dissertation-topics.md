# Deep Dive: Concrete Dissertation Topics for a Non-Technical Theorist

## Question

For each: what is the research question, what methods would be used, who could advise it, what is the technical ceiling, where would chapters be published, and does it have durable relevance if LLM capabilities change dramatically by 2030?

**Key Pattern from This Evidence**

Across all programs, the most successful non-empirical dissertations are:
1. Normative-theoretical with a clear connection to policy (Mazor at HKS)
2. Historical-comparative with careful empirical grounding (several development economics dissertations)

What succeeds: a dissertation that takes a real institutional or normative question seriously, uses whatever method fits (comparative case analysis, simple game-theoretic models, welfare economics frameworks), and engages the economic literature enough to be legible to economists.

What does not succeed: a purely discursive essay with no formal structure or testable claims. Even the HKS political science dissertations use formal models (Robinson, Bates-school), natural experiments, or structured comparative methods.

### Assessment of What Methods Are Actually Required

**The technical floor for an economics PhD program:**
UVA's own admissions page says: applicants need "strong math skills, including a working knowledge of calculus and algebra" with courses in "advanced calculus, linear algebra, intermediate microeconomics, intermediate macroeconomics, real analysis, differential equations, and either mathematical statistics or econometrics" increasing chances. "Most admitted students have GRE quantitative scores near or above the 90th percentile."

This is a real barrier. But it is a barrier to *admission*, not necessarily to dissertation quality. A student who passes the qualifying sequence (typically in year 1-2) can then work on questions that require less mathematical virtuosity than the frontier macro or econometrics work, provided they are contributing theoretically or institutionally.

The minimum mathematical floor for the dissertation topics proposed here ranges from:
- **Low**: Welfare economics, comparative institutional analysis, and political economy of governance can be done with intermediate microeconomics (constrained optimization, game theory at introductory level) and statistical literacy, not frontier econometrics
- **Medium**: Theory of the firm with AI agents requires some formal modeling — simple contract theory, transaction cost economics with algebra
- **Higher but manageable**: Functional distribution theory requires comfort with Sraffa-style price equations (linear algebra over calculus) or basic growth model structure

**Program-specific calibration:**
- UVA Economics (Korinek) is a 30-something ranked program that is competitive but not top-5. It might admit a non-standard candidate with strong research experience, a compelling statement, and GRE quant around 165 (90th percentile).
- Rotman Strategic Management PhD has lower formal math requirements. It does not require econometrics as its core method — it accommodates qualitative and theoretical work in strategy.
- HKS PEG government track is explicitly designed for students whose primary training is political economy and government, not pure math economics. It uses formal models but at a lower intensity than a standard economics PhD.
- MIT Sloan TIES would likely require empirical work as a central component.

## The Five Proposed Dissertation Topics

### Topic 1: The Epistemology of AI-Mediated Economic Research

**Research Question**

When LLMs mediate the research process in economics — generating hypotheses, running simulations, producing synthetic respondents, drafting proofs — what changes about what economics can and cannot claim to know? This dissertation would argue that Korinek's JEL paper on "Generative AI for Economic Research" describes a technological transformation without examining its epistemological implications, and would develop a framework for understanding what LLM-mediated research can and cannot establish.

Specific sub-questions:
- When an LLM generates a synthetic respondent population for a survey, what kind of evidence does the resulting data constitute? What are its truth conditions?
- When an LLM helps derive a mathematical result, does the result have the same epistemic status as a human-derived proof? What does this mean for replicability?
- Korinek's JEL paper notes that LLMs can produce "approximately correct" outputs — how does this transform the field's error-correction mechanisms?

**Methods**

Primarily philosophy of science applied to economics methodology, with case studies of specific AI-assisted research outputs. Uses van Fraassen's constructive empiricism and the contrast class framework (which the user already knows deeply from the Why-Question Violence research) to analyze what claims are made when AI mediates the epistemic process. The dissertation does not require econometrics. It requires: (a) careful reading of the economics methodology literature (Hausman, Blaug, Cartwright, Reiss), (b) case analysis of 4-5 specific AI-assisted economics papers, (c) philosophy of science applied to social science (Kincaid, Rosenberg).

Math requirement: essentially none beyond understanding what formal models are doing at the level of intermediate micro. This is philosophy of economics, not economics.

**Who Could Advise**

Anton Korinek (UVA) is the author of the target JEL paper and would be the ideal advisor — this dissertation directly extends and critiques work he has already done. He has co-authored in the Oxford Handbook of AI Governance, showing comfort with normative-philosophical work. A co-advisor from philosophy of science (UVA has a strong philosophy department with philosophy of social science faculty) would be appropriate.

Alternatively: HKS government track, where Jeffry Frieden or Torben Iversen supervise methodological dissertations; or Oxford GPI, whose November 2024 research agenda explicitly includes epistemology.

**Technical Ceiling**

Low. No econometrics required. Philosophy of science with economics methodology literacy is sufficient. The dissertation might include a simple formalization of the "synthetic respondent" problem using basic decision theory (Savage-style) — this is a one-chapter addition that requires only probability theory, not real analysis.

**Publication Targets**

- Journal of Economic Methodology (the field's home journal for this kind of work)
- Philosophy of Science or Economics and Philosophy (if arguments are rigorous enough)
- Journal of Economic Literature (survey/methodology pieces)
- NBER working paper series (Korinek connection provides entry)
- Oxford Handbook of AI Governance chapters are already in this vein

**Durability**

Very high. If LLM capabilities change dramatically by 2030, this dissertation becomes *more* important, not less. A dissertation written in 2026-2030 about the epistemology of AI-assisted research would be read as foundational work precisely when the field is trying to understand what happened to its knowledge-production practices. The questions here are durable because they are about the logic of inference, not the state of technology.

**Competitive Advantage for Non-Technical Person**

A technically strong economist who uses AI as a research tool is unlikely to step back and ask whether this changes the epistemic status of their findings. The philosophy of science literature that this dissertation requires — van Fraassen, Hausman, Cartwright, Reiss — is almost unknown inside economics departments. The user's existing facility with van Fraassen's framework (demonstrated in the Why-Question Violence research) is a genuine head start. Technical economists would need to learn a foreign literature; this user already knows it.

**Realistic Assessment**

This is the most feasible dissertation on the list for a non-technical entrant. It is also potentially the most impactful in the long run, because it addresses a blind spot that will only grow as AI tools become ubiquitous in economics. The risk is that it may be seen as "too philosophical" by hiring committees at pure economics departments — placement would need to target economic methodology, science studies, or interdisciplinary programs.

### Topic 2: The Social Alignment Problem as a Welfare Economics Problem

**Research Question**

Korinek and Balwit's "Aligned with Whom?" (2022, Oxford Handbook of AI Governance) identifies a fundamental gap: the AI alignment community asks whether AI pursues its operator's goals, but the welfare economics question is whether AI systems optimize for anything that corresponds to social welfare. This dissertation would develop a formal welfare economics framework for what it would mean for an AI system to be "socially aligned" — drawing on Arrow's social choice theory, Amartya Sen's capability approach, and the existing welfare economics of information economics.

The central argument: the standard framing of AI alignment (minimize the gap between AI behavior and human preferences) is vulnerable to Arrow's impossibility theorem and its extensions — there is no social preference relation that satisfies all desiderata simultaneously. A dissertation building this argument would:
1. Show formally why naive Paretian/utilitarian alignment criteria fail for AI systems operating in multi-agent environments
2. Apply the capability approach (Sen/Nussbaum) to AI systems as a partial resolution to Arrow's impossibility
3. Examine what new measurement instruments (beyond GDP, consumer surplus) would be needed to evaluate AI systems' effects on human flourishing

This connects directly to Brynjolfsson's explicit call in the Stanford DEL postdoc posting for "welfare metrics that account for AI's impact on human flourishing."

**Methods**

Formal welfare economics at an intermediate level — Arrow's theorem and its proofs, Rawlsian analysis, Sen's capability approach literature. This is formal theory, but it is mathematical economics at the level of AER:Insights or the Oxford Handbook, not Econometrica. The math is: binary relations, preference orderings, simple proofs by contradiction. No stochastic calculus, no econometrics.

Empirically, the dissertation could include structured case analysis of 2-3 AI systems (e.g., a recommendation algorithm, a labor screening tool, a healthcare diagnostic) and apply the welfare framework to assess whether these systems can be evaluated under standard metrics or require new ones.

**Who Could Advise**

Anton Korinek (UVA) is a co-author of the seed paper. For the Sen/capability approach dimension: Amartya Sen is at Harvard (emeritus but still active); Sabina Alkire (Oxford HDRO, capability approach leader) would be ideal for the theoretical grounding. A committee combining Korinek (UVA Economics) with a philosopher working on well-being or a welfare economist would be strong.

Alternative: HKS, where faculty like Esther Duflo (departures but spirit remains), Stefanie Stantcheva (distributional welfare), or international development faculty work on related welfare theory questions. The 2024 HKS graduate "Clara Sievert" wrote on "economics of culture and health" under Nathan Nunn — adjacent terrain.

**Technical Ceiling**

Moderate. Arrow's theorem requires formal logic and mathematical proof — but it is taught in intermediate graduate microeconomics and is well within reach of someone who has done real analysis. No econometrics required. The main intellectual challenge is developing a coherent synthesis of welfare economics, social choice theory, and the capability approach — which is a philosophical challenge, not a mathematical one.

**Publication Targets**

- Journal of Economic Literature (review-style synthesis of welfare metrics for AI)
- AER:Insights (shorter theory papers — Arrow-style impossibility result applied to AI alignment)
- Economics and Philosophy (for the capability approach dimension)
- Oxford Handbook of AI Governance (Korinek edits/contributes, likely receptive to follow-up chapters)
- Journal of Human Development and Capabilities (capability approach community)

**Durability**

Very high. If AI capabilities change dramatically, the question of whether existing welfare metrics are adequate becomes more urgent, not less. Arrow's impossibility theorem doesn't expire. The capability approach to welfare has been applied across development economics for 40 years and is not AI-specific. This dissertation would still matter in 2040.

**Competitive Advantage for Non-Technical Person**

The intersection of welfare economics, social choice theory, and AI alignment requires reading in four literatures that are almost never read together: welfare economics (standard graduate courses), social choice theory (usually taught to formal economists), capability approach (development economics, typically not taught in AI alignment courses), and AI governance (technical literature). A technical person is unlikely to be fluent in Sen/Nussbaum. A person with philosophy of science and cross-disciplinary background is better positioned to synthesize. The Hayekian knowledge problem background (from the user's research) also connects here: the epistemological question of what AI systems can "know" about preferences is adjacent to Arrow's impossibility and to Hayek's dispersion-of-knowledge argument.

**Realistic Assessment**

This is the dissertation most likely to produce work that the field's leaders would describe as exactly what they called for. Brynjolfsson's explicit articulation of "welfare metrics for human flourishing" as an open research priority, combined with Korinek's "Aligned with Whom?" as the seed paper, means there is a clear intellectual lineage and an obvious advisor. The risk: welfare theory can become too philosophical for economics departments. The dissertation needs at least one chapter with formal results (even simple ones) to be placed by an economics department.

### Topic 3: The Political Economy of Regulatory Capture in AI Markets

**Research Question**

Why do AI companies shape the regulations that govern them, and what are the economic mechanisms that enable this? This dissertation would develop a political economy theory of regulatory capture specific to AI markets, arguing that the standard Stigler-Peltzman framework of regulatory capture is insufficient to explain AI regulation because AI markets have structural properties — extreme returns to scale, information asymmetries about risk, dual-use characteristics — that generate distinctive capture mechanisms not present in earlier technology regulation.

The central claim: AI companies can engage in what the dissertation would call "safety-washing regulatory capture" — voluntarily adopting safety standards and self-regulatory frameworks that appear to constrain the industry but are designed to raise barriers to entry, foreclose competitors, and entrench incumbent models. This is distinct from classic regulatory capture (just lobbying for favorable rules) because it uses safety discourse to achieve competitive ends, making it harder to identify and harder to contest.

Specific sub-questions:
1. What mechanisms does the standard Stiglerian model of regulatory capture miss when applied to AI?
2. How do the EU AI Act, the US AI Safety Institute, and voluntary model cards/safety agreements compare as regulatory frameworks from a capture theory perspective?
3. Do historically similar regulatory moments (nuclear power, pharmaceuticals, financial instruments) predict the AI outcome? What theory of path dependence explains divergence?

**Methods**

Comparative institutional analysis — the core methodology of political economy — combined with process tracing through three historical case studies (nuclear energy regulation, pharmaceutical FDA capture, financial derivatives exemptions) and three contemporary AI governance cases (EU AI Act, US NIST/AISI framework, voluntary frontier AI commitments). No econometrics required. The formal grounding would be a simple Stigler-Peltzman model extended to handle information asymmetry and scale dynamics — this is basic game theory notation, not frontier mechanism design.

This methodology is exactly what Acemoglu and Johnson use in "Power and Progress" — historically grounded, institutionally comparative, theory-informed, with minimal formal machinery.

**Who Could Advise**

Anton Korinek (UVA) with his Korinek/Vipra paper on market concentration as the economic IO background. For the political science dimension: MIT's Simon Johnson (co-author of "Power and Progress," MIT Sloan, explicitly interested in power and technological change) would be an excellent co-advisor. Or Daron Acemoglu himself, whose research on regulatory change and institutions in "Power and Progress" and "Regulating Transformative Technologies" is directly relevant.

Alternatively, HKS government track, where Jeffry Frieden advises political economy dissertations, and where the adjacent question of international regulation of technology has multiple faculty. The 2022 HKS graduate Michael-David Mangini's dissertation "How Commerce Becomes Compromised: Economic Coercion and Credibility in the Age of the Global Economy" (advisor Jeffry Frieden) is a close structural analog.

**Technical Ceiling**

Low to moderate. Comparative institutional analysis is the core method of this dissertation — it requires no econometrics. The formal extension of Stigler-Peltzman uses basic optimization and informal game theory. The quantitative component might involve systematic coding of regulatory texts and lobbying disclosures — this is content analysis, not econometrics. GRE quantitative requirement: probably 155-160 range would be sufficient for HKS government track or MIT Sloan.

**Publication Targets**

- Economic Policy (where Korinek/Vipra on market concentration was published — directly adjacent)
- Journal of Law, Economics, and Organization (regulatory capture literature)
- Annual Review of Economics (review-style piece on AI governance economics)
- Journal of Democracy or Journal of Political Economy (for the democracy/institutional dimension)
- NBER working paper series
- Books: This dissertation has book potential — an Acemoglu/Johnson "Power and Progress"-style argument about AI regulatory capture would be publishable at a university press

**Durability**

High. If AI capabilities change dramatically by 2030, the regulatory capture dynamics become more consequential, not less. The theoretical framework — how do incumbent firms shape regulations that appear neutral but serve their competitive interests — is durable political economy, independent of any particular AI capability level. The three-case historical comparison provides foundations that don't expire.

**Competitive Advantage for Non-Technical Person**

A technically oriented economist studying AI regulation would likely focus on the efficiency dimension: does the regulation maximize welfare? The political economy question — who shapes regulation, through what mechanisms, in whose interest — requires reading Stigler, Peltzman, Olson, and the regulatory capture literature that most AI economists don't engage. It also requires historical analysis (nuclear, pharma, financial derivatives) that economists rarely do. A person with genuine cross-disciplinary breadth — understanding both the economics and the political science of regulatory institutions — has a real edge. The user's research on classical political economy (power, institutions, the political roots of economic outcomes) connects directly here.

**Realistic Assessment**

This is probably the most practical dissertation in terms of placement because the political economy of technology is already an established and growing subfield (Acemoglu/Johnson, Beraja/Yuchtman at MIT, multiple NBER AI conference presentations). It's also the topic where the user's prior background creates the largest competitive advantage. The main risk is that it could become a descriptive exercise rather than a theoretically original one — the distinction between "surveying the AI governance landscape" and "developing a new theory of capture mechanisms" matters enormously for economics placement.

### Topic 4: Revisiting the Coasian Firm in the Age of AI Agents

**Research Question**

Ronald Coase argued in "The Nature of the Firm" (1937) that firms exist because internal direction is cheaper than market contracting when transaction costs are high. His framework predicts that reductions in transaction costs should reduce firm size. If AI agents can negotiate contracts, monitor performance, and coordinate work at near-zero marginal cost, what happens to firm boundaries? More specifically: when AI agents can *act as counterparties* — not just assist human contracting parties — does the Coasian framework need theoretical revision, or does it predict this outcome correctly?

The dissertation would argue that AI agents introduce a qualitative change that Coasian theory does not handle: AI agents are economic actors whose preferences and authority depend on design choices made by principals, not by the agents themselves. This creates an endogenous boundary problem — the same party that controls firm boundaries also controls the agents that execute across those boundaries. Standard Coasian analysis treats the agent/market distinction as categorical; AI blurs it.

Specific sub-questions:
1. What does the "headless firm" (Klein/Wieczorek 2026 arXiv) mean for Coasian prediction about firm size distributions?
2. How does the Williamson/Hart-Moore incomplete contracts framework need to be modified when one party to a contract is an AI agent whose objectives are determined by a third party (the AI developer)?
3. What are the antitrust implications of AI agency — if AI agents are economic actors, should market concentration be measured at the level of AI systems, not just human firms?

**Methods**

Theoretical — contract theory at an intermediate level, using the framework of Grossman-Hart-Moore property rights theory, supplemented with case analysis of actual AI agent deployments (Anthropic's Claude agents, OpenAI operator/assistant frameworks, agentic procurement systems). The formal modeling requires basic algebra and optimization — the Hart-Moore framework uses simple surplus-splitting logic, not advanced stochastic calculus.

This dissertation connects directly to the Coasean Singularity paper (SSRN 2025) and the Headless Firm paper (arXiv 2026) as the existing nascent literature, but positions itself as the economic theory paper that those more speculative pieces call for.

**Who Could Advise**

Primary: Joshua Gans (Rotman Toronto) — Gans works on information economics, innovation, and platform economics. He is a co-author of the "Prediction Machines" framework and has the combination of industrial organization theory background and AI economics interest to supervise this topic. Ajay Agrawal and Avi Goldfarb (also Rotman) complete the committee.

Alternative: For the contract theory dimension, any faculty member who works in property rights theory / incomplete contracts would be appropriate — Chicago, MIT, Harvard all have strong contract theory groups. The Rotman Strategic Management PhD, where Gans is faculty, would accommodate this dissertation with lower formal math requirements than an MIT or Chicago Economics PhD.

**Technical Ceiling**

Moderate. The Hart-Moore framework requires comfort with mathematical reasoning at the level of intermediate graduate theory (optimization, basic algebra, informal proofs). No real analysis or stochastic calculus required. The core formal contribution is a modification of the make-or-buy decision to incorporate the principal-agent relationship with AI developers — this is a 10-page appendix, not a full formal modeling exercise.

**Publication Targets**

- Management Science or Strategic Management Journal (business school publication in the platform economics / technology strategy space)
- RAND Journal of Economics (IO theory with applications)
- AER:Insights (for the formal economic theory result)
- Journal of Economics and Management Strategy
- Books / practitioner outlets: Gans has a track record of producing academic research with practitioner appeal (Prediction Machines was an HBR Press book)

**Durability**

Moderate to high. If AI agents become dramatically more capable by 2030, the question of firm boundaries becomes more important. The theoretical framework — when does Coasian logic break, and how do we modify it — will be increasingly relevant as AI agents handle more economic coordination. Some risk: if a technical economist writes the definitive theory paper on this before 2030, the dissertation's contribution may be partially preempted. The window of opportunity is now.

**Competitive Advantage for Non-Technical Person**

The Coasian firm literature is classical economics — it's older theory that most AI economists don't engage with deeply. A person who knows classical political economy (the user has clearly read deeply in this tradition) and can connect Coase and Williamson to the AI agent context without needing frontier ML knowledge has a real advantage. The question "what changes about the theory of the firm?" is a classical economics question that can be answered without technical AI knowledge, precisely because the answer lies in the institutional economics framework, not in the technology itself.

**Realistic Assessment**

This dissertation is probably the most technically accessible from a business school perspective, and Rotman is a strong placement target that already has the relevant advisor cluster. The risk is that the formal modeling is currently ahead of its time — the "headless firm" literature is mostly arXiv preprints and practice-oriented writing, not peer-reviewed economics. This could be an advantage (being first) or a disadvantage (no established literature to situate the dissertation in). The strongest version of this dissertation spends substantial time on the theoretical grounding (Coase, Williamson, Grossman-Hart-Moore) before advancing the AI extension — this makes it legible to economists even if the AI literature is nascent.

### Topic 5: What the Calculation Debate Actually Shows — A Formal Analysis of the Hayekian Knowledge Problem with LLMs

**Research Question**

The socialist calculation debate (Mises, Hayek, Lange, Lerner) is one of the deepest unresolved problems in economic theory: does a central planner with enough information about preferences and technology produce outcomes equivalent to a market? Lange's answer was yes in principle (you can compute general equilibrium); Hayek's response was no, but on epistemological grounds: prices don't just transmit information, they discover and create it through the ongoing process of exchange. LLMs pose a new version of this challenge: can a model trained on all market data and economic text reproduce the informational function of prices?

This dissertation would do what neither Kiesling (whose Substack piece identifies the gap) nor the Research Square preprint ("The Algorithmic Calculation Problem," February 2026) has successfully done: formally characterize the distinction between data aggregation and the Hayekian market process, derive the conditions under which LLMs can and cannot substitute for market prices, and show what this implies for the long-standing debate about market socialism and central planning.

The central claim: the Lange-Hayek debate has a precise formal resolution that prior literature missed, and LLMs expose it. Hayek's argument is not that computation is impossible but that prices encode *counterfactual* information — what would have been traded under different conditions — that can only be generated through actual exchange processes. LLMs trained on historical data can approximate revealed preferences but cannot generate the forward-looking, counterfactual-sensitive signals that make prices informationally dense. This is a formal claim about information content, not a philosophical gesture.

Sub-questions:
1. Can the Mises/Hayek distinction between "computation" and "economic calculation" be formalized using information theory (Shannon entropy, Kolmogorov complexity)?
2. What exactly does an LLM learn when it trains on price data, and is what it learns the same thing as what prices encode? (This requires engaging with Kiesling's argument about the cognitive dimension of economic calculation.)
3. Under what conditions could an LLM-mediated allocation mechanism replicate market allocation? What are the minimal conditions for failure?

**Methods**

This is formal theory with philosophical grounding. The methods would combine:
- Information-theoretic formalization (Shannon entropy applied to price signals — requires probability theory, not statistics)
- Conceptual analysis of the knowledge problem literature (Hayek, Mises, Boettke, Lavoie, Kiesling)
- A simple two-sided market model comparing price-mediated allocation with LLM-mediated allocation under different information structures

The math required: probability theory and basic information theory (Shannon entropy is a formula involving logarithms and probabilities), simple game theory, and familiarity with general equilibrium concepts at the intermediate level. This is at the edge of what a non-technical person with good mathematical preparation could do — it requires more formal machinery than the other topics, but less than frontier macro or econometrics.

**Who Could Advise**

This is the hardest dissertation on the list to find advising for, precisely because it requires someone who takes the Austrian economics tradition seriously enough to engage with it, while being rigorous enough to require formalization. Potential advisors:
- Anton Korinek (UVA) is the best fit — he is explicitly interested in what AI means for economic theory, and the knowledge problem is one of the questions his research agenda flags
- Peter Boettke (George Mason University) is the leading Austrian economics scholar in the US and would bring deep familiarity with the Mises-Hayek debate, but GMU has a different institutional position than the programs identified
- Anyone working at the intersection of information economics and mechanism design who finds the knowledge problem formalization project interesting

The challenge: this is the most intellectually original project on the list but also the one with the least obvious advisor fit at the top programs. It might work best as a dissertation written at UVA with Korinek as advisor, potentially with a George Mason economist or a philosopher of economics as committee member.

**Technical Ceiling**

High-moderate. The information-theoretic formalization requires real mathematical work, but it is information theory, not econometrics or stochastic calculus. The formal results can be presented in a few pages of appendix notation. The main challenge is conceptual: getting the Hayek-Mises distinction right philosophically before attempting to formalize it. The user's existing work on the philosophy of economic calculation (the Water Theory of Value research shows deep familiarity with value-form theory, Sraffian price theory, and the philosophy of classical political economy) is excellent preparation for this.

**Publication Targets**

- Review of Austrian Economics (the field journal for this tradition — less prestigious but the right venue for the calculation debate component)
- Journal of Economic Literature (if a synthesis of the calculation debate + LLMs is framed as a review)
- Economic Policy (for the policy implications chapter on market socialism and AI planning)
- Philosophy and Economics journals
- NBER working paper (if Korinek is involved)
- This topic is also potentially the one that could be developed into a book for a general/academic audience, given the inherent drama of the calculation debate

**Durability**

Very high. The socialist calculation debate has been running for 100 years. This dissertation would give it a new form — does AI rehabilitate market socialism? — that will be relevant as long as AI continues to improve. Even if LLMs by 2030 are dramatically more capable, the formal question about whether they can substitute for market prices is still open and still matters.

**Competitive Advantage for Non-Technical Person**

The Austrian economics tradition and its philosophical grounding — Mises on economic calculation, Hayek on the knowledge problem, Boettke on the institutional economics of socialist calculation — is almost entirely unknown to mainstream economics PhD students. A person who already thinks in terms of the philosophy of social science, who has read value-form theory and price theory in the classical tradition (as the Water Theory of Value research shows), is in a much better position to engage the Mises-Hayek argument seriously than a technical economist who knows mechanism design but has never read "The Use of Knowledge in Society."

**Realistic Assessment**

This is the most intellectually ambitious topic and the one with the most uncertain placement prospects. It sits between disciplines in a way that could either make it uniquely valuable (original synthesis no one else could have written) or difficult to evaluate (hiring committees won't know which pile to put it in). The best placement scenario would be a general economics department or political economy program that values theoretical breadth. It might be more realistic as one chapter of a broader dissertation rather than the central organizing question — for example, a dissertation with one chapter on the Hayek-LLM problem, one on welfare metrics (Topic 2), and one on market structure implications.

## Key Findings

- The HKS PEG government track demonstrates that normative and theoretical dissertations are viable, but most graduates do empirical work (strong evidence). Outliers like Mazor (normative political philosophy) and Sandbu (preference theory) exist and place in good positions, but are exceptions.

- Rotman Strategic Management PhD is the most hospitable for a non-empirical, theory-focused AI economics dissertation. The Agrawal/Gans/Goldfarb advisor cluster is present, formal math requirements are lower than economics departments, and the Strategic Management field accommodates qualitative-theoretical work.

- UVA Economics is the strongest home for Topics 1, 2, and 5 (the most economics-adjacent work), given Korinek's explicit interest in governance, welfare metrics, and the epistemology of AI research. UVA admits students with GRE quant at or above 90th percentile and expects a math coursework record — real analysis, linear algebra, calculus.

- Topics 3 and 4 (regulatory capture and theory of the firm) could work in business school programs (Rotman, MIT Sloan) with lower formal math requirements.

- Topic 5 (Hayek-LLM knowledge problem) is the most intellectually original and the most difficult to place — it sits in an under-institutionalized space between Austrian economics, information theory, and AI governance.

- The field's leaders (Brynjolfsson, Korinek, Agrawal) are explicitly calling for research on welfare metrics, governance, and theoretical foundations — confirming these are not marginal topics (strong evidence, primary sources).

- The Carvalho Oxford working paper "The Political-Economic Risks of AI" (January 2025) is a working paper from an economics department that is explicitly non-empirical, uses minimal formal modeling, and addresses political economy of democratic stability under AI. This is a real-world existence proof that such work is publishable in economics. (moderate evidence — one example)

- Math floor is real. UVA requires real analysis and expects 90th+ percentile GRE quant. A non-technical person would need 1-2 years of serious preparation to be a competitive applicant at a standard economics PhD program. Business school PhDs (Rotman, MIT Sloan TIES) have lower bars.

## Assessment

The five topics proposed here represent a genuine spectrum of feasibility and ambition. The most feasible (Topic 1, epistemology of AI-mediated research; Topic 3, regulatory capture) require the least formal mathematics and connect most directly to the user's demonstrated background in philosophy of science and classical political economy. The most ambitious (Topic 5, Hayek-LLM formalization) requires more mathematical preparation and has the least certain placement path.

What unifies all five topics is a structural argument about where the user's genuine comparative advantage lies: not in doing empirical economics better than technically trained economists, but in doing something they are unlikely to do at all — namely, taking the field's foundational theoretical assumptions seriously enough to interrogate them. Whether that is the epistemological assumptions of AI-mediated research, the welfare function assumptions of AI alignment, the Coasian assumptions of the theory of the firm, or the information-theoretic assumptions of the market process — all five topics attack a theoretical blind spot that technical economists have, not a gap in their empirical toolkit.

The strongest single recommendation: Topic 2 (welfare metrics / social alignment as welfare economics problem) paired with Topic 3 (regulatory capture in AI markets) as the basis for a three-essay dissertation, with Korinek at UVA as primary advisor. This pairing is mutually reinforcing — the welfare theory chapter grounds the normative framework, and the regulatory capture chapter shows what happens when that framework is absent from governance. Together they answer a coherent question: what does it mean to govern AI in the interest of human flourishing, and why are current institutions failing to do so?

The user's strongest existing assets for a PhD application in this direction: the Water Theory of Value research (demonstrates ability to work in classical political economy at a sophisticated level) and the Why-Question Violence research (demonstrates facility with van Fraassen-style epistemological analysis applied to social science). Both are directly relevant to the proposed dissertation topics and would make for an unusual and compelling statement of purpose.
