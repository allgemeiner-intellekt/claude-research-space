# Deep Dive: Alternative PhD Paths for AI × Economics Research

## Question

What PhD programs outside traditional economics departments could work for a non-technical person who wants to do serious research on the economic and social implications of AI — governance, welfare metrics, political economy of AI — without needing to pass a real analysis qualifying exam?

## Investigation

I investigated four program categories: public policy PhDs, business school PhDs, iSchool PhDs, and interdisciplinary AI/technology programs. What I found reshapes the question significantly. The key division is not "economics vs. everything else" but rather "discipline-trained vs. interdisciplinary." Most serious doctoral programs, even outside economics departments, either require substantial quantitative training or deliver weaker placement outcomes. The exception is a narrow category of programs — primarily iSchools and Oxford's OII — that genuinely accommodate non-technical scholars while preserving academic credibility.

## Program-by-Program Findings

### PUBLIC POLICY PhDs

#### Harvard Kennedy School — PhD in Political Economy and Government (PEG)

**Status: Closed.** As of Fall 2022, HKS stopped admitting students to the PEG program. This is the most important single finding for this category. The program that many people think of when they imagine the ideal hybrid (political economy + government, housed at a policy school, not a pure econ department) no longer exists.

Students are now redirected to:
- The **PhD in Public Policy (PPOL)**, which has four tracks including an Economics track. The Economics track requires Econ 2020a/b (Harvard PhD-level micro), one course in political theory, and two courses in empirical methods — this is essentially a standard economics PhD with policy framing.
- The **PhD in Government** (Harvard GSAS), a political science PhD.

The PPOL Science, Technology and Policy Studies track would be the most relevant surviving path. But all PPOL tracks require multivariable calculus as a prerequisite, and the Economics track requires passing through Harvard PhD microeconomics. **The math floor has risen, not fallen.**

Source: https://hks.harvard.edu/educational-programs/doctoral-programs/phd-political-economy-government
Source: https://hks.harvard.edu/admissions-aid/doctoral-program-admissions/admissions-requirements

#### Princeton SPIA — PhD in Public Affairs

Princeton's SPIA PhD offers two research clusters: Security Studies, and **Science, Technology, and Environmental Policy (STEP)**. The STEP cluster is directly relevant — it focuses on information technology policy, among other areas, and explicitly "helps practitioners develop a deeper understanding of the nature of scientific and technological problems."

Key details:
- **GRE: Optional** for the PhD (required for the MPA)
- **Funding**: Full tuition + stipend for all admitted students, no separate application needed
- Admits ~8 students per year total (4 per cluster), which is extremely small
- Master's degree recommended but not required
- Applications evaluated on "aptitude for courses in economic and quantitative analysis" — the committee reviews transcripts for this

The STEP faculty have joint appointments in science and engineering departments. The admission page notes the committee "evaluates applicants' aptitude for courses in economic and quantitative analysis." This is not an econ PhD, but it's not soft either. Recent STEP dissertations include work on climate, biotech policy, and information technology. It is genuinely interdisciplinary but skews toward natural/physical sciences for most of its faculty. **An AI governance dissertation would be possible but unusual here** — the program is more about environmental and security policy with some IT policy.

Sources: https://spia.princeton.edu/graduate-admissions/phd-public-affairs
https://spia.princeton.edu/graduate-admissions/phd-public-affairs/application

#### Chicago Harris — PhD in Public Policy Studies

Harris does offer a doctoral program (PhD in Public Policy Studies), but I could not retrieve the specific program page. What I found about Harris generally: it is explicitly quantitative and "data-driven" at the master's level, describing itself as applying "rigorous modeling and analysis." Chicago's intellectual culture generally runs harder on quant than peer institutions. The program likely involves heavy econometrics.

Based on the overall Harris culture and reputation, this program almost certainly requires passing through a serious methods sequence. Chicago is not where you go to avoid quantitative training — it is where you go to get more of it.

Source: https://harris.uchicago.edu/admissions/applying-to-harris

#### Assessment of Public Policy PhDs

The most promising surviving path in this category is Princeton SPIA STEP. The HKS PEG program is gone. Chicago Harris is likely to be quant-heavy. Princeton STEP is genuinely interdisciplinary but small (4 spots per year), focused partly on physical sciences and environment, and while the GRE is optional, the committee still looks at quant aptitude.

**Compared to an econ PhD**: Public policy PhDs are generally somewhat less math-intensive than econ, but not dramatically so at top programs. Placement into academic jobs is weaker than from economics departments — most PPOL/public policy PhD graduates go into policy organizations, think tanks, and government rather than tenure-track economics positions. If the goal is academic research on AI governance, that can be fine. If the goal is publishing in top economics journals, these programs are not positioned for that.

### BUSINESS SCHOOL PhDs

#### Toronto Rotman — PhD in Strategic Management (or Economics)

This is the most strategically relevant program given that Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb — the authors of "Prediction Machines" and the intellectual center of the economics-of-AI field — are all at Rotman.

**Admissions requirements:**
- Master's degree (or occasionally exceptional bachelor's with A- average)
- GRE strongly recommended; average admitted quantitative GRE score: **166/170** — this is 94th percentile
- 15-20 students admitted from 600-700 applicants (~2-3% acceptance rate)
- Specializations: Accounting, Business Economics, Finance, Marketing, Operations Management, Organizational Behaviour, **Strategic Management**

**Funding**: Generous by Canadian standards — CAD $40,000/year (years 1 and 5) or $33,000/year (years 2-4) plus full tuition coverage through year 5.

**The math bar**: The average admitted quant GRE of 166 signals that admitted students have strong quantitative backgrounds. The program lists among areas of study in Strategic Management topics like competitive dynamics, platform economics, and technology strategy — all of which involve quantitative methods. For the Economics specialization, the coursework would overlap heavily with the economics PhD.

**Dissertation character**: Rotman Strategic Management dissertations tend to be empirical strategy papers — field experiments, regression discontinuity designs, structural models of industry dynamics. "Essays on the Economics of Algorithms, Markets, and Organizations" (Lindsey Raymond, 2024, placed presumably at a business school) and similar recent MIT TIES titles suggest this is the genre. Less mathematical than a pure theory dissertation in econ, but not qualitative.

**The Agrawal-Gans-Goldfarb factor**: These three are the most important scholars working on the economics of AI and they are accessible at Rotman. They advise students in the Economics and Strategic Management tracks. If you could get admitted and land one of them as an advisor, the intellectual fit would be near-perfect for AI × economics work.

**Assessment**: High math bar at admission (GRE 166 average), but the dissertation is empirical strategy rather than theory or structural econometrics. A strong quant background is required. **A non-technical person could not realistically be admitted without substantial upskilling first.**

Sources: https://www.rotman.utoronto.ca/programs/phd-program/admissions/
https://mitsloan.mit.edu/programs/phd/technological-innovation-entrepreneurship-and-strategic-management (for Rotman student profiling context)

#### MIT Sloan — TIES PhD (Technological Innovation, Entrepreneurship, and Strategic Management)

TIES is one of the most prestigious homes for exactly this kind of research. Faculty include Pierre Azoulay (science funding, star scientists), Danielle Li (personnel economics, innovation), and Fiona Murray (science policy). Recent dissertations:
- "Essays on the Economics of Algorithms, Markets, and Organizations" (Lindsey Raymond, 2024)
- "Essays on Biomedical Innovation" (Wesley Greenblatt, 2023)
- "Essays on the Production of Ideas" (Soomi Kim, 2023)
- "Essays on the Role of Metrics in Innovation" (Jane Wu, 2022)

**Admissions requirements**: Strong quantitative background (calculus, linear algebra, statistics). GRE or GMAT required.

**Critical finding on math**: The TIES FAQ explicitly raises "How much mathematical preparation do I need?" — suggesting this is a real concern for applicants. The coursework sample is revealing: Year 1 includes **14.381 Statistical Methods in Economics** (MIT economics PhD core), **API-111 Microeconomic Theory I** (Harvard Kennedy School economics PhD core), and 14.382 Econometrics in spring. This is essentially the first year of the MIT/Harvard economics PhD. Students with economics as their reference discipline go through the same methods training as economics PhD students.

The program emphasizes that students must choose a "reference discipline" (economics or sociology) and complete that discipline's requirements. For economics, this means passing through PhD-level micro and econometrics.

**Who should NOT apply**: The program is explicit that it is "not for everyone" and explicitly trains people for academic research careers. Placement is at "top US and international institutions."

**Assessment**: TIES requires essentially passing the same first-year coursework as an MIT/Harvard economics PhD student. This is **not a lower-math path** than a traditional econ PhD. The difference is the research questions are applied (innovation, entrepreneurship, AI) rather than theoretical economics. But the training rigor is comparable. A non-technical person cannot do TIES without becoming technical first.

Sources: https://mitsloan.mit.edu/faculty/academic-groups/ties/phd-program
https://mitsloan.mit.edu/faculty/academic-groups/ties/coursework-sample
https://mitsloan.mit.edu/programs/phd/application-information

#### Wharton — Management PhD

**Specializations**: Entrepreneurship, Human Resources, Multinational Management, Organizational Behavior, Organizational Theory, **Strategic Management**

**Admissions**: GRE or GMAT required; scores typically in the top 10%. Full fellowship (tuition + stipend) for all admitted students.

**Curriculum**: Requires a statistics sequence (STAT 5200-5210 — graduate-level statistics or econometrics). Second-year qualifying exam in specialization. Second-year paper required.

**Math**: The required stat sequence is serious (graduate-level econometrics path for "macro" students in strategy). The strategy track would involve some econometrics. However, the org behavior and organizational theory tracks can run on more qualitative/experimental methods.

**Dissertation character**: The Strategic Management track at Wharton produces empirical strategy research. The Organizational Behavior track can be more experimental/psychological. Technology and Innovation Strategy is one of the management electives. There is room to work on AI + economic implications through strategy or OB lenses.

**Assessment**: Less math-intensive than TIES, more flexible in research methods (qualitative methods explicitly offered as a track), but still requires a graduate statistics sequence. The Strategic Management track explicitly uses econometrics. **More accessible to a non-technical person than TIES or Rotman Economics, but still requires quantitative work.**

Sources: https://doctoral.wharton.upenn.edu/management/
https://mgmt.wharton.upenn.edu/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/Management-PhD-Handbook-2025-2026.pdf (the full curriculum document)
https://doctoral.wharton.upenn.edu/application-requirements/

#### Assessment of Business School PhDs

Business school PhDs differ from economics PhDs primarily in the unit of analysis (firms, industries, entrepreneurs vs. markets and aggregate variables) and in the dissertation style (three empirical essays rather than one theoretical contribution). They are not dramatically less mathematical at the top programs — TIES is as demanding as a Harvard economics PhD in first-year methods. Rotman requires a 166 quant GRE. Wharton requires graduate statistics.

Placement for business school PhDs is into business school faculty positions, which is a different (and arguably more accessible) market than economics departments. There are more faculty jobs in management/strategy than in economics, and the standards, while high, are less dominated by a single methodological paradigm.

**The strategic insight**: If the goal is writing papers on AI × economics for a business school audience (Management Science, Strategic Management Journal, Journal of Economics and Management Strategy), a business school PhD is the right path. If the goal is writing for economists (AER, QJE), this path will not deliver that credential.

### iSCHOOL PhDs

iSchools (Schools of Information) are genuinely different from the programs above. They are the one category where serious academic work on the economic and social implications of AI is possible without a mathematics PhD-level training requirement.

#### Berkeley School of Information — PhD in Information Science

- Accepts 3-7 students per year from 100+ applications
- **No GRE required** (dropped in 2021)
- Full funding for all admitted students
- "We welcome students from a diverse set of backgrounds; some will be technically educated, some educated in the humanities and social sciences"
- Areas of study include **Information Economics and Policy**, Information Law and Policy, Social Aspects of Information, HCI

The "Information Economics and Policy" area is directly relevant. Faculty include scholars working on platform economics, algorithmic systems, and digital policy. The admissions statement explicitly invites social science and humanities backgrounds. **This is the most explicitly non-technical-friendly admission among serious research programs.**

Source: https://www.ischool.berkeley.edu/programs/phd/admissions
https://www.ischool.berkeley.edu/programs/phd

#### Michigan School of Information — PhD in Information

- Fully funded for all admitted students
- Multidisciplinary approach
- Students place in "tenure track faculty positions, post doctoral research and industry positions"
- Research spans human-computer interaction, social aspects of information, economics and policy, information systems design
- No GRE listed as required in the admissions materials found

UMSI is known for having faculty at the intersection of technology and social science, including work on platform governance and AI policy.

Source: https://www.si.umich.edu/programs/phd-information

#### Cornell Information Science — PhD

- **No GRE required or accepted** — explicitly states "The Information Science department does not require or accept the GRE Test"
- Full funding for all admitted students (tuition, health, stipend)
- Areas include "markets, technology policy, algorithmic mechanism design, social and information networks"
- Faculty research spans economics, social science, and technical approaches
- Two campuses: Ithaca and NYC (Cornell Tech)
- Admits students to a first-year advisor — matching to faculty is critical

The "markets, technology policy" and "algorithmic mechanism design" areas are directly relevant to AI × economics. Cornell IS has faculty working on platform economics, competition policy, and the economics of information systems. Cornell Tech in NYC has strong industry connections for AI policy work.

Source: https://infosci.cornell.edu/phd/admissions

#### Oxford Internet Institute — DPhil in Information, Communication and the Social Sciences

This is a standout option for a non-US path. Key features:
- 3-4 years full-time (or 6-8 part-time)
- Cohort of ~49 students, 69% international, average age 32
- Faculty explicitly accepting DPhil students include **Carl-Benedikt Frey** (Dieter Schwarz Associate Professor of AI & Work — the author of "The Future of Employment" paper on automation) and **Greg Taylor** (Professor of Digital Markets and Competition)
- Recent dissertation topics include "AI, News, and the Transformation of the Public Arena" and "Framing Gender Bias in the Design of AI Recruitment Technology" (2024)
- Career destinations: academia, tech firms, government, NGOs
- Funding: Clarendon Scholarships (competitive), ESRC Grand Union DTP (social science pathway)

The presence of Carl-Benedikt Frey as programme director is significant. He is one of the most-cited economists working on AI and labor markets. Working with him on an OII DPhil would give a non-technical researcher direct access to the most relevant intellectual community for AI × economic implications.

The program explicitly distinguishes itself from the "DPhil in Social Data Science" which requires strong quantitative/computational skills. The ICSS DPhil "might draw on quantitative, qualitative, computational or mixed methods" — it explicitly accommodates non-computational approaches.

Source: https://www.oii.ox.ac.uk/study/dphil-in-information-communication-and-the-social-sciences/

#### Assessment of iSchool PhDs

iSchool PhDs are the most accessible entry point to serious academic research on AI × economics without requiring mathematics PhD training. They are not soft degrees — placement into tenure-track positions at research universities does happen. But they are genuinely interdisciplinary, accept social science methods, and do not require passing a real analysis qualifying exam.

**Tradeoff**: iSchool PhDs place graduates primarily into information science faculty positions (at i-schools, communication departments, information systems programs), not economics departments. The credential signals expertise in "information science" or "technology and society," not economics. For someone interested in AI governance, welfare implications, and political economy of AI — and who wants to work in a policy-adjacent or interdisciplinary academic role — this is a reasonable career path.

**The Cornell IS advantage**: No GRE, explicit focus on "markets, technology policy, algorithmic mechanism design," full funding, and Cornell's prestige. This may be the single best-fit program for a non-technical person wanting to do serious academic work on the economics and governance of AI.

**The OII advantage**: Carl-Benedikt Frey as a potential supervisor, the specific research culture around AI and work/labor markets, Oxford's international prestige, and the explicit accommodation of non-computational scholars.

### INTERDISCIPLINARY AI/TECHNOLOGY PROGRAMS

#### Oxford Future of Humanity Institute — CLOSED April 2024

FHI was closed on April 16, 2024, after Oxford's Faculty of Philosophy declined to renew the contracts of remaining FHI staff. The causes were multiple: administrative friction with the Faculty of Philosophy since at least 2020 (hiring and fundraising freeze), the reputational damage from Nick Bostrom's racist email surfacing in early 2023, and the collapse of effective altruism's credibility following the FTX/Sam Bankman-Fried scandal. FHI was the intellectual originator of "AI governance" as a research field, but it no longer exists as a PhD-granting vehicle.

What replaced FHI: its intellectual legacy has dispersed. The Leverhulme Centre for the Future of Intelligence (Cambridge), the Centre for the Study of Existential Risk (Cambridge), the Centre for Long-Term Resilience, and various AI safety organizations continue adjacent work. The OII at Oxford is probably the closest surviving institutional home for AI governance research at Oxford that offers doctoral training.

Sources: https://404media.co/institute-that-pioneered-ai-existential-risk-research-shuts-down
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future_of_Humanity_Institute

#### Stanford — HAI and Ethics & Society Review

Stanford's Human-Centered AI initiative (HAI) is not a PhD-granting program. It is a cross-campus research initiative that funds fellowships, runs policy programs, and hosts events. The Ethics & Society Review (ESR) is a research group, also not a degree program. Stanford offers a "Tech Ethics & Policy Summer Fellowship" for graduate students to work in DC — but this presupposes enrollment in a Stanford PhD program in some other field.

If the goal is to do an AI governance PhD at Stanford, the realistic home would be a Social Science PhD (political science, sociology, economics) or a Stanford CISAC/international policy program, using HAI as a research affiliate hub. There is no standalone "AI governance PhD" at Stanford.

Source: https://hai.stanford.edu/ethics-and-artificial-intelligence

#### Carnegie Mellon Heinz College — PhD in Information Systems & Management or PhD in Public Policy & Management

CMU Heinz offers two PhD tracks relevant to this space:
- **PhD in Information Systems & Management**: Key research areas include "Economics of Digitization," machine learning and large-scale data analytics, information security and privacy. Faculty include Michael D. Smith and Rahul Telang (media industry and copyright), Pedro Ferreira (technology in media and education), Beibei Li (generative AI).
- **PhD in Public Policy & Management**: Focus on crime/drug policy, energy, health economics, labor economics — less directly focused on AI.
- **Joint PhD in Machine Learning and Public Policy**: Available, but this requires comfort with machine learning technical depth.

The IS&M track is the most relevant. The program "prepares students with a deep understanding of the technical and organizational aspects of information systems" — the word "technical" is doing real work here. The curriculum requires a "Quantitative Methods Cluster" of courses in statistics, econometrics, and machine learning, plus technology classes.

CMU's institutional identity as a technical university means that even the policy-adjacent programs carry more technical weight than comparable programs elsewhere. The IS&M PhD is genuinely situated at the intersection of technology, economics, and policy — but the math bar is real.

Source: https://heinz.cmu.edu/programs/phd-programs/information-systems-management

#### Georgetown CSET and Other Think-Tank Adjacent Paths

The Center for Security and Emerging Technology (CSET) at Georgetown is a policy research organization, not a PhD program. It produces important work on AI governance, technical talent flows, and national security implications of AI. Some of its researchers hold PhDs from other programs and do CSET work as a career destination. Georgetown Law's Institute for Technology Law & Policy runs AI governance series but does not appear to offer a dedicated AI governance PhD.

A "PhD in Technology Policy" from Georgia Tech's School of Public Policy (where Daniel Schiff, a CSET speaker and assistant professor of technology policy, received his degree) is a real and relatively under-recognized path. Georgia Tech's Ivan Allen College houses this. The program sits in a major technical university with close proximity to policy-relevant faculty.

Source: https://cset.georgetown.edu/event/whats-next-in-ai-governance/

## Key Findings

1. **The Harvard PEG program is closed** (since Fall 2022). The most famous non-economics-department political economy PhD no longer admits students. (Strong evidence — official HKS page.)

2. **TIES at MIT Sloan requires effectively passing MIT/Harvard economics PhD first-year coursework** — microeconomic theory and econometrics at the PhD level. It is not a lower-math alternative to an econ PhD. (Strong evidence — official TIES coursework sample page.)

3. **Rotman Strategic Management has an average admitted quant GRE of 166** (94th percentile). It is not accessible to a person without strong quantitative background. (Strong evidence — Rotman admissions page.)

4. **Princeton SPIA STEP** is a genuinely interdisciplinary program with no GRE requirement for PhD applicants, full funding, and a focus that includes information technology policy. It admits ~4 students per year to the STEP cluster. Placement and prestige are strong. (Strong evidence — SPIA admissions pages.)

5. **Berkeley I School, Michigan SI, and Cornell IS are the most accessible serious programs** for a non-technical person wanting to do academic research on AI + economics/society. They do not require GRE (Cornell explicitly rejects it), welcome social science backgrounds, and provide full funding. Cornell IS's explicit focus areas include "markets, technology policy, algorithmic mechanism design." (Strong evidence — admissions pages.)

6. **Oxford OII's DPhil in ICSS** is the best non-US option. Carl-Benedikt Frey (AI and labor markets) and Greg Taylor (digital markets and competition) are accepting students. The program explicitly accommodates non-computational methods and distinguishes itself from the computational "Social Data Science" DPhil. (Strong evidence — OII program page.)

7. **FHI is closed**; no direct successor PhD program exists at Oxford for AI governance research. The OII is the closest surviving home. (Strong evidence — Wikipedia and 404media report on FHI closure.)

8. **Wharton Management PhD** is a middle ground — requires graduate statistics but offers qualitative research methods tracks, and the Organizational Theory or Technology and Innovation Strategy tracks could support AI governance work. Less math-intensive than TIES. (Moderate evidence — Wharton Management PhD handbook.)

9. **CMU Heinz IS&M PhD** is genuinely at the AI × policy frontier and could support this work, but requires a quantitative methods cluster including econometrics and machine learning. (Moderate evidence — Heinz program page.)

## Assessment

### The core tension

Every program that places graduates into serious academic research careers requires significant quantitative training. The programs that are genuinely accessible to non-technical people (iSchools, OII) place graduates into a different academic world than the programs that require heavy math (econ departments, business schools with economics tracks). Neither path is wrong — but they lead to different careers.

### Clearest fit: programs where non-technical work is legitimate and valued

**Cornell Information Science** is probably the single best-fit program for this profile: no GRE required, full funding, explicit focus on technology policy and markets, strong placement, and the institutional legitimacy to do serious work on AI governance without needing to pass a real analysis exam. The advisor-match model means the key variable is finding a faculty member working on AI × economics questions — and Cornell IS has that faculty.

**Oxford OII** is the best international option. Carl-Benedikt Frey is one of the few senior faculty in any department whose primary research agenda is exactly "AI and the economy" — and he directs the ICSS DPhil. The program is genuinely welcoming of non-computational methods. The prestige of Oxford travels well for policy careers.

**Berkeley I School** is slightly less focused on economic/policy questions and more on HCI and social aspects of information, but the "Information Economics and Policy" area is real, and Berkeley's prestige is high. The no-GRE policy since 2021 reflects a genuine commitment to diverse backgrounds.

### Programs that require upskilling first (but are worth it for the right person)

**Rotman Strategic Management** (with Agrawal/Gans/Goldfarb) and **MIT TIES** are the intellectually ideal homes for AI × economics research — but they require becoming quantitatively sophisticated first. A non-technical person who spent 2-3 years doing intensive upskilling (real analysis, statistics, econometrics, game theory) could plausibly apply and succeed. The payoff would be the highest-prestige entry point to this research community.

**Princeton SPIA STEP** is the sweet spot in public policy PhDs — genuinely interdisciplinary, strong funding, optional GRE, and a research focus that can accommodate AI governance work. But four spots per year for the STEP cluster makes admission extremely competitive, and the program skews toward physical and environmental sciences.

### What is not a viable path

**HKS PEG** (closed). **Chicago Harris** (too quant-heavy for a non-technical entrant). **MIT TIES without quantitative preparation** (the coursework sample shows this is an economics PhD under a different name).

### The placement question

The placement trajectory differs sharply by program type:
- **Economics-adjacent business school PhDs** (TIES, Rotman): tenure-track positions at top business schools, publications in Management Science and strategic management journals
- **Public policy PhDs** (SPIA STEP): policy research organizations, government, some academic positions in public policy programs
- **iSchool PhDs** (Cornell IS, Berkeley I, Michigan SI, OII): positions at iSchools, communication departments, information systems programs, policy organizations, industry research
- **None of these** produce economists who publish in AER or QJE — that requires an economics department PhD

For someone whose goal is academic research + policy influence on AI governance without necessarily being an economist, the iSchool and OII paths are fully legitimate. The field of "technology and society" produces important and influential work. But it is a different career than "economist who works on AI."
