# Survey: Who Holds the Territory in LLM × Economics, and Where Would a New PhD Entrant Fit?

## Summary

The field of AI/LLM economics is real, well-funded, and growing fast — but it is dominated by a recognizable cluster of technically fluent economists at a handful of elite institutions. The core group (Acemoglu/Johnson at MIT, Autor at MIT, Brynjolfsson at Stanford, Korinek at UVA) does theoretical and empirical work that requires strong quantitative foundations. Publication happens at top-5 general economics journals (QJE, AER, JPE, ReStud, Econometrica) and in field outlets. The NBER is the main conference infrastructure. Most active PhD students in this space are technically strong.

However, there is a genuine and largely unoccupied niche: the theoretical and humanistic study of AI's implications for economic methodology, political economy, and governance frameworks. The frontier researchers are themselves calling for this work — Korinek's research agenda explicitly lists "governance," "geopolitics," "human well-being," and "information flows" as underresearched. Acemoglu's work on "Power and Progress" and democratic stability is explicitly humanistic and historically grounded. The gap is between what the field is currently producing (empirical labor market studies, macro growth models, LLM-as-research-tool papers) and what the field's own most prominent researchers say needs to happen.

The most important surprise: the field's gap for theory/philosophy is not primarily inside economics PhD programs. It sits at a disciplinary boundary — between economic methodology, STS (science and technology studies), AI governance, and political economy — that very few institutions have set up to train PhDs to inhabit. This creates both an opportunity and an institutional challenge.

## Key Sources

### Anton Korinek — Research Page and NBER Reporter (2024)
- **Link**: https://www.korinek.com/research and https://www.nber.org/reporter/2024number4/economics-transformative-ai
- **Key content**: Korinek (UVA Economics / Darden / GovAI affiliate) is arguably the single most expansive thinker in the space. His research spans macro growth theory, labor markets, income distribution, AI governance, and welfare philosophy. He explicitly co-authored the Oxford Handbook of AI Governance. His NBER article articulates a dual agenda: economic analysis of AI's impacts, and using AI to transform economic research practice. His collaborators include Joseph Stiglitz (on labor/distribution) and GovAI (AI safety alignment). Recent NBER WP 34256 with Brynjolfsson and Agrawal explicitly calls for more research on governance, geopolitics, information flows, and human wellbeing — areas with low current coverage relative to their stated importance.
- **Assessment**: High authority. Korinek is explicitly welcoming of interdisciplinary and governance-oriented work in his research agenda documents. His institutional home at UVA Economics/Darden makes him a plausible advisor for theory-oriented students, and he holds a GovAI affiliation that bridges to AI safety/governance discourse. He seems unusual in the field for writing jointly with humanities-adjacent thinkers (Stiglitz, governance scholars).

### Brynjolfsson, Korinek, Agrawal — "A Research Agenda for the Economics of Transformative AI" (NBER WP 34256 / Communications of ACM forthcoming 2026)
- **Link**: https://cepr.org/system/files/2024-12/Korinek%20paper.pdf (CEPR version) and https://www.nber.org/papers/w34256
- **Key content**: The most comprehensive articulation of what the field sees as its frontier questions. Key areas: economic growth, innovation, income distribution, decision-making power, geopolitics, information flows, AI safety, and human well-being. Methodological approaches explicitly listed include "theoretical modeling, new economic indicators, welfare metrics, task-level assessments, and AI-agent simulations." Importantly, the paper calls for interdisciplinary collaboration. The "information flows" and "human well-being" sections are the least developed in the current literature — these are closest to humanistic/epistemological territory.
- **Assessment**: Very high authority — this is the field's own road map. Brynjolfsson is at Stanford (Digital Economy Lab, HAI), Agrawal at Rotman/Toronto. The paper comes out of a 2024 Asilomar workshop with research assistance from Basil Halperin (MIT PhD, now UVA postdoc) and Philip Trammell (Oxford/GPI, incoming UVA asst. prof.).

### Daron Acemoglu — "What Do We Know About the Economics of AI?" (MIT News, December 2024)
- **Link**: https://economics.mit.edu/news/daron-acemoglu-what-do-we-know-about-economics-ai
- **Key content**: Acemoglu (MIT Institute Professor, Nobel 2024) is the field's most prominent skeptic of AI hype. His key contribution is distinguishing "machine usefulness" from "worker replacement" in AI development — a conceptual/theoretical distinction, not an empirical one. His paper "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI" (Economic Policy, 2024) estimates modest near-term productivity gains (1.1–1.6% GDP over 10 years). His collaboration with Simon Johnson (MIT Sloan) on "Power and Progress" (PublicAffairs, 2023) is explicitly political economy — about who captures the gains from technological change. He has a recent AER:Insights paper on "Regulating Transformative Technologies" (with MIT doctoral student Todd Lensman) that develops optimal adoption timing theory. This is a good example of theory-first work coming out of MIT.
- **Assessment**: Highest authority. Acemoglu's willingness to engage with historical and institutional frameworks (EP Thompson, David Ricardo) sets a precedent for humanistically-informed economic theory. His MIT students tend to be technically strong (he co-authored the regulating paper with a PhD student), but his intellectual interests explicitly extend to governance and political economy.

### Brynjolfsson, Li, Raymond — "Generative AI at Work" (QJE 2025)
- **Link**: https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/140/2/889/7990658
- **Key content**: The most-cited empirical paper on LLM deployment in the workplace. Studies AI-assisted customer support workers and finds that access to a generative AI tool raised worker productivity by 14% on average, with largest gains for low-experience workers. Published in the Quarterly Journal of Economics — the most prestigious labor/applied micro outlet. This is the flagship model of "AI economics" empirical work: a randomized field experiment with clean identification.
- **Assessment**: High authority. Represents the dominant methodological template in the field — causal identification, field experiment, labor economics focus. A student who cannot produce work like this is unlikely to be placed by top programs' empirical track. However, this paper's findings (AI as complement to low-skill workers) directly enable the theoretical questions Korinek is calling for about redistribution and welfare.

### Korinek and Stiglitz — Multiple Papers (2018–2021)
- **Link**: Summarized at https://www.korinek.com/research
- **Key content**: A body of work analyzing worker-replacing technological progress, income distribution, and strategies for inclusive growth. The collaboration with Stiglitz (a heterodox/institutionalist economist) is notable for its explicit political economy framing and for engaging with welfare concepts beyond standard neoclassical frameworks. "Artificial Intelligence, Globalization, and Strategies for Economic Development" (NBER WP 28453, 2021) deals with how AI may undo gains from globalization — a political economy argument with major implications for the Global South.
- **Assessment**: High authority. The Korinek-Stiglitz collaboration suggests a real intellectual openness to non-mainstream economic frameworks within the field. Stiglitz's role as a politically engaged heterodox economist brings political economy instincts into the AI-economics conversation.

### NBER "Economics of Artificial Intelligence" Conference — Fall 2024 Program
- **Link**: https://www.economicsofai.com/nber-conference-toronto-2024 (agenda) and https://www.nber.org/conferences/economics-artificial-intelligence-fall-2024
- **Key content**: The annual NBER AI conference (Toronto, Sept 2024) included papers on: online business models and consumer welfare (Acemoglu, MIT); automation and political preferences (Petrova, UPF; Schubert, UCLA; Yildirim, UPenn); algorithmic fairness (Blattner/Nelson/Spiess, Stanford); regulating innovative industries; AI and nation building (Reich, Northwestern); innovators' political power to shape regulation (Beraja/Yuchtman). The mix is revealing: there is substantial political science/political economy content alongside the labor economics. Annie Liang (Princeton) presented on algorithm design and fairness frontiers — a game theory/mechanism design paper. This suggests mechanism design and political economy are welcome.
- **Assessment**: This is the highest-status conference for the field. The 2024 program shows theory and political economy are valued, not just empirics. The presence of game theory (Liang) and political economy of innovation (Beraja, MIT) signals openings for non-empirical work.

### Stanford HAI — Graduate and Postdoc Fellows 2024–25
- **Link**: https://hai.stanford.edu/news/stanford-hai-welcomes-2024-25-graduate-and-postdoc-fellows
- **Key content**: The 2024–25 HAI fellow cohort includes Basil Halperin (MIT PhD, incoming UVA econ assistant professor — works on macro growth and AI), Philip Trammell (Oxford PhD, incoming UVA econ assistant professor — decision theory, game theory, growth theory), and Andreas Haupt (MIT CSAIL PhD — economics of personalization, LLM transparency/legibility). Julia Irwin's profile is striking: a postdoc with a PhD in film and media from UC Berkeley researching the "history and philosophy of AI as a lens for understanding AI safety, values alignment, and human-computer interaction." This is the closest analogue to a humanistic/STS researcher being supported in the HAI ecosystem.
- **Assessment**: HAI clearly has room for non-technical scholars (Irwin's profile is explicitly humanities-based), but she is a postdoc and an anomaly among the broader cohort, which skews toward engineering and CS. The economics-specific fellows (Halperin, Trammell) are technically strong MIT-Oxford trained economists. HAI does fund theory-adjacent work but the pathway through is interdisciplinary studies, not conventional economics PhD programs.

### Stanford Digital Economy Lab — Postdoc Posting (2026)
- **Link**: https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/about/careers-and-opportunities/full-time-postdoctoral-fellow-the-intersection-of-economics-and-transformative-ai/
- **Key content**: Brynjolfsson's DEL is recruiting for a 2026 postdoc explicitly focused on "reimagining economic theory and metrics for an AI-powered future." Research topics include: new economic indicators, welfare metrics that account for AI's impact on human flourishing, task-level assessments, AI safety with economic perspectives, concentration of power, and geopolitics. This is an explicit call for theory-oriented work. Required: PhD in economics, CS, or "related field." Proficiency in Python/R listed as a skill — quantitative skills still required.
- **Assessment**: This posting confirms the gap Brynjolfsson himself sees in his own research program. It also makes clear that the default assumption is quantitative competence. However, it raises the question of whether someone with strong theoretical foundations and interdisciplinary depth (but no empirical economics training) could qualify — the posting says "related field" and doesn't say "empirical track."

### Korinek — "Generative AI for Economic Research" (JEL, December 2023)
- **Link**: https://genaiforecon.org
- **Key content**: Published as the lead article in the Journal of Economic Literature (the field's most prestigious review journal). Lays out how LLMs can be used across the entire economics research workflow — literature reviews, coding, drafting, simulations. This paper has a secondary implication for the PhD question: it signals that the field considers LLMs first and foremost as research tools, not as objects of study in themselves. This is different from how STS scholars or philosophers would approach LLMs.
- **Assessment**: High authority. The distinction between "LLMs as tools for economists" vs. "LLMs as subjects of economic inquiry" is a fundamental bifurcation. Korinek's JEL paper is about the former; most of the work listed in his research agenda is about the latter. A non-technical PhD student would likely be positioned in the latter camp, which is less developed and more genuinely open.

### Agrawal, Gans, Goldfarb — "Prediction Machines" (HBR Press, 2018/2023 updated)
- **Link**: https://agrawal.ca/prediction-machines-book
- **Key content**: Foundational popular-academic book framing AI as a technology that radically lowers the cost of prediction. "Power and Prediction" (2022) extends the analysis to consider how prediction changes power dynamics within firms and industries. These books (Rotman/Toronto group) established the canonical economic framing of AI — it's a factor price shock to prediction costs, which reshapes decision-making under uncertainty. This is theoretical economics applied accessibly, not empirical work.
- **Assessment**: High authority. Agrawal, Gans, and Goldfarb represent the Canadian-Toronto school of AI economics — more institutionalist and accessible than MIT's formal modeling approach. Gans has broad interests including information economics, platform economics, and innovation. This group's work shows that theoretical-framing contributions ("AI is a prediction cost shock") can be highly influential without requiring ML expertise.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The field is roughly 8–10 years old in its modern form, with an accelerating pace since 2022. There are now several distinct clusters:

1. **Labor economics cluster (MIT, Stanford)**: Acemoglu/Autor/Brynjolfsson. Empirical focus on task exposure, wage effects, productivity. Uses administrative data, field experiments, natural language processing on job postings. Home journals: QJE, AER, Journal of Labor Economics.

2. **Growth/macro theory cluster (MIT/UVA/Oxford/GPI)**: Acemoglu's regulating tech paper, Korinek/Trammell on growth under transformative AI, Halperin. Formal theoretical models. Home journals: AER, JPE, Economic Policy, Annual Review of Economics.

3. **Governance/political economy cluster (MIT, MIT Sloan, Oxford)**: Acemoglu/Johnson's "Power and Progress," Beraja/Yuchtman on political power and regulation, Korinek/Stiglitz on income distribution. More interdisciplinary, historically informed. Home journals: JEL, JPE, Journal of Democracy, NBER book volumes.

4. **AI-as-tool cluster (UVA, Toronto, Stanford)**: Korinek's JEL papers, Agrawal/Gans/Goldfarb's Prediction Machines framework. Conceptual framing papers, practitioner-oriented. Home venues: JEL, NBER volumes, books.

5. **Theory/mechanisms cluster (Princeton, Chicago)**: Annie Liang's fairness-accuracy frontier paper, mechanism design for algorithmic systems. Technical formal theory. Home journals: Econometrica, AER.

### Agreements Between Sources

- AI will have significant economic effects (even Acemoglu's skeptical estimate of 1.1–1.6% GDP is significant).
- The distributional question (who captures gains) is central and poorly understood.
- The "direction of innovation" (complementary to workers vs. replacing workers) is a policy lever, not a natural law.
- Empirical methods (field experiments, causal inference) dominate actual publications.

### Conflicts Between Sources

- **Scale of effects**: Korinek/Brynjolfsson expect transformative/explosive scenarios; Acemoglu is a measured skeptic, seeing mostly "so-so automation." This is a genuine empirical disagreement but also a framing difference.
- **Technical orientation**: Mainstream economics PhD training is heavily quantitative; some of the governance-oriented work in the field (Oxford's GPI, AI safety economics) draws on philosophy and decision theory in ways that sit uncomfortably inside standard econ programs.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

This is the most important section for the PhD entrant question.

**Gap 1: Economic methodology and epistemology of AI-assisted research.** Korinek's JEL paper describes how LLMs can run simulations, generate synthetic respondents, and assist with mathematical derivations — but does not ask what this does to economics as a knowledge-producing discipline. What kinds of claims become possible or impossible when AI mediates the research process? This is a philosophy-of-science question that economists have almost entirely ignored. The Kaul (2022) paper "3Es for AI: Economics, Explanation, Epistemology" (Frontiers in AI) engages this directly but is published outside economics and has only 4 citations — invisible to the mainstream.

**Gap 2: Political economy of AI market structure.** Korinek's paper with Jai Vipra on "Concentrating Intelligence" (Economic Policy, 2025) examines market concentration in AI. But the broader political economy of why particular AI trajectories get funded and pursued, and how regulatory capture works in the AI industry, is largely absent from economics literature. It exists in STS and critical data studies but in forms that economists don't read (or take seriously).

**Gap 3: Welfare metrics for AI-transformed economies.** The DEL postdoc posting explicitly names this as an open problem: "Creating welfare metrics that account for AI's impact on human flourishing." Standard GDP and consumer surplus metrics are increasingly inadequate. This is a theoretical problem requiring engagement with welfare economics, capability approaches (Sen/Nussbaum), and the philosophy of well-being. Essentially no economics papers on AI engage with capability approaches — the gap between Korinek's welfare language and Sen's welfare frameworks is striking.

**Gap 4: AI and the theory of the firm/organization.** The CMR paper "From Coase to AI Agents" (Berkeley CMR, 2025) and arXiv papers on "The Headless Firm" gesture at this problem — how does the Coasian theory of the firm change when AI agents become economic actors? This is foundational economic theory, not empirics. Very few working papers address it seriously.

**Gap 5: Global South and development economics of AI.** Korinek/Stiglitz's 2021 paper is the main academic work here, and it's from 2021. The question of how AI changes the economics of development, trade, and comparative advantage for non-OECD economies is poorly served relative to its importance. This intersects with political economy, development theory, and international economics.

**Gap 6: Philosophy of AI alignment as an economics problem.** Korinek's paper with Balwit "Aligned with Whom?" (Oxford Handbook of AI Governance, 2024) asks a normative question: whose preferences should AI systems optimize for? This is a welfare economics and social choice theory question that connects directly to Arrow's impossibility theorem, mechanism design, and political philosophy. It is almost entirely undeveloped within formal economics.

### Surprises

1. **The most prominent field economists are explicitly inviting theoretical and humanistic contributions.** Korinek's "research agenda" paper lists governance, geopolitics, human well-being, and information flows as priority areas. Acemoglu's work cites EP Thompson and David Ricardo's intellectual evolution. This is not a field that is hostile to non-empirical work — it is a field that is currently thin on it.

2. **The Princeton 2025 job market list has almost no AI focus.** Scanning Princeton's 2025 PhD candidates: "Making Talk Cheap: Generative AI and Labor Market Signaling" is the only AI-adjacent paper in a 20-candidate cohort. This suggests AI+economics PhD production is still concentrated at MIT, Stanford, UVA, and Toronto — not broadly distributed across top programs.

3. **"LLM as subject of economic theory" is genuinely early.** There are papers on LLMs as research tools, LLMs' labor market effects, and LLMs' use in market research — but almost no papers on LLMs as economic actors with preferences, strategies, or welfare implications. This is philosophically rich territory and almost entirely unoccupied in economics.

4. **The Stanford HAI fellowship includes a humanities postdoc doing AI history/philosophy.** Julia Irwin (PhD in film and media, UCB) was funded as a Stanford HAI postdoc to research "history and philosophy of AI as a lens for AI safety." This is not an economics role, but it shows that HAI-adjacent institutions do fund non-technical scholarly work on AI. The pathway is through interdisciplinary centers, not econ departments.

5. **Market concentration in AI is being modeled formally but not from a political economy perspective.** Korinek/Vipra's paper on market structure in AI is neoclassical industrial organization. The political economy mechanisms — how incumbents use regulatory capture, intellectual property, and compute access to entrench themselves — are not formal economics. They exist in antitrust scholarship and critical media studies but not in economics working papers.

6. **The NBER AI conference (2024) included a session on "AI and Nation Building"** by Bryony Reich (Northwestern), suggesting the field is moving toward questions of state formation, institutional change, and nationalism — areas where political scientists and historians have more to say than most economists.

## Advisor/Institutional Landscape for Theory-Oriented Candidates

### MIT Economics
- **Acemoglu**: Technically demanding but genuinely interested in political economy, history of technology, and institutional analysis. Nobel laureate. His paper "Regulating Transformative Technologies" (with Todd Lensman) is formal theory, so he does take PhD students doing theory. However, entering MIT economics requires passing a standard quantitative sequence.
- **Autor**: Labor economics focus, more empirical. Less likely advisor for a theory-oriented student.
- **Simon Johnson** (MIT Sloan): Co-author on "Power and Progress" — more politically oriented, less technically demanding in research style. Located at Sloan, not econ department.

### Stanford
- **Brynjolfsson**: Digital Economy Lab. Works on measurement, growth, labor. The DEL postdoc description explicitly asks for theory-oriented work — but with quantitative skills. Likely requires empirical competence in PhD students.
- **HAI**: Julia Irwin's profile shows humanistic scholars can be funded here, but as postdocs, not as PhD students in economics.

### University of Virginia
- **Korinek**: Economics/Darden. The most explicitly interdisciplinary researcher in the field. GovAI affiliation means connections to AI safety and governance communities. His research with Stiglitz shows comfort with heterodox economic thinking. UVA economics would be a less competitively toxic environment than MIT/Stanford for a non-standard candidate. Korinek explicitly works on governance, alignment, political economy — a non-technical PhD student aligned with these themes would find more intellectual space here than anywhere else.

### University of Toronto (Rotman)
- **Agrawal, Gans, Goldfarb**: Prediction Machines authors. More book-oriented, conceptually accessible work. Located in a business school (Rotman), which historically has higher tolerance for interdisciplinary and theory-focused work. Gans works on information economics and platform economics with theoretical sophistication. This might be a more hospitable institutional home for a non-standard PhD candidate.

### Harvard Kennedy School / Oxford
- **Political Economy and Government PhD (HKS)**: Exists but bridges political science and economics. Might suit a candidate focused on AI governance and political economy of AI.
- **Oxford / GPI (Global Priorities Institute)**: Philip Trammell came from here. GPI works on decision theory, growth theory, and AI — more philosophical flavor than standard econ PhD programs. Trammell himself has degrees in economics and mathematics but GPI's culture is distinctly philosophical.

### What Departments Can Advise Theory-Oriented AI+Econ Dissertations?

The honest answer is that very few pure economics departments can do this well. The candidates are:
- **UVA Economics** (Korinek) — most promising for theory + governance + political economy
- **MIT Economics** (Acemoglu, if the student passes the quant filter) — formal theory track
- **Toronto Rotman** (Agrawal/Gans) — business school tolerance for conceptual work
- **Interdisciplinary programs**: Stanford HAI-adjacent programs, HKS PhD, Oxford GPI affiliation, UCL Economic Policy and Political Economy PhD

**The structural problem**: Top-5 economics PhD programs require passing a mathematical economics/statistics qualifying sequence. A student without CS/ML background but with strong theoretical/humanistic skills can pass this sequence, but it requires disciplined preparation (real analysis, linear algebra, probability theory, statistics). It is a gateway that filters out most humanistic scholars before they can pursue the interesting questions.

## Publication Landscape

### Where AI+Economics Work Gets Published

**Top-tier general journals** (most prestigious):
- QJE: Brynjolfsson/Li/Raymond "Generative AI at Work" (2025)
- AER/AER:Insights: Acemoglu "Regulating Transformative Technologies" (2024); Acemoglu "When Big Data Enables Behavioral Manipulation" (forthcoming)
- JPE: Less AI-specific coverage but where macro theory lands
- Econometrica: Mechanism design for algorithmic systems (Liang et al.)

**Field journals**:
- Journal of Economic Literature: Korinek's "Generative AI for Economic Research" (lead article, 2023) — explicitly a survey/synthesis outlet, where non-empirical work is most welcome
- Journal of Labor Economics: Labor market effects papers
- Economic Policy: Acemoglu "Simple Macroeconomics of AI" (2024); Korinek/Vipra market structure paper (2025)
- Annual Review of Economics: Acemoglu/Johnson "Learning from Ricardo" (2024)

**NBER working papers** are the pre-publication distribution mechanism for almost everything in this field.

**Books**: "Power and Progress" (Acemoglu/Johnson, PublicAffairs 2023), "Prediction Machines" / "Power and Prediction" (Agrawal/Gans/Goldfarb, HBR Press), Oxford Handbook of AI Governance (Korinek et al., 2024) — books are how political economy and governance arguments reach broader audiences.

**New venues**: Economics and Computation (ACM EC) — hybrid CS/econ venue growing in importance. AIES (AAAI/ACM AI, Ethics, and Society) — for alignment and governance work.

### Is There a Publication Path for Non-Empirical, Non-ML Contributions?

**Yes, but narrow**:
- JEL explicitly publishes surveys and conceptual/methodological articles. It is the most hospitable venue for theory-without-empirics.
- AER:Insights publishes shorter, more accessible theoretical contributions.
- Annual Review of Economics welcomes review essays that synthesize theory.
- NBER volumes (like the forthcoming "Economics of Transformative AI" volume) are explicitly open to essays.
- Oxford Handbook of AI Governance (already published) is the flagship venue for governance/normative work.
- Journal of Democracy published Korinek's democracy/inequality piece — showing that interdisciplinary outlets are used.

**The constraint**: A PhD student needs placement. Placement requires working papers that can be discussed at seminars with economists. Theory papers need to be formal enough to be taken seriously in that context. A purely discursive or philosophical paper will not get you an economics job.

## Funding Landscape

### Major Centers/Grants

- **Stanford HAI**: Funds both technical and humanistic work, runs fellowship programs for grad students and postdocs. Not limited to CS — but economics students need HAI faculty sponsorship.
- **Stanford Digital Economy Lab (SIEPR/HAI co-sponsored)**: Brynjolfsson's lab. Explicitly funded to study TAI economics. Has both grad fellows and a postdoc line.
- **MIT Shaping the Future of Work Initiative**: Hosts Acemoglu/Autor. Funds research on AI and labor markets. Has a policy and public engagement focus.
- **GovAI (Centre for the Governance of AI), Oxford**: Affiliated with Korinek. Funds work on AI governance including economics approaches. Fellowship programs.
- **Alfred P. Sloan Foundation**: Funds several NBER programs including economics of digitization.
- **OpenAI, Anthropic (external funding)**: These organizations fund external research. OpenAI's economic research team produced "GPTs are GPTs" (Eloundou et al., 2023) — 500+ citations. This is a pipeline for technically-adjacent work.
- **World Bank, IMF, BIS**: Korinek has affiliations here. International development angle on AI economics.

### Are PhD Students Funded Well?

In top US economics PhD programs: yes, generally — 5-year packages with stipend, tuition, and health insurance are standard. Stanford, MIT, Harvard, Princeton all fully fund their econ PhDs. The AI-specific funding (HAI fellowships, DEL funding) adds supplement or summer research support. The funding environment for AI+economics PhD students is probably better than average, given the field's prominence and the availability of industry-adjacent funding.
