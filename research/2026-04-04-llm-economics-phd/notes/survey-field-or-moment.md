# Survey: Is "LLM × Economics" a Field or a Moment?

## Summary

The evidence points toward "emerging field with genuine permanence risk" rather than a simple yes/no answer. The institutional markers are real: a recurring NBER conference series on AI economics, dedicated postdoctoral fellowships at Stanford's Digital Economy Lab, a 2019 University of Chicago Press volume defining a research agenda, and a cascade of foundational papers since 2022. But "LLM × Economics" as currently practiced is a cluster of largely separate sub-programs — AI as research tool, economics of AI labor effects, and economics of AI market structure — that share a topic but not necessarily a theoretical core. The comparison with historical technology-economics waves reveals a clear pattern: the ones that became permanent subfields did so by producing durable theoretical frameworks that couldn't be reduced to "applying existing tools to a new domain." Platform economics did this. Internet economics (the late-1990s variety) largely did not. The question for LLM × economics is whether the phenomenon generates genuinely new theory or only new data.

## Key Sources

### Korinek, "The Economics of Transformative AI" (NBER Reporter, December 2024)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/reporter/2024number4/economics-transformative-ai
- **Key content**: The most comprehensive single statement of the emerging research program. Korinek outlines two parallel agendas: (1) economic analysis of transformative AI's structural effects — growth, labor markets, inequality, geopolitics — and (2) AI as tool for economic research. He draws the analogy to the Industrial Revolution explicitly: transformative AI may end the "Industrial Age" by making human-level intelligence reproducible, removing labor as the key bottleneck factor. He references working papers on scenario planning, growth models, and market structure of foundation models (with Vipra). Notably, he co-leads a broader "research agenda for economics of transformative AI" with Brynjolfsson and Agrawal (Stanford Working Paper, November 2024).
- **Assessment**: Primary source; Korinek is the most prominent figure explicitly framing this as a field. His NBER affiliation and Journal of Economic Literature publication are credibility signals. Perspective: optimistic about permanence; he has a personal research bet on this area.

### Korinek, "Generative AI for Economic Research: Use Cases and Implications for Economists" (JEL, 2023)
- **Link**: DOI:10.1257/jel.20231736 | Semantic Scholar ID: 1332261d94c1de6e5e9d5b6191644b0fe53eaa42
- **Key content**: Published in the *Journal of Economic Literature* — the most prestigious review venue in economics — this 165-citation paper catalogs how LLMs can serve as research tools across ideation, writing, data analysis, coding, and mathematical derivations. Covers six categories of LLM capabilities from "experimental" to "highly useful." Argues economists can gain significant productivity via micro-task automation. JEL codes: A11 (Role of Economics), C45 (machine learning), D83, I23, O33.
- **Assessment**: High credibility. The JEL publication is a signal that the profession's gatekeepers view this as a legitimate area for a major review paper. However, the paper is primarily methodological/practical (AI as tool), not theoretical economics of AI. 165 citations is strong but not overwhelming for 2023-24 in a fast-moving field.

### Eloundou, Manning, Mishkin & Rock, "GPTs are GPTs: An Early Look at the Labor Market Impact Potential of Large Language Models" (2023)
- **Link**: arXiv:2303.10130 | Semantic Scholar ID: 5501d00310b06e00351295529498cc684187148d
- **Key content**: 522 citations — the highest citation count of any directly relevant paper found. Classifies ~80% of U.S. workers as having at least 10% of their tasks exposed to LLMs; ~19% exposed for more than 50% of tasks. Notably cross-disciplinary (authors from OpenAI and economics). Key theoretical move: argues LLMs exhibit general-purpose technology (GPT) properties — the double meaning of "GPTs are GPTs" is intentional. Applies Brynjolfsson/Mitchell task-based exposure framework to LLMs.
- **Assessment**: High empirical impact. Citation count suggests it has become a reference paper, though it's from OpenAI (not pure academia) and is largely an exposure study rather than economic theory. The GPT/GPT pun aside, the general-purpose technology framing is analytically important — it connects the LLM moment to a pre-existing theoretical tradition.

### Agrawal, Gans & Goldfarb, "The Economics of Artificial Intelligence: An Agenda" (UChicago Press, 2018/2019)
- **Link**: Semantic Scholar ID: 0a4ce49d6e52e79e42e42843277e0ca246144e5a (introduction)
- **Key content**: The volume that arguably launched "economics of AI" as a recognized subfield. NBER-organized; contributors include major economists across labor, IO, macro, finance. The introduction is the founding document of the field's self-definition. 164 citations for the introduction alone. Key framing: AI as "prediction machine" — reducing cost of prediction as the unifying economic concept. This is the closest analogue to two-sided markets theory having a core concept (cross-group externalities).
- **Assessment**: Very high credibility — this is the field's founding document. The "prediction machine" framing is intellectually important, but it slightly predates LLMs as a phenomenon and arguably applies better to narrow ML than to LLMs.

### Korinek & Vipra, "Concentrating Intelligence: Scaling and Market Structure in Artificial Intelligence" (NBER WP 33139, 2024)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/papers/w33139
- **Key content**: Analyzes the industrial organization of foundation model markets. Key finding: significant economies of scale and scope in compute, data, and talent create concentration tendencies and market tipping risk. Analyzes vertical integration risks and policy remedies. 29 citations already despite being November 2024.
- **Assessment**: Important for the "economics of AI" (as distinct from AI as tool) strand. Represents the industrial organization perspective on AI — a natural extension of existing IO theory to a new domain. This is the kind of paper that looks permanent: IO economists will write about AI market structure regardless of whether LLMs specifically survive.

### Brynjolfsson et al., "Generative AI at Work" (QJE, 2024/2025)
- **Link**: https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/140/2/889/7990658
- **Key content**: Causal study of a large customer service firm deploying an AI assistant. Workers given access to AI showed 14% productivity improvement; least experienced workers benefited most. Published in *Quarterly Journal of Economics* — one of the top-3 economics journals. This is the kind of paper that distinguishes a field: credible causal identification (randomized rollout), top-journal publication, and a finding that speaks to both labor economics and productivity measurement.
- **Assessment**: Very high credibility. QJE publication is the single strongest signal that this area has crossed into mainstream economics. The Brynjolfsson brand (he co-founded MIT's IDE and has published AI-productivity research since the 1990s) adds institutional weight.

### NBER "Economics of Artificial Intelligence" Conference Series
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/conferences/economics-artificial-intelligence-fall-2024
- **Key content**: Recurring conference (at minimum Fall 2023 and Fall 2024 documented), housed under the NBER's Productivity, Innovation, and Entrepreneurship program. There is also a separate "Economics of Transformative AI Workshop" (2025) focused on firms. These are not one-off events — they have become annual.
- **Assessment**: High institutional signal. NBER conferences are by invitation and selective; a recurring conference constitutes proto-institutionalization. Comparable to how the NBER Digital Digitization project produced sustained platform economics work.

### Stanford Digital Economy Lab: Postdoctoral Fellowship in Economics × Transformative AI (2026)
- **Link**: https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/about/careers-and-opportunities/full-time-postdoctoral-fellow-the-intersection-of-economics-and-transformative-ai/
- **Key content**: Fellowship led by Brynjolfsson starting Fall 2026, two years, focused on "reimagining economic theory and metrics for an AI-powered future." Explicit mission: "developing the economic theories, metrics, and policies needed to guide a world powered by transformative AI."
- **Assessment**: Strong field-formation signal. Dedicated postdocs in an area is a lagged indicator of field institutionalization — they train the next generation of researchers. The framing "reimagining economic theory" is notable: it claims theoretical novelty, not just application.

### Acemoglu, Autor, Hazell & Restrepo, "Artificial Intelligence and Jobs: Evidence from Online Vacancies" (JLE, 2022)
- **Link**: Semantic Scholar ID: df5f3ffe15207eb6ae2f00f3ccc818625b9bfbe7
- **Key content**: 476 citations. Uses vacancy data to show AI-exposed establishments reduce non-AI hiring while changing skill requirements. Aggregate labor effects "too small to be detectable" at macro level despite firm-level substitution. Extends Acemoglu-Restrepo task-based framework to AI specifically.
- **Assessment**: Very high credibility (top-5 author combination; Journal of Labor Economics publication). Represents the labor-side strand of AI economics. The finding that aggregate effects are small is important for debates about AI's current vs. eventual impact.

### Korinek & Stiglitz, "Artificial Intelligence and Its Implications for Income Distribution and Unemployment" (NBER WP, 2017/2018)
- **Link**: Semantic Scholar ID: 940374c38aed0ae2503d1678b8d25004d727e59b | https://doi.org/10.3386/w24174
- **Key content**: 310 citations. Theoretical paper examining how AI affects income distribution and unemployment through skill-complementarity and task-automation channels. Co-authored with Stiglitz (Nobel laureate). One of the early theoretical framing papers.
- **Assessment**: High credibility. Important because it shows the economics-of-AI program extends to macroeconomic theory, not just empirical labor studies.

---

### Rochet & Tirole (foundational platform economics work, 2003-2006)
- **Link**: The founding papers (e.g., Rochet & Tirole 2003) established two-sided markets theory. Tirole won the Nobel in 2014, partly for this work.
- **Key content**: The platform economics / two-sided markets wave began ~2003 with Rochet-Tirole, Armstrong, and Wright. It created a genuinely new theoretical concept (cross-group externalities requiring different pricing logic than standard IO). By 2014, it was established enough that the Nobel committee cited it. It now appears in IO textbooks and shapes antitrust law.
- **Assessment**: The archetypal example of a technology-economics wave that became a permanent subfield. Key structural features: (1) a novel theoretical concept that couldn't be reduced to existing tools, (2) empirically relevant (credit cards, then Google, Apple, etc.), (3) generated decades of follow-on work in IO and antitrust, (4) institutional adoption (FTC, EU competition authorities).

---

### Cryptoeconomics / Blockchain Economics (2015-2023)
- **Links**: arxiv.org/abs/2407.15715 (survey, 2024); Cambridge University Press cryptoeconomics textbook (2024); Management Science special issue 2023
- **Key content**: Blockchain/crypto economics had a wave of papers 2015-2022 on mechanism design for decentralized systems, token economics, DeFi. The 2024 arxiv survey "Cryptoeconomics and Tokenomics as Economics: A Survey with Opinions" argues cryptoeconomics is becoming a distinct field. A Cambridge textbook exists. Management Science ran a special issue "Advances in Blockchain and Crypto Economics" in November 2023. The CBER Forum maintains a literature catalog.
- **Assessment**: Mixed signals. Unlike platform economics, cryptoeconomics has not produced a core theoretical concept that changes how economists think about non-crypto markets. Much of the work is either CS-adjacent mechanism design or finance-adjacent token valuation. The 2022 crypto winter killed speculative interest but serious academic work continued. Verdict: niche subfield, not a broad field transformation. The comparison with platform economics is instructive: platform economics theory turned out to matter for Google, Facebook, Amazon, app stores — markets with hundreds of billions in economic activity. Cryptoeconomics theory matters mainly for crypto markets.

---

### Behavioral Economics / Neuroeconomics comparison
- **Links**: Society for Neuroeconomics website; JEL codes D9x (behavioral economics established codes)
- **Key content**: Behavioral economics is the clearest historical analogue for "technology changes how economics is done." It began as a wave (Kahneman-Tversky experimental work, 1970s-80s), became institutionalized by 2000 (JEL codes, dedicated journals, Nobel prizes to Thaler 2017, Kahneman 2002), and is now fully mainstreamed. Neuroeconomics had a similar trajectory but narrower scope. Key differentiator: behavioral economics challenged core assumptions of standard theory (rationality), not merely adding a new application domain.
- **Assessment**: The analogy to LLM × economics is suggestive but imperfect. Behavioral economics's staying power came from revealing systematic failures of the rational actor model — i.e., it was theoretically disruptive, not just empirically novel. The question for LLM × economics is: does zero-marginal-cost cognition challenge core theoretical assumptions (production functions, comparative advantage, information economics) in a similarly disruptive way?

## Landscape Assessment

### State of knowledge

The area currently has three largely separate strands:

**Strand 1: AI as a research tool for economists.** This is the Korinek JEL paper, the practical LLM-for-research literature. High current activity, rapid turnover (the field is chasing a moving target as models improve). Likely to persist as a technique but not to constitute a "field" — similar to how "using Stata for econometrics" is not a field.

**Strand 2: Economics of AI (labor markets, productivity, inequality, market structure).** This is the Brynjolfsson-Rock-Syverson productivity J-curve work, the Acemoglu-Autor-Restrepo task model applied to AI, the Korinek-Stiglitz distributional work. This is the most mature strand. It uses standard economic theory and empirical methods to study AI as a new general-purpose technology. This strand clearly has staying power — economists have always studied the effects of major technologies (electrification, computers, internet) and AI is simply the next wave.

**Strand 3: Theoretical economics of AI cognition.** What does it mean for economic theory when cognitive work approaches zero marginal cost? How do production functions, comparative advantage, and information economics change when intelligence becomes reproducible? This is the thinnest strand — the Korinek-Stiglitz distributional papers and the Korinek-Trammell growth paper are here — and arguably the most important for permanent field formation. If this generates genuinely new economic theory (not just applying Romer's ideas about ideas), it could constitute a field.

### Agreements across sources

- LLMs have general-purpose technology characteristics (wide domain applicability, economies of scale, improving over time). This is broadly agreed.
- The labor market effects, while theoretically large, are empirically moderate in the current period. Acemoglu et al. and Brynjolfsson et al. agree on this.
- Economists need new frameworks for measuring output and productivity in an AI economy (Brynjolfsson's productivity measurement work).
- The institutional infrastructure is building: NBER conferences, top-journal publications, dedicated fellowships.

### Conflicts and tensions

- **Optimists vs. skeptics on economic impact**: Korinek-Stiglitz warn of potentially catastrophic inequality; Acemoglu-Restrepo emphasize that aggregate effects are small so far; Brynjolfsson finds productivity gains concentrated in lower-skill workers (a relatively benign finding). These are not yet resolved empirically.
- **Field vs. application**: There is real tension between AI × economics being a permanent field (with its own theory, journals, PhD training) versus being a temporary label for "applying economic analysis to AI" that will be absorbed back into labor economics, IO, macro, and growth once the excitement subsides. The Internet economics wave of the late 1990s largely followed the absorption path — "internet economics" is not a distinct field today, but the insights are now just part of industrial organization (network effects, switching costs, etc.).
- **LLM-specific vs. AI-general**: Much of the literature is framed around AI broadly, not LLMs specifically. This is intellectually appropriate but suggests the LLM moment may be too narrow a frame — the real question is whether AI as a general-purpose technology creates a permanent subfield, not whether LLMs specifically do.

### What distinguishes permanent subfields from fads — historical pattern

From the historical survey, three features seem to distinguish technology-economics waves that became permanent subfields:

1. **New theoretical concepts, not just new applications**: Platform economics gave us cross-group externalities with distinct pricing implications. Behavioral economics gave us systematic biases challenging rationality. Information economics (Akerlof, Spence, Stiglitz) gave us adverse selection, signaling, and screening. By contrast, "internet economics" in the late 1990s was largely applying existing price theory to digital goods — and it was absorbed back into IO rather than persisting as a distinct field.

2. **Empirical relevance that persists beyond the wave**: Platform economics became permanent partly because Google, Facebook, and Apple kept growing. The theory stayed relevant as the technology matured. Cryptocurrency economics risks not having this property if crypto remains a niche financial instrument.

3. **Training and hiring effects**: Behavioral economics created new PhD dissertations, new hiring lines, and new courses. This positive feedback loop is a strong indicator of permanence. The emergence of Stanford DEL postdocs and Stripe's "Economics of AI Fellowship" suggests this loop may be starting for AI economics.

### Where is "LLM × Economics" on this spectrum?

The strongest case for permanent field status:
- It is generating top-journal publications (QJE, JEL) in large volume
- It has NBER institutional backing
- It connects to genuinely open theoretical questions (growth under reproducible intelligence, market structure of foundation models)
- The underlying technology is clearly not going away; models will improve and deployment will broaden

The strongest case for "moment" status:
- No single theoretical concept has emerged analogous to "cross-group externalities" or "reduction in prediction costs"
- The most-cited papers (Eloundou et al.'s 522 citations, Brynjolfsson's generative AI at work) are empirical exposure/productivity studies, not theoretical advances
- The fastest-growing strand (LLMs as research tools) will likely be superseded by better tools every 18 months, making specific findings obsolete
- Much of the current activity looks like a specific technology's hype cycle, not intellectual foundation-building

### Gaps and under-explored areas

- **Comparative institutional analysis**: The history of how platform economics institutionalized (through Tirole's influence, through NBER digitization project, through antitrust relevance) has not been systematically compared to the current AI economics wave.
- **Theory of zero-marginal-cost cognition**: Surprisingly little economics work directly addresses what it means theoretically when the marginal cost of a cognitive task approaches zero. This is the "prediction as input" question pushed much further. Romer's ideas-as-nonrival goods model is the closest precedent but may be insufficient.
- **Differentiation within "LLM × economics"**: The three strands above (tool, impact, theory) are not clearly distinguished in the literature. This creates confusion about what "the field" even is.
- **Blockchain as negative comparison case**: A systematic comparison of why blockchain economics has not achieved the field-formation success of platform economics would be illuminating for predicting AI economics' trajectory.

### Surprises

1. **The NBER "Economics of Transformative AI" framing is emerging as distinct from "Economics of AI"** — the Korinek-Brynjolfsson-Agrawal 2024 agenda paper introduces this as a separate program, suggesting the researchers themselves see "LLMs capable of cognitive work" as a qualitative shift from "AI as prediction technology."

2. **The Eloundou et al. "GPTs are GPTs" paper is an OpenAI paper, not a university economics paper**, yet it has 522 citations — the most of any directly relevant LLM × economics paper. This reflects the unusual situation where the technology creators are also producing economics research about their technology. The analogy would be if AT&T researchers had written the foundational papers on network externalities.

3. **The comparison with internet economics is sobering**: Despite the dotcom boom generating enormous interest in "internet economics" around 1998-2002, there is no AEA section, JEL code category, or named subfield called "internet economics" today. The relevant insights were absorbed into IO, information economics, and digital economics. This is a serious base rate consideration for whether "LLM × economics" will persist as a named thing.

4. **The theoretical gap is more serious than it appears**: The founding document of the field (Agrawal-Gans-Goldfarb's "prediction machine" framing) applied better to 2018-vintage ML than to LLMs. There is no equivalent framing for what LLMs do economically — and the lack of a clear reductionist concept ("LLMs reduce the cost of X") may be because LLMs are genuinely general-purpose in a way that resists this framing.
