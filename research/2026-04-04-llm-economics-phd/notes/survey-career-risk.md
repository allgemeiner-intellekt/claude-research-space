# Survey: Career Risk Calculus — PhD vs. Job, and Obsolescence Risk

## Summary

The 2025-2026 economics job market is in historic crisis: academic postings are down 38-44% from prior averages, the tech "Plan B" has weakened due to layoffs and a shift toward CS-adjacent hires, and the Federal Reserve system is tightening recruitment due to operating losses. Starting an economics PhD right now means entering a 5-6 year pipeline into what is currently the worst recorded academic job market in modern history. At the same time, industry demand for economists who understand AI is real but volatile. The calculus depends heavily on program pedigree, research design choices, and how one defines "success." Historical analogs from internet economics and platform economics suggest that early entrants to a technology wave can do extraordinarily well — but they need to be at the right department, with the right advisors, working on problems that outlast the current state of the technology.

## Key Sources

### "The Collapse of the Econ PhD Job Market" (Chris Brunet, Sept 2025)
- **Link**: https://www.chrisbrunet.com/p/the-collapse-of-the-econ-phd-job
- **Key content**: Detailed analysis of JOE and EJM data showing a "perfect storm": academic hiring freezes, Federal Reserve budget tightening, and tech sector pullback all converging simultaneously. Described as a structural shift, not a cyclical blip.
- **Assessment**: Opinion piece by a market observer, not peer-reviewed, but his analysis is corroborated by multiple data sources. High-signal for current conditions.

### "The Academic Economics Job Market Is Contracting-Fast" (Econ-Jobs / Vincent Baus, Dec 2025)
- **Link**: https://econ-jobs.com/media/academic-economics-job-market-is-contracting/
- **Key content**: EJM data showing academic openings down ~25% versus 3-year average; assistant professor openings down 10% YoY; North America jobs down ~39% vs. prior season and ~44% vs. 3-year average. Applications per posting up 36% YoY. With ~1,400 new PhDs competing for ~400 tenure-track US spots, the squeeze is severe. Tech sector now prefers data scientists and CS-adjacent roles over pure economists — "skill sets in causal inference are increasingly automated or handled by CS-adjacent roles."
- **Assessment**: Numeric data from EJM (the main job market clearinghouse); credible and specific. Industry observer source, not academic journal, but very close to the actual data.

### "Thoughts on the 2025-2026 Economics Job Market" (Zoe Plakias, Feb 2026)
- **Link**: https://www.zoeplakias.com/post/thoughts-on-the-2025-2026-economics-job-market
- **Key content**: 1,773 JOE jobs as of Dec 28, 2025 — the lowest in the 2019-2025 window. But 80% of JOE postings are academic; the non-academic market (NGOs, government, industry) is broader. Environmental economics is robust. Tech industry — "just a few years ago a common offramp from academia" — has been letting economists go through Seattle and Bay Area layoffs. USAID gutting reduced international development roles. H-1B fee jump ($100K per new visa) is severely restricting non-citizen PhD options in the US.
- **Assessment**: Careful synthesis by a working applied economist with hands-on knowledge of the job-posting landscape. Not peer-reviewed but detailed, specific, and data-grounded.

### "Economics Job Market Update: November 2025 — A Historic Low" (Paul Goldsmith-Pinkham, Nov 2025)
- **Link**: https://paulgp.com/2025/11/24/joe-market-update-november.html
- **Key content**: As of week 47, cumulative JOE postings at 760 — 38% below last year, 17% below pandemic-impacted 2020, and 37% below 2015-2019 average. Finance jobs showed resilience relative to other sectors. Code and data publicly available.
- **Assessment**: Yale economist; high-credibility. Quantitative analysis from primary JOE data.

### "The Bull Market for Economists Is Over" (NYT, Noam Scheiber, July 2025)
- **Link**: https://www.nytimes.com/2025/07/28/business/economics-jobs-hiring.html
- **Key content**: Mainstream press confirmation of the trend. Notable for the subheadline framing this as "an ominous sign for the economy" — not just an academic job market story.
- **Assessment**: NYT; paywalled, could not retrieve full content, but cited by multiple other sources as the mainstream press acknowledgment.

### "Tech Industry Jobs for Econ PhDs — Part 1: the Landscape" (Scarlet Chen, 2020)
- **Link**: https://scarlet-chen.medium.com/tech-industry-jobs-for-econ-phds-54a276dda80b
- **Key content**: Detailed landscape from a Stanford PhD who went into tech. Lists major employers (Amazon — "one of the largest private employers of economists"; Google, Facebook, Uber, Stripe, LinkedIn). Describes a spectrum from research-oriented to product-embedded roles. This represents the pre-2023 landscape; important as a baseline for the "good old days."
- **Assessment**: First-person practitioner account; very well-regarded in the community though 2020 vintage. The landscape it describes has partially contracted since.

### "Economists (and Economics) in Tech Companies" (Susan Athey and Michael Luca, paper)
- **Link**: https://gsb-faculty.stanford.edu/susan-athey/files/2022/04/economists_in_tech.pdf
- **Key content**: Foundational paper arguing economists bring unique value to tech firms through causal inference, mechanism design, and structural modeling. Athey herself is the paradigmatic case: pioneer in internet/digital economics, Microsoft Chief Economist role 2007-2013, then Stanford, and increasingly focused on AI economics since.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, credible; but describes a 2018-era landscape. Athey's own trajectory is the best single case study of what a bet on tech-adjacent economics can yield.

### Erik Brynjolfsson — Wikipedia biography
- **Link**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erik_Brynjolfsson
- **Key content**: PhD from MIT Sloan 1991 on "Information technology and the re-organization of work: theory and evidence" — started working on IT productivity before the internet boom. Spent 30 years at MIT, moved to Stanford in 2020, founded Digital Economy Lab focused on AI economics. Now explicitly works on "Economics of Transformative AI." Also co-founder of three companies, patent holder, NBER associate. His students (Marshall Van Alstyne, Michael Smith, etc.) became prominent in digital economics.
- **Assessment**: The paradigmatic case of betting early on a technology-economics intersection. His PhD was in 1991, before the internet was public. The technology wave he studied grew to dominate. This is a best-case scenario template. Note: he was at a top-3 institution (MIT), which matters enormously.

### Hal Varian biography
- **Link**: https://people.ischool.berkeley.edu/~hal/bio.html
- **Key content**: UC Berkeley economist (PhD 1973) who spent career on microeconomic theory, then pivoted to information economics in the 1990s, wrote *Information Rules* (1999) with Carl Shapiro, and joined Google as Chief Economist starting May 2002. Has been there ever since, now emeritus professor. Co-authored classic digital economics textbook.
- **Assessment**: The definitive case study of an economist who timed a technology wave well — but critically: Varian was already a distinguished microeconomist before pivoting to internet economics. He did not do a PhD specifically on internet economics; he applied existing economic theory to the internet. This is a crucial distinction for someone considering a PhD now.

### Yannis Bakos biography
- **Link**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yannis_Bakos
- **Key content**: NYU Stern professor, MIT PhD, worked on internet economics and electronic marketplaces in the mid-1990s. His early work "showed that the internet would reduce the search costs of buyers and sellers." Co-invented Flexplay DVDs with Brynjolfsson. Still active in research at NYU. Represents a successful internet-era bet.
- **Assessment**: Good example of a researcher who built a durable academic career out of early internet economics work. The key observation: his early papers were on market microstructure and information economics — durable economic theory — applied to the internet. The economic framework outlasted any specific technology.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

**The academic job market**: Genuinely bad and appears structural, not cyclical. The data from multiple sources — JOE, EJM, and qualitative reporting — converge. North American academic hiring is down 30-44% from historical averages. The tech safety valve has weakened. The Federal Reserve pipeline has tightened. Someone entering a PhD in 2026 will graduate into the 2031-2032 job market. The current contraction began in 2023-2024, driven by university budget pressures (enrollment cliff), Fed losses, and tech restructuring. There is no clear evidence of when or whether it recovers.

**The industry market**: More complex. Tech layoffs are real, but AI-specific hiring is growing (one source: "AI hiring booms while rest of job market stalls"). Anthropic, OpenAI, and other frontier AI labs are building economics teams. The relevant question is not "do economists in tech get hired?" but "which economists in tech are secure?" The shift appears to be away from centralized economics research teams (Uber's Business Economics team, which was gutted in COVID) toward embedded roles with direct product or policy relevance.

**AI+economics demand**: Real but evolving. The demand is for people who can do causal inference, mechanism design, and labor/welfare analysis applied to AI systems — not "AI researchers who know some economics." The non-technical profile is a real constraint here: many of the growing job categories require at minimum strong Python or R proficiency, and ideally familiarity with ML methods.

### Agreements Across Sources

1. The academic economics job market is in historic crisis right now (2025-2026). This is confirmed by quantitative data (JOE, EJM) and qualitative reports.
2. The tech industry econ jobs that provided a "Plan B" have weakened structurally, not just cyclically — with explicit note of preference shifting toward CS-adjacent roles.
3. Environmental/energy economics is the most robust subfield right now. AI economics may be next.
4. Non-academic economist roles (NGO, policy, international organizations) remain more available than JOE data suggests, since JOE is 80% academic.

### Conflicts and Tensions

1. **Is the tech pullback permanent?** Some sources (Econ-Jobs analysis) say the shift toward CS roles is structural. Others (Anthropic Institute launch, Stanford DEL postdoc) suggest real demand for economists at AI companies. The resolution may be that demand for economists is growing *at AI companies specifically* while shrinking at mature tech companies (Amazon, Uber, Google) that already had big economics teams.

2. **Is AI economics "serious economics"?** Sources don't directly address this, but the job market candidates list (Kevin Bryan's site) suggests that the field is being legitimized through top departments (MIT, Princeton, Stanford, Northwestern). However, some of the work — e.g., running surveys on AI adoption, measuring LLM use at firms — may face skepticism from theory-heavy hiring committees.

3. **Timing the wave**: Brynjolfsson started before the internet existed and caught the whole wave. Someone starting a PhD in 2026 is entering *after* the LLM wave has already broken — after ChatGPT, after massive industry deployment. This is different from the 1991-2001 window. Whether the economic research on AI's effects is still "early" enough is a genuine question.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **No actual outcome data on blockchain/crypto economics PhDs**: I could not find tracking data on whether people who did dissertations on crypto economics in 2017-2020 have built successful careers from that work specifically. The Ante (2020) paper gives field-level analysis but not career outcomes. This is a significant gap.

2. **No systematic data on where AI-economics job market candidates actually placed**: Kevin Bryan's list shows who the candidates are but not where they ended up. Placement data would be very informative.

3. **Non-technical positioning**: Very little specific advice about how a non-technical person should approach an AI-economics PhD. The field assumes at minimum strong applied econometrics; the question of how much CS/ML is actually required is unclear from sources.

4. **Historical internet-economics career outcomes**: I found a few paradigmatic figures (Brynjolfsson, Varian, Bakos) but no systematic data on the cohort of people who entered economics PhDs in 1998-2002 focused on internet/digital markets. The figures I found are survivorship-biased success stories.

### Surprises

1. **The 2025-2026 job market is worse than I expected**. The 38-44% drop in academic postings, combined with a 36% increase in applications per posting, is striking. This is not a normal bad year; it looks like a structural realignment.

2. **The tech shift is toward CS, not toward economists**: Several sources explicitly say tech companies are shifting preference from economists to data scientists and CS-adjacent roles as causal inference tools become more automated. This is counterintuitive if you assumed "AI = more demand for AI-understanding economists."

3. **Anthropic launched a think tank with economists in March 2026**: This is the most concrete recent signal that frontier AI labs are hiring economists. The "Anthropic Institute" with explicit economic research focus is a new and real job category.

4. **The AI economics candidates on the market in 2025 are from top departments and often in IO/management, not pure economics**: The field is being built by people who have strong traditional economics credentials *plus* AI knowledge — not by people who bet the whole PhD on AI from the start.

5. **A non-technical background is a bigger constraint than it first appears**: Almost every "AI economist" in the 2025 candidate list has a quantitative background that enables the research. The methodological demands of the field (causal inference, structural modeling, empirical IO) are high. Being "non-technical" in the sense of lacking coding and econometrics skills would be a serious impediment.

## Specific Historical Cases Found

**Brynjolfsson (MIT 1991)**: PhD on IT and work organization, before the internet. 30-year MIT career, then Stanford, now leads AI economics lab. Most successful possible outcome. Key factor: started *before* the wave, at MIT, in a period when the field was not crowded.

**Varian (Berkeley 1973)**: Already distinguished microeconomist when he pivoted to information economics in the 1990s. Wrote *Information Rules* (1999), joined Google in 2002, stayed as Chief Economist ever since. Key factor: was applying existing theoretical toolkit to new domain, not starting from scratch.

**Bakos (MIT)**: Worked on search costs and electronic markets in the 1990s. Sustained academic career at NYU. Key factor: fundamental economics (search costs, market microstructure) applied to internet — the economics survived the internet's evolution.

**Platform economics (Tirole/Rochet)**: Tirole published "Platform Competition in Two-Sided Markets" in 2003, won the Nobel in 2014 partly for this work. But Tirole was already a dominant figure in IO before this — the platform work was an extension of long-established industrial organization theory. Students who wrote dissertations on two-sided markets in 2003-2010 are now tenured faculty in IO at good schools. The field sustained.

**Blockchain/crypto economics (2017-2020)**: No identified prominent success stories from people who *specifically* bet their PhD on blockchain economics in this period. The field produced papers but not academic stars. The technology hype-cycle was too volatile to support a sustained research program.

## Summary Observations for the Review Agent

1. **The base rate is bad**: Entering an econ PhD in 2026 means a historically terrible academic job market in 5-6 years. This is a real and data-backed risk, not speculation.

2. **The AI economics field exists but is pedigree-heavy**: The candidates succeeding in this area are from MIT, Princeton, Stanford, Northwestern. They applied traditional economic frameworks (IO, labor, macro) to AI, with strong quantitative training. A non-technical person entering a lower-tier program to "study AI economics" faces a very different risk profile than a top-program candidate with strong econometrics.

3. **Industry demand is real but shifting**: The emerging demand is at AI-first companies (Anthropic, OpenAI, possibly DeepMind) rather than mature tech. This demand is nascent and may not scale the same way Amazon's economics team scaled in the 2010s.

4. **The theoretical vs. empirical resilience question**: Based on historical cases, economic *theory* applied to technology appears more durable than empirical work on specific technologies. Tirole's two-sided markets theory outlasted any specific platform. Varian's information economics survived because it was about economics of information in general, not about specific internet companies. But this cuts against the non-technical person: theoretical work in economics requires the highest mathematical training.

5. **The best hedge strategies visible from research**: (a) Frame the PhD around durable economic questions (mechanism design, information economics, labor theory) that happen to have LLM applications — not around LLMs per se; (b) Choose a program where the advisors have strong IO, labor, or theory credentials *plus* AI interest, not a program that is marketing itself as "AI economics"; (c) Maintain optionality toward industry by building coding and empirical skills alongside the dissertation; (d) Consider whether the industry path (especially at an AI lab) actually provides comparable intellectual engagement and better financial terms during the years when a PhD would be in progress.
