# Deep Dive: What Does "Just Getting a Job" Actually Look Like?

## Question

For a non-technical person with economics interests, broad intellectual curiosity (political economy, philosophy of science, epistemology), and AI enthusiasm — what are the concrete job options outside a PhD? What do they pay, what do they require, what's the ceiling, and what is the quality of intellectual work on offer?

## Investigation

### The Anthropic Institute: The Most Interesting New Development

The single most significant development for someone fitting this profile is Anthropic launching the **Anthropic Institute** on March 11, 2026, led by co-founder Jack Clark in a new role as "Head of Public Benefit." The Institute consolidates and expands three teams: the Frontier Red Team (capabilities stress-testing), Societal Impacts (real-world AI use), and Economic Research (labor and macroeconomic effects). It is also incubating new efforts on AI progress forecasting and AI's interaction with legal systems.

The founding hires are revealing: Matt Botvinick (formerly Google DeepMind Senior Director of Research and Princeton neuroscience professor), Anton Korinek (UVA economics professor on leave), and Zoë Hitzig (previously studied AI economics at OpenAI). This is a team of senior academics with PhDs. The Institute's stated goal is candid public communication about what building frontier AI reveals about AI's societal risks — and it has a structural advantage no external researcher has: proprietary usage data.

The Institute is **also hiring an "analytical staff"** to pull research together and broadcast it. This is the entry point for a non-PhD applicant. The job posting language suggests this is closer to communications, synthesis, and research operations than original research.

Separately, Anthropic's **Economic Research team** (now part of the Institute) is hiring Research Economists at $300,000–$405,000/year (San Francisco-based). The job description is unambiguous: it requires Python/R/SQL proficiency, econometric methods, causal inference infrastructure, and a job-market paper. This is an explicitly technical PhD-track role. A non-technical economics enthusiast would not be competitive.

**Anthropic's Public Policy team** (kept separate from the Institute to preserve research credibility) is also expanding. They are opening a D.C. office and hiring "to inform and shape AI governance around the world." Policy roles here are more accessible to non-technical candidates but would reward policy/law/government backgrounds more than general economics curiosity.

Source: https://www.anthropic.com/news/the-anthropic-institute

### AI Policy Organizations: The NGO Tier

This is the most accessible entry point for someone without technical skills. The landscape breaks into several sub-types:

**AI safety-adjacent NGOs** (Future of Life Institute, Centre for AI Safety, SaferAI, CLTR):
- FLI hired "US Policy Team Members" in March 2026 — focused on Capitol Hill engagement, bipartisan outreach, tracking legislation, developing educational materials. No technical requirement stated. Salary not posted publicly.
- SaferAI (Policy Associate): Requires "strong ability to engage and coordinate multiple stakeholders including policymakers and scientists." No coding requirement, but expects familiarity with EU AI Act, GPAI Code of Practice, AI risk frameworks.
- CLTR (Centre for Long-Term Resilience, UK): AI Policy Manager salary £65,388–£93,723 (~$85K–$120K USD). Requires experience in AI/tech policy, strong analytical writing. PhD not required but not unusual.

**AI Policy infrastructure organizations:**
- Institute for AI Policy and Strategy (IAPS): Hiring (Senior) Programs Associate at $70,000–$110,000 (D.C. preferred, remote okay). This role manages their fellowship matching program and annual Alexandria Forum. It is administrative/coordination, not research. Applicants who "coach fellows through policy work and career transitions" — this is more program management than intellectual production.

**AI law/policy think tanks:**
- Institute for Law & AI (LawAI, Cambridge UK): Research Scholar $100,000–$140,000; Senior Research Scholar $125,000–$175,000. Requires legal background or extensive federal agency/Congressional policy experience. Not primarily an economics role.

**EU government:**
- European AI Office: Policy Officer ~€4,100–€8,600/month (~$54K–$115K USD). Requires minimum 3 years EU digital policy experience, strong analytical skills. For EU nationals or those willing to relocate to Brussels. Policy-oriented, not technical.

**US government (NIST CAISI):**
- Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI): Now operating under Trump's AI Action Plan, focused on evaluating commercial AI models for national security. Requires U.S. citizenship. Roles are primarily technical (cybersecurity, capability evaluation). Policy roles exist but are in flux given the administration change. In-person only (D.C. or San Francisco).

### AI Company Strategy/Policy Teams: The Tech Tier

At the frontier AI companies, the salary premium for non-technical roles is real but far below technical salaries.

**OpenAI:**
- Policy Manager: ~$155,000–$280,000/year (H-1B filings). Global Affairs / Policy Manager roles specifically noted in salary databases.
- Program Staff: $150,000–$355,000 (broad range, likely reflects seniority).
- Finance Staff: $240,000–$260,000.
- Support and Community Staff: $180,000–$235,000.
- Note: These are *base salaries only*. OpenAI employees reportedly receive an average $1.5M in stock awards, but this is concentrated in technical roles. Non-technical staff's equity is substantially lower.

**Anthropic (Policy/Institute analytical staff):**

**What these roles require at frontier labs:** Frontier AI company policy teams are small and competitive. A typical OpenAI policy hire has a J.D., a government background (FTC, DOJ, NSC), or years in DC policy. Anthropic's new D.C. office implies a pipeline similar to Stripe (Sarah Heck's background) and White House-level connections (her NSC experience). A general economics enthusiast without government, legal, or deep policy experience would not be competitive for these roles without an additional credential.

### Consulting: The Hybrid Path

Management consulting's talent model is changing under AI pressure. McKinsey and BCG have frozen entry-level salaries for three consecutive years:
- McKinsey Business Analyst (undergrad): ~$112,000 base + $18,000 bonus = ~$150,000–$170,000 total.
- McKinsey Associate (MBA): ~$192,000 base + ~$40,000 bonus + ~$30,000 signing = ~$230,000–$270,000 total.
- BCG/Bain: Comparable, with Bain slightly higher on performance bonus.

The strategic picture is complicated: firms are shifting from generalist slide-deck consulting toward multi-year AI transformation implementations. This increases demand for hybrid profiles (technical + business judgment) and reduces the pathway for pure generalists. "Forward-deployed consultants" who vibe-code and build tools are rising at BCG; pure analysts whose main value was research synthesis and PowerPoint are squeezed. AI is doing what junior analysts used to do.

For an economics-background person without an MBA or technical skills: entry into MBB is genuinely difficult. The more accessible tier — Deloitte, EY-Parthenon, strategy boutiques at $90,000–$130,000 — is possible, but the intellectual content is client-facing and reactive. These roles rarely produce original research. They are execution and problem-framing work.

The more interesting possibility is **specialized AI advisory boutiques**. Firms like AlixPartners, Alvarez & Marsal, or newer AI-native boutiques are building practices where an economics + AI literacy profile could land someone early. These roles are harder to map salaries to without insider knowledge, but are likely $80,000–$130,000 for analyst/associate level.

### Think Tanks Without a PhD: The Ceiling Problem

Think tanks are the most philosophically congenial environment for someone with broad intellectual interests in economics and AI. The UChicago GRAD Guide to Think Tank Careers (still the clearest available overview) states plainly:

> "Senior research positions are held by esteemed subject matter experts with advanced graduate degrees and/or extensive professional experience. Job titles at the advanced level include senior fellow or fellow."

The career ladder looks like:
1. Research Assistant ($35,000–$51,000 at Brookings — this is entry-level, typically two-year positions for recent undergrads)
2. Research Analyst/Associate (~$55,000–$80,000)
3. Policy Analyst (~$80,000–$120,000)
4. Senior Policy Analyst (~$100,000–$140,000)
5. Fellow / Senior Fellow — typically requires a PhD or equivalent policy prominence

At **Brookings Institution**: Research Assistants earn $35,000–$51,000. Research Analysts earn somewhat more. Policy Analysts with a few years' experience and a master's degree earn $80,000–$100,000. The ceiling without a PhD is somewhere around Senior Policy Analyst. Becoming a Fellow — where intellectual autonomy and original agenda-setting happen — almost universally requires a PhD or equivalent authority (e.g., former senior government official, recognized journalist).

**80,000 Hours' career review on think tank research** describes it as a plausible early-career path for policy influence, noting it rewards "quantitative analysis" or "ability to synthesize and communicate ideas clearly." They flag that the immediate impact is real but incremental, and that the path is better for building contacts and skills than for doing original intellectual work at the frontier of a field.

The 80,000 Hours framing is relevant: think tanks are better as *launch pads* (into government, politics, or academia) than as lifelong intellectual homes for someone without a PhD. The intellectual ceiling is real.

### The Opportunity Cost Calculation

**PhD stipend:** $40,000–$55,000/year (top programs in economics). Duration: 5–6 years. Total foregone income relative to industry employment: roughly $150,000–$400,000 depending on what industry job is taken.

**Post-PhD academic salary:** $120,000–$160,000 at a research university for assistant professor. Tenure clock is 6 years. Full professor salaries in economics at top institutions reach $200,000–$350,000. But most PhD graduates from even good programs do not get research university positions; the median outcome is a teaching-focused school or policy job with a PhD credential.

**The alternative income stack by path:**

| Path | Entry salary | 5-year salary | 10-year ceiling |
|---|---|---|---|
| Think tank research assistant → analyst | $38K–$45K | $70K–$90K | $120K–$150K (senior analyst/fellow w/ PhD or equiv.) |
| AI policy NGO (FLI, SaferAI, CAIS) | $65K–$90K | $90K–$130K | $150K–$200K (director level) |
| AI company policy team (junior) | $120K–$160K | $160K–$220K | $250K–$350K (senior manager/director) |
| Consulting (Big 4 strategy) | $90K–$132K | $150K–$200K | $250K–$400K+ (partner track) |
| McKinsey BA | $150K–$170K | $230K–$270K (post-MBA Associate) | $300K–$600K+ (Engagement Manager+) |
| PhD → Asst. Professor | $45K (stipend) | $45K | $120K–$160K at year 5 post-PhD |
| PhD → Anthropic Economic Research | $45K (stipend) | $45K | $300K–$405K if hired (highly competitive) |

The opportunity cost of a PhD vs. taking an AI company policy job is significant: a 28-year-old going into a PhD program at $45K/year foregoes 5 years of an AI company policy career that might be $150K–$200K/year. That's roughly $500,000–$750,000 in foregone income before accounting for lost compound savings. Post-PhD, even a successful academic hire at $130K may take another 6 years to hit $180K. The financial case for a PhD is weak unless the goal is specifically academic or the credential is needed to unlock a specific position (like a fellowship at Brookings or a senior research economist role).

### Intellectual Quality: The Real Trade-Off

This is the dimension that matters most for someone described as having "broad intellectual interests" and "cross-disciplinary instincts."

**Think tank work:** The intellectual quality of think tank output varies enormously. At Brookings' Hamilton Project or the Peterson Institute for International Economics, economists produce genuinely rigorous work — but almost all senior researchers have PhDs. Without one, the role is synthesis, policy brief writing, and data marshaling. This can be intellectually satisfying but is fundamentally *reactive*: you are summarizing and communicating others' ideas, not producing original frameworks. The exception is if someone has extraordinary domain authority from another source (political experience, journalism, a distinctive public voice).

**AI company policy teams:** Intellectually stimulating but agenda is company-constrained. The work at Anthropic's Public Policy team is explicitly about "model safety and transparency, energy ratepayer protections, export controls, and democratic leadership in AI." These are important questions, but the framing is determined by the company's interests. There is room for substantive intellectual contribution, but the agenda is not self-directed in the way academic research is.

**The Anthropic Institute's analytical staff:** This is genuinely interesting intellectual territory. The Institute sits at the intersection of frontier model access and macroeconomic/societal questions. "Analytical staff who pull various parts of our research agenda together and broadcast our work" could range from editorial/communications work to something closer to research assistant to Korinek and Hitzig. If it's the latter, it would be intellectually rich — original empirical work on AI's labor market effects, with proprietary data no external researcher has access to. But: this is a supporting role to PhD economists, not an independent researcher role.

**The honest summary:** The intellectual quality ceiling in industry and policy without a PhD is *real but not absolute*. Someone with exceptional written output, deep AI+economics synthesis, and a distinctive voice can build a niche — as a policy blogger, think tank analyst, or program manager at a prestigious AI organization. But the *generative* intellectual work — running your own research agenda, producing original scholarship — is locked behind the PhD at most institutions. The field of AI economics is new enough that this barrier may be softer than in established fields, but it is not gone.

## Key Findings

- **The Anthropic Institute (launched March 2026) is real and hiring** for analytical/communications staff — not just PhDs. This is a live opportunity for someone with synthesis skills and an economics background. (Strong evidence; primary source confirmed: https://www.anthropic.com/news/the-anthropic-institute)

- **Anthropic's Research Economist role pays $300K–$405K** but explicitly requires Python/R/SQL, econometrics, causal inference, and a job-market paper. Not accessible without a PhD or equivalent technical training. (Strong evidence; direct from job posting: http://job-boards.greenhouse.io/anthropic/jobs/5018472008)

- **AI policy NGO roles pay $65K–$130K** at entry/mid level (IAPS Programs Associate $70K–$110K; CLTR AI Policy Manager £65K–£94K; LawAI Research Scholar $100K–$140K). These are accessible without a PhD but reward policy/legal/government experience over general economics curiosity. (Strong evidence; multiple direct job postings)

- **OpenAI Policy Manager earns ~$155K–$280K** base salary. Frontier AI company policy roles are competitive and typically reward government/legal credentials. Non-technical economists face an uphill battle without a government or JD background. (Strong evidence; H-1B filing data via Business Insider)

- **Think tank career ceiling without PhD is at Senior Policy Analyst (~$100K–$140K)** — below the Fellow level where intellectual autonomy lives. The exception is someone with extraordinary alternative authority. (Strong evidence; UChicago guide + Glassdoor salary data + 80,000 Hours career review)

- **McKinsey entry (undergrad) pays ~$150K–$170K** total compensation, frozen for three years. Consulting is shifting toward technical implementation, reducing the value of pure generalist analytical roles. (Strong evidence; Management Consulted 2026 Consulting Salaries Report via Poets & Quants)

- **The PhD opportunity cost is $500K–$750K in foregone income** over a 5-year program if the alternative is an AI company policy job at $150K–$200K/year. (Moderate evidence; calculated from posted salaries and stipend norms)

- **The intellectual quality of non-PhD work is real but reactive**: synthesizing, communicating, and supporting — not agenda-setting original research. The Anthropic Institute's analytical staff role is a notable exception that could offer genuine proximity to frontier research. (Moderate evidence; based on job descriptions and industry structure analysis)

## Assessment

The "just get a job" path is not a cop-out — it is a genuine set of live opportunities, several of which are better compensated and more intellectually stimulating than a PhD stipend with uncertain payoff. The Anthropic Institute's expansion, and Anthropic's broader policy team growth, represents a concrete opening right now (April 2026) for someone who can write clearly about AI economics and policy.

The key structural insight is that the field is bifurcating: the technical research jobs (Research Economist at Anthropic, Research Scientist at OpenAI) are locked firmly behind quantitative PhD training, while the policy/communications/synthesis layer is more accessible but intellectually subordinate. A non-technical person is realistically competing for the latter category.

Where the industry path beats the PhD:
1. **Immediate compensation**: $100K–$160K entry vs. $45K stipend.
2. **Speed of impact**: Policy influence is immediate; academic research has a 5–10 year lag to citation and policy uptake.
3. **Optionality**: 5 years of AI company policy experience opens more doors than 5 years of a PhD program that doesn't complete, in a world where AI disruption is compressing time horizons.

Where the PhD wins:
1. **Intellectual autonomy**: No employer determines your research agenda.
2. **Credentialing lock**: The Fellow/Senior Fellow path at Brookings, the economic research leadership at Anthropic, the IMF/World Bank economist track — all require a PhD or near-equivalent.
3. **The rare upside**: If the person has genuine insight into AI economics and can produce original empirical or theoretical work, the PhD is the amplifier. Anton Korinek getting hired at Anthropic at presumably senior-researcher compensation is the model — but he had a tenured professorship first.

The strongest case for "just get a job" is if the person can identify a specific live role — and the Anthropic Institute analytical staff posting, the IAPS Programs Associate role, or the FLI US Policy Team positions are genuinely worth applying to right now. The stronger case for the PhD is if the person has genuine original research ideas that can't be executed from a policy staff position, or if they want the credentialing that unlocks intellectual leadership 8–10 years from now.

The intellectual trade-off is real and should not be minimized: non-PhD policy work in the AI space is frequently reactive, agenda-constrained, and service-oriented. A person with deep original thinking on AI and political economy may find themselves frustrated supporting a research agenda they didn't set. That frustration, multiplied over a decade, is what drives many mid-career professionals back to graduate school anyway.
