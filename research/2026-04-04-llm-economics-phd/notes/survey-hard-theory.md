# Survey: What Are the Genuinely Hard Theoretical Questions That LLMs Pose for Economic Theory?

## Summary

This question turns out to have a tiered structure. Some angles that *look* theoretically deep (e.g., mechanism design with AI agents) are already being worked on competently by computer scientists and produce incremental results. The genuinely hard puzzles — ones that could anchor a 40-year research program — cluster around three areas: (1) what happens to growth theory when AI can automate the production of ideas itself (not just goods); (2) whether the Hayekian knowledge problem is dissolved, intensified, or transformed by LLMs; and (3) whether functional distribution theory (labor vs. capital shares) needs to be rebuilt from the ground up. The human capital question and mechanism design questions are real but arguably shallower — the former is being formalized quickly, and the latter is dominated by CS/theory-of-computation researchers who may solve the incentive-theoretic puzzles before economists engage.

The most striking surprise: the literature has bifurcated sharply between optimistic growth theorists (Korinek/Trammell, Aghion/Bunel) who think AI could break the Kaldor Facts entirely, and a pessimist like Acemoglu who uses Hulten's theorem to argue the macroeconomic gains are modest (< 0.66% TFP over 10 years). This debate is currently unresolved and maps onto deep structural questions about substitutability between capital and labor at the task level.

## Key Sources

### Acemoglu, "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI" (NBER WP 32487, May 2024)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487 / PDF at https://shapingwork.mit.edu/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/Acemoglu_Macroeconomics-of-AI_May-2024.pdf
- **Key content**: Uses a task-based model (building on Acemoglu & Restrepo 2018) and Hulten's theorem to derive macroeconomic effects of AI as: TFP gains = (fraction of tasks impacted) × (average cost savings per task). Derives a central estimate of ≤ 0.66% TFP gain over 10 years — "nontrivial but modest." The key mechanism is that even if AI improves productivity enormously on easy tasks, hard-to-learn tasks with context-dependent factors and no objective outcome measures will resist automation. Also shows that even when AI improves low-skill worker productivity in certain tasks, this may *increase* inequality (not decrease it) — because the general equilibrium effects depend on which tasks are complemented and which substituted. Predicts AI widens the gap between capital income and labor income, but not as sharply as robotics did.
- **Assessment**: Published in *Economic Policy* 2025. This is the most rigorous formal macro framework currently available. Acemoglu is intentionally conservative; others (Aghion, Korinek) challenge his parameter assumptions. The task-based framework is the dominant paradigm but it has a structural limitation: it assumes AI effects are *separable* at the task level, which may not hold if AI enables entirely new production processes that aren't reducible to task substitution. His own acknowledgment of "hard-to-learn tasks" gestures at a deep theoretical gap he doesn't fill.

### Trammell & Korinek, "Economic Growth Under Transformative AI" (NBER WP 31815, October 2023, revised September 2025)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/papers/w31815 / PDF at https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w31815/w31815.pdf
- **Key content**: Synthesizes multiple growth model strands within a common framework. Core results: (1) If AI enables capital to become a *gross substitute* for labor in production (machines can self-replicate), growth rate dramatically accelerates and labor share plummets toward zero — breaking both Kaldor Facts (stable growth rate and stable labor share). (2) Automating R&D in isolation has *ambiguous* effects — it may not break the growth regime if fundamental constraints on research persist. (3) The joint effect of automating both goods production and R&D is where explosive growth becomes plausible. (4) Wages may rise or fall depending on returns to scale, natural resource constraints, and direction of technical change. The critical theoretical object is the *elasticity of substitution* between capital and labor across all tasks — if this exceeds 1 (gross substitutes), the explosive growth scenario emerges. The paper is careful to present scenarios, not predictions.
- **Assessment**: This is a rigorous review/synthesis from Brookings/UVA, with excellent mathematical appendices. Korinek is the leading theorist taking the "transformative AI" scenario seriously without hand-waving. The key gap it identifies but doesn't fill: we have no good theory of *what determines the elasticity of substitution* at the aggregate level when AI is involved — the current estimates are ad hoc. This is a PhD-program-sustaining question.

### Jones & Tonetti, "Past Automation and Future A.I.: How Weak Links Tame the Growth Explosion" (January 2026, preliminary)
- **Link**: https://web.stanford.edu/~chadj/JonesTonetti_Automation.pdf
- **Key content**: Performs growth accounting using a task-based model to ask: how much of historical TFP growth is due to automation? Result: capital productivity improvement explains most TFP growth (the annual growth rate of capital productivity is at least 5pp larger than the sum of labor and factor-neutral productivity growth). Looks forward: builds an endogenous growth model where both goods and idea production are endogenously automated. Key finding: automation leads growth to *accelerate* but very slowly — by 2040, output is only 4% higher than the counterfactual, and by 2060 only 19% higher. The reason: "weak links" — an elasticity of substitution among tasks less than 1. Even when most tasks are automated, output is constrained by slowly-improving labor on the remaining tasks (bottleneck logic). This is the formal theoretical basis for why the "singularity" story requires overcoming Leontief-style complementarity across tasks.
- **Assessment**: Very preliminary (version 0.3) but from Chad Jones at Stanford GSB. Methodologically serious. The "weak links" concept is the crucial theoretical insight: it suggests that the distributional question (who captures automation gains) is less important than the structural question (whether task complementarity prevents explosive growth at all). This is a deep result with significant implications for Romer/Jones growth theory. The paper explicitly identifies as a remaining gap: what determines which tasks are "weak links"?

### Aghion & Bunel, "AI and Growth: Where Do We Stand?" (San Francisco Fed, June 2024)
- **Link**: https://www.frbsf.org/wp-content/uploads/AI-and-Growth-Aghion-Bunel.pdf
- **Key content**: Uses two approaches: (1) historical parallel with electricity and ICT revolutions, suggesting 0.8–1.3pp additional annual productivity growth; (2) task-based formula (following Acemoglu 2024) but with more optimistic parameter estimates, yielding 0.68pp median. Key theoretical contribution: explicitly distinguishes between AI automating *goods production* (transitory growth effect) and AI automating *idea production* (permanent increase in growth rate). The second channel is undertheorized in the literature. Also flags that lack of competition in AI upstream markets (data and computing power dominated by GAFAMs) could prevent the productivity gains from materializing — a novel application of Aghion's creative destruction framework.
- **Assessment**: This is a policy note, not a full theoretical paper. Credible because of Aghion's stature (Collège de France/INSEAD/LSE) and his decades of work on innovation-led growth. The key contribution for our purposes: it cleanly identifies the *two distinct channels* (goods vs. ideas automation) and argues the literature has underweighted the ideas channel. This is a genuine theoretical gap.

### Kiesling, "AI and Economic Calculation" (Knowledge Problem Substack, April 2024)
- **Link**: https://knowledgeproblem.substack.com/p/ai-and-economic-calculation
- **Key content**: Drawing on a Santa Fe Institute workshop on "The Calculated Economy in the Era of Machine Learning," Kiesling restates the Mises-Hayek calculation debate and argues AI does *not* solve it. The key distinction: Mises was not arguing about *computation* (which Lange/market socialists correctly argued could be handled computationally) but about *economic calculation* — the use of relative prices emerging from exchange of privately-held property rights to calculate opportunity costs. AI can generate, process, and analyze data, but it cannot *react to data and take action in a way that embodies the subjective, personal judgments about opportunity costs*. Quotes Phelan (2020): "A machine makes a poor entrepreneur because it does not care about the significance of one economic judgment over another. In every AI task to date, the importance of one outcome over another is pre-specified by a human." This means: LLMs aggregate data but cannot aggregate *preferences expressed through market action*, which is what makes prices informationally dense.
- **Assessment**: This is a Substack post, not a peer-reviewed paper, but Kiesling is an energy economist at University of Illinois with decades of engagement in Hayekian political economy. The argument is rigorous and draws on Boettke & Candela (2023), Lavoie (1985). The theoretical claim is important: the knowledge problem is not a *computational* problem (which AI could potentially solve) but an *epistemological* one about the nature of knowledge itself. This is one of the most theoretically interesting angles for a PhD program — but it needs formalization. The claim that "economic calculation has an irreducible cognitive dimension" is either true (in which case LLMs don't dissolve the knowledge problem) or false (in which case the whole Hayekian argument against central planning needs revision). There's no formal model here, and that's the gap.

### Research Square preprint, "The Algorithmic Calculation Problem: Why Foundation Models Cannot Solve Socialist Planning" (February 2026)
- **Link**: https://www.researchsquare.com/article/rs-8856269/latest
- **Key content**: (Based on search results — full content was very large; reading the abstract/framing from search.) Argues that LLMs face what the paper calls an "algorithmic calculation problem" — a structural analog to Mises/Hayek's calculation problem. The argument: even if an LLM could process all data in an economy, it cannot generate the forward-looking, preference-revealing signals that emergent market prices encode. The fundamental issue is that market prices aggregate *counterfactual* information (what would have been demanded/supplied under different conditions) that never gets explicitly generated except through the discovery process of actual exchange.
- **Assessment**: Preprint, not yet peer-reviewed. Very recent (February 2026). Appears to be a serious philosophical-economic argument. The credibility of this type of work varies greatly — it needs to be read carefully for whether the "algorithmic calculation problem" is a genuine formalization or a restatement of the Hayekian argument in new clothing. But as a research direction, it represents the most direct confrontation of LLMs with Austrian economic theory.

### Bloom, Prettner, Saadaoui & Veruete, "Artificial Intelligence and the Skill Premium" (NBER WP 32430, May 2024; Finance Research Letters 2025)
- **Link**: https://www.nber.org/papers/w32430 / https://ssrn.com/abstract=4826037
- **Key content**: Proposes a nested CES production function with three capital types: physical capital, industrial robots, and AI. Key assumption (from the literature): robots predominantly substitute for low-skill workers; AI mainly substitutes for high-skill workers. Main result: AI *reduces* the skill premium as long as AI is more substitutable for high-skill workers than low-skill workers are for high-skill workers. This is a direct, formal challenge to the common narrative that "AI mostly hits routine/low-skill jobs." The model shows skill compression at the top is possible — consistent with Minniti et al. (2025) empirical findings from European regions.
- **Assessment**: This is peer-reviewed (Finance Research Letters 2025). The model is elegant but deliberately simple. The key theoretical question it opens without answering: what *determines* the elasticity of substitution between AI and different worker types? The paper takes this as a parameter; a fuller theory would explain it endogenously. Harvard (Bloom), Vienna, and Paris VIII authorship — credible.

### Espinal Maya, "Augmented Human Capital: A Unified Theory and LLM-Based Measurement Framework for Cognitive Factor Decomposition in AI-Augmented Economies" (arXiv:2604.01066, April 2026, working paper)
- **Link**: https://arxiv.org/html/2604.01066v1
- **Key content**: Decomposes human capital into three orthogonal components: H^P (physical-manual), H^C (routine-cognitive), H^A (augmentable-cognitive). Derives a "corrected Mincerian wage equation" and shows the standard specification is *misspecified* in AI-augmented economies. Uses LLM-generated augmentability scores for 18,796 O*NET tasks mapped to 440 Colombian occupations. Key empirical finding: the wage return to augmentable cognitive capital increases with AI adoption in the formal sector (+5.1%, p<0.001) but is *negative* in the informal sector (-4.4%) — labor market institutions, not technology access, determine who captures augmentation rents. The augmentation premium at the 90th wage percentile is 19 times larger than at the 10th — AI is inequality-increasing *within* the cognitive work category.
- **Assessment**: This is a recent working paper (April 2026) from EAFIT University in Colombia. The theoretical contribution — decomposing the Mincerian equation to account for AI augmentability — is genuinely novel and builds on Becker/Mincer in a principled way. Credibility is moderate (not yet peer-reviewed, developing country application). But the framework is broadly applicable and the formality/informality finding is a significant theoretical insight: it suggests the *institutional complementarity* question (who can capture gains from AI augmentation) is as important as the technological question.

### Dütting, Mirrokni, Paes Leme, Xu & Zuo, "Mechanism Design for Large Language Models" (arXiv:2310.10826, October 2023, WWW 2024)
- **Link**: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2310.10826
- **Key content**: Investigates auction mechanisms for AI-generated content. Identifies three unique challenges: (1) LLMs encode preferences as *probability distributions over outputs* (not value functions), requiring a new model of agent preferences; (2) LLMs require randomization for good performance — deterministic mechanisms perform poorly; (3) the stochastic output means standard revenue equivalence and strategy-proofness results need rethinking. Proposes token-by-token auction format with single-dimensional bids. Proves equivalence between two incentive properties and monotonicity condition on output aggregation. Extends second-price rule design to this setting.
- **Assessment**: Published at WWW 2024, from Google Research and University of Chicago. This is a technically serious CS/econ theory paper. The challenge it identifies for mechanism design is real but somewhat bounded: it's the problem of designing *specific auction formats* when agents are LLMs, not the deeper question of whether the *revelation principle* itself holds when agents can use LLMs to strategize. This distinction matters for whether the theoretical problem is deep or tractable.

### Hao et al., "Game-Theoretic Lens on LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems" (arXiv:2601.15047, January 2026)
- **Link**: https://arxiv.org/html/2601.15047v1
- **Key content**: Survey paper organizing LLM-based multi-agent research around four game-theoretic elements: players, strategies, payoffs, and information. Identifies that LLMs do not consistently play Nash equilibrium strategies — they exhibit patterns more consistent with level-k reasoning or cognitive hierarchy models. Key finding: LLM strategic behavior is sensitive to framing (prompt engineering), which means the *strategy space* for LLM agents is different from the strategy space assumed in classical mechanism design. The "revelation principle" in standard mechanism design assumes agents can compute best responses; LLMs can simulate but not guarantee optimal strategic behavior.
- **Assessment**: Survey paper from HKUST Guangzhou, Beihang, Harbin IT, etc. — credible technically but not from a top-5 economics department. The game theory and mechanism design findings have real implications for economics, but the economic framing is underdeveloped. The key theoretical gap it gestures at: the revelation principle relies on agents being able to compute their true preferences and report them — but if LLMs are acting as agents, and LLMs exhibit systematic biases in strategic reasoning, what happens to incentive compatibility? This is a genuinely open question.

### Minniti et al. (2025), "AI innovation and the functional distribution of income" (European regional evidence, CEPR summary published March 2026)
- **Link**: https://gecmagz.com/ai-and-the-distribution-of-income-between-capital-and-labour/ (CEPR summary)
- **Key content**: Examines 238 European regions, 2000-2017. Finds a robust negative association between AI patenting intensity and labor's income share: a doubling of AI patent intensity reduces the labor share by 0.5–1.6 percentage points. Crucially, high- and medium-skilled workers experience the *strongest* reductions in their income share (wage compression at top), while low-skilled workers decline less (even seeing mild employment growth). This is the opposite of the classical "low-skill displacement" narrative. The mechanism: AI raises productivity but the gains go to capital, not labor, even for high-skill cognitive workers. The finding is: "AI not only raises efficiency but also reallocates the gains from innovation, amplifying the share of income accruing to capital."
- **Assessment**: The CEPR summary is secondary — the actual paper appears in a peer-reviewed journal (2025). European regional data is a credible empirical laboratory. This finding creates a theoretical puzzle: why does the labor share decline even for high-skilled workers? The Bloom et al. (2024) model provides one explanation (AI substitutes for high-skill tasks). But this sits awkwardly with augmentation narratives. The functional distribution question — capital vs. labor, not just high-skill vs. low-skill — is where this connects to classical political economy.

### Kambhampati, "Polanyi's Revenge and AI's New Romance with Tacit Knowledge" (Communications of the ACM, February 2021)
- **Link**: https://cacm.acm.org/opinion/polanyis-revenge-and-ais-new-romance-with-tacit-knowledge/
- **Key content**: Argues that deep learning's success in *perceptual* intelligence (vision, speech) is actually evidence that AI works well on tacit knowledge tasks (where humans have no explicit theory), while AI still struggles with tasks where we *do* have explicit knowledge and causal theories. The claim is that the current "learning-only" AI paradigm is "quixotic" — it refuses to accept explicit knowledge that could massively improve performance. Implications for the Hayek debate: Hayek's tacit knowledge argument assumed tacit knowledge is what markets coordinate, and that it *cannot be made explicit*. But LLMs appear to be extracting something from human text that approximates tacit knowledge transmission. Kambhampati argues this is an illusion — LLMs are learning statistical patterns in text, not the underlying causal/explicit knowledge.
- **Assessment**: This is an opinion piece in CACM (2021), so pre-GPT-4. But the theoretical point about the distinction between perceptual tacit knowledge (which AI handles through statistics) and deliberative tacit knowledge (embedded in expert judgment, market action, contextual evaluation) remains important. Kambhampati is ASU, credible AI researcher. For economic theory: the question is whether LLM competence in language tasks (which involve some tacit knowledge) generalizes to the kind of tacit knowledge that Hayek thought was non-formalizable — specifically, *economic valuations* that emerge from personal circumstances, time constraints, and local information.

### Lucier, Immorlica et al., "Agentic Markets: Equilibrium Effects of Improving Consumer Search" (arXiv:2603.25893, March 2026)
- **Link**: https://arxiv.org/html/2603.25893v1
- **Key content**: Formally models "agentic markets" — two-sided markets where consumers and businesses delegate search/interaction to AI agents. Key theoretical finding: *cheaper* search improves consumer surplus and learning, but *more informative* search can *degrade* both unless the market learns as much as consumers about the products (e.g., by "reading the transcripts" of agentic conversations). The mechanism: more informative search reduces inter-business competition by allowing consumers to match more precisely to products, which allows businesses to raise prices at equilibrium. This weakens competition in ways that price theory didn't anticipate. Also: if search is informative but the market doesn't observe what the agent learned, information asymmetries *increase* in new ways.
- **Assessment**: This is from Microsoft Research and Yale — high credibility. Published March 2026, very recent. This is one of the most directly relevant papers for the "price theory in AI-mediated markets" question. The finding that more informative AI search can reduce consumer surplus (by weakening competition) is counterintuitive and has deep implications for antitrust, platform economics, and Kiesling's argument that AI creates new scarcities. The model is elegant but limited to symmetric markets and search goods — doesn't address experience goods or credence goods.

### Kiesling (2024 — additional marginalism argument, referenced in search results)
- Note: The original prompt mentioned Kiesling's argument that "marginalism is strengthened by AI because new scarcities emerge." This specific claim is visible from her substack persona and the Santa Fe Institute workshop context. The specific paper making the "new scarcities" argument is the April 2024 substack post above. The argument: when routine cognitive tasks become near-zero marginal cost, this creates *new scarcity in the complementary factors* — specifically, contextual judgment, trust, verification, attention, and credibility. These are the new scarce goods that markets will price. This is Kiesling's extension of marginalism, not a contradiction of it.
- **Assessment**: The claim is theoretically interesting but not yet formalized. It predicts a *shift in the margin* — from cognitive task execution to cognitive task *direction* and *validation*. For price theory, this means the relevant scarce goods are now things like "verified expertise," "trusted judgment," "attention of a known-credible human." These are fundamentally different goods from information itself, and they may not have the normal properties (excludability, rivalrousness, measurability) that standard price theory assumes.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The field is in an active but fragmented state. There is a clear divide between:

**Empirical/applied economics** (the dominant mode): Researchers are running natural experiments on GPT productivity effects, task exposure analyses (Eloundou et al.'s "GPTs are GPTs"), and labor market studies. This is high-quality work that will eventually dominate journals. But it doesn't touch the hard theoretical questions.

**Formal macro/growth theory**: A small group (Trammell/Korinek, Jones/Tonetti, Aghion/Bunel, Acemoglu) is building formal models. They broadly agree on the task-based framework as the starting point but disagree sharply on parameter values (especially elasticity of substitution) and the importance of the ideas-automation channel. This is where the deepest theoretical questions live.

**Austrian/price theory**: Almost no formal work. There's commentary (Kiesling, Mises Institute podcasts, Cosmos Institute Substack) and one preprint (the "algorithmic calculation problem" paper). The theoretical gap here is the largest — no one has formalized what it would mean for AI to "solve" the knowledge problem, or what "new scarcities" look like in equilibrium.

**Mechanism design/game theory**: Active, dominated by CS and theory-of-computation researchers (Dütting et al., the Hao et al. survey). Economic theorists haven't engaged seriously. The existing work is technically sophisticated but economically thin.

### Agreements Across Sources

1. The task-based framework (Acemoglu-Restrepo) is the dominant theoretical paradigm for thinking about AI's labor market effects. Nobody has proposed a fundamentally different framework.

2. AI will widen the gap between capital and labor income. This appears in Acemoglu (2024), Trammell/Korinek (2023), Minniti et al. (2025), and Bloom et al. (2024). The *magnitude* is disputed; the *direction* is not.

3. The standard Mincerian wage equation is misspecified in AI-augmented economies. This is asserted in Espinal Maya (2026) and implicitly in most other work that decomposes task types.

4. The ideas-automation channel is the most uncertain and potentially most consequential. Aghion explicitly says this is undertheorized.

### Conflicts Between Sources

1. **Magnitude of TFP gains**: Acemoglu: ≤ 0.66% over 10 years. Aghion/Bunel: 0.68–1.3pp *per year*. Trammell/Korinek: potentially 100%+ GDP gain if AGI approaches. Jones/Tonetti: acceleration but slow, with "weak links" taming the explosion. This is a factor-of-100 disagreement driven by different parameter assumptions about elasticity of substitution and the scope of task automation.

2. **Skill premium direction**: Bloom et al. (2024) find AI reduces skill premium (AI substitutes for high-skill tasks). Espinal Maya (2026) finds AI augments high-skill workers (premium for augmentable cognitive skills increases). These can be reconciled only if the high-skill/AI relationship is non-monotonic: AI first augments, then substitutes as capabilities improve. Neither paper models this dynamic.

3. **Knowledge problem**: Kiesling argues AI does *not* dissolve the Hayekian knowledge problem because economic calculation is irreducibly cognitive. The "algorithmic calculation problem" preprint agrees. But neither has engaged seriously with whether LLMs' ability to encode and reproduce expert judgment represents a qualitative change in what "tacit knowledge" means.

### Genuinely Hard Theoretical Questions (Depth Assessment)

**Tier 1: Genuinely hard, under-explored, research-program-sustaining**

1. **The ideas-automation singularity question**: When AI can automate R&D, does the growth rate approach infinity, or do "weak links" in the production structure prevent it? Jones/Tonetti (2026) give the best current answer ("weak links tame the explosion") but their model is preliminary and raises more questions: *what determines which tasks are weak links?* Is there a general theory of task complementarity that predicts when the bottleneck moves? This is a fundamental open question in growth theory.

2. **The Hayekian knowledge problem in formal economic theory**: No one has built a formal model of what it means for AI to "aggregate dispersed knowledge" in a market sense. The Kiesling/Phelan argument (AI handles computation but not economic calculation) needs formalization. A PhD project could formalize the distinction between "data aggregation" and "preference revelation through exchange" and derive when AI reduces and when it intensifies the coordination failures Hayek identified. This would have implications for the Lange-Hayek debate (does AI rehabilitate market socialism?) and for the theory of the firm.

3. **Functional distribution theory under cognitive automation**: Why does the labor share decline even for high-skilled workers when AI advances? The classical political economy literature (Ricardo, Marx) would say this is about *bargaining power* and the social relations of production, not just task substitutability. Modern economists have no good theory of *why* the labor share is what it is — the Kaldor Facts (constant labor share) are empirically regularities with no deep theoretical explanation. If AI breaks the labor share Kaldor Fact (as Trammell/Korinek predict), this demands a new theory of factor shares. This is possibly the deepest question in the landscape.

**Tier 2: Hard, active, but may be solved by others before economists fully engage**

4. **The revelation principle with LLM agents**: Does incentive compatibility survive when agents can use LLMs to simulate strategic behavior? Dütting et al. (2024) gives a partial answer for specific auction formats. The deeper question — whether the revelation principle holds in general when agents are AI-augmented — requires a formalization of what "agent preferences" means when preferences are represented as LLMs rather than utility functions. This is genuinely hard, but CS researchers are attacking it.

5. **Price theory in AI-mediated markets**: Lucier et al. (2026) show informative AI search can reduce consumer surplus. The general question — how do price-setting, market discovery, and equilibrium work when AI mediates all consumer-seller interactions — is large. But this is being attacked by IO economists and platform economists; it may not require fundamental revision of price theory, just careful extensions of search theory and matching models.

**Tier 3: Real but shallower than they appear**

6. **The Becker/Mincer framework and near-zero marginal cost of cognitive labor**: The Espinal Maya (2026) decomposition shows the Mincer equation needs augmentation. But this is an empirical/measurement task, not a fundamental theoretical one. The *returns* to education may shift, but the *framework* (skills → productivity → wages) remains intact; it just needs new variables. A graduate student can do this in 3 years; it doesn't require 40 years.

7. **GPT as general purpose technology**: Brynjolfsson and others argue AI fits the GPT framework (slow diffusion, long lags before productivity gains, complementary innovations required). This is historically informed but doesn't generate new theoretical results — it's analogy, not analysis.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **No formal model of the knowledge problem with LLMs**: The Austrian economics literature is responding to AI in commentary form, but no one has built an actual model. This gap is significant because the Lange-Hayek debate is one of the deepest questions in economics, and AI poses an entirely new challenge.

2. **The "new scarcities" question is unformalized**: Kiesling's claim that AI creates new scarcities (in judgment, verification, credibility, attention) is theoretically important but has no formal model. What are the equilibrium prices of these new goods? Do they exhibit increasing or decreasing returns? How do they interact with AI's near-zero marginal cost goods?

3. **No theory of the direction of technical change with LLMs**: Standard growth theory treats the direction of technical change (labor-augmenting vs. capital-augmenting) as either exogenous or determined by factor prices in a directed technical change framework. But AI as a technology has distinctive properties — it is better at some tasks (language, pattern recognition) than others, and its improvement follows a different trajectory than previous capital goods. A theory of *why* AI automation follows the particular task-coverage trajectory it does would be new.

4. **The bargaining power dimension is absent**: All the models above treat wages as determined by marginal products. But the empirical literature on wage stagnation (e.g., Autor's work on labor market concentration) suggests bargaining power matters a lot. If AI reduces workers' outside options by making their skills more substitutable, this has labor-share effects that a purely competitive model misses. The connection between AI, monopsony power, and functional distribution is underdeveloped.

5. **Welfare theory for AI-mediated experience goods**: Lucier et al. (2026) handle search goods. Experience goods (where quality is revealed only after purchase) and credence goods (where quality can't be evaluated even after purchase) may be affected differently by AI mediation. The welfare analysis of AI recommendation systems for experience and credence goods is missing.

### Surprises

1. **The Acemoglu pessimism is structurally different from the Luddite pessimism**: Acemoglu isn't worried that AI will destroy all jobs. He's worried it will have *disappointing* macroeconomic effects — smaller productivity gains than hyped, while still concentrating gains in capital. This is a more theoretically interesting claim than the job-apocalypse narrative.

2. **Jones/Tonetti's "weak links" finding**: The growth explosion may be tamed not by anything humans do (policy, redistribution) but by production structure. If tasks are Leontief complements, even massive automation of some tasks leaves output bounded by the remaining unautomated tasks. This is a purely structural limit on AI growth impact — it doesn't require market failure or policy intervention. It's also testable.

3. **The Espinal Maya (2026) formality finding**: The binding constraint on capturing AI augmentation rents in developing countries is *labor market institutions* (formal vs. informal employment), not technology access. This is a surprising implication of the Mincerian decomposition — it suggests AI could be highly inequality-increasing in developing countries not because of job displacement but because informal workers can't contract into augmentation relationships.

4. **Kiesling's inversion of the computation/calculation distinction**: The mistake in the socialist calculation debate wasn't thinking you could compute general equilibrium — Lange was right that you *can* compute it. The mistake was thinking computation *is* economic calculation. AI makes this mistake newly relevant: powerful language models can simulate market behavior, but simulating is not the same as the discovery process of actual exchange. Whether this distinction survives when AI agents are actually *doing the buying and selling* (as in agentic markets) is a completely open question.

## Key Concepts and Pointers for Deep Dives

- **Trammell/Korinek** is the best map of growth-theory scenarios. The deep question it surfaces: when does the elasticity of substitution exceed 1 across the full task space?
- **Kiesling/Boettke-Candela** is the best entry to the knowledge problem angle. The gap: formalization.
- **Jones/Tonetti "weak links"** is the most surprising recent result and may be under-discussed. The pending question: what determines which tasks are "weak links" and whether this can change as AI improves?
- **Acemoglu vs. Aghion/Bunel** debate on task parameters is the active frontier of macro. The disagreement is partly empirical (what fraction of exposed tasks are economically profitable to automate?) and partly structural (is the ideas-production channel quantitatively important?).
- **Lucier et al. "Agentic Markets"** is the most rigorous current work on price theory in AI-mediated markets. The key finding (informative AI search can reduce consumer surplus by weakening competition) deserves more attention from economists.
- **Espinal Maya (2026)** represents a new methodological approach: using LLMs as *measurement instruments* for economic variables (task augmentability). This is potentially a new econometric toolkit.
