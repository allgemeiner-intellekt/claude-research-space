# Review — Round 1

## Overall Assessment

The surveys are impressively thorough on the intellectual landscape — Q1 (field or moment), Q2 (hard theory questions), and Q3 (who holds the territory) are covered with real depth, specific sources, and genuine analytical insight. The survey agents found strong material and organized it well. However, the research has a significant blind spot: it is much better at answering "is LLM x Economics intellectually interesting?" than at answering the user's actual decision problem, which is "should *I* do this, given who I am and what my alternatives are?" The career risk survey (Q4) has good job market data but lacks concrete specificity about (a) what the user's realistic alternatives look like, (b) what it actually takes to get admitted to the programs identified as promising, and (c) whether there are non-economics PhD paths that might be better fits. The research currently reads as excellent preparation for writing about the field, but insufficient preparation for writing honest advice to this specific person.

## Per-Question Evaluation

### Q1: Is "LLM x Economics" a field or a moment?
- **Coverage**: Strong. The three-strand decomposition (AI-as-tool, empirical AI economics, theoretical AI economics) is the survey's best analytical contribution. The historical comparisons (platform economics survived because Rochet-Tirole created new theory; internet economics was absorbed back into IO; blockchain economics produced no stars) are well-supported with specific evidence. The "what distinguishes permanent subfields from fads" framework (novel theoretical concepts, persistent empirical relevance, training/hiring feedback loops) is original and useful.
- **Strengths**: The internet economics comparison is genuinely sobering and well-evidenced. The observation that the most-cited papers are empirical exposure studies, not theoretical advances, is a sharp diagnostic. The Korinek "Economics of Transformative AI" vs. "Economics of AI" distinction is a real finding.
- **Gaps**: The historical comparisons, while directionally right, are still somewhat assertion-heavy. We know platform economics survived and internet economics was absorbed — but *how long did it take to become clear which was which?* Someone entering in 2003 (Rochet-Tirole's publication year) would not yet have known that two-sided markets would become permanent. The timing question matters enormously for the user: at what point in a technology wave does it become safe to bet on permanence? This is answerable with more historical specificity but hasn't been traced.

### Q2: What are the genuinely hard theoretical questions LLMs pose for economic theory?
- **Coverage**: Excellent — the best of the four surveys. The tiered structure (Tier 1: ideas-automation singularity, Hayekian knowledge problem, functional distribution; Tier 2: revelation principle with LLM agents, AI-mediated price theory; Tier 3: Mincerian updates, GPT-as-GPT framing) is well-reasoned and supported by specific papers. The Acemoglu vs. Aghion/Bunel disagreement on TFP gains is identified as a factor-of-100 divergence and correctly traced to parameter assumptions about elasticity of substitution.
- **Strengths**: The Jones/Tonetti "weak links" finding is genuinely surprising and well-explained. The Kiesling formalization gap (Austrian economics has commentary but no models) is a real finding that matters for dissertation design. The Lucier et al. "agentic markets" paper (informative AI search reducing consumer surplus) is an excellent find. The survey correctly identifies where the theoretical frontier actually is, not just where the publication volume is.
- **Gaps**: The survey describes what the hard questions are but doesn't go far enough in assessing which ones are *tractable for a non-technical PhD student*. The Tier 1 questions (ideas-automation singularity, knowledge problem formalization, functional distribution theory) are identified as research-program-sustaining — but they also require serious mathematical economics (growth theory, general equilibrium, mechanism design). The survey needs to connect the intellectual landscape to the user's actual capabilities more explicitly. What would a dissertation on the Hayekian knowledge problem actually look like? What math would be required? Who would advise it?

### Q3: Who holds the territory, and where would a new entrant fit?
- **Coverage**: Good, with real institutional detail. The identification of UVA (Korinek) and Toronto (Rotman/Agrawal-Gans-Goldfarb) as most hospitable for a non-standard candidate is well-supported. The observation that the field's leaders are calling for governance, welfare metrics, and political economy work — but that standard PhD programs don't train for it — is the survey's most important finding for the user.
- **Strengths**: The Stanford HAI Julia Irwin example (humanities PhD funded as HAI postdoc) is a useful data point. The Kevin Bryan job market candidates list provides real primary data on who is currently in the pipeline. The observation that AI economics candidates are concentrated in business school tracks (Kellogg, Rotman, Sloan) rather than pure economics departments is a genuine and useful finding. The publication landscape analysis (JEL welcomes non-empirical work; AER:Insights for shorter theory) is practical.
- **Gaps**: Three significant gaps:
  1. **Admissions reality**: The survey says top-5 programs require passing a mathematical qualifying sequence and mentions real analysis, linear algebra, probability, and statistics — but doesn't go deeper. What does the user actually need to do to get *admitted*? What GRE scores, what math coursework, what kind of pre-doctoral experience? This is the most decision-relevant information for someone considering applying, and it's almost entirely missing.
  2. **Alternative PhD paths**: The survey mentions HKS (Political Economy and Government PhD), Oxford GPI, and interdisciplinary programs in passing but doesn't investigate them. For a non-technical person with cross-disciplinary interests, a public policy PhD, an information science PhD, a business school PhD in strategy/organizations, or even an STS PhD might be more realistic and potentially equally productive paths to working on these questions. This is a major gap.
  3. **What "non-technical" actually means in practice**: The survey repeatedly notes the user is non-technical but never precisely defines what this implies for feasibility. Can a non-technical person learn enough math in 1-2 years of preparation to pass MIT's qualifying exams? What is the realistic preparation timeline? Are there programs that have lower technical bars but still produce graduates who work on these questions?

### Q4: What's the career risk calculus — PhD vs. job, and obsolescence risk?
- **Coverage**: Good on the macro job market picture (the 38-44% decline data is specific and well-sourced), but thin on the specific alternatives the user faces.
- **Strengths**: The job market data is concrete, multi-sourced, and alarming. The Brynjolfsson/Varian/Bakos historical cases are well-chosen and the correct lesson is drawn (they applied durable economic theory to new technology, and they were already at top institutions). The blockchain comparison (no academic stars produced) is a useful negative case. The Anthropic Institute finding is timely and real.
- **Gaps**: Three significant gaps:
  1. **What does the industry alternative actually look like?** The user is considering "just getting a job." But what job? The survey mentions tech economist roles, AI lab positions, and policy roles in passing but never concretely describes what a non-technical person with economics interests and cross-disciplinary instincts would actually do at an AI company, a think tank, or a policy shop. What are the actual job titles? What do people in these roles do day-to-day? What do they earn? What are the intellectual trade-offs vs. a PhD? This is arguably the most important gap in the entire research — you cannot evaluate a PhD bet without a clear picture of the counterfactual.
  2. **The 5-year bet and obsolescence**: The survey mentions that LLM capabilities may change dramatically during a PhD but doesn't trace through what this means concretely. What happens to a dissertation on, say, "LLM effects on legal services labor markets" if LLMs become dramatically more capable by 2030? The historical analogs (internet economics dissertations from 1998-2002) are mentioned but not traced to specific outcomes. How many of those people are still in academia? Did their dissertations age well? This is addressable with more targeted research.
  3. **The opportunity cost in dollar terms**: A 5-6 year PhD at a stipend of ~$40-50K/year vs. industry earnings of $100-200K+/year represents a $300K-900K opportunity cost (not counting career trajectory effects). The survey doesn't quantify this or compare it to the expected earnings premium from having a PhD vs. not.

## Deep-Dive Assignments

### Assignment 1: The industry counterfactual — what does "just getting a job" actually look like?
- **Question to answer**: For a non-technical person with economics interests, cross-disciplinary instincts, and enthusiasm about AI, what are the specific roles, organizations, and career paths available *right now* (2026) without a PhD? What do these roles pay, what do people do in them, and what are the intellectual trade-offs vs. academic research?
- **Why it matters**: The user's actual decision is PhD vs. job, not PhD vs. nothing. Without a concrete picture of the alternative, the PhD assessment hangs in the air. This is especially important because the user is non-technical — the industry roles available to them are different from those available to someone with a CS degree.
- **Where to start**: Search for roles at AI policy organizations (e.g., AI Now Institute, Partnership on AI, Centre for AI Safety), think tanks (Brookings, RAND, AEI), AI companies' policy/strategy teams (Anthropic's policy team, OpenAI's public policy team, Google DeepMind's governance team), consulting firms with AI practices, government roles (OSTP, NIST AI Safety Institute, EU AI Office). Look for actual job postings, salary data, and career trajectories of people in these roles. Also search for "AI policy career" or "AI governance career" guides.

### Assignment 2: Alternative PhD paths — beyond the economics department
- **Question to answer**: What PhD programs outside traditional economics departments could allow someone to work on the intersection of AI and economic/social theory? Specifically investigate: (a) public policy PhDs (HKS, Princeton SPIA, Chicago Harris), (b) business school PhDs in strategy, organizations, or management (Rotman, Wharton, MIT Sloan), (c) information science / iSchool PhDs (Berkeley, Michigan, Cornell), (d) interdisciplinary programs like Oxford's Future of Humanity Institute affiliates or Stanford's EESOR. For each: what are the admissions requirements, what does a dissertation look like, where do graduates place, and how does the technical bar compare to an economics PhD?
- **Why it matters**: The survey identified that the intellectual gaps in LLM x Economics (governance, methodology, welfare metrics, political economy) sit at disciplinary boundaries. A pure economics PhD may be the wrong vehicle for a non-technical person who wants to work on these boundary questions. If a public policy PhD or a business school PhD offers a lower technical bar, relevant advisors, and adequate placement into roles where the user could do this work, that changes the entire calculus.
- **Where to start**: Look at HKS's Political Economy and Government PhD requirements and recent dissertations. Look at Toronto Rotman's PhD program (since Agrawal/Gans/Goldfarb are there). Look at MIT Sloan's PhD in Technological Innovation, Entrepreneurship, and Strategic Management. Search for "AI governance PhD" or "technology policy PhD" programs. Check placement records.

### Assignment 3: What does it actually take to get into a top economics PhD program?
- **Question to answer**: For the specific programs identified as promising (UVA, Toronto Rotman, MIT), what are the realistic admissions requirements? Specifically: (a) What math courses are required or expected? (b) What GRE quantitative scores are competitive? (c) How important is pre-doctoral research experience (RA positions)? (d) Are there successful applicants from non-quantitative backgrounds, and if so, what did their preparation look like? (e) What is the realistic timeline for a non-technical person to prepare a competitive application?
- **Why it matters**: The survey says the qualifying sequence requires real analysis, linear algebra, probability, and statistics. But it doesn't say whether someone who hasn't taken these courses can realistically prepare in 1-2 years, or whether programs like UVA or Toronto Rotman have lower bars than MIT. This is the practical bottleneck question. If the answer is "you need 2 years of full-time math preparation plus a pre-doctoral RA position to have a shot," that dramatically changes the timeline and the PhD-vs-job calculus.
- **Where to start**: Search for economics PhD admissions guides (there are well-known ones by Athey, Stock, and others). Look at UVA Economics PhD requirements specifically. Look at Toronto Rotman PhD admissions. Search for "non-traditional economics PhD applicant" or "career changer economics PhD" experiences. Check whether Korinek or the UVA program have specific admissions criteria that are more or less accommodating of non-standard backgrounds.

### Assignment 4: Concrete dissertation topics for a non-technical theorist
- **Question to answer**: Given the intellectual landscape mapped in Q2 and Q3, what are 3-5 *specific* dissertation topics that a non-technical but theoretically sharp person could realistically execute? For each topic: (a) What is the research question? (b) What methods would be used? (c) Who could advise it? (d) What is the technical ceiling? (e) Where would it be published? (f) Does it have durable relevance even if LLM capabilities change dramatically?
- **Why it matters**: The survey identifies exciting intellectual territory (knowledge problem formalization, functional distribution theory, welfare metrics for AI economies) but doesn't connect these to what a specific person could actually *do*. A dissertation needs to be scoped to a person's skills. "Formalize the Hayekian knowledge problem with LLMs" is a direction, not a dissertation. We need concrete examples of what the dissertation would contain, what it would prove or show, and whether it requires mathematical sophistication that the user may not have.
- **Where to start**: Use the Tier 1 and Tier 2 questions from survey-hard-theory.md as starting points. Look at recent dissertations at UVA and Toronto in related areas. Consider which of the identified gaps (welfare metrics, theory of the firm with AI agents, political economy of AI market structure, Kiesling's "new scarcities" formalization) could be addressed with more discursive/conceptual methods vs. which require heavy formalism. Pay particular attention to the political economy and governance gaps — these may be where a non-technical person has the strongest comparative advantage.

## Verdict

**CONTINUE** — with all four assignments above.

The intellectual landscape is well-mapped, but the research is currently better at describing the field than at helping the user make a decision. The four deep dives above address the most decision-relevant gaps: what the alternative to a PhD looks like concretely, whether there are better-fitting PhD paths than a traditional economics department, what the admissions barrier actually involves, and what a feasible dissertation would look like for this specific person. Without these, the final report would be intellectually interesting but practically incomplete — it would tell the user a lot about LLM x Economics as a field and not enough about whether *they* should enter it, and how.
