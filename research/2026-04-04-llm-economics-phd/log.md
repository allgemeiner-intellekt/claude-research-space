# Research Log: LLM × Economics as a PhD Bet

## Scoping

**User's prompt:** Considering a PhD in economics; wants to evaluate whether "LLM × Economics" is a good area to pursue. Shared Kiesling's Substack on AI price theory. Also considering finding a job instead. Non-technical background, cross-disciplinary interests.

**What's actually interesting here:** The literal question ("should I do this PhD?") requires answering several deeper questions that the user probably hasn't decomposed yet. The strategic PhD decision is downstream of understanding what the field actually *is* — whether it has theoretical depth or is just applied hype, who controls it, and what happens when the technology moves faster than a dissertation timeline.

### Narrowed questions

**Q1: Is "LLM × Economics" a field or a moment?**
Past waves of "technology × economics" (internet economics, crypto/blockchain economics, platform economics) had very different fates. Some became permanent subfields with deep theoretical cores; others dissolved into applied work or faded when the technology matured. What structural features distinguish the ones that lasted? Where does LLM × Economics sit on that spectrum — and is the answer different for different sub-areas within it?

**Q2: What are the genuinely hard theoretical questions LLMs pose for economic theory?**
Not "measure the effect of ChatGPT on productivity" (empirical, will be done to death by 2028) but the deep theory puzzles: What happens to human capital theory when cognitive labor has near-zero marginal cost? How does price theory work when AI mediates market discovery? Does the Hayekian knowledge problem dissolve or intensify? These are the questions that could sustain a 40-year research program — or might turn out to be less deep than they appear.

**Q3: Who holds the territory, and where would a new entrant fit?**
The landscape of researchers, departments, publication venues, and funding. Is this field dominated by CS people doing econ, or econ people using AI? Are the senior people who could advise a PhD student actually in econ departments? What's the institutional map — and where are the gaps that a non-technical person with broad theoretical interests could exploit rather than compete head-on with ML engineers?

**Q4: What's the career risk calculus — PhD vs. job, and obsolescence risk?**
A PhD is a 5-6 year bet. LLM capabilities may change dramatically in that window. What happens to your dissertation if the technology leaps past your assumptions? Compare to historical cases: people who did PhD theses on internet economics in 1998, on blockchain in 2018. How did those bets pay off? And how does this compare to the opportunity cost of entering industry now, when AI-economics expertise is scarce and valuable?

### Why these questions

Q1 determines whether there's a *there* there. Q2 determines whether the intellectual substance is deep enough to be worth a PhD rather than a blog. Q3 determines whether the user specifically — non-technical, theoretically inclined — has a viable niche. Q4 forces the strategic comparison that the user is actually facing. Together they should produce an honest assessment, not cheerleading.

## Survey phase

Dispatched 4 survey agents in parallel (one per question). All completed.

**Key findings from surveys:**

- **Q1 (field or moment):** Three strands exist: AI-as-tool (technique, not field), empirical economics of AI (solid but applied), and theoretical AI economics (thinnest but most promising). Historical comparison: internet economics was absorbed back into IO; platform economics survived because Rochet-Tirole created genuinely new theory. LLM × economics is still searching for its equivalent theoretical innovation. Most-cited papers are empirical exposure studies, not theoretical advances.

- **Q2 (hard theory):** Three genuinely deep questions emerged: (1) the ideas-automation singularity and what determines bottleneck tasks (Jones & Tonetti 2026), (2) the Hayekian knowledge problem vs. LLMs — can AI aggregate counterfactual valuations or only data? (3) functional income distribution if Kaldor facts break. Shallower: human capital updates are tractable PhD work (not 40-year programs), mechanism design with AI is being colonized by CS, GPT-as-GPT framing is analogy not theory.

- **Q3 (landscape):** MIT (Acemoglu/Autor), Stanford (Brynjolfsson), UVA (Korinek), Toronto (Agrawal/Gans/Goldfarb) dominate. The field's leaders are explicitly calling for governance, welfare metrics, and political economy work — but standard PhD programs don't train for it. UVA (Korinek) and Toronto identified as most hospitable for a theory-oriented, non-technical entrant.

- **Q4 (career risk):** The 2025-2026 econ job market is historically bad (academic postings down 38-44%). Tech companies shifting from economists to CS roles. The people who won past technology-wave bets either started very early, applied durable theory frameworks, or were already established. Blockchain/crypto econ produced no academic stars. Best hedge: anchor dissertation in durable economic theory that *happens to* have AI applications, rather than being *about* LLMs.

## Review round 1

Reviewer verdict: **CONTINUE**. The intellectual landscape is well-mapped but the research was better at describing the field than at helping the user make a personal decision. Four deep-dive assignments issued: (1) the industry counterfactual, (2) alternative PhD paths, (3) admissions reality, (4) concrete dissertation topics.

## Deep-dive phase

Dispatched 4 deep-dive agents in parallel. All completed.

**Key findings from deep dives:**

- **Industry counterfactual:** The Anthropic Institute (launched March 2026, Korinek + Hitzig + Botvinick) is the most important concrete development — hiring analytical staff, accessible to non-PhD. AI policy roles pay $120K–$280K vs. PhD stipend ~$45K. But non-PhD roles are predominantly reactive/supporting, not agenda-setting. The think tank ceiling without a PhD is real: you stall at Senior Policy Analyst (~$100-140K). Opportunity cost of PhD: ~$500K–$750K in foregone income.

- **Alternative PhD paths:** Harvard PEG (the classic "political economy without econ PhD") closed to new admissions in 2022. MIT TIES is effectively MIT-level math. The real non-technical-friendly options are iSchools (Cornell IS, Berkeley I School, Michigan SI) and Oxford's OII DPhil (Carl-Benedikt Frey is programme director). Princeton SPIA STEP possible but only 4 admits/year to the relevant cluster. The strategic fork: (a) become quantitative first → target Rotman/UVA, or (b) enter Cornell IS/OII now for differently-positioned but serious academic work.

- **Admissions reality:** The math bar is genuinely non-negotiable. 77% of econ PhD applicants have taken real analysis. Average GRE quant among *applicants* (not admits) is 167.69/170 (~97th percentile). UVA is relatively accessible (94th percentile median). Realistic timeline from non-quantitative background: 3–5 years of preparation *before* the PhD starts (18-24 months math → 1-2 years predoc → then 5-6 year PhD). Rotman PhD is the most accessible named program.

- **Dissertation topics:** Five concrete topics proposed, ranked by math requirement. Best fit for this specific user: (1) epistemology of AI-mediated economic research (lowest math, extends Korinek's JEL paper), (2) social alignment as welfare economics (Arrow + Sen applied to AI), (3) political economy of regulatory capture in AI markets (comparative institutional, no econometrics needed). Strongest recommendation: Topics 2+3 as three-essay dissertation with Korinek at UVA. Most intellectually original: Topic 5 (formalizing Hayekian knowledge with LLMs) but hardest placement path.

## Review round 2

Verdict: **SATISFIED**. The research is ready for report writing.

Key synthesis guidance from reviewer: The user faces three fundamentally different bets (economics PhD, alternative PhD, skip PhD), not a binary decision. The report should take a position: Bet B (non-economics PhD at Cornell IS or Oxford OII) is probably the right answer for this specific person, unless they are willing to spend 3+ years becoming quantitative first, in which case Bet A (UVA with Korinek) is the highest-ceiling play.

Four tensions to resolve in synthesis: (1) the timeline paradox (3-5 years prep may consume the window), (2) the "become quantitative" vs. "find non-quantitative path" fork, (3) intellectual quality vs. credentialing trade-off, (4) the "just get a job" option is stronger than initially apparent but has a real ceiling.

Moving to report writing.
