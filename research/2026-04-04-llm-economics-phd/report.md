# Three Bets: LLM × Economics and the PhD Question

You asked whether pursuing a PhD in "LLM × Economics" is a good idea. The honest answer is that the question dissolves into three fundamentally different bets, each with a different timeline, a different risk profile, and a different version of what your life looks like in 2035. The single most important finding from this research is not about the field — it's about you. A non-technical person with cross-disciplinary instincts and philosophical depth faces a genuine strategic fork, and the right choice depends on what you're actually optimizing for.

Here is the essential picture. "LLM × Economics" is real but thin where it matters most. The institutional infrastructure is impressive: recurring NBER conferences, a QJE publication, Stanford postdocs, and the Anthropic Institute hiring economists [1][2][3]. But the field's most-cited papers are empirical exposure studies — who gets displaced, by how much — not theoretical advances [4]. The deeper questions that could sustain a 40-year research program (What happens to growth theory when AI automates the production of ideas? Does AI dissolve or intensify Hayek's knowledge problem? Why does the labor share decline even for high-skilled workers under cognitive automation?) are identified by the field's own leaders but barely worked on [5][6][7]. The field is calling for exactly the kind of work a theoretically inclined person could do. But the institutions that could train you to do it — economics PhD programs — require 3–5 years of mathematical preparation you don't currently have. This is the central tension: the intellectual opportunity is genuine, the institutional pathway is brutal, and the window may not stay open forever.

## What the field actually is (and isn't)

"LLM × Economics" is not one field. It's three separate strands wearing a trench coat:

**Strand 1: AI as a research tool.** Korinek's JEL paper on using LLMs for literature reviews, coding, and drafting is the landmark here [8]. This strand has enormous current energy and will be obsolete as a research topic within five years. Using Stata was never a field; using GPT-5 won't be either.

**Strand 2: Empirical economics of AI.** The Brynjolfsson-Li-Raymond QJE paper showing 14% productivity gains for AI-assisted customer service workers [3], the Acemoglu-Autor-Hazell-Restrepo labor market studies [9], the Eloundou et al. "GPTs are GPTs" exposure analysis [4]. This is the mature, publication-generating core of the field. It uses standard econometric methods applied to a new technology. It will persist — economists have always studied major technologies — but it doesn't require or produce new economic theory. It's applied labor economics with a timely data source.

**Strand 3: Theoretical economics of AI.** What happens to growth theory, the theory of the firm, price theory, and welfare economics when cognitive labor approaches zero marginal cost? This is the thinnest strand, and the only one that could constitute a genuinely new field. The field's own leaders — Korinek, Brynjolfsson, Agrawal — published a research agenda paper in 2025 explicitly calling for work on governance, welfare metrics, information flows, and the political economy of AI [10]. Almost no one is answering that call.

The historical comparison is instructive. Platform economics (Rochet-Tirole, 2003) became a permanent subfield because it produced a genuinely novel theoretical concept: cross-group externalities with distinct pricing implications that changed how economists think about markets far beyond the internet [11]. Internet economics of the late 1990s produced no such concept — and there is no "internet economics" subfield today. The insights were absorbed back into industrial organization. Blockchain economics (2017–2022) is the cautionary case: enormous energy, dedicated conferences, a Cambridge textbook, and no identifiable academic stars or lasting theoretical contribution [12].

Where does LLM × Economics sit? It has the institutional momentum of platform economics and the empirical relevance to sustain it — AI is obviously not going away. But it has not yet produced its Rochet-Tirole moment: a theoretical concept that forces economists to think differently about something they already study. The "prediction machine" framing from Agrawal-Gans-Goldfarb was a good start, but it was designed for narrow ML, not LLMs [13]. The field is searching for its unifying concept, and that search is itself a research opportunity.

## The hard theoretical questions — and which ones are actually hard

Not all "deep" questions are equally deep. The investigation surfaced a clear hierarchy:

**Genuinely hard, research-program-sustaining:**

*The ideas-automation singularity.* When AI can automate the production of ideas (not just goods), does the growth rate explode? Jones and Tonetti's preliminary 2026 paper gives the most surprising answer in the field: probably not, because "weak links" in production structure — tasks that resist automation and are complementary to everything else — tame the growth explosion [5]. But their model opens more questions than it answers. What determines which tasks are weak links? Can this change as AI improves? Is there a general theory of task complementarity that predicts when bottlenecks move? This is a fundamental open question in growth theory with real formal machinery behind it.

*The Hayekian knowledge problem.* Kiesling's argument, building on a Santa Fe Institute workshop, is precise and underappreciated: the mistake in the socialist calculation debate wasn't thinking you could compute general equilibrium — Lange was right that you can. The mistake was thinking computation *is* economic calculation [14]. Prices don't just transmit information; they aggregate *counterfactual valuations* — what people would have traded under different conditions — that only emerge through the discovery process of actual exchange. LLMs can aggregate data but cannot generate these counterfactual signals. A February 2026 preprint calls this the "algorithmic calculation problem" [15]. Neither Kiesling nor the preprint has formalized this distinction. The gap between the argument and the model is where a PhD project lives.

*Functional income distribution under cognitive automation.* Minniti et al. (2025) found across 238 European regions that AI patenting reduces the labor share even for high-skilled workers — the opposite of the "low-skill displacement" narrative [16]. Bloom et al. (2024) offer a model: AI substitutes for high-skill tasks more than low-skill ones, compressing the skill premium [17]. But nobody has a theory of *why* the labor share is what it is in the first place. The Kaldor Facts (stable labor share, stable growth rate) are empirical regularities without deep theoretical explanation. If AI breaks them — as Trammell and Korinek predict [6] — we need new theory. This connects directly to classical political economy: Ricardo, Marx, and the question of who captures the gains from technological change.

**Real but shallower than they appear:**

The Becker-Mincer human capital update is already being formalized. Espinal Maya's 2026 paper decomposes the Mincerian wage equation for AI-augmented economies with LLM-generated augmentability scores [18]. It's solid PhD work, but it's tractable — three years, not forty. Mechanism design with AI agents is being competently attacked by CS researchers at Google and UChicago [19]. Economists may arrive late. The "GPT as general-purpose technology" framing is historical analogy, not theory — it doesn't generate new results.

**The most striking disagreement in the field** is between Acemoglu's conservative estimate (≤0.66% TFP gain over 10 years from AI) and the Aghion-Bunel/Korinek-Trammell models that predict potentially explosive growth [20][6][21]. This is not a small disagreement — it's a factor of 100, driven by different assumptions about the elasticity of substitution between capital and labor across all tasks. Nobody has a good theory of what determines this elasticity. A PhD that answered that question would be one of the most important economics dissertations of the decade.

## Who you'd be competing with — and where you'd fit

The territory is held by a small, elite cluster: Acemoglu and Autor at MIT, Brynjolfsson at Stanford, Korinek at UVA, Agrawal-Gans-Goldfarb at Toronto Rotman [22]. They publish in QJE, AER, JEL. They meet annually at the NBER AI conference. Almost all active PhD students in the field are technically strong — the 2025 job market candidates doing AI work come from MIT, Princeton, Stanford, Northwestern, and PSE, with strong IO, labor, or macro foundations [23].

But there's a genuine gap. The field's own leaders are explicitly calling for work on governance, welfare metrics, political economy, and epistemological questions — and almost no one is producing it [10]. The Brynjolfsson-Korinek-Agrawal research agenda paper names "information flows," "human well-being," and "governance" as priority areas with the least current coverage. The Stanford Digital Economy Lab postdoc explicitly asks for "welfare metrics that account for AI's impact on human flourishing" [24]. Acemoglu's "Power and Progress" with Simon Johnson is comparative political economy — not empirical labor economics [25].

The gap is real. The question is whether you can position yourself to fill it.

## The three bets

### Bet A: Become an economist

Spend 18–24 months learning real analysis, linear algebra, probability, and statistics. Apply to pre-doctoral research positions — MIT's Mullainathan/Rambachan "BikeShop" lab runs a predoc explicitly focused on AI × economics [26]. Complete a 1–2 year predoc. Apply to PhD programs — UVA (Korinek, median admitted GRE quant at 94th percentile) or Toronto Rotman (Agrawal/Gans/Goldfarb, average admitted GRE quant 166) would be the targets [27][28]. Complete a 5–6 year PhD.

**Total timeline: 8–11 years from now to PhD.**

The math bar is genuinely non-negotiable. 77% of economics PhD applicants have taken real analysis. The average GRE quant score among *applicants* (not admits) is 167.69 out of 170, roughly the 97th percentile [29]. UVA is relatively accessible at the 94th percentile, but "relatively accessible" still means competing with a pool where everyone has taken real analysis and most have done pre-doctoral research.

**What you'd do:** Write a dissertation anchored in durable economic theory that happens to engage AI. The strongest version: Topics 2 and 3 from the dissertation design — a welfare economics framework for AI alignment (extending Korinek and Balwit's "Aligned with Whom?" using Arrow's impossibility and Sen's capability approach) paired with a political economy of regulatory capture in AI markets (extending the Stigler-Peltzman framework to handle AI's distinctive properties) [30][31]. Three essays. Korinek at UVA as primary advisor.

**Your competitive advantage:** The field needs someone who can read Hayek, Arrow, and Sen together — and most PhD students trained on empirical methods can't. Your Water Theory of Value work shows you can engage classical political economy at a serious level. Your Why-Question Violence work shows facility with van Fraassen-style epistemology applied to social science. These are directly relevant.

**The risk:** The 2025–2026 economics job market is the worst on record — academic postings down 38–44% from historical averages, applications per posting up 36% [32][33][34]. The tech "Plan B" has weakened as companies shift from economists to CS roles [35]. By the time you graduate (2034–2037), the field may be mature, the window may have closed, and you'll have foregone $500K–$750K in income relative to an industry career [36].

**When this bet makes sense:** If you want intellectual autonomy above all else. If you're willing to spend a decade becoming the person who could write these papers. If the prospect of doing supporting work on someone else's research agenda for the rest of your career is genuinely intolerable.

### Bet B: Do a different PhD

Skip the years of math preparation. Apply now (or with minimal preparation) to programs that accept non-technical scholars and allow serious work on AI and economic/social questions.

**The most promising programs:**

*Cornell Information Science.* No GRE required or accepted. Full funding. Explicit focus areas include "markets, technology policy, algorithmic mechanism design" [37]. Genuinely welcomes social science backgrounds. Strong placement record. The research questions you'd ask are the same — the credential and the audience are different.

*Oxford Internet Institute DPhil.* Carl-Benedikt Frey — one of the most-cited economists working on AI and labor markets — directs the programme and accepts DPhil students [38]. The programme explicitly accommodates non-computational methods. Oxford's prestige travels well for policy careers. Average student age is 32.

*Berkeley School of Information.* No GRE required since 2021. "Information Economics and Policy" is a named area. They explicitly invite "students from diverse backgrounds, including humanities and social sciences" [39].

**Total timeline: 4–5 years to PhD.**

**What you'd do:** Similar questions to Bet A, but positioned within information science or technology and society rather than economics. The epistemology of AI-mediated research (Topic 1) and the political economy of regulatory capture (Topic 3) work perfectly in these programs. You'd publish in the Journal of Economic Methodology, Economics and Philosophy, Information Systems Research — respectable outlets, but not AER or QJE.

**The risk:** You will not be an economist. Hiring committees at economics departments won't know what to do with your application. You'll place into information science faculty positions, policy schools, or interdisciplinary programs. This is a different career — not a lesser one, but a different one. If your goal is publishing in top economics journals and being taken seriously by Acemoglu's seminar, this is not the path.

**What's gone that many people don't know:** Harvard Kennedy School's Political Economy and Government (PEG) PhD — the classic "political economy without being a pure economist" program — closed to new admissions in Fall 2022 [40]. MIT Sloan's TIES program, which sounds like a less-math alternative, actually requires passing MIT/Harvard PhD-level microeconomics and econometrics in year one [41]. The "easy" alternatives are fewer than people think.

**When this bet makes sense:** If you want to do serious academic work on AI and society but don't want to spend 3–5 years learning real analysis first. If you're comfortable being read by policy people and technologists rather than by economists. If you think the most important questions about AI's economic implications sit at disciplinary boundaries that economics departments are poorly equipped to address — and you may be right about this.

### Bet C: Skip the PhD

Take a job now. The Anthropic Institute, launched March 2026 with Korinek, Zoë Hitzig, and Matt Botvinick, is hiring analytical staff to support its economics research [42]. AI policy organizations are expanding — IAPS, Future of Life Institute, SaferAI, the EU AI Office [43]. OpenAI's policy team pays $155K–$280K [44]. Think tanks will hire you as a research analyst at $55K–$90K, rising to Senior Policy Analyst at $100K–$140K within five years.

**Total timeline: immediate.**

**The ceiling:** Without a PhD, you will top out at Senior Policy Analyst or equivalent. The Fellow-level roles at Brookings and RAND — where intellectual autonomy lives — almost universally require a PhD or equivalent authority [45]. The Anthropic Institute's analytical staff role is a supporting role, not a principal investigator role. You will be synthesizing and communicating other people's ideas, not generating your own research agenda. The think tank ceiling is real, and the intellectual quality of non-PhD work is — honestly — predominantly reactive.

**The opportunity cost runs the other way too.** Five years in a PhD program at $45K/year while an AI policy career at $150K–$200K/year is available represents $500K–$750K in foregone income. But if you spend those five years in a supporting role and discover at 35 that you want to set your own research agenda, you'll face the same PhD decision with less time and more to lose.

**When this bet makes sense:** If you value immediate impact over eventual autonomy. If you're not sure you want a research career and need to test the waters. If the specific role — the Anthropic Institute analytical position, proximity to Korinek and Hitzig, access to proprietary Claude usage data — is interesting enough to be worth doing for its own sake, not as a consolation prize.

## What I actually think

The research points toward **Bet B as the best fit for who you are right now** — specifically Cornell IS or Oxford OII. Here's why.

Bet A (the economics PhD) has the highest ceiling but the most punishing timeline. Eight to eleven years is not a rounding error. The mathematical preparation alone (18–24 months of real analysis, linear algebra, and statistics) is a genuine full-time commitment that delays everything else. And the economics job market you'd graduate into is historically bad, with no clear evidence of recovery. The people who won past technology-wave PhD bets — Brynjolfsson, Varian, Tirole — were either already at elite institutions before the wave, or were applying existing theoretical frameworks from established positions [46][47]. None of them started from a non-quantitative background and spent a decade becoming economists in order to study a technology that was moving faster than their dissertation timeline.

Bet C (skip the PhD) is financially rational and immediately gratifying, but it concedes intellectual autonomy permanently — or at least until you go back to graduate school in your mid-30s, which is what many people in reactive policy roles end up doing anyway. The frustration of supporting someone else's research agenda, compounded over a decade, is a real cost that doesn't show up in salary tables.

Bet B threads the needle. Cornell IS or Oxford OII lets you start serious academic work on AI governance and political economy within a year, not a decade. The questions you'd ask — epistemology of AI-mediated research, welfare metrics for AI, political economy of regulatory capture — are the same questions the field's leaders are calling for. You'd work alongside people studying markets, technology policy, and algorithmic systems. You'd graduate in 4–5 years with a credential that positions you for policy schools, interdisciplinary faculty positions, and governance roles.

The trade-off is real: you won't be publishing in the AER or attending Acemoglu's seminar. But the questions that interest you — the ones where your comparative advantage lies — are questions that sit at disciplinary boundaries economics departments are poorly set up to address. A person who can read Hayek, Arrow, Sen, and van Fraassen together, who can do comparative institutional analysis with the rigor of Acemoglu's "Power and Progress" without needing frontier econometrics, may find that an information science or technology-and-society program is not a consolation prize but the right institutional home.

**Unless** you discover, after honest reflection, that intellectual autonomy within academic economics specifically — being recognized as an economist, publishing in economics journals, attending NBER conferences — is what you actually want. In that case, Bet A is the only honest path, and you should start taking real analysis this summer. But go in with open eyes: the preparation timeline is 3–5 years before the PhD starts, the PhD is 5–6 years, the job market is terrible, and the field may be mature before you finish.

The worst bet is the unexamined one — starting a PhD "in LLM × Economics" at a mid-tier program without the mathematical preparation, the right advisor, or a clear sense of which of these three paths you're actually on.

## Open questions

- **What determines the elasticity of substitution between AI and labor at the aggregate level?** This is the key parameter in the Acemoglu vs. Aghion/Korinek debate, and nobody has a theory of it. A dissertation that answered this question would be field-defining, but it requires serious mathematical economics.

- **Can the Hayekian distinction between computation and economic calculation be formalized?** Kiesling's argument and the "algorithmic calculation problem" preprint identify the gap. If formalized, this has implications for the century-old Lange-Hayek debate and for whether AI-mediated central planning is theoretically possible.

- **What happens to the Coasian theory of the firm when AI agents can negotiate contracts?** The "headless firm" literature is nascent (mostly arXiv preprints). The economic theory hasn't caught up to the phenomenon.

- **Is there a welfare metric for AI economies that doesn't collapse into either utilitarianism (vulnerable to Arrow's impossibility) or GDP (increasingly irrelevant)?** Brynjolfsson's DEL postdoc explicitly asks for this. Sen's capability approach is the obvious starting framework, but no one has applied it to AI.

- **What happened to the people who did blockchain economics dissertations in 2017–2020?** Systematic placement data for this cohort would be enormously informative for the timing question, but it doesn't appear to exist.
