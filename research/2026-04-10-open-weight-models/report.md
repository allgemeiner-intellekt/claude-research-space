# Anthropic and Open-Weight Models: Costly Action Under Trajectory Uncertainty

Three days before this report was written, on April 7, 2026, Anthropic announced that its most capable model — Claude Mythos Preview — would not be released to the public at all [1, 2]. Not closed-API only, the way Claude has always been. *Not released*. Access is restricted to roughly fifty vetted defensive cybersecurity organizations under Project Glasswing — Apple, Google, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Amazon, the Linux Foundation, and forty-plus others — bound by terms limiting use to defensive work [1, 3]. The model successfully exploited 181 Firefox JavaScript-engine vulnerabilities in red-team testing where Claude Opus 4.6 succeeded twice [4]. Anthropic committed up to $100M in usage credits to Glasswing partners and donated $4M in cash to open-source security foundations [1]. The model card — 244 pages — exists; the weights do not, and on Anthropic's stated plan they never will be deployed at scale until safety mitigations catch up.

This is the cleanest empirical test of Anthropic's position that has ever existed, and it is the right place to begin. The lazy reading of the Anthropic-on-open-weights debate treats it as a thinly disguised business move: a company that sells API access argues that API access is the only safe way to deploy AI; how convenient. The Mythos Preview event makes that reading harder to sustain. A pure moat-maximizer does not unilaterally forgo deployment of its best model. It does not pay the opportunity cost of foregone API revenue, lost benchmark prestige, and ceded market share to the next generation of competitor models — all without external regulation forcing the choice. Anthropic just did. It is the first general-release withholding of a frontier model since OpenAI's GPT-2 staged release in 2019 [5], and it happened in the same eight-month window in which OpenAI reversed its 2023 closed-models stance and shipped gpt-oss-120b under Apache 2.0 [6, 7]. The contrast is almost theatrical.

But the costly-action story is not the whole story, and a research report that stopped there would be doing the work of Anthropic's PR team. The deeper point is that the *whole framing* — safety argument versus business argument, sincere versus cynical — is the wrong analytical move. What you actually have is a coordination problem under irreversibility and trajectory uncertainty, in which the safety case is structurally a precautionary argument rather than a measured-harm argument, the strategic case is structurally a rent-seeking dynamic regardless of any individual actor's intentions, and these two things coexist. The interesting question is not "which is it, really?" The interesting question is what epistemic posture an intellectually honest reader should adopt when both are true at once. That is the question this report tries to answer, and it is the question the rest of these pages are about.

## The position, in the negative

The first thing to notice about Anthropic's position on open-weight release is that it does not exist as a stand-alone document. There is no "Anthropic's Case Against Open Weights" essay corresponding to Mark Zuckerberg's "Open Source AI Is the Path Forward" [8] or to Ilya Sutskever's bluntly self-critical "we were wrong, flat out" interview at the GPT-4 launch [9]. The closest things are scattered: Dario Amodei's interview comments distinguishing "open weights" from "open source" (the philosophical point being that you cannot read or modify a model's logic by examining its weights, so the open-source software analogy is a category error) [10]; the Responsible Scaling Policy, which contains weight-security provisions but never explicitly addresses public release; and now Mythos Preview, which is the position made manifest. If you want to know what Anthropic believes, you have to read it off behavior and structural commitments. This is itself a notable fact — it suggests the company has made a calculation that the cost of articulating the position openly (creating a target for critics, commiting to a specific frame) exceeds the benefit of being clear.

The structural commitment lives in the Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP), now in its third version [11]. The RSP defines AI Safety Levels (ASL); each level pairs a Capability Threshold with Required Safeguards covering both deployment and security. ASL-3 — activated for Claude Opus 4 in May 2025 — explicitly requires "increased internal security measures that make it harder to steal model weights" against threat models including "sophisticated non-state attackers" [12]. This is the load-bearing logical move: if your safety framework treats weight protection as central, then *releasing* weights is not a separate decision but a violation of the framework's premise. The RSP never says "we will not release open weights" because, by its own logic, it does not need to. ASL-3 capability and open release are mutually exclusive at definitional level. Anthropic's position is therefore not "we believe open-weight release is wrong" but "the safety architecture we have committed to makes open-weight release of capable models internally incoherent."

Compare this to the rest of the frontier-lab landscape and the picture sharpens. OpenAI traveled the longest distance: from "freely collaborate" in its 2015 founding documents, through the 2019 staged GPT-2 release, to Sutskever's "if you believe AGI is going to be extremely, unbelievably potent, then it just does not make sense to open-source... I fully expect that in a few years it's going to be completely obvious to everyone that open-sourcing AI is just not wise" [9], and then — in a remarkable inversion — to the August 2025 release of gpt-oss-120b with a justification framed almost entirely in geopolitical "soft power" terms ("democratic AI" versus "autocratic AI") and almost no mention of the safety arguments that drove the closure two years earlier [6]. Meta has gone the other direction in slow motion: maximalist open-weight identity around Llama 1–4, then a quiet July 2025 carve-out for "superintelligence" from Zuckerberg himself ("we'll need to be... careful about what we choose to open source") [13]. Google operates a clean dual track without ideological commentary: Gemma open-weight, Gemini closed [14]. Anthropic is alone in having held the same structural position from founding to present — a fact that is either evidence of principled consistency or evidence that the safety argument was always a brand commitment and would have been hard to reverse without reputational cost. Both readings are defensible. They are not mutually exclusive.

## The empirical case is a trajectory argument

The honest reckoning starts with the question of what is actually known about the harm open-weight release enables. The literature here is far thinner and far less alarming than the rhetoric of the debate suggests, and the part that should matter most to an economist is the *structure* of the evidence rather than its magnitude.

In January 2024, RAND published the most rigorously controlled empirical test of LLM bioweapons uplift that exists: dozens of researchers in a seven-week red-team exercise role-playing as malign actors planning a biological attack, with some teams given LLM access plus the internet and others given only the internet, judged on plan viability by an expert panel [15]. The result was a clean null. No statistically significant difference between teams. The "unfortunate outputs" the LLMs produced were verified, in every case, to be available via a few clicks on CDC and academic websites. The team that scored highest happened to have LLM access but its winning plan came from academic literature; the AI was redundant. This is the strongest single piece of empirical evidence in the entire debate, and it cuts hard against the precautionary case as it was being deployed in 2024.

The result has been weaponized — by both sides. The honest reading requires three corrections that the survey notes for this report initially missed and that the deep-dive round caught.

First: RAND 2024 tested 2022–2023-vintage models — roughly GPT-3.5 and early GPT-4 class. The authors themselves explicitly flagged that their result did not reveal "how close" the frontier was to the threshold where uplift would become real, and warned that the rapid improvement curve made the question deserve continued monitoring [15]. A null result against a moving target tells you about the moment of measurement, not the trajectory.

Second: the Virology Capabilities Test (VCT) — a benchmark of 322 multimodal questions sourced from 57 PhD-level virologists and tested for Google-proofness — found that OpenAI's o3 scores 43.8% on virology troubleshooting tasks and outperforms 94% of expert virologists on questions matched to their declared specialties [16]. The headline number is real. But the headline framing is misleading without context: virologists themselves only score 22.1% on their own specialty subsets, because VCT specifically targets the tacit lab knowledge that lives in hallway conversations rather than textbooks, and individual specialists lack access to the field's full distributed knowledge. VCT measures whether a model has synthesized the wisdom-of-the-crowd of academic virology — an underappreciated capability — but it explicitly does not measure "hazardousness per se" [16]. It does not measure attack planning. It measures the cognitive prerequisites that may or may not translate into uplift.

Third — and this is the key piece — in December 2025 RAND itself published a follow-up paper (Brent and McKelvey, PE-A3853-1) that updated its 2024 finding for 2024-vintage models [17]. The same institution that found no risk eighteen months earlier now reported that Llama 3.1 405B, GPT-4o, and Claude 3.5 Sonnet "successfully provide accurate instructions and guidance for recovering a live poliovirus from a construct built from commercially obtained synthetic DNA," and that the same pattern would extend to other pathogenic viruses. RAND was updating itself. The trajectory is the story.

What this means is that the empirical case for restricting open-weight release at the frontier is a *trajectory* argument with specific narrow empirical anchors, not a measured-deaths argument. Anyone citing RAND 2024 as settling the question is overclaiming. Anyone citing Ryan Greenblatt's well-known "100,000 expected fatalities per year" estimate from Anthropic-adjacent AI safety research as a measurement is also overclaiming — Greenblatt himself labels it explicitly as a Fermi estimate built on COVID's death toll multiplied by an assumed 0.1%/year pandemic-probability prior, with his own conclusion being that even granting the 100K figure, open-weight models at the threshold would be net-beneficial because of their value for alignment research and societal awareness [18]. Both sides of the debate have been citing this number out of its own author's stated context.

The honest summary, which should matter to anyone making policy from outside the labs: there is essentially no published evidence that open-weight LLMs have caused mass-casualty harm, the *measured* uplift over internet baselines is small for current open-weight models on standard tasks, but the *capability* trajectory for relevant biological knowledge is real and crossed a threshold roughly between 2024 and 2025. The Anthropic ASL-3 activation in May 2025 explicitly cited VCT improvements as part of its reasoning, while also explicitly noting that it had "not yet determined whether Claude Opus 4 capabilities actually require the protections of the ASL-3 Standard" [12]. The Mythos Preview decision is even more explicit: it is precautionary about cybersecurity uplift, not retrospective about harms already caused.

This trajectory structure has a property economists should immediately recognize: it is asymmetric in its costs of being wrong. Wait for measured harm to justify restriction, and by the time you have it the weights for the harm-causing model are already out and uncallable. Restrict before measured harm, and you forgo the diffuse benefits of open-weight ecosystems based on what may turn out to be a trajectory that bent. This is structurally identical to climate-policy debates in the 1990s: the asymmetry between irreversible damage and forgone economic activity does not fix the question of which way to bet, but it does explain why honest people who agree on the empirical facts can land on opposite sides of the policy. The interesting move is not to demand that one side concede; it is to recognize what kind of argument is actually being had.

## The strategic dimension is real, and unsentimental economists should say so

The economics-trained reader's natural instinct will be to suspect that whatever Anthropic publicly says about safety is at least partly downstream of the fact that closed-weight API access is its entire business model. This instinct is correct, and the literature supporting it is more substantial than the AI policy debate usually acknowledges. It is also less damning than the strongest version of the cynicism would suggest. Both of those things have to hold for the analysis to be honest.

The right analytical frame is Bruce Yandle's "bootleggers and baptists" theory from the public choice tradition: durable regulations emerge when moral reformers (baptists) and self-interested incumbents (bootleggers) coincidentally want the same rule for different reasons [19]. The baptists provide political legitimacy and rhetorical air cover; the bootleggers provide lobbying muscle and continuity. Yandle's original 1983 essay was a Cato magazine piece, not peer-reviewed — but the framework has been formalized in *Public Choice* (Shogren 1990 [20]; Yandle 2021 [21]) and *Constitutional Political Economy* (McLaughlin, Smith and Sobel 2019 [22]), and it sits as a natural complement to George Stigler's foundational 1971 theory of economic regulation [23]. The framework has been applied to digital regulation in *The Independent Review* (Stagnaro 2020 [24]) and is alive in working empirical research across sectors. An MSc Economics student deploying the framework is on solid academic ground if they cite the peer-reviewed lineage rather than the magazine essays.

What the framework predicts for AI regulation is crisp: incumbent frontier labs will support compliance regimes whose absolute costs they can absorb but whose marginal-cost burden is higher for smaller competitors and open-source alternatives. The compute thresholds in California's SB 1047 ($100M training-cost trigger) are exactly this structure — they do not apply to Anthropic's existing models, do not apply to current Llama models, but would create compliance overhead that deters entrants. The political behavior around SB 1047 is close to a revealed-preference test for which firms benefit from which framing. Anthropic supported the bill, but only after negotiating out the government oversight committee that would have created costly accountability for itself [25]. OpenAI opposed the bill, citing federal-preemption concerns. Meta and Yann LeCun opposed it vigorously on grounds that it would burden open-source releases. The pattern tracks competitive interests almost perfectly: firms with closed-weight API businesses leaned supportive (after carving out the parts costly to them), the firm whose AI strategy depends on open-weight viability opposed [26]. None of this proves bad faith, but it does show that the bootleggers-and-baptists prediction holds up empirically.

Two further pieces of academic evidence sharpen this. First, Lancieri, Edelson and Bechtold's forthcoming *Theoretical Inquiries in Law* article on AI regulation applies a competition-law and law-and-economics frame to show how AI firms behave strategically with regulatory levers, drawing on historical analogs from privacy law, competition policy, and cloud computing [27]. Second, Gutiérrez, Rykaczewski and Vulcheva's 2026 SSRN event study on the EU AI Act finds that *larger* AI companies experience positive abnormal stock returns when regulation tightens, while smaller firms experience negative returns — exactly the distributional pattern bootleggers-and-baptists predicts, expressed in financial market data [28]. The Azoulay, Krieger and Nagaraj NBER paper "Old Moats for New Models" (2024) provides the supporting industrial-organization theory: tight control over complementary assets (compute, data, talent) produces oligopolistic market structure in AI, and the paper concludes pointedly that "the best hopes for a vibrant open source AI ecosystem might rest on the presence of a 'rogue' technology giant who might choose openness as a strategic weapon against other incumbents" [29]. That technology giant is Meta. The framing is exactly right.

So far, this is the strongest version of the cynical reading: the safety argument is convenient cover for a coordination of incumbent interests, the distributional consequences of safety regulation track the economic incentives, and you should not expect the rhetoric to be reliable. But here is where the analysis has to be careful, because the cynical reading runs into two facts that it cannot easily explain.

The first is the Mythos Preview event, with which this report opened. A pure rent-seeker does not voluntarily pay opportunity costs of this magnitude. The model exists; the system card is published; the capability is real; competitors will catch up within a generation. Withholding is costly and its benefits accrue diffusely. The cynical reading would predict that Anthropic would deploy Mythos Preview through its API with aggressive safety classifiers and capture the revenue, perhaps with a separate pause for some narrow risk class. Instead it released the model only to a vetted defensive consortium and committed $100M in API credits to subsidize their use. This is not in the playbook for a company optimizing for short-term position, and it is not the kind of action that makes sense if the safety argument is purely instrumental.

The second is that the strongest non-commercial arguments for being cautious about open-weight release at the frontier are not coming from people with commercial stakes. Yoshua Bengio — Turing laureate, no commercial interest, at a non-profit institution — has written explicitly that open-sourcing of *current* AI systems "may plausibly currently be more beneficial than detrimental to safety because they enable AI safety research in academia while current systems are apparently not yet powerful enough to be catastrophically dangerous in bad hands or with loss of control" but argues that the asymmetry of catastrophic misuse becomes binding at higher capability levels [30]. This is a threshold-conditional position, not a blanket anti-open stance, and it is structurally distinct from the commercial labs' framing. It is also routinely flattened in policy debates that need a citation for blanket closedness.

The right reframing is not "is the safety argument real or pretextual?" but "are the actors paying costly action that they would not pay if it were pretextual, and is the underlying empirical concern grounded in something more than convenient story?" By that standard, Anthropic does pass — voluntary ASL-3 activation, voluntary Mythos withholding, willingness to engage critics in the RSP. By the same standard, the broader regulatory edifice fails: compliance regimes that disproportionately burden challengers are exactly what bootleggers-and-baptists predicts, and the safety arguments deployed in their support should be evaluated on their merits rather than accepted on the credentials of the people deploying them. Both things are true at once. The economist's instinct to ask "cui bono?" is correct; the conclusion "therefore the argument is wrong" does not follow.

## A cleaner reframing: coordination under irreversibility

Here is the analytical move I think is actually right, and it borrows from a part of economics the AI policy literature has not yet fully absorbed.

The open-weight question is not best understood as a tradeoff between safety and access, or between caution and innovation. It is best understood as a coordination problem under irreversibility and trajectory uncertainty. Three features make it structurally difficult in ways that distinguish it from familiar policy debates.

First, the action space is asymmetric in time. Once weights are released, they cannot be recalled. There is no equivalent of a recall, no way to patch deployed models, no way to revoke access. This is why even otherwise pro-open researchers like Bengio treat the irreversibility question as the core analytical issue. The asymmetry favors precaution at any given threshold but does not by itself determine where that threshold should be.

Second, the empirical question — does this model meaningfully uplift dangerous capability beyond the counterfactual? — is the wrong unit of analysis to settle the policy question, because the rate of capability change is large enough that any answer is rapidly stale. The RAND 2024 result was correct for the 2022–2023 models it tested. The RAND 2025 update was a revision driven by capability movement, not by improved methodology. By the time you have a definitive answer for any specific model, the next-generation model exists. This is why "show me the bodies" framing of safety skepticism, while rhetorically powerful, is in fact a category error: the policy question is not about the model you have now but about the one that will exist in eighteen months given that you cannot recall the one you released yesterday.

Third, the strategic environment includes a coordination problem between firms. If Anthropic is the only frontier lab withholding capable models, the marginal safety benefit may be small and the competitive cost large. If all frontier labs withhold, the marginal safety benefit is much larger but the coordination is fragile (any defector captures the market). This is essentially the same structure as nuclear non-proliferation, climate commitments, and antitrust restraint — a common dilemma where individual rationality and collective rationality diverge. The Responsible Scaling Policy framework, with its tiered capability thresholds and explicit invocation of national-security-grade weight protection at higher levels, is in part an attempt to make Anthropic's commitment legible and externally verifiable so that other labs can coordinate around the same standard. Whether they will is the actual question. Meta's July 2025 carve-out for superintelligence and OpenAI's August 2025 reversal both suggest that the coordination is currently weakening, not strengthening.

This reframing has a property that I think the user — and economists generally — should find clarifying: it dissolves the moral question. You do not need to decide whether Anthropic's leadership is sincere or self-interested in order to evaluate their position. You need to ask whether the institutional architecture they are building is the kind that can credibly handle a decision under irreversibility and capability uncertainty when individual incentives and collective interest diverge. By that standard, the RSP has real strengths — it specifies thresholds in advance, it commits the company to costs that are publicly verifiable, it activated ASL-3 before being fully sure the threshold had been crossed — and real weaknesses, primarily that it depends on Anthropic remaining a lab with reasonable governance and is not robust to changes in management or competitive pressure. The question for an outside observer is not "do I trust Anthropic?" It is "does this look like a framework that could survive being adopted by an actor I would not trust?"

The Mythos Preview event sits in the middle of this question. It is the cleanest demonstration available that Anthropic, as currently constituted, is willing to bear costly action consistent with the framework's logic. It is also exactly the kind of action that becomes politically harder under competitive pressure and impossible without internal conviction. Whether the framework will hold is an empirical question that this report cannot answer; what the report can say is that the framework is more interesting and more honest than its critics typically acknowledge, and more contingent on specific institutional choices than its defenders typically acknowledge.

## What this means for forming your own view

The user came to this report with a stated intuition: open-weight models benefit many people and the industry; they also carry real risks; Anthropic's position is worth taking seriously as a reference point. After this investigation, I think that intuition is approximately right but in need of calibration along three axes.

First: separate the question of *current* models from the question of *frontier* models. Almost all the strongest pro-open arguments — Stella Biderman's argument that closed safety filters are theater [31], Narayanan and Kapoor's argument that safety cannot be a property of model weights divorced from deployment context [32], Jeremy Howard's argument that closed-weight monopoly is itself a safety risk [33] — are correct for *current* models in ways that the lab-side safety community does not adequately engage with. They become weaker, but not invalid, as you move to the frontier. The Bengio position — "open is currently net-positive for safety; the question is what to do at higher capability thresholds" — is the most defensible all-things-considered position for someone who has not yet committed to a side. It also happens to be the position the actual non-commercial safety researchers hold, contrary to the way they are usually cited.

Second: hold the bootleggers-and-baptists framework as a *background prior* on regulatory proposals, not as a conclusion about specific actors. The incumbent labs are reliably going to support compliance regimes that burden their competitors and call them safety. This will happen regardless of any individual's intentions. The right epistemic posture is to evaluate every regulatory proposal asking *cui bono?* — and to be skeptical of bills whose distributional consequences benefit the lobbyists — without using that skepticism to conclude that the underlying safety concern is fake. Both can be true. The European Union's AI Act is an excellent test case; the financial market data already show the predicted distributional pattern [28].

Third: what would change your mind? The most useful question to ask about your own beliefs is what evidence would update them. For the open-weight question, the leading indicators are: (a) the rate at which the open/closed capability gap is closing or widening (it has been closing rapidly — the MMLU benchmark gap collapsed from ~17 percentage points in 2023 to under 1 point in 2025 [34]); (b) whether the next generation of "uplift" red-team studies replicates the RAND 2025 finding for 2025–2026 models on broader task classes; (c) whether other frontier labs follow Anthropic's lead on costly withholding or whether Mythos Preview turns out to be a one-off; and (d) whether the regulatory architecture being built around safety arguments turns out to be predominantly procedural (good for capture) or predominantly capability-conditional (good for trajectory tracking). Watch those four things. Your view should move with them.

If you push me for a personal take: Anthropic's structural position is more honest than the rhetoric of the field gives it credit for, *and* the broader regulatory ecosystem being built around frontier-AI safety arguments is on track to consolidate power in the frontier labs in ways that should worry anyone who finds the bootleggers-and-baptists framework persuasive. These two things are not in tension; they describe different parts of the system. The right intellectual posture is to take the safety case seriously where it produces costly action (the RSP, Mythos Preview), to be unsentimental about the strategic case where it produces compliance burden on competitors (SB 1047 dynamics, EU AI Act distributional effects), and to treat any actor who claims the answer is obvious in either direction as a partisan whose argument is downstream of their interests.

For an economics student considering an LLM × economics PhD path, there is also a specific career-relevant observation hiding in this material: the academic literature on AI regulatory political economy is roughly five years behind the practitioner debate. There are three peer-reviewed papers that touch the regulatory-capture question for AI (Wei et al. 2024, Metcalf 2025, Lancieri et al. 2025), and zero papers applying bootleggers-and-baptists specifically to open-weight regulation in a peer-reviewed venue [35, 36, 27]. The empirical and theoretical work has not been done. This is not a closed field; this is an open frontier. The fact that the practitioner debate is being conducted by think tanks and Substack writers is itself a research opportunity for someone with formal training. The intersection of public choice economics, IO theory of platform markets, and the political economy of AI safety is one of the most underserved patches of academic real estate currently available. It is also the kind of patch where a good empirical paper could have substantial policy influence simply by existing.

## Open questions

1. **Will Mythos Preview hold?** Anthropic's stated plan is restricted access for the indefinite future. The first useful test of whether this is precedent or one-off will come with whatever model Anthropic releases next: does the company go back to API-default deployment, or does the Glasswing structure become a recurring pattern? The next eighteen months will answer this, and the answer matters enormously.
2. **Does any other frontier lab follow?** OpenAI's gpt-oss release moved in the opposite direction, and Meta is tightening rather than loosening. If Anthropic remains the only lab withholding capable models from public deployment, the safety case for closure becomes a unilateral commitment with eroding marginal value, and the bootleggers-and-baptists prediction (that the incumbent will defect when competition forces it) becomes the test. If Google or another lab adopts a similar withholding posture, the coordination problem starts looking solvable.
3. **What happens to the capability gap?** The MMLU collapse from 17 points in 2023 to under 1 point in 2025 is the single most important quantitative trend in this debate. If the gap continues to close, structured access loses its technical basis as a safety mechanism — you cannot maintain a perimeter around capabilities everyone can already run locally. If the gap reopens (as it might if the next generation of frontier capabilities depends on training infrastructure that open-source projects cannot match), the structured-access argument regains force. This is an empirical question with no current consensus answer.
4. **Where is the academic political-economy literature on AI regulation?** As of April 2026, there is essentially no peer-reviewed empirical work testing the bootleggers-and-baptists prediction for AI safety regulation specifically. The framework is well-established in adjacent sectors and the IO theory exists, but the AI-specific application is happening in think-tank pieces and SSRN preprints. The first rigorous event study on US/EU AI regulation that specifically tracks distributional consequences across firm size and openness posture would be a high-impact paper. (A further question is whether the Wei et al. 2024 "academic capture" channel [35] can be empirically established for the AI safety advocacy community.)
5. **What is the right governance framework for the trajectory case?** The Centre for Future Generations "Beyond the Binary" report [37] proposes tiered, capability-conditional release as an alternative to the open/closed binary. Anthropic's RSP is a unilateral version of the same idea. Could a multilateral version be built — and would the political economy actually support it, given the bootleggers-and-baptists pressures pulling in the other direction? This is the question on which the difference between "structured access as a safety mechanism" and "structured access as a competitive moat" actually turns.
