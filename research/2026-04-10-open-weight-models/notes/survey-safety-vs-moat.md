## Key Findings, Organized Thematically

### 1. The Bootleggers-and-Baptists Framework Applied to AI

The best single framing for this question comes from the classical public-choice economics literature: Bruce Yandle's "Bootleggers and Baptists" theory (originally 1983, summarized in a 2011 PERC retrospective at https://www.perc.org/2011/02/01/bootleggers-and-baptists-in-retrospect/). The theory holds that durable regulations tend to emerge when two groups coincidentally want the same law for different reasons — moral reformers ("Baptists") who want the rule on principle, and incumbent industries ("Bootleggers") who want it for rent-seeking advantage. The Baptists provide political cover; the Bootleggers provide lobbying muscle.

Nicholas Thielman's April 2026 essay in FUSION (R Street Institute) applies this framework directly and in detail to AI safety regulation. He identifies:

- **Baptists**: the AI safety advocacy community (existential risk researchers, some ethicists, some legislators) who sincerely believe frontier AI poses catastrophic societal risk
- **Bootleggers**: incumbent frontier AI firms (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind) whose market position is threatened by open-source alternatives — and who support regulations that would, by definition, impose compliance costs too high for smaller or open-source players to bear

Thielman's key mechanism: regulations structured around compute thresholds (like SB 1047's $100M training cost trigger) or licensing requirements effectively immunize the firms already at frontier scale while burdening future entrants and open-source competitors. The safety narrative provides crucial political cover — it is very hard to oppose a bill framed as preventing AI from taking over power grids.

Bruce Yandle's own 2024 Reason piece documents Sam Altman's 2023 Senate testimony calling for licensing and safety standards — and notes the structural parallel to tobacco companies endorsing health regulations that codified their market positions.

Pragmatic AI Labs (2025) goes further, comparing OpenAI's calls for banning DeepSeek to Microsoft's late-1990s "Halloween Documents" strategy — internal memos leaked from Microsoft outlining a plan to use regulatory and legal mechanisms, FUD, and proprietary extensions to undermine Linux/open-source. The parallel: OpenAI proposed to the Trump administration that the US government ban "state-controlled" Chinese models like DeepSeek — despite DeepSeek's open models containing no data-exfiltration mechanisms, and being freely hosted by Microsoft, Amazon, and Perplexity. The "national security" framing served to advance a competitive interest.

The Springer (2025) peer-reviewed paper in AI & Society, "AI safety and regulatory capture," formalizes these dynamics academically. The De Gruyter (2025) law review paper (Lancieri, Edelson, Bechtold) examines the same phenomenon from a comparative regulatory competition angle — governments and companies both behave strategically, using safety and IP frameworks to entrench competitive positions.

### 2. The SB 1047 Debate as a Revealed-Preference Test

California's SB 1047 (2024) is close to a natural experiment for detecting whose interests aligned with which positions. The bill mandated safety testing and kill switches for frontier models costing over $100M to train.

Positions revealed:
- **Anthropic**: Cautious but tilting supportive after amendments — "benefits likely outweigh costs" (Dario Amodei letter to Gov. Newsom, August 2024). Anthropic specifically helped shape the revised bill, which removed a government oversight committee.
- **OpenAI**: Opposed — said it created "uncertain legal environment" and threatened AI's growth; preferred federal regulation (which would have been weaker and harder to enforce).
- **Google/Alphabet**: Expressed concerns, sent letters opposing.
- **Meta/Yann LeCun**: Explicitly opposed — LeCun posted that the bill would be "harmful to research." Meta's concern was specifically that it could burden open-source models and hold Meta responsible for policing downstream use.
- **Elon Musk**: Supported (despite running a competing AI lab, xAI — his incentive structure is more complex).
- **AI safety community** (Hinton, Bengio, Kokotajlo): Supported.

The pattern is economically legible: the firms with primarily API/closed-weight businesses (Anthropic, Google) were ambivalent-to-supportive. The firm whose main AI competitive advantage is open weights (Meta) opposed it vigorously. OpenAI, which both has closed frontier models AND faces massive competition from open models, opposed it — possibly because it feared enabling a new regulatory body, or because its legal/compliance infrastructure was already stretched. The $100M threshold also fails to cover Anthropic's Claude while being too high to burden Meta's open releases — the bill's design didn't actually target the competitive open-weight threat.

Crucially: Anthropic got the government oversight committee removed during negotiations — reducing the oversight burden it would itself face. This is consistent with regulatory capture: support a bill after negotiating out the provisions most costly to yourself.

### 3. Yann LeCun's Argument

LeCun (TIME interview, Feb 2024; various X posts) makes the following claims:

1. Current LLMs are not capable of causing the catastrophic harms that safety advocates claim — they can't "invent new things," they regurgitate public data, so the bioweapons-via-LLM risk is basically zero for current systems (he cites the RAND Corporation study on LLMs and bioweapon recipe generation).
2. The existential risk framing is "preposterous" — it commits the fallacy that intelligence entails desire for dominance, which he argues is not supported by human psychology (a contested claim, rebutted by MindPrison and others).
3. Open source is an affirmative good: it prevents cultural homogenization, preserves democratic access to information infrastructure, and stops any single company (including Meta) from having monopolistic control over AI. He draws the Wikipedia analogy — you cannot have crowdsourced knowledge maintained by a for-profit entity in California.
4. The security framing inverts: keeping models closed means society never develops defensive capabilities. "My good AI against your bad AI" — progress speed matters more than secrecy.
5. **Implicit but unmistakable**: LeCun is articulating that the closed-model safety argument serves the competitive interests of OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google — not any genuine safety purpose.

What LeCun does NOT say explicitly (but the implication is clear): Anthropic in particular is in the awkward position of arguing for closed weights while having a business model entirely predicated on being the gatekeeper of those weights. His argument is that the safety rationale is, at minimum, overdetermined by the commercial incentive.

### 4. Jeremy Howard / fast.ai's Power Concentration Argument

Howard's November 2023 essay "AI Safety and the Age of Dislightenment" makes a different but related argument:

The core logic: IF AI becomes the powerful general-purpose technology that safety advocates say it is, THEN restricting model weights to closed API access concentrates immense power in the hands of a small number of companies. The regulation designed to prevent catastrophic AI outcomes may itself create the conditions for a catastrophic power imbalance. Those with full access to models have unbounded advantages over those limited to "safe" service interfaces.

Howard isn't arguing that safety concerns are pretextual — he's arguing that the proposed remedy (closed weights + API gatekeeping) would create worse safety outcomes by concentrating power. This is a genuine safety argument against closed models, not a market-competition argument.

The fast.ai essay specifically critiques the "Frontier AI Regulation" whitepaper co-authored by researchers at major safety-focused institutions — and notes it proposes exactly the kind of licensing regime that would entrench incumbents.

The LongtermWiki structured access analysis (2026) confirms Howard's concern is real: Anthropic holds 32% of the enterprise LLM market, with structured access being its core business model. The MMLU gap between open and closed models collapsed from 17.5 percentage points in 2023 to 0.3 points in 2025. The safety-through-closure argument is rapidly losing its technical basis.

### 5. The "Structured Access" Argument — The Strongest Safety Case for Closed Weights

Toby Shevlane's 2022 arXiv paper is the foundational academic articulation of why API-only deployment is a safety mechanism. The argument:

- Open dissemination of model weights allows unlimited fine-tuning, removes safety guardrails, and enables local deployment without monitoring
- API access allows developers to monitor usage, revoke access from bad actors, patch vulnerabilities without user action, and implement output filtering
- This is not "security through obscurity" — it's a control-point argument: the provider can intervene in real time, which you cannot do with weights in the wild

The LongtermWiki analysis (2026) validates that this works in practice: Anthropic's monitoring detects 80-95% of misuse patterns, maintains jailbreak rapid-response procedures, and revoked access to bad actors (documented cases in 2025). These capabilities genuinely exist and genuinely depend on closed weights.

Anthropic's ASL-3 deployment (May 2025) is concrete evidence of this: ASL-3 security measures explicitly include "egress bandwidth controls" to prevent weight theft, and deployment measures include "Constitutional Classifiers" monitoring inputs/outputs for CBRN (chemical, biological, radiological, nuclear) misuse patterns. This is real engineering, not just marketing.

**The key crux**: the structured access argument is strongest when the frontier capability gap is large (closed models significantly more capable than open ones). When that gap collapses — which it now nearly has — the safety benefit of closed weights erodes. You can't maintain a safety perimeter around capabilities everyone can run locally.

### 6. Historical Analogies

**Crypto Wars (1990s) — The Encryption Analogy**
The US government attempted in the 1990s to restrict strong encryption (Clipper Chip backdoor, export controls) on national security and law enforcement grounds. Phil Zimmermann released PGP precisely to force the issue. The government eventually lost — encryption proliferated globally, and the consensus in the security community is that this was correct. The Mondaq (2026) article "From Clipper Chips to Claude" explicitly draws the parallel.

The key lesson: governments claiming security grounds for restricting dual-use cryptographic tools were wrong — both about the factual security risk and about the feasibility of restriction. Strong encryption spread anyway; the damage was to US software competitiveness and civil liberties, not to adversaries' ability to encrypt.

The Peter Hall et al. (arXiv/AAAI 2025) paper "The Pitfalls of Security by Obscurity and What They Mean for Transparent AI" draws the cryptography lesson directly: the security community spent decades establishing Kerckhoffs' principle (a system should be secure even if everything about it except the key is public knowledge). Security through obscurity fails because:
1. Vulnerabilities exist whether or not adversaries know about them
2. Wide visibility enables faster discovery and patching of flaws
3. Closed systems accumulate hidden vulnerabilities; open systems crowd-source security review

The paper argues that AI safety advocates who argue for closed weights as a safety mechanism are repeating the security-through-obscurity error.

**Limits of the analogy**: Encryption is mathematically structured — the key IS the security. AI model weights are more like a manufacturing process than a key; the trained weights don't generalize to building new, more dangerous models. A bad actor with Llama 4 can still only do what Llama 4 does. But more powerful future models may not hold — if model weights encode dangerous capabilities (bioweapons uplift, cyberattack automation), then the analogy breaks down. This is the crux Anthropic's ASL-3 framework is designed to address.

**Nuclear Technology — The Dual-Use Analogy**
AI safety researchers like Sam Altman and Demis Hassabis have invoked nuclear weapons as a governance analogy — suggesting a global AI oversight body modeled on the IAEA. CSET's Kahn and Horowitz (2025) and Emily Worlock (European Studies Review, 2026) both argue this analogy is flawed in critical ways:

1. Nuclear weapons require physical, traceable materials (plutonium, uranium). AI is software — "the very opposite of nuclear weapons" in terms of non-proliferability.
2. Nuclear weapons are state-owned; AI development is primarily private and commercial.
3. Nuclear weapons have no dual-use benefit that outweighs the risk in peacetime. AI's beneficial applications are massive.
4. IAEA-style control is feasible for nuclear because there are only ~10 state actors. AI has thousands of developers globally.

The nuclear analogy may itself be serving a rhetorical purpose — it implies that frontier AI labs occupy the role of nation-states with unique, irreplaceable capability, justifying IAEA-style centralization around those labs. This is convenient for the labs.

**Pharmaceutical / FDA Analogy**
The AI Now Institute's 2024 report "Lessons from the FDA for AI" is the best treatment of this. The FDA model created significant barriers to entry that consolidated the pharmaceutical industry, but also produced verified safety — the US pharma market produces drugs that people travel internationally to obtain. The question: did FDA approval requirements serve public safety or industry incumbents?

The honest answer from the AI Now report: both. FDA-style premarket approval does raise barriers to entry and does advantage incumbents (who have capital to fund trials). But it also produced genuinely safer drugs. The key lesson for AI: the challenge is industry capture of the regulatory apparatus itself — incumbents will try to define the standards that benefit themselves.

For AI, the risk is that "safety standards" for frontier models are set by the very firms whose interests lie in making those standards expensive to comply with. Section 4 of the AI Now report specifically identifies "avoiding industry capture" as one of four key challenges for FDA-style AI regulation.

### 7. What the Economics Literature Says

The Brookings/GovAI paper (Vipra & Korinek, Sept 2023) on "Market Concentration Implications of Foundation Models" is the most rigorous economics treatment. Key findings:

- The market for the most capable AI models has strong natural monopoly tendencies (high fixed training costs, low marginal deployment costs, network effects from data)
- This calls for a two-pronged response: antitrust to prevent monopolies from propagating vertically, AND quality/safety regulation to replace the market discipline that monopoly erodes
- The analysis is notable because it treats safety and market structure as genuinely linked — both require regulation — rather than treating them as separable

The De Gruyter (2025) law review paper (Lancieri et al.) adds that governments themselves behave strategically in AI regulatory competition — the EU's AI Act, US export controls on chips, and safety frameworks all serve geopolitical and economic interests alongside any genuine safety purpose.

The PERC retrospective on bootleggers and baptists (2011) emphasizes that the entanglement of moral and economic motives is not unusual — it's the normal condition for durable regulation. The question is whether the regulatory outcome serves the public interest, not whether the industry motives are pure.

## The Strongest Version of the "It's Just a Moat" Argument

The case that closed-model safety arguments are primarily pretextual rests on five pillars:

1. **Revealed preferences in lobbying**: Frontier labs have consistently supported regulations (compute thresholds, licensing requirements) that impose compliance costs proportional to scale of operation. These regulations coincidentally burden open-source competitors and new entrants more than the incumbents themselves. The pattern of which bills they support and which they oppose tracks their competitive interests more reliably than any stated safety principle.

2. **The commoditization threat**: As Pragmatic AI Labs (2025) documents, the open-weight gap collapsed from 17+ MMLU points in 2023 to 0.3 points in 2025. DeepSeek R1 matched OpenAI o1 at a fraction of the training cost. The economic moats protecting companies operating at multibillion-dollar losses evaporate when open-source alternatives arrive. Regulatory barriers serve the function that scale advantages no longer provide.

3. **The motivational structure of Anthropic**: Anthropic exists as a company only because people pay to use its API. If model weights were freely available, the revenue disappears. The safety argument for closed weights is therefore inseparable from the company's survival argument. This doesn't make the safety argument wrong — but it makes it impossible to assess independently of the commercial interest.

4. **Selection of which safety concerns to emphasize**: Labs emphasize existential risks from capabilities they don't yet have (AGI, bioweapons uplift, autonomous cyberattack) while downplaying near-term harms from existing closed systems (bias, misuse of deployed models, power concentration). The existential framing demands the most restrictive regulatory response and most benefits incumbents.

5. **Historical pattern from Microsoft**: The Halloween Documents demonstrate that the playbook of using safety/quality/reliability FUD to suppress open-source competition is not new. The parallel between Microsoft's 1990s anti-Linux strategy and current frontier labs' anti-open-weight strategy is uncomfortably close.

## The Strongest Version of the "No, It's Genuinely About Safety" Argument

The case that the closed-weight safety argument is principled, not pretextual:

1. **The structured access argument is technically coherent**: You cannot monitor, patch, or revoke access to weights in the wild. Closed API deployment genuinely enables safety interventions that open weights preclude. Anthropic's ASL-3 deployment measures are real engineering, not marketing — Constitutional Classifiers, egress bandwidth controls, jailbreak rapid-response procedures. These exist and work (to some degree). This is not security-through-obscurity; it's an active control-point argument.

2. **The CBRN uplift problem may be real at sufficient capability levels**: For current models, LeCun is right that bioweapons uplift is minimal. RAND's study found current LLMs don't meaningfully help with bioweapon development. But this may not hold as capabilities increase. Anthropic's ASL-3 trigger (activated for Claude Opus 4) represents a genuine empirical finding: models are getting closer to meaningful CBRN capability uplift. The precautionary logic is coherent even if premature for current systems.

3. **The problem of motivated reasoning cuts both ways**: Meta has the opposite financial incentive — LeCun's salary depends on Llama being legally deployable and Meta's open strategy working. His dismissal of safety concerns is as over-determined by commercial interest as Anthropic's concern is. The fact that arguments correlate with financial interest doesn't tell us which arguments are correct.

4. **Voluntary restraint in the absence of external pressure**: Anthropic activated ASL-3 protections before it had definitively determined Opus 4 crossed the capability threshold. This is costly in business terms (reduced model performance due to classifiers, compute overhead for monitoring). A pure moat-maximizer would not do this voluntarily. This is some evidence of genuine safety motivation, not just regulatory capture.

5. **The power concentration argument cuts for closed weights too**: If a single bad actor (state or non-state) gains access to superintelligent capabilities before alignment is solved, unrestricted model weight distribution accelerates the timeline for that outcome. The structured access argument is not just about monitoring — it's about slowing the proliferation of dangerous capabilities until alignment catches up. This is a coherent safety case even without any commercial interest.

## Gaps and Assessment

### What the sources agree on:
- The safety and competitive arguments are entangled and cannot be cleanly separated at the level of individual actors' motives
- The Bootleggers-and-Baptists framework describes the political economy well — genuine safety advocates and self-interested incumbents have formed an inadvertent coalition
- Compute-threshold based regulation (SB 1047, various EU provisions) disproportionately advantages incumbent frontier labs and disadvantages open-source competitors
- The nuclear analogy is poor and serves industry interests
- The MMLU gap between open and closed models has collapsed; the technical basis for safety-through-closure is eroding rapidly

### Where sources diverge:
- Whether future capability levels will make closed-weight deployment a genuine safety necessity (LeCun: no; Anthropic: yes-and-we're-already-there)
- Whether the power-concentration risk of closed-model APIs is worse than the proliferation risk of open weights (Howard: closed is worse; Shevlane/Anthropic: open is worse)
- Whether regulatory capture is already happening or is just a risk to guard against

### Under-explored areas:
1. **Empirical work on the actual uplift question**: The RAND study on LLMs and bioweapons is from early 2024 and covered relatively weak models. There is no equivalent rigorous study for Claude Opus 4 or equivalent frontier systems. Anthropic's ASL-3 trigger relied on internal evaluations with limited external validation. The whole debate turns on an empirical question that hasn't been publicly and rigorously settled.

2. **Comparative analysis of regulatory drafting**: No source systematically analyzed who wrote the actual text of safety regulations that passed (EU AI Act, forthcoming US proposals) and whose fingerprints appear in the drafting. This is the classic test for capture — not who lobbied, but who wrote the final language.

3. **The "race to the bottom" mechanism**: If one major lab releases weights and others are forced to follow to stay competitive (as happened with Meta's Llama forcing others to respond), the closed-weight safety argument has a coordination-problem dimension that none of the sources engage with rigorously.

4. **The intermediate case — tiered/timelocked releases**: Some labs release models with delayed weight publication (6-12 months after API deployment). This partially separates the safety argument (newest capabilities need monitoring) from the moat argument (monetize newest capabilities exclusively). No source analyzes whether this approach resolves or just relocates the tension.

### Surprises:
- The MMLU gap collapse to 0.3 points by 2025 is starker than expected and materially undermines the technical basis for structured access as a safety mechanism — this finding (from LongtermWiki's 2026 analysis) is under-discussed in mainstream policy debates
- Anthropic's decision to activate ASL-3 before definitively crossing the threshold (paying real costs voluntarily) is moderate evidence of genuine safety commitment — this is not behavior a purely rent-seeking firm would exhibit
- The fast.ai argument (Howard, 2023) that closed weights create a worse safety outcome via power concentration is the most sophisticated safety-oriented argument for open weights, and it is largely ignored by the mainstream policy debate which treats "open vs. closed" as a safety vs. openness tradeoff rather than a safety vs. safety tradeoff

### Overall Assessment:
The safety and moat arguments are **structurally inseparable** at the level of firm-level analysis for a company like Anthropic. You cannot distinguish them from the outside, and possibly not from the inside either — motivated reasoning operates below the level of conscious awareness. What economics offers is not a method for reading minds but a framework for evaluating outcomes: regulations that use safety-framing to raise rivals' costs without proportionate safety benefits should be opposed regardless of the sincerity of the advocates.

The most honest conclusion: the safety argument has real technical content (structured access genuinely enables certain safety interventions), is probably premature for most current risks, and may become more legitimate as capabilities increase. The competitive-moat argument is real and is the primary driver of lobbying activity. Both things are true simultaneously. The regulatory danger is that the moat argument colonizes the institutional infrastructure built for the safety argument — which is exactly what "Bootleggers and Baptists" predicts.
