# Deep Dive: Academic Pedigree of the Bootleggers-and-Baptists Framework for AI Regulation

## Question

Is the bootleggers-and-baptists (B&B) framing for AI regulation present in serious peer-reviewed economics, public choice, or political economy literature? Or is it only a think-tank framing? How confidently can a careful MSc economics student deploy this framework?

## Investigation

### The original Yandle framework and its actual publication status

Bruce Yandle's 1983 piece "Bootleggers and Baptists: The Education of a Regulatory Economist" was published in *Regulation*, the Cato Institute's policy magazine (May/June 1983). It is not a peer-reviewed journal article. It is a practitioner essay grounded in Yandle's experience as a government regulatory economist. Several secondary sources mistakenly cite it as appearing in the *AEI Journal on Government and Society* — this appears to be a citation error that has propagated; the canonical source is the Cato Regulation archive.

This matters: the 1983 original is intellectually sharp and widely influential but methodologically informal. It belongs to the genre of "regulatory economist reflects on what he observed." It is not a hypothesis test, not a formal model, and not peer-reviewed. The survey note's characterization of the Yandle Reason (2024) piece as one of only two sources deploying the frame is therefore accurate for the AI-specific application, but understates the framework's academic depth in the broader public choice literature.

### Peer-reviewed formalizations of B&B

The framework has been formalized and published in serious peer-reviewed venues since 1990:

**Shogren (1990), "The optimal subsidization of Baptists by bootleggers," *Public Choice* 67: 181–189.** This is a formal game-theoretic model in the flagship peer-reviewed journal of public choice economics. Shogren shows the conditions under which bootleggers optimally fund Baptist advocacy — the formal mechanism underlying the casual story. This is the strongest academic foothold.

**McLaughlin, Smith & Sobel (2019), "Bootleggers, Baptists, and the risks of rent seeking," *Constitutional Political Economy* 30: 211–234.** A peer-reviewed paper in a Springer journal focused on institutional economics and public choice. 8 citations.

**Yandle (2021), "George J. Stigler's theory of economic regulation, bootleggers, baptists and the rebirth of the public interest imperative," *Public Choice* 193: 23–34.** Yandle himself published a theoretical development of B&B in *Public Choice*, explicitly situating it within the Stiglerian tradition. 9 citations.

**Simmons, Yonk & Thomas (2011), "Bootleggers, Baptists, and Political Entrepreneurs," *The Independent Review* 15(3): 367–381.** Extends the framework to analyze political entrepreneurs who mediate between bootleggers and baptists. This is the most analytically complete treatment of the coalition formation mechanism. Published in a peer-reviewed quarterly journal.

The Stiglerian ancestry is itself deeply established: Stigler (1971) "The Theory of Economic Regulation," *Bell Journal of Economics*, has 2,841 citations on Semantic Scholar and is one of the most influential papers in IO economics. B&B is best understood as a descriptive complement to Stigler's capture theory: where Stigler shows that industry demands regulation, Yandle shows the political mechanism — moral advocates provide public cover for that demand.

### Application to digital and technology regulation

Before AI became the specific focus, B&B had been applied to digital/technology regulation:

**Stagnaro (2020), "Bootleggers and Baptists Go Digital," *The Independent Review* 25(2).** Applies the B&B framework to digital economy regulation in Italy, identifying how incumbent platform and telecom firms (bootleggers) used privacy and consumer-protection advocates (baptists) to secure regulations that raised barriers to entry for competitors. This is the only peer-reviewed application of B&B to digital markets and maps directly onto the open-weight AI debate. The logic is essentially the same: established players advocate for compliance regimes they can absorb but competitors cannot.

**Morriss, Yandle, Adler & Meiners (2015), "Baptists, Bootleggers & Electronic Cigarettes,"** applying B&B to FDA regulation — established tobacco firms and health advocates jointly supporting strict FDA oversight of e-cigarettes for different reasons.

The broader B&B literature is active across sectors. Sotirov et al. (2017) apply it to EU timber regulation (55 citations — the most-cited peer-reviewed B&B application). Anastasi et al. (2025) apply it to short-term rental bans in *European Journal of Political Economy*. The framework is not dormant; it is a working analytical tool in peer-reviewed applied public choice research.

### The AI-specific literature: what actually exists

Three peer-reviewed or near-peer-reviewed pieces address regulatory capture in AI governance, though none uses B&B framing directly:

**Wei, Ezell, Gabrieli & Deshpande (2024), "How Do AI Companies 'Fine-Tune' Policy? Examining Regulatory Capture in AI Governance," *Proceedings of the AAAI/ACM Conference on AI, Ethics, and Society*, pp. 1539–1555.** Peer-reviewed conference proceedings. 18 citations. Based on 17 expert interviews with AI policy specialists. Identifies six mechanisms of industry influence: agenda-setting, advocacy, academic capture, information management, cultural capture through status, and media capture. Uses a sociological regulatory capture framework (closer to Dal Bó and Laffont-Tirole than to Yandle). Does not cite Yandle. Key finding: experts were most concerned with regulation that over-emphasizes certain policy goals over others — consistent with the B&B prediction that the baptist coalition's preferred goals end up shaping the regulatory agenda.

**Metcalf (2025), "AI safety and regulatory capture," *AI & Society* (Springer), DOI: 10.1007/s00146-025-02534-0.** Peer-reviewed journal article. The title directly names the phenomenon B&B describes. Its tldr: "explores a variety of harms and injustices that captured AI-safety regulations are likely to create and reviews proposals that might mitigate the problem of regulatory capture of AI safety." Approaches the topic from AI ethics rather than public choice economics.

**Lancieri, Edelson & Bechtold (2025), "AI Regulation: Competition, Arbitrage & Regulatory Capture," forthcoming *Theoretical Inquiries in Law*.** Law & economics paper from Georgetown Law Center, Northeastern, and ETH Zurich. The abstract explicitly addresses companies "behaving strategically and using regulatory levers to protect their interests" and firms "trying to capture the regulatory framework." Draws on historical examples from competition policy, privacy law, IP, and cloud computing. This is the most rigorous peer-reviewed law-and-economics paper in the vicinity of what B&B framing is doing for AI, though it deploys competition-law rather than public choice vocabulary.

### Empirical evidence consistent with the B&B prediction

**Gutiérrez, Rykaczewski & Vulcheva (2026), "European Union's AI Act: Protecting the Public at the Cost of Innovation?" SSRN Working Paper (DOI: 10.2139/ssrn.6314258).** Uses event study methodology on stock market returns around key EU AI Act events. Key finding: larger companies experience *positive* abnormal returns when regulation gets stricter (cost-increasing events), while smaller companies and high-R&D firms experience negative returns. This is precisely the B&B distributional prediction expressed in financial market data: strict regulation redistributes rents from small challengers to large incumbents. Not yet peer-reviewed, but the methodology is standard finance/IO economics.

**Azoulay, Krieger & Nagaraj (2024), "Old Moats for New Models: Openness, Control, and Competition in Generative AI," NBER Working Paper 32474.** Published as a book chapter in NBER's *Entrepreneurship and Innovation Policy and the Economy* (2025). Provides the IO foundation: tight control over complementary assets (compute, data, talent) will produce oligopolistic market structure in AI. The paper concludes, pointedly: "the best hopes for a vibrant open source AI ecosystem might rest on the presence of a 'rogue' technology giant who might choose openness as a strategic weapon against other incumbents." This maps exactly onto the Meta/Llama situation. The paper does not use B&B framing but provides the economic rationale for *why* incumbent frontier labs would rationally favor regulatory regimes that constrain open-weight models.

### What is missing in the academic literature

The specific application of B&B to open-weight AI model regulation — which the FUSION/Thielman and Yandle/Reason pieces attempt — has no peer-reviewed academic parallel as of April 2026. The gap is:

1. No formal empirical test of whether AI safety advocacy is correlated with or funded by incumbent AI labs (the academic capture channel Wei et al. flag is noted but not established empirically for the safety-advocacy community specifically).
2. No peer-reviewed game-theoretic model applying Shogren-style analysis to the specific coalition structure in AI governance.
3. No peer-reviewed paper that directly names and tests the B&B coalition in AI regulation rather than the more diffuse "regulatory capture" framing.

The think-tank pieces are filling a gap the academic literature has not yet caught up to. This is common: B&B was first applied in practitioner writing to climate regulation years before Brandt & Svendsen's peer-reviewed treatment. The gap is real but it does not invalidate the framing — it means the student is at the frontier.

## Key Findings

- **The Yandle 1983 original is not peer-reviewed** (it is a Cato magazine essay), but B&B has been formalized in *Public Choice* and *Constitutional Political Economy* since 1990. The academic pedigree is real via Shogren (1990), McLaughlin et al. (2019), and Yandle (2021). [Strong evidence]

- **B&B has been applied to digital regulation in one peer-reviewed paper**: Stagnaro (2020) in *The Independent Review*. The logic maps directly onto AI. [Moderate evidence — one paper, ideologically aligned journal, but peer-reviewed]

- **Regulatory capture in AI is addressed in peer-reviewed literature**: Wei et al. (2024) in AAAI/ACM AIES proceedings, Metcalf (2025) in *AI & Society*, and Lancieri et al. (2025) in *Theoretical Inquiries in Law*. None use B&B framing but they address the same underlying phenomenon. [Strong evidence that the phenomenon is academically recognized]

- **Empirical evidence consistent with the B&B prediction exists for AI**: Gutiérrez et al. (2026) event study finds large firms gain on regulatory tightening. Azoulay et al. (2024) NBER paper provides IO theory of why incumbents favor regulation. [Moderate evidence — rigorous methodology, not yet peer-reviewed for the former]

- **No peer-reviewed paper applies B&B specifically to open-weight AI regulation** as of April 2026. The FUSION and Reason pieces are at the frontier. [Confirmed gap]

- **The framework's strongest academic analog** in other sectors is Sotirov et al. (2017) on EU timber regulation (55 citations), showing the framework is alive and productive in peer-reviewed work. [Strong evidence of framework validity in adjacent domains]

## Assessment

The bootleggers-and-baptists framework is academically defensible and analytically sharp. An MSc economics student is not standing on think-tank rhetoric if they deploy it carefully. They are standing on: (a) a recognized tradition in public choice economics with peer-reviewed formalizations in *Public Choice* and *Constitutional Political Economy*; (b) Stigler's (1971) regulatory capture theory as the theoretical ancestor; and (c) a growing empirical literature on AI regulation that, while not using B&B vocabulary, addresses the same regulatory dynamics.

The right epistemic posture for deployment is:

**Cite the peer-reviewed public choice foundation**: Yandle (2021) in *Public Choice*, McLaughlin et al. (2019) in *Constitutional Political Economy*, Shogren (1990) in *Public Choice*, and Stigler (1971) as the theoretical ancestor. These are the citations that establish you are doing economics, not quoting magazines.

**Acknowledge the closest peer-reviewed applications to digital/AI**: Stagnaro (2020) for the digital analog, Wei et al. (2024) and Lancieri et al. (2025) for AI regulatory capture (even without B&B framing).

**Cite the empirical corroborating evidence**: Gutiérrez et al. (2026) for market reactions consistent with the B&B distributional prediction, Azoulay et al. (2024) NBER for the IO theory.

**Be explicit about what is missing**: The specific coalition structure in AI governance — whether safety advocacy by EA-adjacent researchers and incumbent AI labs constitutes a genuine B&B coalition — has not been empirically tested in the academic literature. Wei et al.'s "academic capture" channel is relevant here, but their evidence is qualitative expert interviews, not systematic. A student should present B&B-for-AI as applying an established framework to a new case where the formal academic literature is still catching up, not as settled empirical consensus.

One further caveat: the B&B framing has an ideological center of gravity. Most of its peer-reviewed applications appear in journals (Constitutional Political Economy, The Independent Review) associated with libertarian or classical liberal economics. A careful student should note this and acknowledge the legitimate counterarguments — that AI regulation might genuinely serve safety goals, that the baptist-bootlegger coalition may be structurally coincidental rather than strategic, and that the Wei et al. (2024) literature shows incumbent influence without necessarily proving it is channeled through safety advocacy. The framing is a useful analytical lens but it should be held in tension with competing explanations, not wielded as a rhetorical cudgel.
