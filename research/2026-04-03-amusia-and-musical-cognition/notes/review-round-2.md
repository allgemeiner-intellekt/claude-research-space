## Per-Question Evaluation

### Q1: Is congenital amusia a broken music module or a general auditory deficit?

- **Coverage**: Excellent. The survey established the fronto-temporal network consensus, and the deep dives have enriched this with mechanistic detail. The Samiee et al. (2022) finding -- auditory cortex locally over-coupled but under-signaling to frontal regions -- provides the most specific mechanistic account available. The dyslexia deep dive adds the mirror-lateralization argument, which reframes the modularity question productively: it is not that amusia is "domain-general" but that the same class of neurodevelopmental vulnerability can disrupt domain-specific networks in either hemisphere.
- **Strengths**: The AF tractography controversy (Chen et al.) is properly contextualized. The cortical malformation hypothesis is traced to primary evidence (Hyde 2007) and connected to the dyslexia parallel (Galaburda 1985). The implicit/explicit dissociation is now deeply sourced through the consciousness bottleneck deep dive.
- **Gaps**: None that would materially weaken the report. The environmental sounds question (how do amusics process pitch in sirens, bird calls?) remains unstudied in the literature, but this is a genuine gap in the field, not a gap in the investigation.

### Q2: The tonal language paradox

- **Coverage**: Excellent after the prevalence deep dive. The paradox has been effectively dissolved: when the same instrument (MBEA) is used with within-sample norming, Mandarin and Canadian prevalence are virtually identical (3.4% vs. 3.2%). The apparent discrepancy between 1.5% (Peretz & Vuvan 2017) and 3.4% (Nan et al. 2010) is explained by criterion differences (multi-gate vs. 2 SD cutoff) and sample size (N=20,000 vs. N=117). The Liu et al. (2023) study at N=500K shows a real mean shift in melody discrimination but explicitly does not measure prevalence.
- **Strengths**: The deep dive identified the specific methodological incomparabilities: different instruments (DTT vs. MBEA vs. online AMUSIA), different thresholds (2 SD vs. multi-gate), different sample sizes. The finding that the 4% baseline traces to a single 1980 study using a different instrument is important and well-documented. The Henry & McAuley (2013) SDT critique and the Vuvan et al. (2017) Montreal Protocol response add methodological sophistication.
- **Gaps**: The confidence interval around the Nan et al. N=117 estimate (approximately 0.9%--8.5%) could be stated more prominently -- this alone shows the comparison is underpowered. But the deep dive does note this. No further investigation needed.

### Q3: Heritability of musical sub-abilities

- **Coverage**: Strong from the survey alone. The Mosing 2014 MZ discordant-pairs finding is the centerpiece and is thoroughly covered. The Mosing 2024 finding (heritability increases with practice) and the Hambrick G-by-E interaction are well-sourced. The AP genetics (8q24.21, locus heterogeneity, critical period) are mapped.
- **Strengths**: The "practice is heritable" finding (40--70%) is correctly identified as the most counterintuitive result. The distinction between auditory discrimination heritability and performative skill heritability is appropriately flagged. The Wesseldijk cognitive PGS work connects musical genetics to domain-general cognitive architecture.
- **Gaps**: The absence of twin studies specifically for clinical amusia was identified in Round 1 and remains unfilled. However, the dyslexia deep dive partially addresses this by showing that amusia's genetic architecture may overlap with better-characterized dyslexia genetics -- this is a productive framing for the report even without amusia-specific GWAS data. The gap is acknowledged rather than hidden, which is sufficient.

### Q4: Perception vs. production

- **Coverage**: Very strong. The three-locus model (perceptual encoding, sensorimotor mapping, motor execution) is well-established. The consciousness bottleneck deep dive has now connected this to Q1 by showing that implicit perception feeds motor compensation even when explicit perception fails (Hutchins & Peretz 2013, Suchy et al. 2025).
- **Strengths**: The Pfordresher inverse model account and the Hutchins LDR model are both covered. The ~17% poor-pitch-singer prevalence vs. ~1.5% amusia prevalence is a striking comparison that the report can use effectively. The beat deafness case (Phillips-Silver 2011) with its EEG entrainment data provides the rhythmic parallel to the pitch story.
- **Gaps**: Production-only poor-pitch singers have never been neuroimaged -- this was flagged in Round 1 and remains a gap in the literature itself. Not actionable for further investigation.

## Cross-Question Integration

The deep dives have achieved what Round 1 identified as the most important missing element: cross-question connections that support a unified argument. Specifically:

**The consciousness bottleneck as central thesis.** The deep dive traced the implicit/explicit dissociation across pitch amusia (MMN normal, P300 absent), beat deafness (neural entrainment normal, behavioral synchronization fails), vocal production (unconscious compensation despite absent conscious detection), and emotional prosody (preserved intensity ratings, impaired categorization). The thesis is strong enough to serve as the report's central argument, with two important refinements:

1. The MMN is not unconditionally normal -- it degrades under complexity (Quiroga-Martinez 2020). The accurate framing is "pre-attentive detection of basic acoustic change is preserved, but the neural implementation is abnormal and fails to transmit to higher-order networks."

2. Memory is a second, partially independent bottleneck (Albouy 2016 temporal encoding constraint). The report should present a two-stage model: encoding speed in auditory cortex + transmission failure across the right fronto-temporal pathway.

**The prevalence question as methodological case study.** The deep dive effectively converts the "tonal language paradox" from a substantive puzzle into a methodological lesson about cross-cultural comparison. This is a strength: it shows the investigation going beyond surface claims to examine the evidentiary basis. The real finding is not "tonal languages protect against amusia" but "tonal language experience shifts the population mean for pitch discrimination without affecting the tail" -- which connects back to the consciousness bottleneck (categorical/conscious pitch processing is enhanced by tonal language; pre-categorical encoding is not).

**The dyslexia connection as modularity test.** The mirror-lateralization hypothesis (same developmental mechanism, opposite hemispheres, 30% bilateral overlap) is the strongest challenge to domain-specific modularity and connects Q1 (what kind of deficit?) to Q3 (genetics). The report can use this to argue that amusia belongs to a family of fronto-temporal connectivity disorders rather than being a unique music-specific condition.

## Remaining Tensions

Two tensions exist in the material that the orchestrator should engage with rather than smooth over:

1. **The Albouy complication.** The consciousness bottleneck story is cleanest when framed as "auditory cortex computes normally, transmission to frontal cortex fails." But Albouy et al. (2013) showed auditory cortex dynamics are abnormal even at early stages, and Albouy (2016) showed encoding speed is reduced. The reconciliation offered by the deep dive (Norman-Haignere 2016: pitch-responsive cortex exists and is selective in fMRI, but temporal dynamics are abnormal) is plausible but not fully settled. The report should present this as a refined two-stage model rather than a clean single-bottleneck story.

2. **Beat deafness evidence quality.** The parallel between pitch amusia and beat deafness ERP signatures is theoretically compelling but rests on very small N (primarily single-case studies). The report should use this parallel but flag the evidence level honestly.

Neither tension requires additional investigation -- both are manageable in the writing phase through appropriate framing.
