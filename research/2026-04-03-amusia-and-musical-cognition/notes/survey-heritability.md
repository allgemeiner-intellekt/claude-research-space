## Key Sources

### Mosing et al. 2014 — "Practice Does Not Make Perfect: No Causal Effect of Music Practice on Music Ability"
- **Link**: DOI 10.1177/0956797614541990 | Psychological Science, 25(9): 1795–1803
- **Key content**: The flagship study. N = 10,539 Swedish twins from the STAGE cohort (Swedish Twin Registry), ages 27–54. Measured: total lifetime hours of music practice (self-reported across four age intervals), and three auditory discrimination sub-abilities (pitch, melody, rhythm) via the Swedish Musical Discrimination Test (SMDT).

  **Core findings:**
  - Music practice is substantially heritable: 40–70% (69% in males, 41% in females), comparable to the heritability of the music abilities themselves.
  - Phenotypic correlations between practice and ability were moderate (rs = .18–.36), replicated past literature.
  - Bivariate ACE modeling showed the practice–ability association was almost entirely genetic (A correlations = .33–.57) — the nonshared environmental (E) correlations were all small and nonsignificant (.00–.06).
  - Critical test: within-pair MZ differences in practice hours showed no association with within-pair differences in ability (regression coefficients for melody, pitch, rhythm all nonsignificant, rs = .02–.05). Twin pairs with practice differences as large as 20,000+ hours showed no ability advantage for the more-practicing twin.
  - Interpretation: the practice–ability correlation is largely due to genetic pleiotropy — the same genes that make you good at music also make you inclined to practice. The association is not causal.

- **Assessment**: Published in Psychological Science (top-tier), peer-reviewed, very large sample (N > 10,000), uses genetically-informative twin design with a key internal causal test (MZ discordant pairs). The SMDT itself had been validated in the same dataset (Ullén et al. 2014, same registry). Limitation: music ability measured as auditory discrimination, not performance skill; the authors themselves acknowledge results might differ for other ability measures. Uses retrospective self-report for practice hours. Highly credible; one of the most methodologically strong papers in this literature.

### Mosing, Verweij, Hambrick, Pedersen & Ullén 2024 — "Testing the Deliberate Practice Theory: Does Practice Reduce the Heritability of Musical Expertise?"
- **Link**: DOI 10.3390/jintelligence12090087 | Journal of Intelligence, 12(9):87
- **Key content**: A direct test of the DP theory's prediction that heritability should decrease as practice accumulates (since practice supposedly "bypasses" genetic constraints). N = 6,471 twins (1,302 complete pairs), using the same Swedish Twin Registry dataset. Used a bivariate Cholesky decomposition with practice hours as moderator.

  **Core findings:**
  - Average heritability of musical expertise = ~50%; shared environment = ~25%; residual = ~25%.
  - As practice hours increased, variation in musical expertise decreased overall — but this was entirely due to decreased shared-environmental and residual variance, not decreased genetic variance.
  - The absolute genetic component was unaffected by practice amount; the *relative* heritability (proportion) therefore *increased* with more practice.
  - This is the opposite of what DP theory predicts.

- **Assessment**: Direct replication and extension of 2014 results with a larger sample, using more sophisticated moderation analysis. Open access. Low citation count so far (1) likely due to recency. The finding that heritability of expertise *rises* with more practice is counterintuitive and deserves attention. The key methodological question is whether residual variance captures mostly measurement error or true environmental variation.

### Ullén, Mosing, Holm, Eriksson & Madison 2014 — "Psychometric Properties and Heritability of the Swedish Musical Discrimination Test (SMDT)"
- **Link**: DOI 10.1016/j.paid.2014.01.057 | Personality and Individual Differences, 63: 87–93
- **Key content**: The foundational measurement paper for the SMDT. N = 6,881 twins. Heritability estimates for the three SMDT sub-scales:
  - Melody discrimination: ~61% (females), ~31% (males)
  - Rhythm discrimination: ~52% (females), ~41% (males)
  - Pitch discrimination: ~30% (females), ~12% (males)
  - Note: pitch discrimination had the *lowest* heritability and the highest residual/nonshared environmental component, suggesting more plasticity or measurement noise in this sub-ability.
  - Internal consistency good: Cronbach's alpha .79–.89.

- **Assessment**: Published in a respectable differential psychology journal. Provides the key heritability numbers for specific musical sub-abilities. The sex differences in heritability are notable and largely unexplained. These are not heritabilities for *absolute pitch* but for general auditory discrimination.

### Drayna, Manichaikul, de Lange, Snieder & Spector 2001 — "Genetic Correlates of Musical Pitch Recognition in Humans"
- **Link**: DOI 10.1126/SCIENCE.291.5510.1969 | Science, 291: 1969–1972
- **Key content**: 136 citations. Published in *Science*. Used a large sample of female twins from the UK Twins Registry, testing pitch recognition with a "Distorted Tunes Test" (ability to detect wrong notes in familiar tunes). This is a different musical ability from absolute pitch — it's a form of melody/pitch memory.
  - MZ concordance = 75%; DZ concordance = 57%.
  - TLDR: heritability of musical pitch recognition is "primarily due to highly heritable differences in auditory functions not tested by conventional audiologic methods."
  - The study primarily demonstrated genetic underpinning using classical twin design but was not a linkage study per se.

- **Assessment**: High-prestige journal, large sample, seminal study. No specific genes identified in this paper — it establishes heritability but does not map loci. Its distinction from absolute pitch (it tests relative pitch/melody) is sometimes elided in popular accounts. The concordance numbers (MZ 75%, DZ 57%) are meaningful but note the DZ concordance is also high, suggesting significant shared environmental contribution.

### Theusch & Gitschier 2011 — "Absolute Pitch Twin Study and Segregation Analysis"
- **Link**: DOI 10.1375/twin.14.2.173 | Twin Research and Human Genetics, 14(2): 173–178
- **Key content**: 71 citations. Used data from a large online survey/pitch-test website (perfectpitch.ucsf.edu; >16,000 participants). Twin analysis: 14 MZ pairs, 31 DZ pairs.
  - MZ casewise concordance = 78.6%; DZ casewise concordance = 45.2% (p = .018). Significantly different, supporting genetic contribution.
  - Segregation analysis of 1,463 families: segregation ratio pD = .089. This is far below .25 (autosomal recessive) or .5 (dominant), indicating AP is not inherited in simple Mendelian fashion.
  - References their prior linkage study (Theusch et al. 2009): significant linkage to chromosome **8q24.21** in European-ancestry families, with evidence for *locus heterogeneity* (different loci in different ethnic groups).
  - MZ concordance < 100% means absolute pitch is not purely genetic; environmental factors (musical training) are documented contributors. Two discordant MZ pairs both differed in tonal musical training history.

- **Assessment**: Well-designed, large N for a rare ability, methodologically sound. The MZ concordance of 78.6% is often cited as evidence of strong genetic contribution, but the DZ concordance of 45.2% is also notable (higher than expected if purely additive genetic) — suggesting shared environmental factors also play a role, consistent with the critical period model.

### Theusch, Basu & Gitschier 2009 — "Genome-Wide Study of Families with Absolute Pitch Reveals Linkage to 8q24.21 and Locus Heterogeneity"
- **Link**: American Journal of Human Genetics, 85: 112–119 | 127 citations
- **Key content**: Genome-wide linkage analysis of AP families. Found a significant peak at **chromosome 8q24.21** in European-ancestry families. Important finding: evidence for *genetic heterogeneity* — the genetic architecture differs between ethnic groups (East Asian vs. European). AP is not caused by a single gene or locus.

- **Assessment**: The key molecular genetics paper for absolute pitch. 127 citations indicates genuine impact. Limitation: linkage analysis identifies chromosomal regions, not specific genes; no specific causal variant identified. Locus heterogeneity means the search for "the AP gene" may be a misguided framing.

### Gregersen, Kowalsky, Kohn & Marvin 2001 — "Early Childhood Music Education and Predisposition to Absolute Pitch: Teasing Apart Genes and Environment"
- **Link**: DOI 10.1002/1096-8628(20010122)98:3<280::AID-AJMG1083>3.0.CO;2-6 | American Journal of Medical Genetics, 98: 280–282 | 146 citations
- **Key content**: Survey of 1,067 music students from 13 U.S. institutions. Key finding: markedly higher rate of AP among Asian students. The study found that early musical training (before age 7) is a necessary but not sufficient condition for AP — most people who train early don't develop it. Among those who train early:
  - Asian students: ~32% had AP
  - Non-Asian students: ~7% had AP (starting before age 6)
  - This population difference is interpreted as evidence for *genetic predisposition varying by ethnicity* (or possibly tone-language exposure creating a pre-formed pitch-labeling system).
  - The crucial point: early training alone cannot explain the difference; genetic variation must also be involved.

- **Assessment**: Widely cited, important for establishing the gene-environment interaction framework for absolute pitch. Limitations: self-report, no genetic verification, and the Asian/non-Asian difference could partly reflect cultural factors beyond genetics (e.g., tone-language exposure, pedagogical differences).

### Hambrick & Tucker-Drob 2014/2015 — "The Genetics of Music Accomplishment: Evidence for Gene-Environment Correlation and Interaction"
- **Link**: DOI 10.3758/s13423-014-0671-9 | Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 22: 112–120
- **Key content**: 6 citations. 800+ twin pairs from the National Merit Scholarship sample. Distinguished from the Swedish studies by using a different outcome (music *accomplishment* — a broader indicator including achievement, not just discrimination).
  - Found **gene-environment correlation**: genetic effects on music accomplishment are partly mediated by genetic effects on practice (people with higher genetic potential also practice more).
  - But only ~25% of the genetic effect on accomplishment runs through practice; the other ~75% of the genetic contribution operates independently of practice amount.
  - Found **gene-environment interaction**: genetic effects on music accomplishment are *amplified* among those who practice more. The genetic potential "matters more" in those who engage with music.
  - This is the "musician talent grows best in musical soil" finding.

- **Assessment**: Clever design, important finding. The G×E interaction finding is theoretically significant — it suggests that the question "nature or nurture?" is wrong; both are needed. The sample is older and more selective (National Merit scholars) than the Swedish registry, which limits generalizability. Low citation count for a 2014 paper, possibly because the findings are complex and not easily summarized.

### Macnamara, Hambrick & Oswald 2014 — "Deliberate Practice and Performance in Music, Games, Sports, Education, and Professions: A Meta-Analysis"
- **Link**: Psychological Science (corrigendum: DOI 10.1177/0956797618769891) | 433 citations (one version)
- **Key content**: Meta-analysis of 88 studies across multiple domains. For music specifically:
  - Practice accounted for approximately **21% of variance** in music performance (r ≈ .46, r² ≈ .21).
  - Across all domains, the weighted mean was r = .38 (r² ≈ .12), though music showed one of the stronger correlations.
  - The main claim: even at its strongest (music), practice explains less than a quarter of performance variance. Most variance remains unexplained — by factors likely including genetics, working memory, starting age, and other traits.

- **Assessment**: High impact, very widely cited, top-tier journal. The meta-analysis has been contested by Ericsson on definitional grounds (Ericsson & Harwell 2019 argue many included studies don't meet the definition of "deliberate practice"), and by Debatin et al. 2021 on individualization grounds. Hambrick's retort is that the critique can always be used to immunize the DP hypothesis against falsification. The ~21% figure for music is now the standard "adversarial" benchmark against Ericsson's framework.

### Ericsson & Harwell 2019 — "Deliberate Practice and Proposed Limits on the Effects of Practice..."
- **Link**: Frontiers in Psychology, 2019 | 239 citations
- **Key content**: Ericsson's rebuttal of Macnamara et al. They reanalyze excluding studies that don't meet their original definition of DP (individualized, with immediate feedback, requiring concentration). After exclusion, they claim practice explains 29–61% of variance (with attenuation correction). They also argue that the genetic evidence so far shows "remarkably small amounts of variance" — though they carve out height and body size.
- **Assessment**: Well-cited, represents the strongest case for the DP framework. The definitional argument is legitimate but also somewhat unfalsifiable — if any inconsistent result can be dismissed as "not real DP," the theory becomes difficult to test. Their claim about small genetic effects ignores the Mosing 2014 evidence that genetic influences on practice itself confound the practice–ability correlation.

### Wesseldijk, Mosing & Ullén 2024 — "Gene-Environment Interaction in Expertise Acquisition: Practice Effects on Musical Expertise Vary by Polygenic Scores for Cognitive Performance"
- **Link**: Heliyon (open access) | 4 citations
- **Key content**: Uses *measured* genotypes (polygenic scores for cognitive performance) rather than classical twin modeling. ~3,800 genotyped Swedish individuals. Findings:
  - Higher polygenic scores for cognitive performance predicted higher musical achievement and discrimination AND more practice (the last effect attenuates when controlling for education).
  - Practice effects on musical expertise were stronger for individuals with higher genetic predisposition for cognitive performance — a G×E interaction replicated in molecular genetics data.
  - Consistent with Hambrick & Tucker-Drob 2014 finding but in a molecular (not just twin) framework.

- **Assessment**: Cutting-edge methodology (GWAS-derived polygenic scores) applied to music cognition. Small citation count reflects recency. Open access in Heliyon (respectable but not elite). The cognitive PGS finding suggests genetic influences on music skill are partly *domain-general* (mediated through general cognitive ability), not just music-specific.

### Tan, McPherson, Peretz, Berkovic & Wilson 2014 — "The Genetic Basis of Music Ability"
- **Link**: Frontiers in Psychology (open access) | 90 citations
- **Key content**: A review paper synthesizing molecular genetics findings on music ability. Summary of specific genetic associations (though all require replication):
  - **Chromosome 4**: Multiple loci implicated in singing and music perception (from Finnish family studies by Järvelä and colleagues; genes related to motor control and auditory processing).
  - **Chromosome 8q**: AP and music perception (Theusch et al. 2009, above; also Gregersen et al. synesthesia-AP overlap study).
  - **AVPR1A (arginine vasopressin receptor 1A, chromosome 12q)**: Linked to music perception, music memory, and music listening in Israeli studies (Ukkola et al. 2009 Finnish study; Granot et al. studies).
  - **SLC6A4 (serotonin transporter gene, chromosome 17q)**: Associated with music memory and choir participation.
  - Note: These are all association/linkage findings requiring replication; effect sizes are small; none of these represent replicated causal mechanisms.

- **Assessment**: Useful synthesis. Frontiers in Psychology is respectable but lower-tier than Science or Psychological Science. The identified genes represent provisional leads, not established causal mechanisms. The AVPR1A findings are particularly interesting because the gene is involved in social bonding and is well-studied in the context of prosociality and pair-bonding.

### Wesseldijk, Mosing & Ullén (2016) — "Investigating Cognitive Transfer within the Framework of Music Practice: Genetic Pleiotropy Rather Than Causality"
- **Link**: Developmental Science, 2016 | 78 citations
- **Key content**: Tests whether music practice causes cognitive transfer effects (e.g., improved IQ or executive function). Using co-twin control analyses in the same Swedish registry: when genetic factors are controlled, music practice does not causally improve cognitive abilities. The associations between music practice and cognitive outcomes are explained by shared genetic factors (genetic pleiotropy) — the same genes that incline you to practice also incline you toward better cognitive performance.
- **Assessment**: Extends Mosing 2014 to the cognitive transfer literature, which has been a major source of claims for music education benefits. The finding challenges a significant body of correlational research (e.g., "children who take music lessons have higher IQ"). 78 citations suggests this has been noted by the broader field.

### Wesseldijk & Ullén 2021 — "Music and Verbal Ability: A Twin Study of Genetic and Environmental Associations"
- **Link**: DOI 10.1037/aca0000401 | Psychology of Aesthetics, Creativity, and the Arts | 18 citations
- **Key content**: N = 1,336 male twins. Music aptitude-verbal ability association explained ~50% by shared genetic factors, ~35% by nonshared environment. Co-twin control analysis found no causal effect of early music training on verbal ability at age 18. Consistent with the broader pleiotropy finding.

### Deutsch et al. 2006 — "Absolute Pitch among American and Chinese Conservatory Students: Prevalence Differences and Evidence for a Speech-Related Critical Period"
- **Link**: Journal of the Acoustical Society of America | 271 citations
- **Key content**: AP prevalence was 4× higher among Chinese-speaking students at a U.S. conservatory than among English-speaking students, when matched for early training onset. At the same U.S. conservatory: ~60% of students who had begun musical training before age 5 and spoke a tone language (Mandarin/Cantonese) had AP, vs. ~14% of non-tone-language speakers beginning training at the same age. Interpretation: tonal language creates a pre-existing auditory categorical labeling system (a "critical period" for pitch memory in language development) that carries over to music.
- **Assessment**: High-impact, 271 citations, published in a rigorous acoustics journal. Provides the most compelling evidence for a *linguistic* critical period that functions analogously to (or as) a musical AP critical period. The effect is large and robust. The causal mechanism proposed (tonal language primes categorical pitch labeling) is plausible but not yet fully mechanistically established.

### Hambrick, Oswald, Altmann, Meinz, Gobet & Campitelli 2014 — "Deliberate Practice: Is That All It Takes to Become an Expert?"
- **Link**: Intelligence, 45: 34–45 (referenced extensively in Mosing 2014 and Hambrick & Tucker-Drob 2014)
- **Key content**: The original Hambrick et al. meta-analytic challenge to DP theory. Music and chess domains. Practice explained ~1/3 of variance in musical performance (r² ≈ .30 after reliability correction in chess; in music somewhat lower). Suggests working memory, IQ, and genetic factors fill the unexplained variance.
- **Assessment**: The companion paper to Macnamara et al. 2014; published in Intelligence (a solid but not elite journal in this context). Together these two papers mark the opening of the formal empirical attack on the DP framework.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The twin and genetic research on musical ability has reached a fairly consistent set of conclusions:

1. **Heritability of musical sub-abilities is moderate to substantial.** Rhythm discrimination: ~40–52%; melody discrimination: ~31–61%; pitch discrimination: ~12–30% (lowest and most variable, possibly a "softer" trait or more easily trained). These numbers are from the large Swedish registry studies, which are the best-powered data available. The wide ranges partly reflect sex differences (females show higher heritability for melody and rhythm in this dataset).

2. **Music practice itself is heritable (40–70%).** This is the Mosing 2014 sleeper finding. People are not equally likely to practice — those who practice more are partly genetically predisposed to do so. This means naive correlations between practice and ability confound genetic influence.

3. **The causal effect of music practice on auditory discrimination ability is zero or near-zero, when genetic confounding is removed.** The MZ discordant-pairs test in Mosing 2014 is direct and elegant. More practice did not help the twin who practiced more. Note the important caveat: this is for *general auditory discrimination*, not for specific performative skills (sight-reading, technique, repertoire) where practice effects are uncontroversial.

4. **Absolute pitch is a special case: strong genetic component (MZ concordance ~79%) but not purely genetic (MZ < 100%), requires early tonal musical training during a critical period (~before age 7), and shows significant ethnic differences in prevalence that appear to be partly genetic and partly explained by tonal language exposure.** The chromosome 8q24.21 linkage is the best molecular lead, but AP is genetically heterogeneous with different loci in different populations. The inheritance pattern is complex (segregation ratio ~0.09, far from simple Mendelian).

5. **Gene-environment interaction exists in music accomplishment.** Hambrick & Tucker-Drob 2014 and Wesseldijk et al. 2024 both find that genetic potential for music is most fully expressed in those who practice. This is the "enabling" model: practice doesn't create talent, but talent grows most in practiced individuals. The G×E picture is more nuanced than either "genes determine everything" or "practice explains everything."

6. **Deliberate practice explains approximately 21% of variance in music performance (meta-analytically).** This is substantially less than Ericsson's original claims but more than zero. The debate over what counts as "deliberate" practice (Ericsson vs. Hambrick definitional dispute) is largely a semantic one that obscures the underlying agreement: practice is necessary but far from sufficient.

### Agreements Between Sources

- All twin studies agree that musical ability has a significant genetic component.
- All studies agree that practice and ability are correlated phenotypically.
- The Mosing group and the Hambrick group converge on the finding that the practice–ability correlation is confounded by genetic factors.
- All absolute pitch researchers agree that AP requires both genetic predisposition AND early musical training.

### Conflicts and Tensions

- **The core disagreement between Ericsson and Hambrick/Mosing** is about whether the residual variance (after genetic factors) is genuinely causal from practice or from other non-genetic factors. Ericsson concedes some genetic role for body size in sports but resists extending this to cognitive domains.
- **The Platz et al. meta-analysis** (referenced in Hambrick 2014 reply) found a higher correlation (r = .61 corrected) between practice and music performance, vs. Macnamara's lower estimate. This is partly a methodological dispute about inclusion criteria.
- **Deutsch's tonal-language findings** create a fascinating interpretive problem: is the high AP rate in tone-language speakers evidence for *genetic* differences, or *developmental critical period* differences, or both? Gregersen 2001 suggests both.
- **The Drayna 2001 finding** (DZ concordance = 57% for pitch recognition) is surprisingly high — higher than would be expected under a simple additive genetic model. This suggests either shared environmental contributions or gene-environment interaction inflate DZ similarity.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **Rhythm heritability is under-studied** relative to pitch. Most genetic studies focus on pitch or absolute pitch. The "beat induction" component of rhythm (which has separate genetic architecture from pitch, based on the Mosing data showing distinct heritabilities) deserves dedicated genetic investigation.

2. **GWAS for general musical aptitude**: Unlike AP, genome-wide association studies for general musical discrimination ability are largely absent. The polygenic score work (Wesseldijk 2024) uses cognitive PGS as a proxy, not a music-specific PGS. This is partly because musical aptitude is hard to measure cheaply at scale.

3. **Molecular basis of the observed heritability**: The chromosome loci found (4q, 8q, 12q, 17q) account for only a small fraction of the estimated heritability. The "missing heritability" problem in music parallels what is seen in most complex behavioral traits.

4. **Critical period mechanics for absolute pitch**: The window appears to close around age 7–9, but the biological mechanism (what maintains the window, why it closes) is not established. The Deutsch tonal-language evidence suggests pitch-category formation during language acquisition, but the neural mechanism linking this to later AP acquisition is unclear.

5. **Sex differences in heritability**: The SMDT study found notably higher heritability for females on melody and rhythm. This has not been followed up with targeted investigation.

6. **Longitudinal genetic studies**: Nearly all work is cross-sectional. It is unknown whether heritability estimates change across development (though Hambrick & Tucker-Drob speculate that genetic effects may strengthen with age, consistent with gene-environment interaction models).

### Surprises

1. **Music practice is 40–70% heritable.** This is the most counterintuitive finding in the literature. The common assumption is that practice is purely a behavioral choice (effort, discipline, opportunity), but the data suggest it is partly under genetic influence — likely mediated through traits like personality (conscientiousness, intrinsic motivation, enjoyment) and underlying aptitude itself (people practice more when they're good at something).

2. **Heritability of musical expertise *increases* with more practice** (Mosing 2024). This is the direct opposite of Ericsson's prediction and implies that highly practiced musicians show *more* variation explained by genetics, not less. The interpretation is that as environmental variation is "optimized away" (everyone who practices intensively is in a good musical environment), the remaining variation is increasingly genetic.

3. **The MZ discordant-pairs test is very clean.** Some pairs differed by 20,000+ hours of practice and still showed no ability difference within pairs. This is a strong causal test that cannot be explained by confounders the way correlational data can.

4. **The chromosome 8q24.21 locus for absolute pitch** is in a region that has also been implicated in several cancers and developmental disorders — it's a gene-dense region with broad biological importance, not a music-specific region.

5. **The genetics of music ability is pleiotropically entangled with mental health.** The Wesseldijk 2023 study found that genetic propensity for major depression and bipolar disorder predicts music engagement — not because music causes mental illness, but because the same genes predispose both. This suggests that the "tortured artist" connection may have a genuine (if modest) genetic substrate.

## Key Papers Not Found / Limits of This Survey

- The Järvelä et al. Finnish family/GWAS studies on chromosome 4 (referenced in Tan et al. 2014) were not retrieved directly in this search; the findings are reported secondhand from the review paper.
- The Granot et al. / Ukkola et al. AVPR1A studies were not retrieved directly.
- The Coon & Carey 1989 study (one of the first twin studies of musical ability, cited in Mosing 2014) was not retrieved; its findings are mentioned in Mosing's text.
- Baharloo et al. 1998/2000 (the foundational AP family aggregation studies from UCSF) were not retrieved directly but are cited extensively in Theusch & Gitschier.
- No studies on genetic architecture of *congenital amusia* were found — the Jobling 2014 review mentions 39% first-degree relative prevalence but notes no gene hunts have been done. This is a notable gap.
