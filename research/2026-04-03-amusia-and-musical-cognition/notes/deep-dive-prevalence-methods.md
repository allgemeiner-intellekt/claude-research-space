# Deep Dive: Amusia Prevalence Numbers Across Tonal vs Non-Tonal Language Populations

## Question

Are the amusia prevalence figures of ~3.4% (Nan et al. 2010, Mandarin speakers) and ~1.5–4% (various Western samples) methodologically comparable? Is the "tonal language paradox" framing supported by the data, or does it collapse under scrutiny of diagnostic criteria and sampling?

## Investigation

### The Origin of the 4% Figure

The canonical 4% figure for Western prevalence originates from Kalmus & Fry (1980, *Annals of Human Genetics*). They used the **Distorted Tunes Test (DTT)** — a test in which participants listen to familiar folk melodies with occasional wrong notes and must identify whether a melody was played correctly. This is a fundamentally different kind of task from the MBEA. The DTT relies on prior familiarity with the test melodies (which disadvantages amusics who cannot assimilate melodies normally) and has a strong ceiling effect documented in later literature. The DTT's 4% figure was derived from a British adult sample of unspecified size. This is the sole empirical basis for the "4% prevalence" that appears in virtually all introductory characterizations of amusia.

### Nan et al. 2010 (Brain) — The Mandarin Study

**Full text obtained from peretzlab.ca.**

Nan, Sun, & Peretz (2010) recruited **117 Mandarin speakers without declared musical problems** from two Beijing universities, plus **22 self-declared amusic individuals** separately recruited via advertisements targeting people with music difficulties. The second group is not a prevalence sample — they were recruited specifically because they had difficulties. The prevalence estimate comes entirely from the first (normal) group.

**Diagnostic tool:** The full 6-test **MBEA** (Montreal Battery for Evaluation of Amusia) in its standard form. This is the same instrument Peretz's lab uses in Canadian/Western samples. The instructions and stimuli used were the same as in Peretz et al. 2003.

**Threshold:** 2 standard deviations below the mean of the 117 "normal" Mandarin speakers themselves — not below the Canadian normative mean. This is a critical methodological point: the cutoff was computed *within* the Mandarin sample.

**Result:** Out of 117 non-declared-amusic Mandarin speakers, **4 scored below the within-sample 2 SD cutoff** (71.7%), yielding a prevalence estimate of **3.4%** (4/117). The paper also reports a comparison cutoff for Canadian norms: 22.4 out of 30 items (74.7%), vs. 21.5 (71.7%) for Mandarin speakers.

**Key comparison data (Table 1 in the paper):**
- Mandarin normal group (n=117): Global MBEA mean = 86.9% (SD 7.6), cutoff = 71.7%, amusia rate = 3.4%
- Canadians (n=190): Global MBEA mean = 87.9% (SD 6.6), cutoff = 74.7%, amusia rate = 3.2%

This is the **most important finding for the cross-linguistic comparison**: when the same test (MBEA) and the same threshold method (2 SD below own-sample mean) are applied, both groups yield virtually identical prevalence: **3.4% Mandarin vs. 3.2% Canadian**. Nan et al. explicitly note this in their Discussion: "Among the 117 Mandarin speakers tested with the MBEA, 3.4% were classified as amusic, with a prevalence rate that is close to the 4% reported in Western countries (Kalmus and Fry, 1980)."

This means Nan et al. 2010 does **not** report an anomalously low tonal-language prevalence. They report roughly equivalent prevalence to what Western studies find with the same instrument.

### Peretz & Vuvan 2017 — The 1.5% Figure

**Full abstract/content obtained from Nature's open-access PDF.**

Peretz & Vuvan (2017, *European Journal of Human Genetics*) present what they call "the first large-based measure of prevalence" with **20,000 participants** — by far the largest sample to date. The study does not rely on self-referral.

The key methodological detail: they used **three objective tests and a questionnaire**. The three tests are from the online AMUSIA test battery (developed by Peretz et al. 2008 in *Music Perception* and validated against the full MBEA). Critically, the paper specifies that amusia diagnosis required **poor performance on all three tests simultaneously plus self-reported difficulty**. This is a more conservative multi-gate criterion than the MBEA 2 SD cutoff.

Result: **1.5%** prevalence, with slightly more females than males.

The paper explicitly acknowledges the discrepancy with the 4% figure, noting that 4% came from "a single estimate based on a single test from 1980" (the DTT). The 1.5% figure arises from a stricter criterion (multiple tests, large sample, no self-referral bias).

### The Provost (2010) Thesis — The Intermediate Figure

Provost's Master's thesis (University of Montréal, advisor Peretz) used the online AMUSIA test with **1,100 participants** and found a global prevalence of **11.6%** with the online test — but when broken down by type: pitch deafness = 1.5%, pitch memory amusia = 3.2%, pitch perception amusia = 3.3%, beat deafness = 3.3%. The 1.5% that Peretz & Vuvan (2017) cite for "congenital amusia" maps specifically onto the **pitch deafness** subtype, which corresponds most closely to the classic definition.

This is important: the 3.2% that some sources cite as Western prevalence corresponds to pitch memory amusia in Provost's typology — a related but distinct subtype. The figure used depends entirely on which diagnostic threshold and which subtype one is counting.

### Henry & McAuley 2013 — The SDT Critique

**Paper obtained from Max Planck repository (PDF). Key arguments reconstructed from citations in later literature plus paper title and abstract:**

Henry & McAuley (2013, *Music Perception* 30:480–496) argue that the MBEA's 2 SD cutoff criterion violates signal detection theory principles. The core problem: if the MBEA's subtests have chance performance levels that are not at 0% (most are 2-AFC or similar forced-choice designs with chance at 50%), then the 2 SD cutoff may be counting individuals who are genuinely guessing randomly as "impaired" rather than genuinely impaired but performing above chance. They argue that a d-prime criterion provides a more principled threshold. Under SDT analysis, the number of genuine amusics may be smaller than the 2 SD criterion suggests — which would tend to push prevalence **downward** from the 4% baseline. This critique was influential enough that Vuvan et al. (2017, *Montreal Protocol for Identification of Amusia*) explicitly addressed it, arguing that the MBEA should be used as a **screening tool**, not a standalone diagnostic instrument.

### The Montreal Protocol (Vuvan et al. 2017)

Vuvan, Paquette, Mignault-Goulet, Royal, Felezeu & Peretz (2017, *Behavior Research Methods*) issued a formal protocol in response to the critiques. Key findings:
- They argue the MBEA should be treated as the **first step in a multi-stage screening process**
- The 2 SD cutoff yields acceptable sensitivity but imperfect specificity, meaning some false positives exist
- They recommend adding d-prime analyses alongside percent correct, plus contextual measures (self-report, questionnaire)
- **Crucially:** they do not endorse cross-cultural use of the MBEA with **foreign-language normative cutoffs** — the norms should be derived from the local population

### Liu et al. 2023 (Current Biology) — The Half-Million Study

**Source: ScienceDaily press release + PMC full text (large).**

Liu, Hilton, Bergelson & Mehr (2023, *Current Biology* 33:1916–1925) conducted a massive web-based citizen science study with **~500,000 participants from 203 countries speaking 54 languages**, including 19 tonal languages.

**Critical methodological point:** This study did **not** measure amusia prevalence. It measured **mean performance differences** on three musical tasks:
1. Melody discrimination (fine-grained pitch: is this melody the same or different?)
2. Rhythm discrimination (is the beat in time?)  
3. Pitch in tune (is the vocalist singing in tune?)

The tasks used adaptive difficulty — participants received harder or easier versions depending on their performance. This is a continuous performance measure, not a diagnostic binary.

**Findings relevant to the tonal language question:**
- Tonal language speakers were **better on average at melody discrimination** (fine-grained pitch)
- Tonal language speakers were **worse on average at beat-based rhythm**
- Neither group differed on the "singing in tune" task
- The melody/rhythm advantage/disadvantage was equivalent to about half the benefit of music lessons

**What this study cannot tell us:** Because it uses continuous measures with adaptive difficulty, it cannot generate a prevalence estimate for amusia. It measures population-level mean differences, not the tail of the distribution. The finding that tonal speakers are *on average* better at melody discrimination does not imply they have fewer amusics at the bottom tail — the distribution shift could leave the amusia rate unchanged if the whole distribution shifts rightward together. The authors make no claim about prevalence.

### The "1.5% vs. 4%" Discrepancy Within Western Populations

This is the clearest example of how diagnostic criteria drive prevalence estimates:

| Study | N | Method | Threshold | Prevalence |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Kalmus & Fry 1980 | Unspecified UK adults | Distorted Tunes Test | Unstated formal threshold | ~4% |
| Cuddy et al. 2005 | University students | MBEA + DTT | 2 SD below mean | Low (few difficulties) |
| Provost 2010 (thesis) | 1,100 adults | Online AMUSIA test | Multiple subtypes | 1.5% (pitch deafness), 11.6% overall |
| Peretz & Vuvan 2017 | 20,000 adults | Online AMUSIA + questionnaire | Multi-gate | 1.5% |
| Nan et al. 2010 | 117 Mandarin + 190 Canadian (for comparison) | Full MBEA | 2 SD below within-sample mean | 3.4% (Mandarin), 3.2% (Canadian) |

The 4% → 1.5% drop is largely explained by:
1. The DTT has a ceiling effect and uses familiar melodies, meaning non-amusics find it easy and amusics who have limited familiarity with folk songs also struggle — inflating the rate
2. The AMUSIA online test, combined with a multi-gate criterion (must fail multiple tests, not just one), produces a more conservative estimate
3. Population differences in recruitment: Kalmus & Fry used unselected general population; Peretz & Vuvan's 20,000-participant study was a large-scale systematic screening

### Cross-Linguistic Comparisons Using the Same Instrument

The only study that explicitly used the **same diagnostic instrument (MBEA) with the same threshold method** applied to both a tonal and a non-tonal language group is **Nan et al. 2010** itself — and it found **virtually identical rates** (3.4% Mandarin vs. 3.2% Canadian, when using a within-sample 2 SD cutoff in both).

No study has done a single-design, properly powered cross-linguistic comparison using a fixed external cutoff and large equal samples from both groups. The Peretz lab's own norms on the MBEA website were derived from Canadian (French/English) samples; applying these norms to Mandarin speakers would introduce a bias, but Nan et al. wisely used within-sample norming instead.

The Liu et al. 2023 study with 500,000 participants is the most powerful cross-linguistic study but explicitly did not measure amusia prevalence — only mean performance shifts on adaptive continuous tasks.

## Key Findings

1. **Nan et al. 2010 used the standard MBEA, not a culturally adapted version.** The same stimuli and procedure as the Canadian norms were used. The threshold was computed within the Mandarin sample (2 SD below the Mandarin mean of 86.9%), yielding a cutoff of 71.7%. The resulting prevalence of 3.4% is nearly identical to the 3.2% from the Canadian comparison group in the same paper. *[Strong evidence — primary source read in full.]*

2. **The "4% in Western populations" traces to a single 1980 study using a different instrument (DTT) with different stimulus properties.** It is not directly comparable to MBEA-based estimates. *[Strong evidence — paper identified and characterized.]*

3. **The 1.5% figure (Peretz & Vuvan 2017) reflects a stricter multi-gate criterion** applied to 20,000 participants using the online AMUSIA tests (shorter, adaptive versions of MBEA subtests), requiring failure on multiple tests and self-reported difficulty. It is not methodologically equivalent to a 2 SD MBEA cutoff. *[Strong evidence — abstract and key findings obtained from open-access PDF.]*

4. **The tonal language paradox as originally framed (i.e., that tonal speakers "should" have less amusia) was never robustly supported by Nan et al. 2010**, which found equivalent rates. The paper's own conclusion was that tone language experience does not reduce amusia incidence. *[Strong evidence — primary source read.]*

5. **Liu et al. 2023 (N≈500,000) did not report prevalence.** It reported mean performance differences on continuous adaptive tasks showing tonal speakers are better at melody discrimination on average. This cannot be used to argue for lower amusia prevalence in tonal speakers — it is a different kind of measurement. *[Strong evidence — study design obtained from press release and PMC text.]*

6. **Henry & McAuley 2013 argued that the 2 SD cutoff on the MBEA over-identifies amusics** due to failure to apply signal detection theory, suggesting true prevalence is lower than 2–4%. This critique was addressed by Vuvan et al. 2017, who argued for using MBEA as a screening step rather than a standalone diagnostic. *[Moderate evidence — paper identified and characterized; full text not read due to size constraints.]*

7. **No published study uses the same diagnostic instrument with the same fixed external cutoffs in a single design comparing tonal and non-tonal language speakers at scale.** The closest is Nan et al. 2010, which compared 117 Mandarin speakers (within-sample normed) with 190 Canadians from existing norms — finding equivalent rates. *[Strong evidence — based on comprehensive search of primary literature.]*

## Assessment

**The cross-cultural comparison is at best weakly supported and at worst methodologically invalid if built on the 4% vs. 1.5–3.4% contrast — but for the opposite reason to what the review agent suspected.**

The numbers most commonly cited in the "tonal language paradox" framing are:
- "4% in Western populations" (from Kalmus & Fry 1980, DTT, a methodologically weaker instrument)
- "3.4% in Mandarin speakers" (from Nan et al. 2010, MBEA)

These use different instruments, and the 4% figure almost certainly overestimates true prevalence relative to MBEA-based estimates. When Nan et al. 2010 compared their Mandarin sample to Canadians **using the same MBEA instrument**, they got 3.4% vs. 3.2% — essentially identical.

The more interesting paradox is the **1.5% vs. 3.4%** split: both use MBEA-related instruments, but the 1.5% comes from a stricter multi-gate criterion applied to a much larger sample (20,000 vs. 117). The sample size difference alone matters: with only 117 participants in Nan et al. 2010, the confidence interval around 3.4% is enormous (approximately 0.9% to 8.5% at 95% CI), which completely encompasses the 1.5% figure.

**The tonal language paradox framing therefore has two distinct problems:**

1. **Instrument mismatch**: Comparing DTT-based 4% estimates to MBEA-based Chinese estimates is comparing apples to oranges. When the same instrument is used (MBEA), the rates are equivalent.

2. **Criterion mismatch**: The 1.5% from Peretz & Vuvan 2017 uses a stricter multi-gate criterion than the 2 SD MBEA cutoff used in Nan et al. 2010. These are not equivalent thresholds.

**What remains genuinely interesting**: Liu et al. 2023 provides strong evidence that tonal language experience shifts the *mean* of melodic pitch discrimination ability upward — this is a real effect. But mean shifts and prevalence of disorder at the tail are distinct phenomena. A tonal speaker population could show a higher average melody discrimination score while still having the same proportion of individuals with severe pitch processing deficits (amusia). The two measures are not interchangeable.

**Where uncertainty remains**: No study has administered the same diagnostic battery with the same externally validated, cross-culturally normed cutoffs to large (N > 500) samples of both tonal and non-tonal speakers simultaneously. The Nan et al. 2010 study is the best attempt, but its N=117 for the tonal group makes prevalence estimates highly unstable. The Liu et al. 2023 study could in principle be analyzed for tail prevalence (what fraction of tonal vs. non-tonal speakers score at floor on the melody task), but this analysis does not appear to have been reported.
