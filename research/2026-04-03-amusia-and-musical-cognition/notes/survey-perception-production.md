# Survey: Perception vs. Production in Musical Ability — Landscape Map

**Question**: Where is the line between perception and production in musical ability, and what does the dissociation reveal about architecture?

---

## What Was Searched

Ran multiple searches across Semantic Scholar and Exa covering:
- Amusia ERP/MMN/P300 unconscious pitch processing
- Poor-pitch singers with normal perception
- Prevalence of "tone deafness" vs. clinical amusia
- Sean Hutchins' work on singing accuracy
- Sensorimotor integration in vocal pitch production
- Beat deafness as a separate dissociation
- Training effects in amusia
- Arcuate fasciculus and auditory-motor connectivity
- Dalla Bella/Berkowska phenotype taxonomy
- Pfordresher inverse model of poor-pitch singing

## Key Sources

### 1. Pfordresher & Brown (2007) — "Poor-Pitch Singing in the Absence of Tone Deafness"
- **Link**: DOI:10.1525/MP.2007.25.2.95 | *Music Perception* 25(2):95–115
- **Key content**: The foundational empirical demonstration that poor-pitch singing and perceptual deficits are dissociable. Large-scale study showed that ~17% of people are poor-pitch singers, yet the majority of them have normal pitch perception. Singing inaccuracy is not just a symptom of not hearing pitch properly.
- **Assessment**: 204 citations. Peer-reviewed. Landmark paper. The key methodological advance was measuring both perceptual ability (tone discrimination tasks) and singing accuracy independently in the same participants. Establishes the production-side bottleneck as a real phenomenon.

### 2. Berkowska & Dalla Bella (2009) — "Acquired and Congenital Disorders of Sung Performance: A Review"
- **Link**: DOI:10.2478/v10053-008-0068-2 | *Advances in Cognitive Psychology* 5:69–83
- **Key content**: Synthesizes the "phenotype" landscape of poor singing. Explicitly identifies multiple failure points in the vocal sensorimotor loop (VSL): (1) pitch perception failure, (2) working/long-term memory failure for pitch sequences, (3) sensorimotor integration failure (can perceive the target, cannot translate to motor action), (4) motor execution failure (knows what to do but can't physically do it). Documents dissociations in both directions: amusics with perceptual deficits who also sing poorly; poor-pitch singers with intact perception; and (rare) cases of intact singing despite perceptual deficit. Introduces distinction between absolute vs. relative pitch failures in production.
- **Assessment**: 85 citations. Peer-reviewed review. Major contribution: maps multiple loci of failure, resisting the single-mechanism account.

### 3. Dalla Bella, Berkowska & Sowinski (2011) — "Disorders of Pitch Production in Tone Deafness"
- **Link**: DOI:10.3389/fpsyg.2011.00164 | *Frontiers in Psychology* 2:164 (Open Access)
- **Key content**: Extends the phenotype taxonomy. Key finding: in their lab's data, poor-pitch singers showed selective inaccuracy in the music domain without comparable deficits in speech pitch imitation. This directly supports architecturally separable mechanisms for music and language pitch production, not just a generic motor control problem. Argues poor-pitch singing is typically a sensorimotor integration problem rather than a perceptual one: the signal is received but not translated to action correctly.
- **Assessment**: 56 citations. Peer-reviewed. Open access. Important because it localizes the bottleneck: perception → motor mapping, not perception itself.

### 4. Hutchins, Zarate, Zatorre & Peretz (2010) — "An Acoustical Study of Vocal Pitch Matching in Congenital Amusia"
- **Link**: *Journal of the Acoustical Society of America* (2010)
- **Key content**: Amusics show impaired vocal pitch matching relative to controls, and their pitch-matching errors are correlated with their perceptual discrimination thresholds. This is the production-impaired-because-perception-is-impaired case: when the perceptual input is degraded, motor output follows. Key finding: amusics' pitch matching is less accurate but they still shift pitch in response to feedback (just less precisely), suggesting partial preservation of sensorimotor feedback mechanisms.
- **Assessment**: 59 citations. Peer-reviewed. Hutchins is a central figure in the field; this paper is the production-side complement to Peretz's perceptual work.

### 5. Hutchins & Peretz (2013) — "Vocal Pitch Shift in Congenital Amusia (Pitch Deafness)"
- **Link**: *Brain and Language* (2013)
- **Key content**: Directly tests whether amusics compensate for altered auditory feedback during vocalization. Amusics do shift pitch in response to feedback perturbations, but show a reduced magnitude of compensation relative to controls. This is critical: even when their explicit perception fails (they can't consciously detect small pitch changes), the sensorimotor feedback system is partially operative at some level. Raises the question of which perceptual representation drives the motor correction — possibly an implicit one.
- **Assessment**: 26 citations. Peer-reviewed. Points toward implicit/pre-attentive processing still feeding into the motor loop even in amusia.

### 6. Hutchins & Moreno (2013) — "The Linked Dual Representation (LDR) Model of Vocal Perception and Production"
- **Link**: DOI:10.3389/fpsyg.2013.00825 | *Frontiers in Psychology* (Open Access)
- **Key content**: Reviews the full landscape of perception-production dissociations and proposes the LDR model. Key claim: vocal pitch information can be encoded as (a) a symbolic/abstract representation (e.g., a named pitch class) or (b) a motoric representation (a stored action pattern). These are partly independent. Dissociations arise depending on which representation is intact. Explains why some people can sing accurately despite poor verbal pitch labeling, and why others have good labels but poor execution. Model predicts that singing accuracy should correlate with auditory imagery ability (motoric representation shares substrate with imagery). Also explains the "sing with yourself" effect: poor-pitch singers are better when copying their own voice.
- **Assessment**: 36 citations. Open-access review. Synthesizing theoretical model. Important because it offers a single framework that reconciles diverse findings. Limitation: dual-representation models are often unfalsifiable if not constrained.

### 7. Pfordresher & Mantell (2014) — "Singing with Yourself: Evidence for an Inverse Modeling Account of Poor-Pitch Singing"
- **Link**: DOI:10.1016/j.cogpsych.2013.12.005 | *Cognitive Psychology* 70:31–57
- **Key content**: Tests the inverse model account of poor-pitch singing. An inverse model translates a desired auditory target into motor commands. Finds that poor-pitch singers perform significantly better when they imitate their own voice (heard back in real time) vs. an external model — because the inverse model mapping from their own voice is more practiced. Also shows that poor-pitch singers have distorted or impoverished auditory imagery. Interprets poor-pitch singing as fundamentally a sensorimotor mapping problem, not a vocal motor execution problem.
- **Assessment**: 55 citations. Peer-reviewed empirical paper. Influential for identifying the locus of the bottleneck as the auditory→motor translation step, not the motor execution per se.

### 8. Pfordresher & Halpern (2013) — "Auditory Imagery and the Poor-Pitch Singer"
- **Link**: DOI:10.3758/s13423-013-0401-8 | *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review* 20:747–753
- **Key content**: Shows that percentage of trials sung in tune correlates with self-reported vividness of auditory imagery (BAIS scale). Singing accuracy is linked to how richly people can internally represent sound — supporting the LDR model's claim that a motoric/imagery representation underlies production accuracy.
- **Assessment**: 56 citations. Peer-reviewed. Clean behavioral result. Limitation: self-report imagery measures have noise.

### 9. Hutchins, Larrouy-Maestri & Peretz (2014) — "Singing Ability Is Rooted in Vocal-Motor Control of Pitch"
- **Link**: *Attention, Perception & Psychophysics* (2014)
- **Key content**: Argues that the primary determinant of singing accuracy is vocal-motor control (the ability to move the voice smoothly through pitch space), not perception. Even among people with good perceptual discrimination, those with poorer vocal-motor control sing less accurately. Distinguishes perception, motor planning, and motor execution as three distinct stages.
- **Assessment**: 38 citations. Peer-reviewed. Important for further fragmenting the "production" side.

### 10. Paquette, Mignault Goulet & Rothermich (2013) — "Prediction, Attention, and Unconscious Processing in Hierarchical Auditory Perception"
- **Link**: DOI:10.3389/fpsyg.2013.00955 | *Frontiers in Psychology* (Open Access)
- **Key content**: Review/commentary paper that assembles the ERP evidence for the perception-consciousness dissociation in amusia. The key empirical basis: in amusics, small pitch deviants (<1 semitone) elicit a normal MMN (pre-attentive mismatch detection, ~100–250 ms) but fail to elicit a normal P300 (the attention-dependent, conscious awareness response, ~300–500 ms). This means the amusic auditory cortex detects the pitch deviant at the pre-conscious level — the error signal exists — but cannot be brought to conscious awareness. The bottleneck is between early auditory processing and conscious perception, not at the sensory level.
- **Assessment**: 8 citations (commentary format). But references the key original empirical papers: Peretz et al. 2005, Mignault Goulet et al. 2012, Moreau et al. 2013. Provides a clear theoretical synthesis.

### 11. Zendel et al. (2022) — "Relationship Between Acoustic and Musical Pitch Processing in Adolescents"
- **Link**: *International Journal of Developmental Neuroscience* (2022) — includes Peretz as co-author
- **Key content**: In adolescents with poor pitch processing, early ERPs (ERAN, MMN) are similar to normal controls, but late positivities (P300, P600) are absent — exactly mirroring the adult amusia ERP signature. Double dissociation: roughly a third of children who would be missed by a pure acoustic discrimination screen (they can detect a deviant tone) but fail on musical pitch discrimination. Shows the MMN/P300 dissociation appears in development, not just acquired amusia.
- **Assessment**: 2 citations (recent). Peer-reviewed. Extends the amusia ERP signature developmentally.

### 12. Loui, Alsop & Schlaug (2009) — "Tone Deafness: A New Disconnection Syndrome?"
- **Link**: DOI:10.1523/JNEUROSCI.1701-09.2009 | *Journal of Neuroscience* 29:10215–10220
- **Key content**: Diffusion tensor tractography (DTI) reveals that tone-deaf individuals have reduced arcuate fasciculus (AF) connectivity compared to controls. The AF is the white-matter tract connecting auditory cortex (temporal lobe) to frontal motor planning areas — the structural substrate of the auditory-motor loop. Furthermore, superior AF branch predicted pitch discrimination ability; inferior AF branch predicted sound production accuracy. This is a double dissociation at the anatomical level: two different portions of the same tract serve the two directions of the loop.
- **Assessment**: 274 citations. High-impact peer-reviewed paper. Most direct structural evidence that the perception-production dissociation maps onto separable anatomical components of a single tract. Limitation: DTI is correlational; causality uncertain.

### 13. Chen et al. (2018) — "The Lateralized Arcuate Fasciculus in Developmental Pitch Disorders: Left for Speech and Right for Music"
- **Link**: *Brain Structure and Function* (2018)
- **Key content**: In Mandarin amusics, right AF anomalies predict music pitch deficits, while left AF integrity predicts speech pitch processing. This fine-grains the Loui result: it's not just reduced AF overall, but a hemispheric specialization within the tract — right for music, left for speech — that can dissociate.
- **Assessment**: 21 citations. Peer-reviewed. Directly addresses the speech/music dissociation within the same AF system.

### 14. Wilbiks, Vuvan, Girard, Peretz & Russo (2016) — "Effects of Vocal Training in a Musicophile with Congenital Amusia"
- **Link**: *Neurocase* (2016)
- **Key content**: Case study of Tim Falconer — confirmed amusic who loves music and attempted 18 months of formal vocal training. He showed temporary improvements in singing accuracy that faded when training ceased. Crucially: he did not improve on pitch perception tasks. The training effects were shallow and unstable. DTI imaging revealed reduced AF connectivity. Authors argue the structural deficit in AF is likely irreversible and places a ceiling on training gains. Small sustained improvement in rhythm/meter perception occurred.
- **Assessment**: 21 citations. Single case study (limitation), but compelling because the participant is well-characterized pre/post with structural imaging. Suggests training helps marginally at the production side without touching the perceptual deficit.

### 15. Phillips-Silver, Toiviainen, Gosselin et al. (2011) — "Born to Dance but Beat Deaf: A New Form of Congenital Amusia"
- **Link**: DOI:10.1016/j.neuropsychologia.2011.02.002 | *Neuropsychologia* 49(5):961–969
- **Key content**: Documents "Mathieu," a case of beat deafness — a congenital inability to synchronize movement to an auditory beat despite otherwise normal pitch perception and musical interest. Mathieu could perceive pitch normally and had normal spontaneous motor tempo (he could tap without an external stimulus), but failed completely at sensorimotor synchronization to a beat. This shows that beat/meter perception-action coupling can be selectively disrupted independently of pitch processing.
- **Assessment**: 211 citations. Landmark case study. Establishes beat deafness as a real, dissociable condition from pitch-based amusia.

### 16. Lagrois & Peretz (2019) — "The Co-occurrence of Pitch and Rhythm Disorders in Congenital Amusia"
- **Link**: *Cortex* (2019)
- **Key content**: Tests 10 pitch-deaf individuals for beat processing. 6 out of 10 showed beat-finding disorders, even with percussion stimuli (removing confound of pitch variation). Pitch and beat processing abilities were correlated. Complicates the clean dissociation from the Phillips-Silver paper: while beat deafness can occur without pitch deafness, pitch deafness often co-occurs with beat difficulties. They are not fully independent.
- **Assessment**: 18 citations. Peer-reviewed. Important corrective to overly clean dissociation claims.

### 17. Mathias, Lidji, Honing, Palmer & Peretz (2016) — "Electrical Brain Responses to Beat Irregularities in Two Cases of Beat Deafness"
- **Link**: *Frontiers in Neuroscience* (2016)
- **Key content**: ERP study of beat-deaf individuals. Like pitch-amusics, beat-deaf individuals showed a preserved MMN to beat omissions (pre-attentive detection intact) but abnormal P3b (conscious/attention-dependent processing compromised). The parallel architecture to pitch amusia is striking: in both cases, the brain detects the violation at an early stage, but conscious processing fails. Provides P3b abnormalities as a marker of beat deafness.
- **Assessment**: 21 citations. Open-access peer-reviewed paper. Directly parallels the pitch amusia ERP story, supporting a general principle about the consciousness bottleneck.

### 18. Wise & Sloboda (2008) — "Establishing an Empirical Profile of Self-Defined 'Tone Deafness'"
- **Link**: DOI:10.1177/102986490801200102 | *Musicae Scientiae* 12:26–3
- **Key content**: People who self-identify as "tone deaf" were tested on perceptual discrimination and singing accuracy. The vast majority of self-identified "tone deaf" individuals had normal pitch perception; their problem was exclusively in singing performance. True clinical amusia (MBEA-defined) did not characterize most self-reporters. Self-assessment massively overestimates perceptual impairment; the actual deficit is usually production-side.
- **Assessment**: 100 citations. Peer-reviewed. Crucial for establishing that colloquial "tone deafness" is almost entirely a production phenomenon mislabeled as a perceptual one.

### 19. Liu et al. (2013) — "Individuals with Congenital Amusia Imitate Pitches More Accurately in Singing Than in Speaking"
- **Link**: *Attention, Perception & Psychophysics* (2013)
- **Key content**: Amusics showed better pitch imitation accuracy in a singing context than in a speech context — the opposite of what you'd expect if the problem were purely perceptual. The singing context seems to recruit a more effective vocal-motor strategy. Includes Pfordresher and Stewart as co-authors.
- **Assessment**: 36 citations. Peer-reviewed. Somewhat surprising finding that complicates the simple "singing is harder than speech" view.

### 20. Pfordresher et al. (2015) — "On Drawing a Line Through the Spectrogram"
- **Link**: *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* (2015)
- **Key content**: Methodological critique of the practice of categorizing singers as "poor" vs. "good" by a single cutoff. Argues for analyzing vocal pitch imitation on a continuous scale. Demonstrates that categorical classification leads to both false positives and false negatives, and that experimental manipulations on individuals tell us more than population prevalence estimates.
- **Assessment**: 27 citations. Open-access. More methodological than empirical but important for interpreting prevalence claims.

### 21. Peretz, Cummings & Dubé (2007) — "The Genetics of Congenital Amusia: A Family-Aggregation Study"
- **Link**: *American Journal of Human Genetics* (2007)
- **Key content**: First family genetics study of amusia. Strong familial aggregation: first-degree relatives of amusics have ~39% probability of being amusic themselves (vs. ~3% in control families). Heritability estimated at ~70–80%. Amusia has a genetic basis but specific genes were not identified.
- **Assessment**: 242 citations. High-impact genetics paper. Establishes that amusia is neurodevelopmental and constitutional, not acquired — relevant to the training debate.

### 22. Suchy, Behroozmand & Railo (2025) — "Conscious and Unconscious Perception of Pitch Shifts in Auditory Feedback During Vocalization"
- **Link**: *bioRxiv* (2025, preprint)
- **Key content**: Directly tests the conscious/unconscious split in vocal sensorimotor control. Even on trials where participants explicitly report not noticing a pitch shift, a compensatory vocal response is generated. Conscious detection correlates with an "Auditory Awareness Negativity" (AAN) and a Late Positivity (LP). Source localization points to fronto-temporal-parietal networks. The vocal correction loop operates unconsciously; consciousness enhances but does not gate it.
- **Assessment**: 3 citations (preprint). Not yet peer-reviewed (submitted to bioRxiv). Directly relevant to the question of how implicit pitch detection feeds motor production, extending the amusia MMN/P300 story into the production domain.

### 23. Pfordresher & Greenspon (2024) — "Effects of Pitch Range on Singing Accuracy Training"
- **Link**: *Music & Science* (2024)
- **Key content**: Tested whether training with a wide pitch range improves singing accuracy more than narrow range training. Wide-range group showed significant improvement in single-pitch matching but not four-note melodies. Interprets the training effect as partially loosening the sensorimotor mapping restriction, since poor-pitch singers tend to compress their pitch range.
- **Assessment**: 0 citations so far. Recent peer-reviewed paper. Practical training implications, supports the inverse-model/sensorimotor-mapping account.

### 24. Pfeifer & Hamann (2015) — "Revising the Diagnosis of Congenital Amusia with the MBEA"
- **Link**: *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* (2015)
- **Key content**: Critical survey of how the Montreal Battery of Evaluation of Amusia (MBEA) is used in literature. Shows that prevalence estimates depend heavily on which cutoff is applied and how many subtests are required. With strict criteria: ~1.5–4% population prevalence. Three different subtypes of amusics found (some pitch only, some rhythm only, some both). Notes that web-based testing produces systematically different results from lab testing.
- **Assessment**: 38 citations. Peer-reviewed. Important methodological caveat: much of the prevalence literature is not directly comparable across studies.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The field is mature on several fronts:

1. **The dissociation is real and well-documented.** Poor-pitch singing in the absence of perceptual deficits (Pfordresher & Brown 2007) and perceptual deficits in the absence of equally poor singing (Liu et al. 2013) have both been demonstrated with reasonable sample sizes.

2. **There are at least three loci of failure** in the perception-to-production pathway: (a) perceptual representation formation — can the pitch be accurately encoded and retained? (b) auditory-motor translation — can the perceived target be mapped to a motor command (the inverse model)? (c) motor execution — can the command be physically realized? Most clinical amusia involves (a); most lay "tone deafness" involves (b).

3. **Implicit perception is preserved even when explicit fails.** The MMN/P300 dissociation in amusia is one of the cleanest demonstrations in cognitive neuroscience of a processing bottleneck between pre-conscious detection and conscious awareness. The amusic brain detects the pitch deviant (MMN intact) but cannot bring it to conscious awareness (P300 absent). Critically, this preserved implicit detection does feed into the vocal motor system — amusics do show compensation responses to pitch-shifted feedback, just attenuated (Hutchins & Peretz 2013).

4. **Beat deafness is dissociable from pitch amusia but not fully independent.** The Phillips-Silver case of Mathieu remains the clearest evidence for selective beat-deafness; the Lagrois & Peretz (2019) data showing 60% co-occurrence suggests the systems interact more than originally claimed. The ERP signature (MMN intact, P3b impaired) parallels pitch amusia exactly, suggesting a general architecture where pre-attentive detection works but conscious/attentive integration fails.

5. **Structural correlate is identified.** The arcuate fasciculus (Loui et al. 2009) is reduced in tone deafness, with superior and inferior branches predicting perceptual and production abilities respectively. This is a structural double dissociation and the most direct evidence that the two directions of the auditory-motor loop have anatomically distinct substrates.

6. **Training has limited, fragile effects.** The Wilbiks et al. (2016) case study and Pfordresher & Greenspon (2024) data converge on the view that production-side improvements can be achieved with training, but perceptual improvements are essentially absent in true congenital amusia. This maps onto the structural data: if the AF is structurally reduced, training cannot rebuild white matter.

### Agreements Between Sources

- Poor-pitch singing is common (~17% of population), perceptual amusia is rare (~1.5–4%).
- Self-identification as "tone deaf" almost always reflects production deficits, not perceptual ones.
- The sensorimotor loop runs in both directions: perception informs production (feedback), and production shapes perception (prediction).
- Pre-attentive pitch detection (indexed by MMN) is intact in amusia; the failure is in conscious access.
- The AF is the key structural substrate linking auditory and motor regions, and its integrity predicts both perception and production ability.

### Conflicts and Tensions

- **How independent are pitch amusia and beat deafness?** Phillips-Silver (2011) established clean dissociation; Lagrois & Peretz (2019) found 60% co-occurrence in pitch-amusics. These are not reconciled.
- **Is the conscious bottleneck the only failure, or is there also something wrong at the perceptual level?** Some accounts (e.g., Stewart et al.) argue that even early auditory processing is different in amusics, not just access to consciousness. The MMN research mostly shows a normal MMN, but there are reports of atypical MMN morphology in some amusics.
- **Production bottleneck location.** Hutchins argues singing ability is rooted in vocal-motor control; Pfordresher argues it is rooted in sensorimotor mapping (the inverse model). These may both be true at different levels.
- **Amusic singing in tonal vs. non-tonal language contexts.** Several Chinese-speaking amusic studies (Liu et al. 2013, 2016) find that amusics sing pitches more accurately in a singing frame than a speech frame — unexpected if the deficit were purely perceptual. Non-tonal language amusics may show a different pattern.

### Gaps and Under-explored Areas

1. **The production bottleneck has not been precisely located neurally.** We know the AF is reduced overall, but we don't know whether the bottleneck in sensorimotor mapping (inverse model) resides in the same nodes as the perceptual failure.

2. **Implicit pitch detection → motor correction pathway is poorly characterized.** It is clear that even explicit-perception-failing amusics generate some motor compensations (Hutchins & Peretz 2013), but which pre-attentive signal drives this? Is the MMN-generating system directly feeding the motor loop, or is there a third pathway?

3. **Training effects need longitudinal RCT studies.** Current training data is mostly single-session (Pfordresher) or single case (Wilbiks). Whether sustained training changes structural connectivity is untested.

4. **Individual variation within "poor-pitch singers"** is almost certainly heterogeneous — some may have imagery deficits, others pure motor issues, others AF anomalies. No study has mapped these sub-groups onto structural imaging.

5. **Beat deafness neuroscience is sparse.** Only a few cases are described in the literature. The ERP work on beat deafness (Mathias et al. 2016) is small N. Whether beat deafness has its own structural correlate (separate from pitch amusia's AF finding) is unknown.

6. **The "poor pitch singer with intact perception" has not been neuroimaged systematically.** The main structural finding (Loui 2009) used participants who presumably showed both perceptual and production deficits. Does a production-only deficit also correspond to AF anomalies, or a different structural signature?

### Surprises

- The colloquial label "tone deaf" is almost entirely a production mislabel. Most people who say they are tone deaf can hear pitch just fine. This is a significant public misconception with real implications (people avoid singing based on a false self-model).

- The finding that amusics show better pitch imitation in singing than in speech (Liu et al. 2013) is genuinely counterintuitive — singing is typically thought to be more demanding. It suggests that the singing vocal-motor routine may scaffold pitch production in a way that speech production doesn't.

- Implicit processing is not just a theoretical possibility in amusia — it actively drives motor compensation (Hutchins & Peretz 2013, Suchy et al. 2025). The "unconscious" perception is doing real work. This means the pre-attentive system is not just a vestige but a live contributor to the auditory-motor loop, even when consciousness fails.

- The AF result (Loui 2009) showing that superior vs. inferior branches of the same tract predict perception vs. production, respectively, is structurally elegant. Two sub-bundles within one tract = functional double dissociation. This level of specificity was not widely expected.
