## Key Sources

### Ayotte, Peretz & Hyde (2002) — Landmark group study establishing the diagnosis
- **Link**: DOI:10.1093/BRAIN/AWF028 (Brain, 535 citations)
- **Key content**: First major group study demonstrating congenital amusia as a distinct "music-specific disorder." Subjects failed melody discrimination, singing, and music recognition but passed language tasks. The paper explicitly concluded this was a learning disability specific to music.
- **Assessment**: Foundational, high-credibility. Published in Brain. Represents the early, strongly modularist reading of the condition. The "music-specific" framing in the title reflects a theoretical commitment that subsequent work has complicated.

### Peretz & Hyde (2003) — Defining pitch specificity
- **Link**: DOI:10.1016/S1364-6613(03)00150-5 (Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 297 citations)
- **Key content**: Argues that fine-grained pitch perception is the root of amusia, analogous to how fine temporal resolution underlies language. Concludes that musical abilities depend on pitch fine-tuning in ways that language does not. Still within a broadly modularist frame.
- **Assessment**: High-credibility review article in a top journal. Represents Peretz's own theoretical view; data are solid but interpretive emphasis on music-specificity is contestable.

### Peretz & Coltheart (2003) — The modular model
- **Link**: DOI:10.1038/nn1083 (Nature Neuroscience, 793 citations)
- **Key content**: Proposes a formal functional architecture for music processing, explicitly adapted from Fodorian modularity. Posits dedicated modules for melody, meter, rhythm, and memory buffering for tonal sequences. Uses neuropsychological dissociations (amusia without aphasia, and vice versa) as the primary evidence for modular independence. Amusia is interpreted as damage to the pitch contour processor.
- **Assessment**: Very highly cited, published in Nature Neuroscience. The dominant theoretical framework in the field for over a decade. However, it rests heavily on double dissociations from acquired (lesion-based) amusia rather than congenital amusia, and relies on the logic of functional architecture rather than direct neural evidence. Critics note that domain-general pitch deficits would look the same behaviorally in a modular framework if the module is itself pitch-based rather than music-based.

### Hyde, Lerch, Zatorre, Griffiths, Evans & Peretz (2007) — Cortical thickness findings
- **Link**: DOI:10.1523/JNEUROSCI.3039-07.2007 (J. Neuroscience, 325 citations)
- **Key content**: Amusic subjects (n=21) show **thicker** cortex in right inferior frontal gyrus (IFG) and right auditory cortex compared to controls (n=26). Paradoxically, more cortex correlates with worse function. Authors interpret this as cortical malformations (e.g., abnormal neuronal migration) that compromise the normal development of the right fronto-temporal pathway. Accompanied by reduced white matter in right IFG (from VBM in Hyde et al. 2006).
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, published in J. Neuroscience. Highly influential finding. The "when less is better" framing is important: increased cortical thickness signals developmental disruption rather than excess capacity. Supports a neurodevelopmental account that is not strictly modularist but does localize the anomaly to right fronto-temporal regions.

### Loui, Alsop & Schlaug (2009) — Disconnection syndrome hypothesis
- **Link**: DOI:10.1523/JNEUROSCI.1701-09.2009 (J. Neuroscience, 274 citations)
- **Key content**: DTI tractography shows that tone-deaf individuals have reduced arcuate fasciculus (AF) connectivity compared to controls. Both superior and inferior AF branches are implicated, correlating respectively with pitch discrimination and sound production-perception abilities. Authors frame tone deafness as a "disconnection syndrome" — analogous to conduction aphasia, but for pitch. This is domain-general in one sense (the AF serves pitch perception regardless of whether it's music or speech), but specifically auditory-motor in another.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, J. Neuroscience, 274 citations. Key methodological caveat: uses deterministic tractography. The Chen et al. paper below (51 citations) directly challenges this finding.

### Chen, Kumar, Williamson, Scholz, Griffiths, Stewart & Sammler — Tractography algorithm matters
- **Link**: (Semantic Scholar ID 19d9356c68f3f46ffe3496313c5ea076a6b2e720, 51 citations; no full DOI retrieved)
- **Key content**: The ability to detect the arcuate fasciculus in congenital amusia **depends critically on the tractography algorithm used**. When probabilistic rather than deterministic tractography is employed, the AF can be detected in amusics where it appeared absent. The authors argue that prior null-AF findings may reflect methodological artifact rather than true anatomy. This directly complicates Loui et al.'s disconnection narrative.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed; 51 citations. This is a significant methodological critique that has not been widely incorporated into narrative reviews. The AF deficit in amusia is less settled than it appeared.

### Albouy, Mattout, Bouet et al. (2013) — Deficit starts in auditory cortex
- **Link**: DOI:10.1093/brain/awt082 (Brain, 233 citations)
- **Key content**: MEG study showing that the pitch processing deficit in amusia begins in the auditory cortex itself (reduced and delayed responses), not only at higher fronto-temporal levels. Both the encoding of pitch and its short-term maintenance are impaired. Verbal short-term memory is normal. This suggests something goes wrong very early in the processing hierarchy — not just in a "music module" downstream.
- **Assessment**: High credibility (Brain journal), solid MEG methodology. This finding is often cited as evidence against a purely "late-stage" or "memory-only" account of amusia. The auditory cortex abnormality points toward a domain-general low-level perceptual problem.

### Albouy, Peretz, Bermudez, Zatorre, Tillmann & Caclin (2018) — Separate cortical systems for tonal vs. verbal memory
- **Link**: DOI:10.1002/hbm.24416 (Human Brain Mapping, 44 citations)
- **Key content**: fMRI study comparing tonal and verbal short-term memory. Amusics showed reduced right fronto-temporal activity and connectivity for tonal memory, but **normal** left fronto-temporal activation for verbal memory. A crucial group-by-material interaction: controls show tonal > verbal in right hemisphere; amusics show the reverse. Interpreted as evidence for dissociable specialized cortical systems for tonal vs. verbal memory.
- **Assessment**: Strong evidence for some degree of dissociation between tonal and verbal processing. This is one of the best functional neuroimaging pieces supporting a specialized (if not fully modular) tonal memory system. Published in a solid journal, reasonable sample size.

### Patel, Wong, Foxton, Lochy & Peretz (2008) — Speech intonation deficits
- **Link**: DOI:10.1525/MP.2008.25.4.357 (Music Perception, 154 citations)
- **Key content**: About 30% of amusics from both British and French-Canadian samples show difficulty discriminating a statement from a question based on final pitch fall or rise. This pitch-direction deficit in speech is the same deficit as in music — suggesting the problem is not music-specific but pitch-processing-specific.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, 154 citations, collaborative study across two groups. This is pivotal for the domain-general argument: amusia bleeds into speech intonation. The 30% figure is important — it's not universal, suggesting either variable severity of the underlying deficit or partial dissociability.

### Tillmann, Burnham, Nguyen et al. (2011) — Tonal language interference
- **Link**: (Frontiers in Psychology, 103 citations)
- **Key content**: Non-tonal language speakers with amusia show impaired discrimination of Mandarin Chinese and Thai lexical tones, which use pitch for word meaning. Performance was worse than controls for all materials. Concludes a "domain-general pitch-processing deficit" that may compromise the ability to learn tonal languages.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, open access, 103 citations. Important cross-linguistic extension. But note: the subjects are non-native speakers of tonal languages, so transfer to speech processing is being tested in an unusual context.

### Moreau, Jolicoeur & Peretz (2013) — Implicit pitch discrimination preserved
- **Link**: DOI:10.1016/j.bandc.2013.01.004 (Brain and Cognition, 95 citations)
- **Key content**: Using ERP (MMN paradigm), amusics show **normal pre-attentive brain responses** to small pitch differences that they cannot consciously detect. The deficit appears specifically at the attentive/conscious level, not at automatic sensory processing. This is perhaps the most theoretically interesting finding in the literature: the auditory cortex is detecting the pitch change, but the information fails to reach or influence conscious access.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, solid ERP methodology. This finding is replicated across multiple labs and is one of the most robust in the field. It creates a genuine paradox: if the auditory cortex is functioning normally at the automatic level, where does amusia come from? Albouy et al. (2013) partially resolves this by showing the *early* auditory response is abnormal in amplitude, even if the MMN fires. The two findings together point to a fronto-temporal access/routing problem rather than a purely peripheral one.

### Tillmann, Graves, Talamini et al. (2023) — Comprehensive recent review
- **Link**: DOI:10.1016/j.heares.2023.108855 (Hearing Research, 9 citations; recent)
- **Key content**: Reviews behavioral and neural correlates of congenital amusia with a focus on the fronto-temporal network hypothesis. Covers: pitch encoding and memory, extension to speech and emotion, functional and structural connectivity abnormalities, resting-state differences, spared implicit pitch processing, and prospects for rehabilitation. Explicitly frames amusia within a distributed fronto-temporal network rather than a single broken module.
- **Assessment**: Recent authoritative review by a core group (Tillmann, Lyon; Caclin, Lyon; Albouy). Captures the current state of the field. The framing has moved decisively away from simple modularity toward network-level explanations.

### Hoarau, Pralus, Moulin et al. (2024) — Profile of deficits, pitch, speech, and beyond
- **Link**: DOI:10.1016/j.neuropsychologia.2024.108960 (Neuropsychologia)
- **Key content**: Comprehensive behavioral profiling of 18 amusics vs. 19 controls. Confirmed: pitch change detection, pitch direction, and pitch STM are impaired. Also confirmed: emotion recognition impaired in vowels but **not in sentences** (speech provides compensatory cues). Stream segregation and speech-in-noise perception are **not impaired**. Text copying shows atypical eye movements and smaller memory span. Visual attention is preserved. Comorbidity with dyslexia flagged (30% of dyslexics show amusic profile; 25% of amusics show reading difficulties).
- **Assessment**: 2024, peer-reviewed. The most thorough recent behavioral profiling. The comorbidity with dyslexia is striking and suggests shared fronto-temporal white matter anomalies may underlie both conditions — a domain-general developmental neurological picture.

### Liu, Jiang, Pfordresher, Stewart et al. (2013) — Singing in context
- **Link**: DOI:10.3758/s13414-013-0506-1 (Attention, Perception & Psychophysics, 36 citations)
- **Key content**: Amusics imitate pitches **more accurately in singing than in speaking contexts**. This suggests that singing may engage a different pathway (auditory-motor loop?) that partially bypasses the impaired system. Implicates Lauren Stewart's London group, showing the perception/production boundary is porous.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed. This finding is theoretically awkward for modularity: if a broken "music module" causes amusia, why would singing context help? It suggests amusia involves framing/context effects on a shared pitch mechanism.

### Zhang, Shao & Huang (2017) — Deficits beyond pitch: vowel categorical perception
- **Link**: DOI:10.1371/journal.pone.0183151 (PLoS ONE, 43 citations)
- **Key content**: Cantonese-speaking amusics show impaired discrimination not only for lexical tones and pure tones but also for **vowel formant frequency differences** (a non-pitch spectral dimension). Duration (VOT) discrimination is spared. Argues the deficit extends to frequency/spectral processing broadly, not just pitch.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, PLoS ONE, 43 citations. If confirmed, this significantly expands the scope of the deficit beyond anything a "music module" could explain. It starts to look like a general spectral processing problem. This finding is somewhat isolated and needs replication.

### Williamson & Stewart (2010) — Memory for pitch beyond fine-grained discrimination
- **Link**: (Memory, 123 citations)
- **Key content**: Shows that amusia is not merely a fine-grained pitch threshold problem. Even when pitch changes are above threshold, short-term memory for pitch fails. Implicates a specific pitch memory deficit rather than (or in addition to) perceptual acuity alone.
- **Assessment**: Lauren Stewart's group at Goldsmiths/London. Important for establishing that there are two dissociable components to amusia: perception AND memory. This is consistent with the fronto-temporal network model — the STG handles encoding, the IFG handles memory maintenance.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The field has moved substantially over two decades. The early framing — Peretz's modular model (2002–2003) establishing congenital amusia as a "music-specific disorder" — has been progressively challenged from multiple directions without being cleanly refuted.

**What is solid:**
1. Congenital amusia is real, heritable (~80% heritability from family aggregation studies), and affects approximately 1.5% of the population. The Montreal Battery of Evaluation of Amusia (MBEA) provides standardized measurement.
2. The core deficit is in fine-grained pitch discrimination combined with pitch short-term memory. These two components can partially dissociate (some amusics have near-normal thresholds but fail memory tasks).
3. There are consistent neuroanatomical abnormalities: thicker right IFG and right auditory cortex (Hyde et al. 2007), reduced white matter in right IFG, and reduced functional connectivity in the right fronto-temporal network during pitch tasks.
4. Verbal short-term memory is preserved. Right-lateralized tonal memory systems are specifically impaired while left-lateralized verbal systems are intact (Albouy et al. 2018). This dissociation is the strongest current evidence for a specialized tonal memory system.
5. At the automatic/pre-attentive level, the auditory brain appears to detect pitch differences normally (MMN is present), but conscious access to these differences is blocked. This points to a fronto-temporal routing or access problem rather than a purely peripheral one.

**What is contested:**
1. Whether the arcuate fasciculus is actually absent/reduced in amusia remains unresolved. Loui et al. (2009) said yes; Chen et al. (51 citations) said it depends entirely on the tractography algorithm. This methodological controversy has not been cleanly resolved.
2. Whether the deficit is truly music-specific (favored by early Peretz work), pitch-specific (the intermediate position), or spectral-frequency-specific (Zhang et al.'s 2017 vowel data). The empirical picture increasingly favors a "pitch-specific but not music-specific" conclusion, with some evidence for even broader spectral effects.
3. The speech intonation findings are split: ~30% of amusics show statement/question discrimination deficits (Patel et al. 2008). This suggests variable severity, not categorical music-specificity.
4. The comorbidity with dyslexia (30%/25% overlap in both directions) is unexpected under strict modularity. It suggests broader language-related fronto-temporal pathway abnormalities.

### Agreements and Conflicts

**Strong agreement:** The locus is a right fronto-temporal network (right STG, right IFG, and their connectivity), not a unitary "music module." The fronto-temporal network framing is now consensus. No one in the current literature defends classical Fodorian music-module theory.

**The productive disagreement:** Is the right fronto-temporal network specialized for pitch processing per se (domain-general for pitch, not for music), or is there something specifically musical about its organization? The Albouy et al. (2018) tonal vs. verbal STM dissociation argues for something more than just pitch — there's a tonal/verbal segregation in memory that maps onto hemispheric lateralization. This is a weaker form of modularity: not "a music module," but "a pitch-memory system that is systematically different from verbal-memory systems."

**A genuine puzzle:** The implicit/explicit dissociation. Amusics' brains detect pitch differences automatically (normal MMN) but cannot report them consciously. This is the signature of a *disconnection* — sensory processing is intact, but it doesn't feed into conscious access or voluntary decision-making. Loui's disconnection syndrome framing captures this, but the specific white matter tract is unclear (AF dispute). The Albouy 2013 MEG paper complicates this by showing early auditory cortex responses are also abnormal. The two findings (normal MMN + abnormal early MEG response) are not easy to reconcile.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **What happens with environmental sounds?** Almost all studies use tones or music vs. speech. How do amusics do on pitch variations in environmental sounds (sirens, bird calls)? This would sharply test whether the deficit is for "music" per se or for pitch in any domain.
2. **Longitudinal developmental studies.** We have essentially no longitudinal data on how amusia manifests and changes across development. One childhood case study (Lebrun et al. 2012) is inadequate.
3. **The AF controversy needs resolution** using modern probabilistic tractography methods with matched samples. The Loui et al. (2009) finding is widely cited but its validity is in doubt.
4. **Beat deafness as a dissociation.** Phillips-Silver et al. (2011) found cases of "beat deafness" (congenital rhythm amusia) with preserved pitch processing. This is the reverse dissociation from standard amusia, and it is underexplored.
5. **Cross-cultural work in non-tonal language speakers who are native Cantonese/Mandarin speakers.** The tonal language studies mostly test non-native speakers (Tillmann 2011) or compare native speakers of tonal languages to controls. The functional consequences for everyday life in tonal language communities remain understudied.
6. **Individual differences within amusia.** Studies tend to treat amusics as homogeneous. But ~70% of amusics do NOT show speech intonation deficits (Patel 2008). There may be at least two subtypes: those with primarily pitch STM deficits, and those with both STM and perceptual acuity problems.
7. **Genetics.** Family aggregation shows high heritability (Peretz et al. 2007), but no specific genes have been identified. GWAS data would help clarify whether amusia's genetic architecture overlaps with dyslexia or language impairment genes (FOXP2 pathway?).

### Surprises

1. **Thicker cortex = worse function.** The Hyde et al. (2007) "when less is better" result is counterintuitive and important. More cortical thickness in right IFG and auditory cortex predicts worse music processing. This is consistent with cortical malformation (failed pruning) rather than underdevelopment.
2. **Implicit pitch detection is normal.** The MMN finding (Moreau et al. 2013) is genuinely surprising: amusics' brains register pitch changes they cannot consciously detect. This is not what you'd expect from a broken module — it's what you'd expect from a disconnection between sensory processing and conscious access.
3. **Singing context helps.** Amusics are more accurate at pitch imitation in singing than speaking (Liu et al. 2013). This context sensitivity is hard to explain from a module-damage perspective.
4. **Dyslexia comorbidity.** A 25-30% bidirectional comorbidity with dyslexia was not predicted by the modular theory. Both conditions now appear to involve overlapping fronto-temporal white matter anomalies with abnormal neuronal migration — suggesting a shared developmental neurobiology rather than independent domain-specific modules.
5. **Stream segregation is NOT impaired.** Despite pitch being essential to stream segregation (sound scene analysis), amusics perform normally on segregation tasks. This is an important domain-general function that survives amusia intact, suggesting the deficit is more specific than "general pitch processing."

### Overall Assessment

The honest answer to the survey question — "broken music-specific module or general auditory deficit?" — is: **neither cleanly**. The evidence points to a pitch-specific (not music-specific) deficit in fine-grained pitch discrimination and short-term memory, implemented via abnormalities in the right fronto-temporal network (primarily right STG to right IFG connectivity). The deficit extends beyond music into speech intonation and possibly vowel formant processing, but does NOT impair stream segregation, verbal memory, or (under most conditions) speech comprehension. This is consistent with a domain-general pitch processing mechanism that has particular consequences for music because music is the domain most dependent on fine-grained pitch.

The modular story served as a useful organizing hypothesis. The current picture — a distributed network with a selective pitch-memory impairment — is more consistent with general neural architecture principles than with strong Fodorian modularity. The right fronto-temporal network looks less like "the music module" and more like "a pitch-memory network that has been coopted as a substrate for music."
