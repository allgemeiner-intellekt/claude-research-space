# Deep Dive: Primary-Source Verification of CAR Peak Timing and Nocturnal Suicide Risk Claims

## Question

Two load-bearing empirical claims need primary-source verification before the report is written. (1) Does Bowles et al. (2022) actually state the CAR circadian peak at "3:40–3:45am," and if so what are the effect sizes, confidence intervals, sample size, and what does the forced desynchrony protocol actually demonstrate? (2) Does Tubbs/Perlis et al. (JCP 2021) actually report an OR of ~3.2x for 11pm–5am wakefulness, and is the 5am transition sharp or an artifact of binning? Additionally: does "Whose Mind after Midnight?" (Tubbs et al., Sleep 2023 abstract) find that documented depression → 13% lower nocturnal suicide likelihood, and is there a full paper?

---

## Investigation

### Claim 1: Bowles et al. (2022) — The CAR Circadian Peak

**Primary source confirmed.** DOI 10.3389/fnins.2022.995452 exists, is open access, and the full text is accessible via PubMed Central (PMC9669756). Semantic Scholar retrieves the complete abstract; the PDF was fetched directly.

**The 3:40–3:45am figure is verbatim in the paper.** The abstract states: "peaks occurring at a circadian phase corresponding to 3:40–3:45 a.m., with no detectable CAR during the circadian phases corresponding to the afternoon." The results section expands this: Protocol 1 showed a peak at "110° (corresponding to an average time of ∼3:40 a.m.)"; Protocol 2 peaked at "122° (corresponding to an average time of ∼3:45 a.m.)." This is not the survey agent's interpolation — it is the paper's own clock-time framing.

**How the clock-time conversion works.** The paper assigns each cortisol sample a circadian phase (0–359°) based on the individual's DLMO (dim light melatonin onset, set at 0°) and estimated circadian period. The peak phase in degrees is then back-converted to average clock time using the group's average DLMO timing and period. The conversion is participant-population-specific, not a universal formula. This matters for interpretation: "3:40–3:45am" is the average clock time corresponding to the peak circadian phase for this sample, not a universal clock anchor. Individual variation around this mean is not reported as a confidence interval on the clock time itself — only the circadian phase peak is estimated.

**Sample size.** N=34 adults total, but the two protocols used different participants: Protocol 1 = 17 adults (47% female, mean age 53.7±8.3 years); Protocol 2 = 18 adults (72% female, mean age 40.5±9.7 years). This is a small laboratory sample, not a population study. The older-skewing mean age (54 and 41 for the two protocols) is also notable for a paper whose findings are cited in relation to general-population 4am waking — circadian timing shifts toward earlier with age, so this sample may have slightly earlier natural timing than a younger population would.

**Effect sizes and statistics.** The peak-to-trough amplitude of the CAR circadian rhythm was 0.28 μg/dL for Protocol 1 and 0.12 μg/dL for Protocol 2. Both were statistically significant: Protocol 1 p<0.001, Protocol 2 p=0.002. The paper does not provide confidence intervals on the acrophase (peak timing) or on the amplitude — it reports only the amplitude values narratively with p-values for the cosinor fit. This is a genuine limitation: without CIs on the acrophase, the precision of "3:40–3:45am" as a clock time is visually convincing (two protocols agreeing) but statistically unanchored in the publication. No formal confidence interval brackets the peak timing estimate.

**Is the peak sharp or a broad plateau?** The paper fits a sinusoidal (cosinor) model to the data. A cosinor model is mathematically a cosine function — it has a single defined peak point (the acrophase) and is by definition smoothly curved rather than flat-topped. The paper does not explicitly describe the shape, but the model implies the CAR rises and falls symmetrically around the 3:40–3:45am acrophase, with the maximal slope on the ascending and descending flanks — not a flat plateau around 4am. The amplitude is modest (0.12–0.28 μg/dL absolute change), meaning the "peak" is the highest point of a relatively low-amplitude sinusoidal oscillation, not a dramatic spike. The paper also reports "no detectable CAR during the circadian phases corresponding to the afternoon" — which is itself a strong finding: the oscillation is not just modulated, it is effectively zero in the afternoon.

**Is circadian phase modulating CAR magnitude or CAR timing?** This is the conceptually sharpest question. The answer from the paper is: circadian phase modulates **CAR magnitude** — i.e., how large the cortisol rise after awakening is, not when the awakening-triggered rise occurs. The CAR is always defined as the response to awakening; the paper shows this response is biggest when awakening happens to fall at the circadian phase corresponding to ~3:40–3:45am. This is not the same as saying cortisol peaks at that clock hour during uninterrupted sleep. The forced desynchrony protocol distributes awakenings across all circadian phases precisely to isolate this effect. The implication for 4am waking is: if you spontaneously wake at 4am (or are forcibly awakened at 4am), the CAR you experience — the cortisol surge in the 30–60 minutes post-awakening — will be at its largest. This is not a CAR *timed* to 3:40am; it is a CAR *maximally large when triggered* at 3:40am.

**Individual variation and the subset problem.** The paper reports that not all participants showed significant circadian rhythmicity in the CAR: "60% of participants had a significant rhythm" (Protocol 1) and "71% of the detected rhythms were significant" (Protocol 2). This means roughly 30–40% of individuals showed no statistically significant circadian modulation of their CAR. The aggregate peak timing reflects the majority with detectable rhythms; the minority without such rhythms makes the population average noisier than the aggregate statistic suggests.

**Caveats about free-living generalization.** The paper explicitly situates itself as an in-laboratory forced desynchrony study in dim light (<3 lux). The limitations section does not extensively discuss free-living ecological validity, but the methods are inherently artificial: all behavioral factors (meals, exercise, activity) are uniformly distributed across the 24-hour cycle, which never happens in real life. The authors note implications for shift workers as one translational application. The broader caveat — that the CAR circadian peak timing in free-living populations may differ from the dim-light laboratory estimate due to light exposure, social cues, and meal timing acting as zeitgebers — is present only implicitly. The sample's older age profile is also not flagged as a generalizability concern in the paper itself, though it should be for any application to younger adults.

**Summary verdict on Claim 1.** The "3:40–3:45am" figure is verbatim in the paper and not the survey's invention. The effect is statistically significant and internally consistent across two independent protocols. However: (a) no confidence intervals on the clock-time peak are reported; (b) the sample is small (n=34, split across protocols) and older-skewed; (c) the peak represents the maximum CAR *magnitude when awakening coincides with that circadian phase*, not a spontaneous cortisol spike at that clock time; (d) only ~60–70% of participants showed detectable rhythmicity; (e) the cosinor shape implies a gradual sinusoidal curve rather than a sharp spike, so the whole pre-dawn window (roughly 2am–6am) sits in the elevated portion of the CAR circadian oscillation, with 3:40–3:45am as the estimated apex.

### Claim 2: Tubbs/Perlis et al. (JCP 2021) — NHANES Wakefulness and Suicide Risk

**Primary source confirmed.** DOI 10.4088/JCP.20m13820. Full citation: Tubbs AS, Fernandez F, Johnson DA, Perlis ML, Grandner MA. "Nocturnal and Morning Wakefulness Are Differentially Associated With Suicidal Ideation in a Nationally Representative Sample." J Clin Psychiatry. 2021;82(6). Published September 21, 2021.

**The survey agent's "3.2x elevated suicide risk" characterization is wrong for this paper.** The actual OR values in the Tubbs 2021 NHANES paper are:
- Nocturnal wakefulness (11pm–5am): OR = 1.16 per hour awake; 95% CI, 1.08–1.24
- Morning wakefulness (5am–11am): OR = 0.77 per hour awake; 95% CI, 0.70–0.85

The OR of 1.16 per hour is a modest per-hour increment, not a global 3.2x elevation. The 3.2x figure comes from a **different Tubbs et al. paper**: Tubbs AS et al. (2020), JCP 81(2), DOI 10.4088/jcp.19m12964, which used the National Violent Death Reporting System (N=35,338 actual suicides) and found the mean nocturnal IRR across months was 3.18 (SD=0.314) after adjusting for population wakefulness. That paper is about *completed suicides*, not suicidal ideation, and the metric is an incidence risk ratio relative to the proportion of the population awake at each hour, not an odds ratio for ideation per hour of wakefulness. These are measuring different things on different populations with different outcomes. The survey agent conflated the two papers or misattributed the 3.18 IRR to the 2021 NHANES ideation paper.

The 2024 JCP paper (Tubbs et al., DOI 10.4088/JCP.23m15207, "Risk for Suicide and Homicide Peaks at Night") extends the NVDRS analysis to 2003–2017 (78,647 suicides) and finds an even larger effect: 5-fold greater risk at 3AM (aIRR: 5.20; 95% CI, 4.74–5.70) after adjusting for demographics and population wakefulness. So there is a large effect in the completed-suicide wakefulness-adjusted literature — it is just not the 2021 NHANES ideation paper.

**Sample size and prevalence.** The 2021 NHANES paper: N=10,166 participants (51.1% female), NHANES 2015–2018. Suicidal ideation cases: 385 (47.5% female), population-weighted prevalence 3.37% (95% CI, 2.85%–3.87%).

**Is the 5am transition sharp or an artifact of binning?** This is the most important methodological question, and the paper does not fully resolve it. The 11pm–5am and 5am–11am windows appear to be operationalized as fixed bins in the NHANES questionnaire structure rather than being statistically derived from the data. The paper's methodology uses "time to bed" and "time out of bed" from NHANES, which are self-report variables. The choice of 11pm and 5am as breakpoints is not explained as a data-driven threshold — the paper presents these bins as given. The abstract reports differential directionality (harmful before 5am, protective after 5am) but does not test whether 5am is the exact inflection point, whether 4:30am or 5:30am would yield the same qualitative pattern, or whether the breakpoint varies by age or season. The lack of a piecewise regression or threshold-detection analysis means "5am" should be read as an approximate boundary, not a precise circadian clock transition.

**Is the boundary dawn-linked or clock-fixed?** The NHANES data uses fixed clock times (11pm–5am, 5am–11am), and the paper does not attempt to link these to sunrise timing or individual circadian phase. NHANES participants are spread across the US and across seasons, so the correlation between "5am" and dawn varies substantially across the dataset. The paper provides no analysis linking the breakpoint to civil twilight, solar noon, DLMO, or any circadian phase marker. The 5am threshold is a fixed-clock operationalization, not a dawn-linked or circadian-phase-linked one. Whether the protective shift at 5am is because dawn begins (biological), because early-morning alarms begin (social), or because the interval structure of NHANES self-report favors hour-rounded boundary effects cannot be determined from this paper.

**Does the nocturnal OR survive adjustment for depression?** This is a critical finding reported in the abstract and confirmed: the OR for nocturnal wakefulness and suicidal ideation (OR=1.16 per hour) is significant after adjusting for sociodemographic factors and sleep disorder symptoms, **but disappears after adjustment for severity of depression symptoms**. The paper explicitly states this: "even after adjustment for sociodemographic factors and symptoms of sleep disorders, but not after adjustment for the severity of depression symptoms." This means the nocturnal wakefulness effect is partially or fully mediated by depression — depressed individuals are more likely to be awake at night AND more likely to have suicidal ideation, and once you control for depression severity, the independent nocturnal wakefulness effect on ideation attenuates to non-significance. By contrast, the protective morning wakefulness effect (OR=0.77) survives all adjustment models.

**Subgroup analyses.** The 2021 NHANES paper does not report stratified subgroup analyses by age, depression status, or substance use. It adjusts for sociodemographic variables and sleep disorder symptoms as covariates but does not publish interaction terms or stratum-specific estimates.

**Summary verdict on Claim 2.** The survey's framing of "3.2x elevated suicide risk" for the 2021 NHANES paper is incorrect — that figure comes from a separate 2020 paper using completed suicide data (NVDRS), not the NHANES ideation study. The 2021 NHANES paper's actual finding is OR=1.16 per hour of nocturnal wakefulness (modest per-hour increment for ideation). The 5am transition from harmful to protective is real in the data but is a fixed-clock operationalization, not a tested circadian threshold or dawn-linked boundary. The nocturnal OR in the 2021 paper attenuates after depression adjustment, complicating direct causal inference. A stronger version of the nocturnal risk finding exists in the 2020 and 2024 completed-suicide papers (IRR ~3–5 after population-wakefulness adjustment), but these are methodologically different (incidence risk ratios using population wakefulness as offset, not per-hour OR for ideation).

### The "Whose Mind after Midnight?" 2023 Abstract and the Depression Finding

**The abstract exists and is a peer-reviewed conference abstract**, not a full paper. It is published in the Sleep supplement 2023 (DOI: 10.1093/sleep/zsad077.0655), and its lead author is Suzanne Gorovoy with Tubbs, Fernandez, Klerman, Basner, Chakravorty, Perlis, and Grandner. It uses the same NVDRS dataset (78,647 suicides, 2003–2017) as the 2024 JCP paper, and is evidently the conference presentation that preceded or ran alongside the 2024 full paper.

**The "13% lower nocturnal suicide" claim cannot be confirmed from the abstract's reported results.** The abstract lists subgroup interactions that reached statistical significance: age (p<0.001), ethnicity (p=0.014), blood alcohol level (p<0.001), and current partner conflict (p<0.001). The subgroups tested also included sex, race, military service, marijuana positive on autopsy, current depressed mood, and prior suicidal ideation or attempts. However, **the abstract reports only the significant interaction subgroups** — current depressed mood is listed as a variable tested but does not appear in the reported results section of the abstract, meaning it did not produce a statistically significant interaction. This strongly suggests the "depression → 13% lower nocturnal suicide" figure was either (a) from a different version of the analysis, (b) a figure reported verbally at the conference but not in the abstract text, or (c) a misremembered or misattributed statistic.

The 2024 JCP full paper's abstract states: "Nighttime risk was greater among young adults and those intoxicated with alcohol, **but not among those with a history of suicidal ideation or attempts**." Prior suicidal ideation/attempts not modifying nocturnal risk is a null finding (no differential nocturnal elevation for the prior-ideation subgroup), which is different from documenting that depressed individuals are *less* likely to die nocturnally. The null finding for prior ideation is consistent with the 2023 abstract's non-significant depression interaction.

**The most that can be confirmed from primary sources.** What the 2023/2024 Tubbs et al. data show is: (1) the nocturnal peak in suicide risk is significantly modulated by age (sharper at 4am for young adults 15–34; shifted to 6am for older adults 65+), ethnicity, blood alcohol level, and current partner conflict; (2) prior suicidal ideation or attempts does *not* significantly alter the nocturnal risk pattern; (3) current depressed mood did not produce a significant subgroup interaction in the published abstract. There is no primary-source documentation of a "13% lower nocturnal suicide" figure for depression-documented individuals. This specific claim from the survey note appears to be either unverifiable or fabricated/misremembered. The survey agent treated a conference abstract as if it contained a specific quantitative finding that is not in the abstract's text.

**Is there a full paper?** Yes: the 2024 JCP paper (Tubbs et al., DOI 10.4088/JCP.23m15207) is the full peer-reviewed version. It confirms the subgroups that matter (age, alcohol, ethnicity, partner conflict) and explicitly notes that prior ideation/attempts did not modify nocturnal risk. Depression as a documented prior condition is not reported as a significant moderator in this paper.

## Key Findings

- **Bowles et al. 2022 "3:40–3:45am" is verbatim in the paper** (abstract and results section), not the survey's interpretation. Strong evidence. DOI 10.3389/fnins.2022.995452; PMC9669756.

- **The CAR peak timing is derived from circadian phase degrees (110°, 122°) converted to average clock time via individual DLMO**, meaning "3:40–3:45am" is a sample-average estimate, not a universal human constant. No confidence interval on the clock-time estimate is reported. Moderate confidence in the precision.

- **Sample size is small (n=17 and n=18 for the two protocols) and older-skewing (mean ages ~54 and ~41)**; only ~60–70% of individuals showed detectable circadian rhythmicity in CAR. The aggregate finding is statistically significant but the individual-level heterogeneity is substantial. Moderate confidence in generalizability.

- **Peak amplitude is modest** (0.28 μg/dL Protocol 1, 0.12 μg/dL Protocol 2) and the cosinor shape is a smooth sinusoid. The 3:40–3:45am figure marks the apex of a broad pre-dawn elevation, not a narrow spike. The whole window approximately 2am–6am is in the upper half of the oscillation. Moderate evidence.

- **What the CAR peak means**: circadian phase modulates the *magnitude* of the cortisol response upon awakening, not the baseline clock-hour cortisol. If you wake at 4am you get the largest cortisol surge post-awakening; if you wake at 4am but your DLMO is at 10pm your cortisol surge is in the evening. Strong evidence this is the correct interpretation.

- **Tubbs 2021 NHANES paper (DOI 10.4088/JCP.20m13820) is confirmed**: OR=1.16 per hour nocturnal wakefulness (95% CI 1.08–1.24), OR=0.77 morning wakefulness (95% CI 0.70–0.85), N=10,166, n=385 ideation cases. These are the actual figures — not the "3.2x" the survey agent cited for this paper. Strong evidence.

- **The "3.2x" figure comes from Tubbs et al. 2020** (DOI 10.4088/jcp.19m12964): mean nocturnal IRR = 3.18 across months for *completed suicides* using population-wakefulness adjustment on NVDRS data (N=35,338). The 2024 paper extends this to a 5-fold peak IRR at 3am (aIRR 5.20 [4.74–5.70]) on 78,647 suicides. These are incidence risk ratios for completed suicides, not ORs for ideation. The survey conflated distinct papers with different outcomes and metrics. Strong evidence of misattribution.

- **The 5am transition is a fixed-clock bin operationalized from NHANES self-report**, not a statistically derived threshold or dawn-linked boundary. Whether 4:30am or 5:30am would produce the same qualitative pattern is not tested. The protective effect of morning wakefulness (OR=0.77) is more robust (survives all adjustment models) than the nocturnal risk effect (which attenuates after depression adjustment). Moderate confidence in the 5am figure as an approximate boundary; low confidence in its precision.

- **The nocturnal OR (1.16/hr) disappears after depression severity adjustment** — a critical finding not emphasized by the survey. The protective morning OR (0.77) does not disappear. Strong evidence.

- **"Whose Mind after Midnight?" (Sleep 2023 abstract, DOI 10.1093/sleep/zsad077.0655) is a real conference abstract** with the cited authorship. It uses NVDRS data, N=78,647 suicides. The significant subgroup modifiers are age, ethnicity, BAL, and partner conflict. Depression is *not* among the significant modifiers in the reported abstract results. The "13% lower nocturnal suicide for documented depression" figure is not in the abstract text and cannot be verified from any primary source. The survey agent's characterization of this finding should be treated as unverified and potentially inaccurate. Moderate confidence in this assessment (the abstract may be a truncated version that omitted the finding; conference presentations sometimes contain more than the published abstract).

- **The 2024 JCP full paper (DOI 10.4088/JCP.23m15207)** is the published version of the 2023 Sleep abstract. It confirms: age, ethnicity, BAL, and partner conflict modify nocturnal risk. Prior suicidal ideation/attempts does NOT modify nocturnal risk (explicit null finding). Depression status is not mentioned as a significant subgroup modifier. Strong evidence.

## Assessment

**On Claim 1 (Bowles/CAR peak):** The survey's core empirical claim holds up remarkably well against the primary source. The "3:40–3:45am" figure is the paper's own language, appears in both the abstract and the results, and is replicated across two independent protocols. The finding is real and the survey characterization is accurate. The caveats that matter for the report: (1) this is a magnitude-modulation effect, not a spontaneous clock-hour cortisol spike; (2) the sample is small and old; (3) no CIs on the clock-time estimate; (4) the peak is a sinusoidal apex, not a spike — so the pre-dawn window broadly is elevated, with 3:40–3:45am as the estimated centre. The report should use this finding but note these qualifications to avoid overclaiming precision.

**On Claim 2 (Tubbs/NHANES suicide risk):** The survey's framing was materially wrong about which paper the "3.2x" figure comes from. The NHANES ideation paper (2021) shows a modest per-hour OR of 1.16, not 3.2x. The larger effect sizes (IRR ~3–5x for completed suicide) come from NVDRS-based papers (2020, 2024). Both lines of evidence are real and reinforce each other, but they are different papers with different designs, outcomes, and populations. For the report, these should be clearly distinguished: the ideation risk is per-hour and modest; the completed-suicide wakefulness-adjusted risk is large (3–5x) but is a population-level IRR, not an individual OR. The 5am boundary is approximate and fixed-clock, not a tested circadian threshold. The nocturnal OR attenuates with depression adjustment, which is the most important caveat for the report's thesis.

**On the "depression → lower nocturnal suicide" claim:** This specific quantitative finding (13% lower) is not in any accessible primary source. The 2023 Sleep abstract — the only candidate source — does not report it, and the 2024 JCP full paper's explicit null finding for prior ideation/attempts (while not identical to current depression) points in the same direction of no significant nocturnal differential for clinical subgroups. The survey agent's note treats this as a "critical data" point and builds an interpretive structure around it ("depressed suicides die in a different temporal window"). That interpretive structure may well be correct conceptually, but the specific 13% figure should not be cited in the report as a documented finding. What *can* be said from primary sources: depressed mood was tested as a subgroup moderator and did not reach statistical significance for interaction in the published abstract, and prior suicidal ideation/attempts did not significantly alter the nocturnal risk pattern in the 2024 full paper. The absence of a significant depression-subgroup interaction is itself meaningful and should be used honestly rather than replaced with an unverified specific figure.

The core thesis of the report — that 4am occupies a singular neurobiological position as the circadian CAR peak and the transition zone between nocturnal wakefulness risk and morning wakefulness protection — is well-supported by primary sources, with the important correction that the Tubbs 2021 effects are smaller and more complex than the survey characterized them.
