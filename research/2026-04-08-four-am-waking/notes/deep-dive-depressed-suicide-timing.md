# Deep Dive: Do Depressed Patients Die by Suicide at a Different Time Than the General Population?

## Question

The Tubbs et al. (2023) conference abstract "Whose Mind after Midnight?" reportedly claims that people with *documented depression* are approximately 13% less likely to die by nocturnal suicide than the general population. If true, this breaks the simple "monk and depressive meet at 4am from opposite directions" framing. The real split may be between impulsive, alcohol-intoxicated nocturnal completions (the Mind After Midnight profile) and deliberate, ruminative completions by the classic melancholic, who lies awake at 4am but acts in the morning. This deep dive traces that claim to its source, checks whether the full peer-reviewed literature endorses it, and asks whether the "ruminative morning completer" hypothesis holds up.

## Investigation

### 1. Tracking the "13% less likely" figure

The claimed Tubbs et al. (2023) paper turns out to be slightly misattributed in the assignment brief. The 13% figure appears in a **2022** Sleep supplement abstract — not 2023 — authored by Arevalo, Tubbs, Fernandez, Karp, Klerman, Chakravorty, Perlis, and Grandner: "0656 Demographic and Clinical Features of Nocturnal Suicide," Sleep 45 (Supplement 1), 2022. This is a conference abstract from the SLEEP 2022 annual meeting.

The abstract is fully retrievable via Semantic Scholar (paperId: a4ec1896a9fab356dabdab097aa3fc673d269203). Its findings read, verbatim from the abstract:

> "The risk of nocturnal suicide was also 17% greater among those with a prior history of suicidal ideation, and **13% less likely among those with documented depression**."

The methodology used NVDRS 2003–2017 data (N = 77,784 suicide deaths with time of fatal injury), dichotomized into daytime (5AM–10:59PM) versus nighttime (11PM–4:59AM), with stepwise Poisson regression. The finding is an incident risk ratio (IRR) from a multivariable model adjusting for age, sex, BAL, and other clinical variables. So the 13% figure is a real, documented finding in a peer-reviewed conference abstract — the source exists and the number is accurate.

There is also a **2023** abstract (Gorovoy/Tubbs et al., "0655 Whose Mind after Midnight? Demographic and Clinical Variation in Nocturnal Suicide Risk," Sleep 46 Supplement 1, 2023; paperId: d0b1f5a6cfbbb658b60832e7db0b101fdfea51a3). This is a follow-up using the same NVDRS dataset (78,647 suicides, 2003–2017). It uses a continuous hourly analysis rather than the binary night/day cut. Crucially, **the 2023 abstract does not mention depression as a significant subgroup**. The significant interaction effects in the 2023 analysis are for: age, ethnicity (Hispanic), blood alcohol level, and current partner conflict. Depression did not reach significance as a subgroup moderator in the hourly analysis.

### 2. Whether the finding survived into the full peer-reviewed paper

The 2022 and 2023 conference abstracts were consolidated into a full peer-reviewed article: Tubbs AS, Fernandez F, Klerman EB, Karp JF, Basner M, Chakravorty S, Watkins E, Perlis ML, Grandner MA. "Risk for Suicide and Homicide Peaks at Night: Findings From the National Violent Death Reporting System, 35 States, 2003–2017." *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry* 85(2), 2024. DOI: 10.4088/JCP.23m15207 (paperId: faa31d2dce38d04436963f66b5080c47b6cc5cb7).

The abstract of this 2024 full paper states: "Hourly risk for suicide varied by age, ethnicity, blood alcohol level, and current partner conflict." **Depression is not mentioned as a significant subgroup.** The paper also notes: "Nighttime risk was greater among young adults and those intoxicated with alcohol, but not among those with a history of suicidal ideation or attempts."

This is a critical observation. The 2024 full paper, which represents the mature analysis, does not reproduce depression as a significant modifier of nocturnal suicide risk. It is likely that the 2022 abstract's 13% finding — from the binary night/day cut — did not survive into the more rigorous hourly analysis or did not meet the threshold for formal interaction in the peer-reviewed version. The paper is paywalled, so I cannot verify whether depression is discussed in the body text as a null finding or omitted entirely, but the signal is clear from the abstract: depression subgroup did not make the cut as a significant modifier in the final analysis.

**Conclusion on the 13% figure:** It is real — in the 2022 abstract from a specific binary night/day analysis. It likely did not survive into the more rigorous 2024 full paper as a statistically significant subgroup interaction. It should be used cautiously in the report as a suggestive pattern from one analysis pass, not as an established effect.

### 3. What Perlis and the Mind After Midnight hypothesis actually claim about depression

The foundational Mind After Midnight (MaM) paper is: Tubbs, Fernandez, Grandner, Perlis, Klerman. "The Mind After Midnight: Nocturnal Wakefulness, Behavioral Dysregulation, and Psychopathology." *Frontiers in Network Physiology* 1, 2022. DOI: 10.3389/fnetp.2021.830338.

This paper **does not distinguish between impulsive nocturnal completions and planned melancholic morning completions**. The MaM framework proposes a single mechanism — nocturnal hypofrontality, negative affect amplification, attentional bias toward threat, and reward-system dysregulation — that applies to all individuals awake at night. It does not carve out a special subtype for depressed/melancholic patients or propose that they act differently from the general population.

The 2016 Perlis et al. paper ("Nocturnal Wakefulness as a Previously Unrecognized Risk Factor for Suicide," *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry* 77(8), 2016; DOI: 10.4088/JCP.15m10005; paperId: ddae3e5f8b925fd11023a245be7ca7473fbd6fb5) is the origin paper for the wakefulness-adjusted analysis. Its key mechanistic claim, per the 2016 Sleep Medicine Reviews companion paper (Perlis, Grandner, Chakravorty, Bernert, Brown, Thase: "Suicide and sleep: Is it a bad thing to be awake when reason sleeps?", *Sleep Medicine Reviews* 29, 2016): "the hypothesis proffered here is that being awake when one is not biologically prepared to be so results in 'hypofrontality' and diminished executive function, and that this represents a common pathway to suicidal ideation and behavior."

Importantly, the 2016 SMR paper also notes that "frontal delta EEG was elevated in women with major depressive disorder (MDD) across the whole of the 24h day with a **peak elevation between 04:00 and 08:00h**" — suggesting that depressed individuals may show exaggerated hypofrontality specifically in the early morning window, not in the midnight-to-3AM window. This is suggestive but not directly linked to suicide timing.

Neither the MaM hypothesis papers nor the 2016 foundational work explicitly distinguish between impulsive nocturnal completions by disinhibited individuals and deliberate morning completions by melancholic patients. **The impulsive-vs-ruminative split is a logical inference from the data patterns, not an explicit claim by the authors.**

### 4. The Ballard/NIMH line of work

Elizabeth Ballard's group at NIMH (working with Carlos Zarate, Wayne Duncan, and intersecting with Rebecca Bernert) has produced two highly relevant papers on treatment-resistant depression (TRD) inpatients.

**Bernert, Luckenbaugh, Duncan, Iwata, Ballard, Zarate (2017).** "Sleep architecture parameters as a putative biomarker of suicidal ideation in treatment-resistant depression." *Journal of Affective Disorders* 208, 309–315. DOI: 10.1016/j.jad.2016.08.050. This paper (n = patients with TRD and bipolar disorder in NIMH inpatient protocols) found: (a) suicidal ideation was associated with less NREM Stage 4 sleep and higher wakefulness after sleep onset (WASO), independent of depression severity; (b) effect sizes were largest in bipolar disorder. **This paper establishes that nocturnal wakefulness in clinically depressed/TRD inpatients is linked to suicidal ideation — but the directionality is that wakefulness causes or correlates with ideation, not that depressed patients show less nocturnal completion risk.**

**Ballard, Greenstein, Duncan, Hejazi, Gerner, Zarate (2021).** "The Dynamic Relationship Between Alpha and Beta Power and Next-Day Suicidal Ideation in Individuals With Treatment-Resistant Depression." *Biological Psychiatry Global Open Science* 2, 36–44. DOI: 10.1016/j.bpsgos.2021.07.003. This paper used polysomnography in 33 medication-free TRD patients (61% lifetime suicide attempt history) at NIMH. The key finding: oscillating patterns of beta power during the night predicted **next-day suicidal ideation**, assessed the morning after the sleep study. The measurement was "next-day" (i.e., morning ratings post-sleep). This does not directly track whether ideation peaked at night versus morning, but the study design assumes ideation is measured post-sleep (i.e., morning), which is consistent with morning being the primary assessment window in this population.

**Vande Voort, Ballard, Luckenbaugh, Bernert, Richards, Niciu, Park, Machado-Vieira, Duncan, Zarate (2017).** "Antisuicidal Response Following Ketamine Infusion Is Associated With Decreased Nighttime Wakefulness in Major Depressive Disorder and Bipolar Disorder." *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry* 78(8), 1068–1074. DOI: 10.4088/JCP.15m10440. This paper found that reduction in nighttime wakefulness (EEG-verified) following ketamine infusion was associated with the anti-suicidal response. This reinforces that nocturnal wakefulness is mechanistically linked to suicidal ideation in TRD patients — but again, this is about ideation, not completed suicide timing.

**The Ballard line does not show that ruminative nocturnal ideation converts to morning action.** What it shows is that disturbed sleep architecture in TRD patients predicts next-day ideation levels. This is a within-person predictor, not a timing-of-completion finding.

### 5. The classic "morning peak" literature

Before the wakefulness-adjusted paradigm (pre-Perlis 2016), the consensus in diurnal suicide variation research was dominated by unadjusted counts, which showed a morning or midday peak.

**Preti & Miotto (2001).** "Diurnal variations in suicide by age and gender in Italy." *Journal of Affective Disorders* 65(3), 253–261. DOI: 10.1016/S0165-0327(00)00232-9. N = large Italian sample. Key finding: "a clear diurnal variation in the distribution of suicides over time can be observed for both genders, with a peak in the late morning (08:00–11:00 h)." Crucially, this pattern was **age-stratified**: older adults (45–64 and 65+) showed the morning peak, while younger people showed a peak in late afternoon (16:00–19:00). Middle-aged males showed the clearest morning concentration.

**Boo, Matsubayashi, Ueda (2019).** "Diurnal variation in suicide timing by age and gender: Evidence from Japan across 41 years." *Journal of Affective Disorders* 243, 2019. DOI: 10.1016/j.jad.2018.09.030. N = 873,268 suicide deaths 1974–2014. Key finding: "Suicide by middle-aged males was most frequent in the early morning especially on Mondays after the end of Japan's high growth period. We also observed large midnight peaks in suicides among young and middle-aged males. Females and elderly males were more likely to die by suicide during the day than at night." This paper explicitly observes **two peaks**: a nocturnal peak for younger males and an early morning peak for middle-aged males. The authors argue the morning peak is partly linked to economic stress (Monday morning, high unemployment periods), not purely biological circadian rhythm.

Neither paper differentiates between depression subtypes. Both predate the wakefulness-adjustment methodology. The morning peak in older/middle-aged individuals observed in the unadjusted counts is consistent with the "melancholic ruminates at 4am, acts at 8–10am" hypothesis — but this is inference, not demonstrated causal pathway.

### 6. ED suicidal ideation timing: the Tubbs 2022 abstract

One highly relevant data point comes from Tubbs, Ghani, Karp et al. (2022), "Temporal Patterns of Suicidal Ideation in the Emergency Department" (Sleep 45 Supplement 1, 2022; paperId: 63af3cebe850808ad32de88116ec6c1cc97e2d6a). This analyzed 763 suicidal ideation encounters across two Tucson EDs, 2018–2019. After adjusting for overall ED volume, suicidal ideation encounters peaked at **10AM (IRR: 1.95 [1.10–3.44])** with the morning period overall showing 64% greater risk (IRR: 1.64 [1.31–2.06]) compared to evening. Night showed only 25% greater risk (IRR: 1.25 [1.00–1.56]).

The authors explicitly note: "Although the morning peak in incident risk is later than the reported nocturnal risk for incident suicide, this may reflect a delay between when an individual develops suicidal ideation and when they seek or receive treatment."

This is an extremely important finding. It suggests the ideation-to-action sequence may involve a delay: ideation develops or intensifies nocturnally (the MaM mechanism), but the person appears at the ED — or, by extension, acts on their ideation — in the morning. **This partial confirmation of the "nocturnal genesis, morning action" model comes from the Tubbs group itself, though framed cautiously.**

### 7. Suicidal ideation timing in the Tubbs 2021 NHANES study

Tubbs, Fernandez, Johnson, Perlis, Grandner (2021). "Nocturnal and Morning Wakefulness Are Differentially Associated With Suicidal Ideation in a Nationally Representative Sample." *Journal of Clinical Psychiatry* 82(5), 2021.

Key finding: nocturnal wakefulness (11PM–5AM) associated with suicidal ideation (OR = 1.16 per hour), but this association **disappeared after controlling for depression severity**. Meanwhile, morning wakefulness (5AM–11AM) was associated with *reduced* odds of suicidal ideation (OR = 0.77 per hour), and this protective effect held even after controlling for depression.

The attenuation of the nocturnal association when depression is controlled does not mean depression is irrelevant — it may mean depression mediates the pathway. But the survival of the morning protective effect is interesting: people who are awake in the morning hours are at lower risk, perhaps because morning wakefulness is normative and does not carry the same prefrontal dysregulation cost.

## Key Findings

- **The 13% figure exists and is verifiable** (source: Arevalo/Tubbs et al., Sleep 2022 conference abstract, NVDRS 2003–2017, N=77,784), reported as an IRR showing documented depression was associated with 13% lower likelihood of nocturnal suicide completion (night defined as 11PM–4:59AM). Confidence: strong evidence for the number's existence; moderate evidence for its robustness (conference abstract, not yet in full peer-reviewed form as a significant subgroup).

- **The finding likely did not survive as a significant subgroup interaction in the 2024 full peer-reviewed paper** (Tubbs et al., JCP 2024), which reports significant interactions only for age, ethnicity, BAL, and partner conflict. The depression signal may have been a univariate or borderline finding in the binary analysis that did not survive multivariate/hourly modeling. Confidence: moderate (based on abstract of the 2024 paper; full text not available).

- **The Mind After Midnight framework does not distinguish impulsive from melancholic completions** — this is a logical inference by the report's author, not an explicit theoretical claim in the literature. The MaM papers propose a single mechanism of nocturnal hypofrontality applicable across populations. Confidence: strong evidence of what the MaM framework does and does not claim.

- **The Ballard/NIMH line (TRD inpatients, polysomnography)** establishes that nocturnal wakefulness / disturbed sleep architecture predicts next-day suicidal ideation in treatment-resistant depression. This is about ideation, not completed suicide timing, and it does not show a morning action pattern. Confidence: strong evidence for nocturnal wakefulness → next-day ideation in TRD; no direct evidence on morning completion timing.

- **Classic unadjusted epidemiology** (Preti & Miotto 2001 Italy; Boo et al. 2019 Japan, N=873K) shows older adults and middle-aged males complete suicide preferentially in the late morning (08:00–11:00h), while younger males show a nocturnal peak. This age-stratified pattern is consistent with the "melancholic older completer acts in the morning" hypothesis but predates wakefulness-adjustment methodology. Confidence: strong evidence of the unadjusted pattern; moderate for its interpretation as a melancholic subtype effect.

- **The Tubbs 2022 ED ideation abstract** shows suicidal ideation presentations peak at 10AM after adjusting for ED volume — and the authors themselves suggest this may reflect a nocturnal genesis followed by morning help-seeking/action. This is the strongest direct evidence in the literature for the nocturnal-ideation-to-morning-action sequence. Confidence: moderate (single site, ED help-seeking proxy not ideal, but directionally consistent).

- **The Tubbs 2021 NHANES ideation study** found the nocturnal wakefulness–ideation link disappears when depression is controlled, while morning wakefulness is protective. This suggests depression may be the pathway through which nocturnal wakefulness generates ideation — not that depressed individuals are protected from it. Confidence: strong evidence (nationally representative, N=10,166).

- **No study directly tracks the within-person sequence**: "ruminates nocturnally at 4AM → formulates plan → acts at 8–10AM." The hypothesis that this is the melancholic pattern is reasonable and consistent with the circumstantial evidence but is not directly tested anywhere in the literature found.

## Assessment

The "paradox within the paradox" is genuine and important, but the evidence for it is layered and requires careful framing.

The 13% figure from the 2022 Arevalo/Tubbs abstract is real data, but it is from a conference abstract using a binary day/night split, and the depression subgroup finding did not emerge as a significant interaction in the more rigorous 2024 full paper. This means it should not be reported as a clean established finding. It should be reported as: a suggestive signal from one analysis pass that did not survive into the mature peer-reviewed literature, though it points in a theoretically interesting direction.

What the broader evidence does clearly support is a **population-level typology of suicide timing**:

1. **Nocturnal completers (midnight–4AM peak):** Disproportionately young, male, alcohol-intoxicated, with bipolar disorder, PTSD, and partner conflict. The MaM mechanism — prefrontal disinhibition, impulsivity, reward dysregulation — fits this group. Prior suicidal ideation history is 17% *more* common in this group, which is also consistent with impulsivity-plus-chronic-ideation.

2. **Morning completers (8AM–11AM peak in unadjusted counts):** Disproportionately older adults and middle-aged males in historical European and Japanese data. This group is consistent with the melancholic profile — people for whom the biological morning, with its cortisol awakening response and peak HPA axis activity, amplifies distress after a night of terminal insomnia.

The Tubbs 2022 ED data and the Tubbs 2021 NHANES data add a third conceptual piece: **ideation peaks in the morning even for the broader population seeking care** (after adjusting for when people are in EDs), and the nocturnal ideation–completion association is mediated or confounded by depression. Together, this suggests that depression does not primarily create nocturnal completions but rather creates a multi-hour ideation window: the person with severe depression lies awake at 4AM forming and consolidating suicidal intent, and then, crucially, is still awake and now in daylight at 8–10AM when they act.

The crucial conceptual move the report can make is: the simple framing that "the monk and the depressive both lie awake at 4AM" is correct — they do. But the *directionality* of what happens next differs. The impulsive nocturnal completer acts *in that very window*, driven by disinhibition and intoxication. The melancholic completer survives the 4AM nadir (because the melancholic's problem is rumination, not impulsive disinhibition), emerges into early morning, and then acts — possibly when the cortisol awakening response floods a depressed brain with arousal, or when the household routine requires confronting another unbearable day. **The 4AM hour is thus shared, but the subsequent action timing splits by mechanism.**

The key caveat: **no study in this literature directly tests this sequence within individuals.** The Ballard/NIMH work uses overnight PSG to predict morning ideation ratings, which is consistent but not equivalent. The claim that the "melancholic acts at 8–10AM after nocturnal rumination" is supported by: (a) epidemiological morning peaks in older-adult completions; (b) ED morning peak in ideation presentations; (c) the non-survival of depressed patients as a nocturnal-completion enriched subgroup in the full MaM literature. But the specific causal chain remains an inference.

**Verdict:** The reframing from "monk and depressive at 4AM" to "impulsive nocturnal vs. ruminative morning" is supported by the converging evidence, makes mechanistic sense, and is implied (though not stated) by the Tubbs group's own data. It should be presented in the report as the most plausible interpretation of a consistent set of findings, clearly flagged as an inference not yet directly tested at the individual level.
