# The Rent on Four A.M.

There are three things worth knowing before anyone sets a 4am alarm, and none of them appears in the discourse that sold the alarm. The first is historical: clock-time 4am is not an ancient discipline. No pre-modern religious tradition — Benedictine, Sufi, Ayurvedic — ever specified "four in the morning" as a canonical hour, because the equal-hour mechanical clock that makes such a specification thinkable did not exist until roughly 1300 CE, and even then solar-relative calculations continued to govern devotional timekeeping for centuries. The "4am Club" as a brand is a post-2015 artefact, crystallised by Jocko Willink's social-media watch photographs and a 2016 *Wall Street Journal* profile of Tim Cook rising at 3:45. The 5am version of the same product dates, at the most generous reading, to Benjamin Franklin. The 4am version is younger than the iPhone.

The second is biological, and arrives as a quiet coincidence that changes the whole question: the hour the productivity gurus have competitively escalated to is also the apex of the circadian cortisol awakening response. Bowles et al. (2022), running forced-desynchrony protocols in thirty-four adults, report cosinor peaks at 3:40 and 3:45am across two independent cohorts [1]. Whatever time you actually wake, your post-wake cortisol surge is largest when the waking happens to coincide with that circadian phase. The monastics who chose this window for vigil were not simply conquering their bodies — they were, without the vocabulary for it, exploiting the single hour in the twenty-four when the body is most pharmacologically primed for intense, isolated attention.

The third is that this same hour is where depression lives. Terminal insomnia — waking involuntarily between 3 and 5am, unable to return to sleep — is one of the most specific biological markers of melancholic depression, associated with a 2–4 hour phase advance of the entire circadian system and a shortened REM latency that Wehr and Wirz-Justice identified in 1982 and which has replicated ever since [2]. The productivity guru and the depressive with early-morning awakening are not using a metaphor when they both describe 4am as "the hardest hour." They are occupying, by opposite vectors of arrival, the same neurochemical real estate. This report is about what that means, and about whether you can afford the rent.

## 1. The chronobiology: a continuous suppression protocol, not a reset

The mature chronotype literature is one of the more settled areas of sleep science. Twin studies across Finland, Korea, the US, and Brazil converge on heritability estimates of 40–54%, comparable to personality traits [3]. The 2019 Jones et al. GWAS identified 351 loci from 697,828 individuals, with a single PER2 missense variant accounting for roughly 11–12 minutes per allele of intrinsic circadian period and about 7% of its total variance [4]. Czeisler's foundational forced-desynchrony work established a mean intrinsic period of 24.18 hours with a tight standard deviation, and Duffy's 2011 finding that women run a ten-minute shorter period than men is the canonical biological correlate of the epidemiological fact that women are modestly earlier chronotypes until about age forty [5,6]. None of this is in doubt.

What is less appreciated is the ceiling on behavioural phase-shifting, and the relapse kinetics once the intervention stops. Van Andel's RCTs in adults with ADHD and delayed sleep phase — the population most studied because it is the one most desperate to shift — show that combined melatonin and bright-light therapy can advance the dim-light melatonin onset by one to two hours, but *behavioural* sleep timing does not follow without intensive coaching, and two weeks after stopping the protocol the phase advance is gone [7]. Timed exercise in the Thomas et al. trial produced 30–45 minute advances in both morning and evening conditions [8]. These are maintenance protocols, not clock resets. The most striking evidence is Landvreugd's 2025 Netherlands Twin Register study of 20,189 retirees: once the social pressure of employment is removed, polygenic morningness predicts chronotype as strongly as it did before, and people drift back to whatever their genes had always wanted [9]. The biology does not negotiate; it waits.

The cost of fighting it is dose-dependent and real. Roenneberg's 2012 Current Biology paper reports a 33% increased obesity odds per hour of social jetlag [10]; Knutson and von Schantz find a 10% elevation in all-cause mortality for UK Biobank evening chronotypes over 6.5 years after adjustment [11]; Levandovski's Brazilian rural cohort and O'Loughlin's Mendelian-randomisation analysis converge on depression as a causally elevated outcome of misalignment [12,13]. Crucially, Burns et al. (2024) found that the psychiatric cost of evening chronotype is *entirely mediated by sleep inertia* — the morning suffering of being woken at the wrong circadian phase — with no residual effect when evening types live on their own schedule [14]. The harm is not the chronotype. The harm is the mismatch.

None of the misalignment studies directly track the chosen 4am riser, because that population is rare enough and self-selected enough that no cohort has captured it. The mainstream misalignment literature studies shift workers, adolescents forced into early school starts, and the general population's workday-vs-weekend discrepancy. Extrapolating from those populations to an MSc student in their mid-twenties attempting a voluntary 8pm–4am schedule requires one bridge: does that student's circadian system actually shift to match, or are they waking at 4am while their biological clock still thinks it is 7am? The Roenneberg age curve offers partial guidance. Chronotype advances by roughly 1.5–2 hours between ages 20 and 60 [15], which means a 25-year-old attempting a 4am wake is working against a clock that has not yet naturally drifted earlier. The same schedule, attempted at 55, is a substantially cheaper transaction.

## 2. The REM tax: a mechanism in search of an experiment

The "you lose REM" argument has become the most popular biological objection to 4am waking, and it is mechanistically well-grounded but empirically thinner than the popular literature admits. Dijk and Czeisler's 1995 forced-desynchrony paper — the single foundational measurement in the field — established that REM propensity is predominantly circadian, with its crest positioned shortly after the core body temperature minimum, which in a normally entrained adult falls around 4–5am [16]. Slow-wave sleep, by contrast, is homeostatic and concentrates in the first third of any sleep episode regardless of when that episode begins. The implication, superficially, is that a person waking at 4am is terminating sleep exactly at peak REM propensity, losing the final and largest REM cycle the night would have produced.

This is true, with one crucial qualification that the popular literature elides: the Dijk-Czeisler mechanism describes REM's *circadian* timing. If the circadian system has genuinely shifted to match a new 8pm–4am schedule — if the dim-light melatonin onset has moved to 8pm and the temperature nadir has moved to 1am — then the circadian crest of REM shifts with it, and REM architecture is preserved. The tax applies to the misaligned forcer, not to the genuinely entrained early riser. The problem is that the study which would actually test this — an isocaloric comparison of stably-entrained 8pm–4am sleepers against 11pm–7am sleepers in healthy non-shift-worker adults, with polysomnography — does not exist in the indexed literature. I ran extensive searches across Semantic Scholar, Exa, and direct-source retrieval; what is available is forced-desynchrony data (artificial misalignment), chronotype comparisons in which each group sleeps at its own preferred phase (which cannot answer the question), truncation studies in which the late-night group loses total sleep duration rather than simply shifting it, and shift-work data from populations who are circadian-misaligned by definition.

The closest proxy — Zimberg et al. 2023, in shift workers — found that two groups with essentially identical total sleep duration (~6.7 hours) differed by 29 minutes of REM (107 vs 78 minutes, a 37% relative difference) based solely on whether their circadian phase had partially adapted to the schedule [17]. Reiter et al. 2022, running the mirror experiment (normal vs. delayed sleep timing in 72 healthy adults with DLMO-verified chronotype), found that late chronotypes obtained more REM when sleeping later — i.e., when aligned — and early chronotypes lost sleep when forced to sleep late [18]. Colelli et al. 2023, on 2,612 clinical PSG records, found morning types obtained more REM than evening types when sleeping close to their biological phase [19]. Every piece of evidence points the same direction: circadian alignment, not clock time per se, is what determines architecture. The "REM tax" is real — but for the 25-year-old MSc student forcing 4am waking while going to bed at 11pm, not for the mythical monk whose entire circadian system has migrated forward.

In practice, almost nobody in the 4am community is the second kind of person. Entraining a phase advance of three hours requires weeks of strict light exposure, melatonin at fixed pre-sleep timings, complete avoidance of evening blue light, an unshakable 8pm bedtime (including weekends), and — the part the gurus never mention — total darkness at 7pm in a world that does not go dark until 9pm in summer. This is not lifestyle; it is a daily medical protocol run against the architecture of modern civil life. The people who set 4am alarms overwhelmingly go to bed at 11 or midnight and sleep 4–5 hours, which is a genuine sleep restriction plus a genuine circadian misalignment, and which produces exactly the cognitive and affective costs that the sleep-restriction literature documents reliably. The Le et al. 2025 prospective multilevel study found that within-person reductions of 30 minutes per night of REM predict elevated next-day anxiety and tension [20]. The shift-worker mood data in Groeger et al. 2022 show that sleeping at atypical circadian phases reduces positive mood and increases hostility, with deterioration proportional to phase distance [21]. The popular discourse has taken a mechanism that *could* be neutralised by full entrainment and sold it as a lifestyle to people who will never entrain.

## 3. What 4am actually is, historically

The most important thing I learned in this investigation has nothing to do with chronobiology. It has to do with what 4am used to be, and what the current 4am Club is unknowingly buying a cheap counterfeit of.

Three independent streams converge on the pre-dawn hour as morally and spiritually potent, and they almost never get synthesised together. The first is biological. A. Roger Ekirch's work on segmented sleep — "At Day's Close" (2005) and a decade of subsequent *Past & Present* and PMC papers — documents that biphasic sleep ("first sleep" and "second sleep") was the pre-industrial European norm and recurs across Tupinambá, Muscati, and Ashanti ethnographic records [22]. People naturally woke around midnight or shortly after, had a wakeful period of 1–2 hours for prayer, sex, conversation, or tending fires, and then returned to a "second sleep" until dawn. Consolidated eight-hour sleep is an artefact of electric lighting and industrial work discipline. The implication, which Ekirch himself understates, is that pre-dawn waking was probably not a conquest of nature but a formalisation of what the body, left alone with darkness, does anyway. The monk who rose for Vigils at 2am was likely not fighting his biology; he was channelling a naturally occurring wakeful interval toward God.

The second stream is theological, and its convergence across otherwise unrelated traditions is striking. Benedict's Rule (c. 530 CE) places Vigils at "the eighth hour of the night" — a Roman temporal hour that is one-twelfth of nighttime and therefore contracts and expands with the seasons, placing winter Vigils at roughly 1–2am [23]. The theological rationale is the Parable of the Wise Virgins: the bridegroom comes at midnight, and only those awake with trimmed lamps enter. The Ashtanga Hridayam (Vagbhata, c. 7th century CE) prescribes brahma muhurta, the 14th of 15 night-muhurtas, beginning 96 minutes before sunrise and ending 48 minutes before — a solar-relative window that falls at roughly 4:24–5:12am for a 6am sunrise [24]. The Islamic tahajjud tradition, centred on the "last third of the night" in hadith collected in Sahih al-Bukhari and Muslim, is the window in which the divine is said to "descend to the lowest heaven" and petitions are answered [25]. Imam al-Ghazali personifies the night in three voices: "let the worshippers get up" at the start, "let those who stand at length in prayer get up" at midnight, and "let those seeking forgiveness get up" before daybreak. Across traditions the structural claim is identical: the pre-dawn hour has a quality of solitude, of absence of social claims, of cosmological threshold, that makes it uniquely suitable for a particular kind of attention. Christianity, Islam, Hinduism — three independent derivations converging on the same empirical fact about what human minds can do at 4am that they cannot do at 4pm.

The third stream is disciplinary, and this is where the story turns dark. Richard Baxter's *Christian Directory* (1673) opens with a directive on the holy spending of every day whose language is almost indistinguishable from a modern productivity newsletter: "Keep up a high esteem of time; and be every day more careful that you lose none of your time, than you are that you lose none of your gold or silver" [26]. Baxter frames wasted morning hours as sin against divine property. E.P. Thompson's 1967 *Past and Present* essay, "Time, Work-Discipline, and Industrial Capitalism," traced precisely how this Puritan time-thrift ethic became the ideological vehicle for industrial-capitalist time-discipline [27]. The material emblem of the transition is the knocker-upper, the paid profession in Victorian and Edwardian Britain that existed solely to rap on factory workers' windows at 4 or 5am — because alarm clocks were unreliable and expensive until the late 19th century, and because industrial punctuality was a class-enforced discipline before it was a personal aspiration. In Ferryhill, County Durham, miners chalked their shift details onto slate boards mounted on exterior walls so knocker-uppers could read them in the dark. This is the moment early rising stopped being devotional and became coerced. The productivity guru of 2026, waking voluntarily at 4am to "own his morning," is claiming back as virtue what Molly Moore's peashooter once imposed as discipline on people who could not afford to refuse.

And then Benjamin Franklin, in his *Poor Richard's Almanack* (1735), abridges a 1486 English proverb. The original form, from *The Book of St. Albans*, was: "Who so woll ryse erly shall be holy, helthy, and zely" — holy, healthy, and fortunate. Franklin replaces *holy* with *wealthy*, keeps *healthy*, and the triad completes its secularisation [28]. The religious component — the claim that early rising is an act of stewardship over time that one does not own — is quietly deleted, and what remains is a secular proverb which looks identical but means something entirely different. From Franklin to Robin Sharma's "5am Club" (c. 1998) to Jocko Willink's 4:30am watch photos (2015) to Tim Cook's 3:45 in the *WSJ* (2016), the line is continuous. Each iteration strips a further layer of the original devotional content while keeping the structural claim — *this hour is mine, before the world takes it* — and attaches it to a progressively earlier clock hour as the signal inflates in a competitive display. The shift from 5am to 4am in the last decade has no pre-modern analogue; it is a social-media phenomenon whose logic is legible only because a specific clock digit has become publicly displayable as a status signal.

Here is the sharpest version of what all of this implies, and I think it is the most interesting claim this investigation produced. Pre-modern traditions specified *what kind of time* was spiritually potent — liminal time, the hour before dawn, the transition between night and day — and used solar calculations to locate it, because solar cues were the only clocks available and because the phenomenology of the pre-dawn hour is tied to real darkness. The modern 4am norm has severed the liminal quality from its solar anchor and re-attached it to a fixed clock digit. This severance was made possible by the mechanical clock and completed by electric light. Jonathan Edwards, rising at 4am in winter Connecticut in 1728, was waking into genuine darkness, the hearth cold, tallow candle in hand, roughly two hours before civil twilight would arrive — and the phenomenon and the hour were aligned. A Manhattan product manager rising at 4am in January 2026, in a climate-controlled apartment under LED lights, is waking three hours before civil twilight while the phenomenology of dawn has been entirely outsourced to the digit on her phone. The clock hour is identical. The experience is not. The traditions built their systems on the experience. The modern version keeps only the digit, and mistakes the digit for the thing.

## 4. The paradox and its resolution

This is where the 4am Club and the depressive meet. Bowles et al. (2022) is the empirical anchor: under forced desynchrony, the cortisol awakening response has a robust circadian rhythm whose acrophase, converted from the circadian-phase estimate via participant DLMO, falls at 3:40am in Protocol 1 and 3:45am in Protocol 2, with no detectable CAR during the afternoon [1]. The caveats are real and should be stated: the total sample is 35 adults split across two protocols with mean ages of 54 and 41, the peak amplitude is modest (0.28 and 0.12 μg/dL), only 60–70% of participants showed significant circadian rhythmicity, no confidence interval on the clock-time peak is reported, and the cosinor shape implies a smooth sinusoidal elevation rather than a sharp spike. But the direction of the finding is clear and reproducible across the two protocols. The mechanistic interpretation is also clear: circadian phase modulates the *magnitude* of the cortisol surge upon waking, not the clock time of that surge. If you happen to wake at 4am, the cortisol response in the 30–60 minutes following is at its daily ceiling. If you wake at 4pm, there is effectively no CAR at all.

This is what the contemplative traditions were exploiting, and what the 4am Club is still exploiting, whether or not they know it. Peak glucocorticoid arousal, maximal social isolation, prefrontal systems operating at their circadian nadir so the ordinary narrative scaffolding that organises experience is thinner than at any other hour. The monastic tradition described this state in positive terms — "watching for the dawn," the stripping of daytime distraction, the mind made available to God — and the literature of depression describes it in negative terms. Fitzgerald's *Crack-Up* (1936) contains the canonical phenomenological description: "at three o'clock in the morning, a forgotten package has the same tragic importance as a death sentence." St. John of the Cross's *Dark Night of the Soul* (c. 1578–84) was composed in a windowless cell during an eight-month imprisonment and describes the systematic collapse of the cognitive scaffolding by which identity holds itself together. The Perlis/Tubbs "Mind After Midnight" hypothesis, formalised in 2022, names the underlying neurobiology: nocturnal wakefulness produces prefrontal hypofrontality, attentional bias toward negative stimuli, blunted reward processing, and reduced executive function [29]. The mechanism is not the tiredness; it is the circadian mismatch. The monastic vigil-keeper and the depressed terminal insomniac are running on identical hardware in the same hour. What differs is the substrate the hormonal state is acting upon, and the narrative frame available to interpret the resulting arousal.

The empirical picture is more nuanced than the simple "the monk and the depressive meet at 4am" framing, and I want to flag the nuance honestly because an earlier draft of the investigation got it wrong. Tubbs et al. (2020) found a population-wakefulness-adjusted incidence risk ratio of approximately 3.18 for completed suicide at night across the NVDRS database of 35,338 suicides [30]; the 2024 *JCP* extension pushed this to a peak aIRR of 5.20 at 3am on 78,647 suicides [31]. The 2021 NHANES ideation paper found a more modest OR of 1.16 per hour of nocturnal wakefulness for suicidal ideation — and critically, this association *disappeared* after adjustment for depression severity, while the protective effect of morning wakefulness (OR 0.77 per hour, 5–11am) survived all adjustment [32]. A 2022 Arevalo/Tubbs conference abstract reported that documented depression was associated with *13% lower* nocturnal suicide risk in the binary 11pm–5am night vs. day analysis [33] — a finding that did not survive as a significant subgroup interaction in the more rigorous hourly analysis of the 2024 full paper. Preti and Miotto's 2001 Italian data and Boo et al.'s 2019 Japanese analysis of 873,268 suicides across 41 years both document a different pattern in unadjusted counts: older adults and middle-aged males complete suicide preferentially in the late morning (8–11am), while younger males show a nocturnal peak [34,35]. The Tubbs et al. 2022 emergency department analysis found that, after adjusting for overall ED volume, suicidal ideation presentations peak at 10am, and the authors themselves speculate this reflects "a delay between when an individual develops suicidal ideation and when they seek or receive treatment" [36].

No single study proves the following, but the convergent inference is that the nocturnal peak in completed suicide is dominated by a young, male, alcohol-intoxicated, impulsive profile that matches the Mind After Midnight mechanism — prefrontal hypofrontality plus intoxication plus opportunity — while the classical melancholic with terminal insomnia lies awake at 4am ruminating and then acts hours later, once daylight has arrived and the executive systems come back online enough to execute. The 4am hour is shared by the monk, the guru, the impulsive nocturnal waker, and the melancholic. The action timing splits by substrate. I want to be explicit: this typology is an inference from circumstantial evidence and not a directly tested within-person sequence. The within-person experiment — "ruminates at 4am, forms plan, acts at 9am" — has never been done, probably cannot be done ethically, and may not exist as a clean pathway at all. But it is the reading that makes the convergent data make sense, and I think it is the right reading.

What it implies for someone considering voluntary 4am waking is not that they will become suicidal. The overwhelming majority of people awake at 4am are not at meaningful risk of anything catastrophic, and the absolute risk elevations, while real, are small. What it implies is something subtler: the neurobiological state of 4am is not neutral. It is the hour of maximum physiological arousal combined with minimum cognitive regulation, and what that hour *does* to a person depends almost entirely on what they bring to it. The contemplative traditions understood this intuitively and built elaborate scaffolding — liturgy, posture, collective practice, inherited interpretive frames — to shape what the aroused, disinhibited 4am mind would become. The productivity guru who wakes at 4am to check Slack and write a to-do list is also shaping the state, just much more poorly. The aroused disinhibited mind at 4am will amplify whatever content is available. If the available content is Slack and your own anxieties, it will amplify those. If the available content is the Psalms, or *dhikr*, or silent sitting, it will amplify those instead.

## 5. So, is it a good idea?

The question asks what is "good," which is unanswerable without specifying *good for what*. Let me answer the question for each of several substrates, and let you decide which one you are.

**For the typical MSc student in their mid-twenties, not a natural morning chronotype, going to bed around 11pm or midnight, attempting to wake at 4am on willpower alone**: no, it is a bad idea, and not for interesting reasons. You will get 4–5 hours of sleep, cut your peak REM, run a daily sleep restriction, and pay the depression-and-metabolic tax of chronic circadian misalignment that Roenneberg, Knutson, and Burns have measured. You will also not get the thing the 4am lineage is actually selling, because the thing the 4am lineage is actually selling requires a quality of attention that sleep deprivation specifically destroys. This is the saddest version: you suffer without buying what you thought you were buying. If this is your situation, the more honest move is to sleep later, wake around 7 or 8, and treat the 4am fantasy as what it is — a moral display you have been offered in exchange for real physical harm.

**For the rare person willing to turn their life into a daily chronobiology maintenance protocol** — strict 8pm bedtime, no social life after 7pm, melatonin at 6, blackout blinds at sunset, bright light box at wake, the whole apparatus — the biology becomes agnostic. Your circadian system, over several weeks, will shift a couple of hours earlier, your REM architecture will partially re-form around the new schedule, and the Burns 2024 finding suggests you will not pay the psychiatric cost of misalignment because by your new standard you are not misaligned. But at that point you are running a clinical protocol to achieve a benefit whose value is entirely moral and phenomenological. You will have rearranged your life to purchase access to a particular experiential state. The question you should ask yourself is whether you actually want that state, or whether you want to be the kind of person who has that state. These are different questions and the second one is not worth paying for.

**For the person who already wakes naturally before dawn** — the morning chronotype, the older adult (after age 50 the Fischer curve has you roughly 1.5 hours earlier than at age 20), the new parent whose sleep has been involuntarily reorganised — the picture is different. You are not fighting your biology. The entrainment is already there. The question is only what you do with the hour. Here the investigation's most interesting finding becomes directly relevant: the 4am state is a neurochemical amplifier, not a neutral time, and the contemplative traditions have a thousand years of refined practice aimed at using that amplifier well. If you find yourself awake at 4am regularly, the honest recommendation is not "check your email" but something closer to what the Benedictines, the Sufis, and the Ayurvedic physicians recommended: silence, a single focused practice, and no screens. The monks were not wrong about what the hour can do; they were just the last people to know what they were doing.

**For everyone**, the strongest finding of this investigation is also the most deflationary: 4am is not ancient, is not chronobiologically privileged for most people, and is not a shortcut to anything except mild chronic circadian harm. The appeal is moral and aesthetic, and the moral and aesthetic thing on offer is real but is not a clock hour. It is the structural claim — *this time is mine before the world makes its claims* — that every tradition from Benedict to Baxter to Jocko Willink has built a version of. You can honour that claim by waking at 6am in darkness in December, or by sitting silently for twenty minutes before opening your phone at 8, or by writing before breakfast instead of after, or by guarding any unclaimed stretch of your day with the seriousness the monastics brought to Vigils. The 4am ritual is a counterfeit version of this, produced by a culture that has lost the phenomenology of dawn and replaced it with a competitively escalating digit. You do not need to pay the rent on an hour that no one in the modern world actually enters. You can pay the rent on the claim itself, which is cheaper and more durable and does not cost your REM sleep.

The honest answer to your question, then, is: going to bed early and waking at 4am is almost certainly not a good idea, biologically. But the intuition that drew you to ask the question — that there is something to be gained by claiming a time that belongs to no one else — is correct, is old, and is worth taking seriously in some form other than the one being sold to you. Go to bed when you are tired. Wake when your body allows. Guard one hour of the morning as yours, whatever hour that turns out to be. The monks did not care about 4am. They cared about watching for the dawn, and the dawn comes whenever it comes.

## Open questions

1. **Does the isocaloric phase-advance experiment that would definitively settle the REM-tax question exist?** Our investigation concluded it does not, for the specific comparison of stably-entrained 8pm–4am vs. 11pm–7am sleepers under PSG. A better search — or a targeted email to the Dijk, Czeisler, or Mongrain labs — might surface unpublished data. Running the experiment directly would be straightforward and surprisingly cheap.

2. **Is the "impulsive nocturnal vs. ruminative morning" completed-suicide typology empirically decomposable?** The circumstantial evidence is convergent but the within-person sequence has never been tracked. Whether the Ballard/NIMH inpatient cohort's PSG data could be re-analysed for timing-of-ideation-to-action lag is a concrete follow-up.

3. **Has any post-Tridentine Catholic monastic horologium specified a fixed clock hour for Matins, as opposed to a seasonal calculation?** Our historical investigation did not find one, but the primary archives of 18th–19th century Cistercian and Carthusian houses were not searched. This matters because it would be the single counterexample to the claim that fixed-clock 4am is a strictly post-Reformation Protestant invention.

4. **What is the actual distribution of chronotype among self-identified 4am Club members?** No study has surveyed this population. The entire productivity discourse proceeds as if anyone can become a 4am riser; the Landvreugd retirement data and the heritability literature suggest the population that sustains the habit long-term is heavily pre-selected for morningness. If that is true — and it probably is — then the 4am Club is advice that only "works" for people who did not need it.

5. **Would a well-designed contemplative practice at an entrained early-riser's natural wake time replicate the subjective benefits the 4am Club claims?** The structural argument of this report is yes, and the practical test would be a comparison of twelve weeks of 6am structured contemplative practice against twelve weeks of 4am "morning routine." This is a tractable intervention study and, to my knowledge, has not been run.
