# The Architecture of a Bad Argument: Steelmanning and Dismantling Patriarchal Ideology

Patriarchal ideology — the claim that men should govern women, that hierarchical gender arrangements reflect nature or divine will — turns out to have more philosophical architecture than one might expect, but far less structural integrity. This study set out to find the strongest intellectual defenses of patriarchal social order, reconstruct their logic charitably, and then evaluate whether they hold up under scrutiny. The answer, after tracing arguments from Aristotle through Catholic natural law to contemporary evolutionary psychology, is that patriarchal ideology exhibits a consistent and revealing failure: every serious version either proves too little (establishing difference without entailing hierarchy) or rests on empirical claims now known to be false. The most sophisticated traditions — Thomistic complementarity, neo-Aristotelian virtue ethics, even careful evolutionary psychology — do not actually support the conclusions that patriarchal ideology draws from them. In several cases, following the logic carefully leads to anti-patriarchal conclusions.

The single most surprising finding is structural. Across every tradition examined — ancient teleology, divine-right theory, natural law, evolutionary biology — there is a gap that none successfully bridges: the move from "men and women are different" to "men should therefore rule." This inferential bridge is the load-bearing element of patriarchal ideology, and it is almost never explicitly defended. It is assumed, smuggled in, or papered over with rhetoric. When you look for the actual argument connecting natural difference to hierarchical authority, you find either a premise known to be false (Aristotle's claim that women have constitutively defective reason), a premise that requires specific religious commitment (Filmer's biblical genealogy from Adam), or no premise at all — just the assertion that what is natural is good. This last move, the naturalistic fallacy, is the backbone of the most common contemporary patriarchal arguments, and it collapses under the weight of a single question: if "natural" means "good," then why do we build hospitals?

## The Philosophical Traditions: Real Architecture, Rotten Foundations

### Aristotle's Teleological Hierarchy

The oldest and most philosophically developed argument for patriarchal order comes from Aristotle's *Politics* (Book I). His argument has genuine logical structure, which is worth understanding precisely because its failure is instructive.

Aristotle's framework: in nature, the soul governs the body; reason governs passion. These are constitutive hierarchies built into the structure of reality. Different beings possess the deliberative faculty (*to bouleutikon*) to different degrees. Women possess it, but it is *akuron* — "without authority" or "without the capacity to command" (Politics I.13, 1260a12-13). Since political governance requires full deliberative authority, women are fit for household management but not for the *polis*. This is not convention but nature. [1]

The argument fails at three independent points. First, the biological premise is simply false — it rests on Aristotle's embryology, in which women are "misbegotten males" (*Generation of Animals* II.3), a defective product of imperfect generation. No form of modern biology supports the claim that women have constitutively inferior rational capacity. Second, Aristotle's own framework contradicts itself: he grants women sufficient deliberative capacity to manage the household, which involves genuine complexity (economics, child-rearing, inheritance, extended-family relations). If women can deliberate reliably in this domain, the argument for their categorical exclusion from politics is undermined by his own concession. Third, even granting some average difference in deliberative aptitude, the inference from natural capacity to political role assignment requires an additional premise — that roles should be allocated by natural endowment rather than by education, virtue, or consent — which Aristotle never adequately defends. [1]

What makes Aristotle's argument worth understanding is that it is *not* mere prejudice dressed in philosophy. It is a genuine philosophical argument that fails for specific, identifiable reasons. And the pattern of its failure — a false empirical premise doing the heavy lifting, an inferential gap where the normative conclusion should connect to the factual premise — recurs in every subsequent tradition.

### Filmer's Divine-Right Patriarchalism

Robert Filmer's *Patriarcha* (c. 1640, published 1680) is the most systematic pre-modern defense of patriarchal political order. Its argument is triadic: (1) God gave dominion to Adam; Adam's lordship over his children is the seed of all legitimate political authority; (2) the premise of natural equality is self-defeating because "the people" cannot actually exercise power directly; (3) historically, monarchies have been more stable than democracies. The patriarchal chain runs: God → Adam → patrilineal succession → kings as political fathers. [2]

Filmer is more sophisticated than his reputation suggests. Recent scholarship (Tessarolo 2024) shows he had a distinctive theory of fatherhood as *dominium* — a right of ownership, not merely affection. His attack on social contract theory is genuinely sharp: if everyone is naturally equal, how does anyone ever acquire legitimate authority over anyone else? [2]

But Filmer's argument was terminally demolished by Locke in the *First Treatise of Government* (1689), and has never been seriously revived. Locke's refutation targets three fatal weaknesses. First, the biblical grant of dominion to Adam was specifically over animals ("every living thing that moveth"), not over other humans. Second, even if Adam had sovereign authority, there is no mechanism to transmit it to any identifiable modern ruler — the chain of patrilineal succession has no traceable lineage. Third — and this is a proto-feminist move often overlooked — Locke points out that children derive equally from both parents, which means maternal authority has equal claim to paternal authority. Filmer's male-only hierarchy cannot accommodate this symmetry without undermining its own foundation. [3]

The Locke-Filmer exchange is arguably the most decisive philosophical confrontation with patriarchal ideology in Western history, yet it is almost entirely absent from contemporary public debate about gender. This is remarkable. The strongest historical case for patriarchal political order was refuted in 1689, and the refutation has never been answered.

### Catholic Natural Law: The Surprising Inversion

The Thomistic-Catholic tradition is the most philosophically developed contemporary framework that touches patriarchal claims — and it contains a surprise that undermines common assumptions about what "Catholic" patriarchy means.

Aquinas accepted Aristotle's faulty biology and concluded that men have greater rational faculty, making them suited to govern ("in man the discretion of reason predominates," ST I, q. 92). This is straightforwardly subordinationist. But John Paul II's theology of the body (*Mulieris Dignitatem*, 1988) and the Vatican's 2004 letter on the collaboration of men and women explicitly break with this reading. The 2004 letter, signed by Ratzinger, states that male domination of women is a consequence of the Fall — distorted concupiscence, the sign of sin, not the order of creation. "God's decisive words to the woman after the first sin express the kind of relationship which has now been introduced between man and woman... a relationship in which love will frequently be debased into pure self-seeking." [4]

This is a remarkable inversion: the Vatican tradition, properly developed, treats patriarchal domination as a symptom of humanity's fallenness, not as natural law. The complementarity argument — that men and women have genuinely different orientations rooted in their bodily constitution — is retained, but complementarity does not entail hierarchy. You can have genuine difference with equal authority. Many people who cite Catholic tradition in support of patriarchy are citing a tradition that, in its most authoritative contemporary articulation, explicitly rejects their conclusion.

### Confucian Role Ethics: A Genuinely Different Case

Confucianism presents a distinctive case that resists assimilation to the Western pattern. Its framework is not built on claims of natural inferiority but on relational identity: you are constituted by your roles (father-son, husband-wife, ruler-minister). The "Three Obediences" formalized in the Han period codify women's subordination, and yin-yang cosmology assigns feminine and masculine qualities as complementary but functionally asymmetric forces. [5]

But the hierarchy here is positional, not intrinsic to gender. A grandmother commands respect over her son in the same system. The hierarchy shifts with role, age, and context. And contemporary Confucian scholars (Sor-hoon Tan, Chenyang Li) make a credible case that the patriarchal practices codified in Han and Neo-Confucian orthodoxy are historical accretions, not consequences of the core philosophical commitments. Confucian *ren* (humaneness) and the emphasis on moral cultivation apply universally. Unlike Filmer, which is a dead argument defended only by monarchists, Confucian feminism is a live intellectual tradition. [5]

## The Evolutionary Psychology Gambit

The most common sophisticated-seeming contemporary justification for patriarchal norms comes from evolutionary psychology: sex differences in behavior are products of natural selection, gendered social roles reflect evolved human nature, and attempts to eliminate them are utopian social engineering fighting biology.

This argument has a better empirical foundation than the philosophical traditions — some of the underlying findings are real. Meta-analyses confirm that men prefer younger, more attractive mates and women prefer older, higher-status mates across 45 countries (Walter et al. 2020, N=14,399). Physical aggression shows the largest and most consistent behavioral sex difference. Baron-Cohen's empathizing-systematizing framework finds group-level differences in self-report measures at massive sample sizes (N=671,606). These are not fabricated findings. [6, 7, 8]

But the argument from these findings to patriarchal norms fails at three levels.

**The empirical level is weaker than advertised.** Janet Hyde's meta-analysis of 46 meta-analyses found that males and females are similar on most psychological variables; most effect sizes are small or near zero, with large differences found only in motor performance, sexuality, and some forms of aggression. [9] The distributions overlap so heavily that sex is a poor predictor of any individual's traits. The brain, far from dividing neatly into "male" and "female" types, is a mosaic: Joel et al. (2015, PNAS) showed that brains with consistently "all male" or "all female" features are rare. [10] The replication crisis has hit evolutionary psychology meaningfully — the journal *Evolutionary Psychology* has an expected replication rate of 49-72%, with evidence of publication bias inflating effect sizes (Soto & Schimmack 2024). [11] The most normatively loaded claims — menstrual cycle effects on behavior, hormone-driven behavioral cycling — are among the least reliable.

**The inferential level is contested.** Even where sex differences exist, there is a competing explanation: social role theory (Eagly & Wood) argues that biological differences in reproductive roles and physical strength create social roles, which then amplify behavioral differences as a downstream effect. The prediction: as gender equality increases, sex differences should decrease. The evidence is mixed — some differences do decrease, others appear to increase (the "gender equality paradox"). But the paradox itself is contested: the original STEM paper (Stoet & Geary 2018) contained methodological errors identified by Richardson et al. (2020), and even if the pattern is real, "choices made under any social structure reflect the accumulated socialization of that structure, not pure biological expression." [12, 13]

**The logical level is fatal.** The move from "these differences exist" to "patriarchal norms are justified" is the naturalistic fallacy committed at scale. And here is the telling detail: the leading evolutionary psychologists themselves refuse to make this move. Buss and Schmitt (2011) explicitly state that evolutionary psychology has "no political agenda" and that evolved differences do not entail normative conclusions. [14] The is/ought bridge is not defended by the scientists — it is assumed by the popularizers. Jordan Peterson's lobster argument, the most famous popular version, was characterized by marine biologist Bailey Steinworth as cherry-picking one species from vast biological diversity while ignoring cooperative, hermaphroditic, and non-hierarchical species that are equally "natural." [15] The argument is not science; it is motivated selection from science.

Perhaps the most striking piece of evidence against the biological-inevitability thesis comes from the Mosuo of Yunnan/Sichuan, China, who live in matrilineal, matrilocal households where women control land and property. Mattison et al. (2020, PNAS) found that gender disparities in inflammation and hypertension — health outcomes often attributed to "female biology" — are *reversed* in Mosuo society compared to neighboring patrilineal Han communities. What looks like a biological sex difference turns out to be, in significant part, a product of social structure. [16]

## The Dismantling: Where the Arguments Actually Die

The feminist philosophical tradition has developed layered rebuttals to each pillar of the patriarchal case. Some are knockouts; others are genuine ongoing tensions.

**The theological argument is dead.** Locke killed it in 1689. No one has answered his three objections (the dominion grant was over animals, the succession chain is untraceable, maternal authority has equal claim). The only surviving versions are purely confessional — they depend on accepting a specific interpretation of a specific religious text, which is not a philosophical argument accessible to those outside the tradition. Even within Christianity, the same texts yield opposite conclusions (compare Reformed complementarians reading male headship as creation order with JP2 reading male domination as a consequence of the Fall). [3, 4]

**The natural hierarchy argument is mortally wounded.** Beauvoir's *The Second Sex* (1949) delivered the existentialist critique: woman has been cast as the absolute Other, associated with immanence (biology, body, reproduction) while man claims transcendence (freedom, culture, project). But this assignment is historically imposed, not naturally given. The traits used to justify subordination are products of subordination itself — Wollstonecraft made this point in 1792, and it has never been adequately answered: women's apparent irrationality is caused by deliberately stunted education, not by nature. [17, 18]

**The social contract tradition conceals rather than solves the problem.** Carole Pateman's *The Sexual Contract* (1988) demonstrated that classic contract theory (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau) narrates a story in which free individuals create civil society — but this story conceals a prior "sexual contract" in which men divided women among themselves as the foundation of civil peace. The marriage contract, employment contract, and prostitution contract are all expressions of this underlying structure. Pateman's twist: the problem is not that women were excluded from the social contract, but that the very form of contract theory presupposes their subordination. Simply extending contractual rights to women does not solve the problem if the framework itself embeds it. [19] This critique, notably, cuts hardest against mainstream liberalism, not against explicit patriarchalism.

**The biological argument faces a sophisticated methodological demolition.** Jordan-Young spent thirteen years analyzing 400+ studies on prenatal hormone exposure and found no cross-model consistency — the studies use incompatible measures of "hormone exposure" that don't correlate with each other, and results suspiciously align with researchers' assumptions. [20] Cordelia Fine's *Testosterone Rex* showed that testosterone does not function as a simple male-dominance switch; its behavioral effects are context-dependent, reversible, and far less deterministic than popular accounts suggest. [21] Critically, both Jordan-Young and Fine explicitly accept that biology matters — "fetal hormones must matter to the brain, somehow" (Jordan-Young). The critique is scientific, not ideological. They are not claiming sex is socially constructed; they are claiming the science purporting to trace specific behavioral consequences of sex biology is methodologically inadequate.

The current philosophical frontier is Sarah Richardson's "sex contextualism" (2022): there is no single biological variable called "sex" that operates uniformly across all contexts. Which aspects of biological sex are relevant depends on the research question. The "sex" that matters for reproduction is not the same variable as the one that would matter for cognitive performance or political authority. [22] This is not social constructionism — it is a more precise empiricism.

## The Neo-Aristotelian Twist

The most interesting philosophical thread in this investigation is one that initially looked like a threat to the anti-patriarchal argument but turned out to reinforce it.

The naturalistic fallacy — "you can't derive ought from is" — is the central weapon against evo-psych patriarchalism. But the neo-Aristotelian tradition in ethics (Philippa Foot, Alasdair MacIntyre, Rosalind Hursthouse) explicitly claims to bridge the is/ought gap. For Foot, evaluative claims about any organism — good wolf, defective bee — depend on the organism's "life form," what a member of that species characteristically needs to function well. For humans, the crucial move is that our life form essentially includes rationality and sociality. A human who cannot deliberate or cooperate is defective not by cultural convention but by the standard of what humans characteristically are. [23]

Could this framework rescue the patriarchal argument? Could someone argue that male and female humans have different "life forms" grounded in different reproductive biology, generating different normative standards?

No — and the reason is internal to the framework, not imposed from outside. Foot's entire point is that human normativity *transcends* the merely biological baseline. She explicitly says childlessness is not a defect in a human being the way it would be in a rabbit, because rationality transforms what counts as good for us. The framework generates *one* normative standard for humans anchored in capacities — reason, language, social cooperation — that both sexes share. To derive sex-differentiated standards, you would need to treat biological sex as generating separate life forms, which contradicts the framework's key move: that what makes humans distinctive is precisely what we share. [23]

MacIntyre's *Dependent Rational Animals* (1999) makes this even clearer. His argument from universal human vulnerability — our shared fragility as infants, in illness, in old age — derives virtues of acknowledged dependence: justice in networks of care, generosity, practical wisdom oriented toward the goods of others. The logic implies that social arrangements imposing care labor on one sex while denying that sex political recognition are *failures* of these virtues, not natural expressions of them. [24]

And Martha Nussbaum, the most eminent feminist neo-Aristotelian, uses the same framework to argue that patriarchal arrangements systematically deny women the capabilities — practical reason, bodily integrity, political participation — constitutive of human flourishing. Her capabilities approach is the most philosophically developed application of this tradition to gender, and it is explicitly anti-patriarchal. [25]

The result: even the most sophisticated philosophical tradition that claims to bridge the is/ought gap does not yield patriarchal conclusions when its logic is traced carefully. The crude biological appropriation of neo-Aristotelianism depends on a mistake that Foot herself warns against — reading human normativity off the biological baseline rather than off the characteristically human life form. The tradition's strongest practitioners demonstrate that natural teleology, properly understood, generates obligations to protect rational agency in *all* humans, which patriarchal arrangements systematically violate.

## Verdict: Sophisticated Motivated Reasoning

Your intuition was half right. Patriarchal ideology does have more theoretical scaffolding than you might expect — Aristotle's argument has genuine logical structure, Filmer's political theory is sharper than its reputation, and the Thomistic tradition is philosophically serious. It is not all crude assertion.

But your intuition about depth was essentially correct. Across every tradition examined, the same structural weakness recurs: the inferential bridge from "men and women differ" to "men should therefore govern" is never successfully defended. It is either supported by empirical claims now known to be false (Aristotle's biology), dependent on premises accessible only within a specific faith tradition (Filmer's biblical genealogy, Reformed complementarianism), or simply assumed without argument (the naturalistic fallacy in evo-psych popularization). The most intellectually honest contemporary traditions — JP2's complementarity theology, careful evolutionary psychology (Buss & Schmitt), neo-Aristotelian virtue ethics — have all quietly abandoned the hierarchical conclusion while retaining claims about difference. The hierarchy is the part that doesn't survive scrutiny.

What patriarchal ideology turns out to be, at its strongest, is an argument for sexual difference that has been conscripted into service as an argument for sexual hierarchy. The difference between these two claims is enormous, and the slippage between them is where the motivated reasoning lives. Every intellectually serious defense of "patriarchy" examined in this study actually defends something much weaker: that men and women are not identical in their biological constitution and behavioral tendencies. This is an empirical claim, partially supported, and largely uncontested by serious feminists (Jordan-Young, Fine, Nussbaum all accept it explicitly). What is contested — and what fails — is the normative leap: that difference justifies hierarchy, that biology entails governance, that what is natural is good.

The final irony: the strongest philosophical framework for grounding ethics in nature — Foot's neo-Aristotelian naturalism — turns out to be a weapon against patriarchy, not for it. If what makes humans distinctive is our shared capacity for reason, language, and social cooperation, then any social arrangement that systematically denies these capacities to half the species is not a natural order but a natural defect.

## Open Questions

1. **The gender equality paradox needs resolution.** If sex differences in occupational choice genuinely increase in more egalitarian societies, what is the mechanism? The original finding has methodological problems, but later replications suggest something real may be there. This is the empirical question where the patriarchal and feminist accounts make different predictions — tracing it to resolution would be genuinely illuminating.

2. **Why is Locke's First Treatise invisible in feminist discourse?** The most decisive anti-patriarchal philosophical document in the Western canon is almost never discussed in feminist theory or public debate. Is this because feminist philosophy has moved beyond the Filmer-style argument, or because the liberal framework Locke inaugurated is itself suspect (per Pateman)?

3. **The adaptive preferences problem.** Nussbaum raises but does not fully resolve the question of when a preference should be discounted as "distorted" by oppressive conditions without collapsing into paternalism. Women who endorse traditional gender roles may be expressing adaptive preferences shaped by patriarchal socialization — but the philosophical framework for distinguishing adaptive from authentic preferences remains underdeveloped. This has practical consequences for policy.

4. **Confucian feminism as an alternative trajectory.** The Confucian tradition's approach to gender — relational, positional, not grounded in claims of natural inferiority — may offer resources for thinking about gender that Western frameworks miss. A deeper investigation of whether Confucian role ethics can be separated from its patriarchal historical accretions would be valuable, especially given the live scholarly tradition working on this question.

5. **The social coordination argument.** There exists a version of the case for gendered social roles that appeals not to natural capacity or divine will but to coordination efficiency — that asymmetric gender roles in the family function as coordination devices reducing transaction costs. This argument is almost never made rigorously and deserves serious examination, because it is the hardest version to defeat with the naturalistic fallacy objection (it is explicitly consequentialist, not naturalist).
