# Research Log — Patriarchal Ideology: Steelmanning the Strongest Arguments

## Scoping — 2026-04-02

**User context**: Finds patriarchal ideology hard to understand, suspects it has
no real theoretical depth, emotionally disagrees with it. Wants a critical study
narrow enough to gain preliminary understanding.

**Scoping rationale**: The most useful thing is NOT a broad survey of patriarchy
as a sociological concept (the user would find that boring and already agrees with
feminist critiques). Instead: find the *strongest* intellectual articulations of
patriarchal ideology — the steelman — and critically evaluate them. This serves
the user's actual need: understanding *why* smart people have believed this, and
whether their arguments have substance or are sophisticated motivated reasoning.

### Narrowed Questions

1. **What are the strongest philosophical arguments for patriarchal social order,
   and where do they break?** — Trace the lineage from Aristotle's "natural
   hierarchy" through Filmer's divine-right patriarchalism, through contemporary
   conservative arguments (e.g., natural law theory, certain readings of
   Confucian role ethics). Identify which arguments have real logical structure
   vs. which smuggle in their conclusions.

2. **Does evolutionary psychology provide a credible scientific basis for
   patriarchal norms, or does it commit the naturalistic fallacy at scale?** —
   The evo-psych argument (sex differences in behavior → gendered social roles
   are "natural") is probably the most common sophisticated-seeming justification
   today. Evaluate the actual evidence quality and the is/ought gap.

3. **How do the most intellectually serious feminist critics dismantle these
   arguments?** — Not general feminist theory, but the specific counter-arguments
   to the steelmanned positions above. Where are the knockouts vs. genuine
   ongoing tensions?

These three questions form a dialectic: thesis (strongest pro-patriarchy
arguments), antithesis (strongest rebuttals), and critical evaluation of where
the debate actually stands. This should give the user a compact but rigorous
understanding of the intellectual terrain.

## Survey Phase — 2026-04-02

Dispatched three survey agents in parallel:

1. **Philosophical arguments** (survey-philosophy): Covered Aristotle, Filmer,
   Thomistic-Catholic complementarity, Arnhart's Darwinian natural right,
   Confucian role ethics, Reformed complementarianism. Key finding: the Vatican
   tradition (JP2) explicitly rejects male domination as a consequence of the Fall,
   not creation order — a major inversion. The "inferential bridge" from natural
   difference to hierarchical authority is never successfully defended in any tradition.

2. **Evo-psych basis** (survey-evopsych): Covered Hyde meta-analyses, Walter et al.
   mate preferences, Baron-Cohen E-S theory, Joel brain mosaic, gender equality
   paradox, replication crisis data. Key finding: Buss & Schmitt themselves explicitly
   disclaim normative implications; the is/ought move is made by popularizers, not
   scientists. Mosuo matriliny data shows health outcomes attributed to "female
   biology" reverse under matrilineal social structure.

3. **Feminist rebuttals** (survey-feminist): Covered Wollstonecraft, Locke's First
   Treatise, Beauvoir, Pateman, Harding, Longino, Jordan-Young, Fine, Haslanger,
   Butler, Nussbaum, Richardson's sex contextualism. Key finding: Locke's First
   Treatise is the most decisive anti-patriarchal document in Western philosophy
   yet almost entirely absent from feminist discourse.

## Review — Round 1

Reviewer assessed coverage as strong across all three questions. Identified one

## Deep Dive — Neo-Aristotelian Naturalism

Deep-dive agent traced Foot's *Natural Goodness*, MacIntyre's *Dependent Rational
Animals*, and feminist engagements (Nussbaum, Hursthouse, Stohr). Conclusion:
the neo-Aristotelian framework does NOT rescue patriarchal conclusions — it
actually undermines them. The framework grounds normativity in the shared human
life form (rationality, sociality), which both sexes possess equally. MacIntyre's
vulnerability argument implies patriarchal care-labor arrangements are structural
failures, not natural order. Nussbaum uses the same framework explicitly against
patriarchy.
