# Survey: How do the most intellectually serious feminist critics dismantle the strongest arguments for patriarchal social order?

## Summary

The feminist philosophical tradition has produced systematic and, in many cases, decisive rebuttals to the major pillars of patriarchal argument. The knockout arguments are strongest against (1) the theological-genealogical argument (Filmer's Patriarcha demolished by Locke), (2) the natural hierarchy argument (Beauvoir's undermining of Aristotelian teleology via existentialist analysis of "othering"), and (3) the social contract concealment argument (Pateman's exposure of the "sexual contract" hidden within liberalism). The weakest ground for feminist critics is the biological-evolutionary domain, where the debate is genuinely contested: the best feminist philosophy of science work (Jordan-Young, Fine, Fausto-Sterling, Longino) has successfully punctured naive biological determinism but has not — and largely does not claim to — eliminate all biological contribution to sex differences in behavior and cognition. The current philosophical consensus in analytic feminist philosophy (Haslanger, Richardson) has converged on a sophisticated middle position: neither crude constructionism ("sex is just a fiction") nor naive essentialism ("biology is destiny"), but rather context-sensitive analysis of how biological facts and social structures jointly produce observed differences.

The single most surprising finding: Locke's First Treatise — probably the most decisive single document in the history of anti-patriarchal argument — is almost never discussed in public feminist discourse, even though it performed the kill-shot on the strongest pre-modern case for male dominance.

## Key Sources

### 1. Mary Wollstonecraft, *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* (1792)
- **Link**: Primary text; Britannica summary at https://www.britannica.com/topic/A-Vindication-of-the-Rights-of-Woman
- **Key content**: Wollstonecraft's central argument is structurally elegant: she accepts the Enlightenment premise that rational beings deserve rights, then argues that (a) rationality is universal to the human species, not sex-specific, and (b) the apparent irrationality or inferior judgment of women is caused by a deliberately stunted education, not by nature. The logical move is to turn the patriarch's own premises against him. Rousseau had argued in *Emile* that Sophie (woman) should be educated to please men — to be charming rather than rational. Wollstonecraft attacks this as self-defeating even on Rousseau's own terms: a woman conditioned to be a "toy" or "rattle" makes a bad mother, bad citizen, and ultimately a bad companion. She calls marriage under these conditions "legal prostitution" — women are forced into it because their inferior education disqualifies them for economic independence. The argument is simultaneously a critique of patriarchy's internal inconsistency (it degrades the very beings men claim to cherish) and a positive case for reform. The claim that women's apparent inferiority is produced rather than natural is the foundational feminist move — the line from which de Beauvoir's more systematic argument descends.
- **Assessment**: Highly credible primary source. A political polemic, not a technical philosophy treatise — the argument is more rhetorical than rigorous in places. But the core logical move (apparent inferiority = product of oppression, not cause for it) is sound and remains the engine of subsequent liberal feminist argument. Wollstonecraft does not seriously challenge the framework of rationality as the basis for rights — she stays inside Enlightenment liberalism. This is both a strength (makes the argument internally consistent) and a limitation (it concedes that non-rational beings might have fewer rights, a problem she does not address).

### 2. John Locke, *First Treatise of Government* (1689)
- **Link**: Wikipedia summary at https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two_Treatises_of_Government; scholarly analysis by Melissa A. Butler, "Early Liberal Roots of Feminism: John Locke and the Attack on Patriarchy," *American Political Science Review* 72(1), 1978, DOI: https://doi.org/10.2307/1953604
- **Key content**: Filmer's *Patriarcha* (published posthumously 1680) is the most systematic defense of patriarchal order in the Western tradition. Its argument: (1) God gave Adam dominion over all things, including Eve; (2) political authority is a continuation of this original paternal/patriarchal authority; (3) therefore, kings rule as fathers, and subjects owe obedience as children. Locke's First Treatise dismantles this line by line. The argument structure: First, Locke challenges the textual claim — the Bible's grant to Adam was specifically over animals ("every living thing that moveth"), not over other human beings including Eve. The grant of dominion does not straightforwardly yield paternal political authority. Second, Locke attacks the transmission argument — even if Adam had sovereign authority, it cannot be transmitted to any particular monarch because we have no way to identify who among all of Adam's heirs should receive it. Third, Locke points out that Filmer's argument requires the mother to also have authority over children (since children derive equally from both parents), which Filmer cannot accommodate without undermining his male-only hierarchy. This is actually a proto-feminist move: the equality of maternal and paternal generation implies equality of parental authority, which contradicts the patriarchal premise. Butler's 1978 article argues that the entire liberal tradition that grew from Locke implicitly but incompletely challenged patriarchy: Locke himself was inconsistent, departing from traditional patriarchalism in his views on marriage and education but failing to extend full political personhood to women.
- **Assessment**: Locke is a peer-reviewed, canonically authoritative source. Butler's article is a highly-cited piece in the *APSR* (50+ citations). The Filmer-Locke exchange is genuinely the most decisive philosophical confrontation with theological patriarchalism. What makes Locke's refutation so powerful is that it attacks the strongest version of the argument — not some strawman, but the best available defense of male-dominated hierarchical order. The point about maternal authority is particularly decisive: it shows that Filmer's argument, if accepted, proves too much (or too little), because biological parenthood is symmetrical.

### 3. Simone de Beauvoir, *The Second Sex* (1949)
- **Link**: Primary text. Scholarly analysis: "Woman as Other: The Master-Slave Dialectic in Beauvoir's The Second Sex" (Exordium, 2021) at https://exordiumuq.org/2021/12/15/woman-as-other-the-master-slave-dialectic-in-beauvoirs-the-second-sex/; also Kirkpatrick, "Femininity, love, and alienation: the genius of The Second Sex," *Journal of the British Academy*, 2024.
- **Key content**: De Beauvoir's "one is not born, but becomes, a woman" is the slogan, but the argument is more sophisticated than the slogan suggests. The core philosophical move: Beauvoir adapts Hegel's master-slave dialectic to show that woman has been cast as the absolute Other — not as a slave who can achieve transcendence through labor, but as an object who is denied the Hegelian possibility of self-constituting struggle entirely. The patriarchal hierarchy depends on a metaphysical claim: woman is defined by her immanence (biology, body, reproduction, contingency) while man is defined by his transcendence (project, freedom, culture). Beauvoir's critique operates on two levels. First, she argues that the association of woman with immanence and man with transcendence is not a biological necessity but a historically imposed assignment. Women are not inherently immanent — they are treated as such, and this treatment becomes self-fulfilling. Second, she challenges the underlying Aristotelian assumption that certain beings are "naturally" suited to subordinate roles. The existentialist premise is radical: there is no fixed human nature, including no fixed female nature. "Femininity" is a product of situation, not essence. The argument against Aristotle is implicit but structurally decisive: Aristotle's natural hierarchy required that slaves and women had different (inferior) rational capacities by nature. Beauvoir, by demonstrating the social-historical production of the traits used to justify subordination, pulls the empirical rug from under the naturalistic claim.
- **Assessment**: Highly authoritative — *The Second Sex* is one of the central texts of 20th-century philosophy, not just feminist theory. The analysis is philosophically rigorous (grounded in Hegel, Husserl, Sartre). A genuine tension exists: the axiological reading (Kirkpatrick 2024) argues the famous sentence is really about alienated femininity under conditions of hierarchy, not a pure social-construction thesis. This is actually a strength — it means Beauvoir's argument is not committed to the strong claim that sex is entirely socially constructed, only that femininity as a hierarchically subordinating identity is. The limitation is that Beauvoir's existentialism sometimes seems to treat women's failure to assert transcendence as partly their own responsibility — a tension that later feminist critics (Butler, Chanter) have noted.

### 4. Carole Pateman, *The Sexual Contract* (1988)
- **Link**: Wikipedia overview at https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Sexual_Contract; conversation piece: Thompson et al., "The Sexual Contract 30 Years On," *Feminist Legal Studies* 26(1), 2018, DOI: 10.1007/s10691-018-9368-1
- **Key content**: Pateman's argument is one of the most sophisticated and destructive critiques of liberal political theory available. The core claim: the classic social contract theorists (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau) narrate a story in which free individuals consent to political authority and thereby create civil society. What this story conceals is a "prior" sexual contract — an agreement among men that established their right of sexual access to women as foundational to civil society itself. The civil contract creates fraternal patriarchy: men agree to end their war against each other partly by dividing women among themselves. The marriage contract, the prostitution contract, the employment contract, and the surrogacy contract are all analyzed as expressions of this underlying structure. Pateman's key logical move: "The original contract constitutes men's freedom and women's subjection." The problem is not that women were excluded from the social contract (as liberal feminists argue) — it is that the very form of contract theory presupposes women's subordination. Women can "enter" these contracts, but they do so "not as equal individuals but as natural subordinates." Therefore, the liberal feminist strategy of simply extending contractual rights to women cannot solve the problem; it accepts the framework that embeds the problem. Pateman's analysis of the marriage contract is particularly sharp: institutionally, becoming a "husband" gives patriarchal right over the "wife" — the contract transforms a person into property-like status rather than creating a relation between equals.
- **Assessment**: Winner of the 2005 APSA Benjamin E. Lippincott Award (outstanding contribution to political science). Cited across multiple disciplines. Strong and influential, though not without critics. Criticisms cluster around: (a) Pateman's essentialism — her account of "male sex-right" has been accused of treating male dominance as an irreducible fact rather than analyzing its historical variability; (b) she largely ignores how race and class intersect with the sexual contract (addressed subsequently by Charles Mills's "Racial Contract"); (c) she does not adequately address female sexual desire or consensual entry into these contracts. Nancy Fraser's response ("Beyond the Master/Subject Model," 1993) argues that Pateman's framework is too binary to capture the complexity of modern gender relations. These are genuine limitations, but they do not dislodge the core argument about the concealment structure of contract theory.

### 5. Sandra Harding, feminist standpoint epistemology and "strong objectivity"
- **Link**: "Rethinking Standpoint Epistemology: What is 'Strong Objectivity'?" in *Knowledge and Inquiry* (1993); blog summary at https://wordpress.viu.ca/compassrose/feminist-standpoint-epistemology-and-objectivity/
- **Key content**: Harding argues that the dominant ideal of "value-free" science is internally incoherent — not because objectivity is bad, but because conventional objectivity is too weak. The "God-trick" (Donna Haraway's phrase): the pretense that a researcher can occupy a view from nowhere, free from social position. Harding's "strong objectivity" inverts this: genuine objectivity requires examining the researcher as well as the object of research, because social position shapes what questions get asked, what counts as evidence, and what gets reported. Key claim: marginalized groups have an epistemic advantage in spotting the assumptions that dominant groups cannot see — analogous to the Marxist claim that workers understand capital better than capitalists do. This is not a claim that women's science is "better" in some mystical way, but that including diverse standpoints produces less androcentric distortion. Applied to the debate about biological sex differences: a scientific community that is predominantly male and operating with androcentric background assumptions will systematically find what it expects to find. The feminist critique of evo-psych and neuroscience is not that biology doesn't matter — it is that the science being done in these fields has been shaped by assumptions that compromise its evidential value.
- **Assessment**: Harding is a philosopher of science at UCLA, a serious academic. The standpoint epistemology position is well-developed and defended across multiple books. It is not, however, universally accepted — critics argue that the claim of epistemic privilege for marginalized groups risks a kind of reverse essentialism, and that the "strong objectivity" standard is hard to operationalize. The key contribution for our purposes is not the full standpoint theory but the argument that apparent scientific consensus about sex differences cannot be taken at face value without examining how the science was produced. This is a methodological, not a metaphysical, claim — and as such it is defensible on conventional scientific grounds.

### 6. Helen Longino, *Science as Social Knowledge* (1990) and contextual empiricism
- **Link**: Semantic Scholar: Longino in "Science, Objectivity, and Feminist Values," *Feminist Studies* 14 (1988) with Keller, Fausto-Sterling, Harding.
- **Key content**: Longino's contribution is to provide a formal account of how social values enter science without making science arbitrary. Her core argument: scientific hypotheses are always underdetermined by evidence — background assumptions fill the gap between data and theory. These background assumptions are themselves value-laden and socially transmitted. In practice, this means that research programs in sex differences tend to assume that differences observed in one domain (behavior, cognition) have biological roots even when the evidence does not compel this inference. Longino's framework explains how, for example, studies of animal hormones in the 1970s were designed with the assumption that testosterone = male = dominant, and these assumptions shaped which experiments were designed, which results were reported, and how results were interpreted — all before ideological bias in the traditional sense ever enters.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed philosopher of science, now at Stanford. Contextual empiricism is a widely-cited position in philosophy of science that is not specific to feminist theory — it draws on post-Quinean holism. Longino's work is particularly valuable because it explains the mechanism by which social bias enters science without claiming that science is mere ideology. This is more persuasive to scientific audiences than claims about the political nature of knowledge.

### 7. Rebecca Jordan-Young, *Brain Storm: The Flaws in the Science of Sex Differences* (2010)
- **Link**: Harvard University Press; reviewed at https://slate.com/human-interest/2010/10/rebecca-jordan-young-s-masterful-critique-of-the-research-on-the-relationship-between-testosterone-and-sex-difference.html
- **Key content**: Jordan-Young spent 13 years analyzing 400+ studies on prenatal hormone exposure ("brain organization theory") — the claim that fetal testosterone shapes the brain into male or female configurations, determining later behavioral sex differences. Her demolition is methodological, not ideological. Key findings: (1) The studies use inconsistent operationalizations of "hormone exposure" — amniotic fluid, maternal blood, finger-digit ratio, intersex conditions — without these measures correlating with each other. (2) Results are inconsistent across approaches: behaviors supposedly shaped by prenatal testosterone do not show consistent links when measured via different proxies. (3) The studies of CAH (congenital adrenal hyperplasia) girls — the central "natural experiment" — are confounded by chronic illness effects and the medicalization of these girls' bodies and identities. (4) Researchers' findings suspiciously aligned with their assumptions — a classic sign of confirmation bias in research design. Jordan-Young does NOT argue that fetal hormones have no effect on the brain. She explicitly states: "fetal hormones must matter to the brain — somehow." The claim is that the specific behavioral science claiming to have traced this mechanism is methodologically inadequate to support the conclusions drawn.
- **Assessment**: Barnard College sociologist/epidemiologist; expert on research methods. The Slate review (by Amanda Schaffer) characterizes this as "devastatingly smart and definitive." Not a philosopher but a methodologist. The work is strong precisely because it does not rest on ideological objections but on standard scientific methodology critique (internal consistency, replication, operationalization). Limitation: the work is ten years old and the field has continued to develop. Some subsequent meta-analyses (e.g., a 2016 meta-analysis on the Bateman Gradient across species) have supported original sexual-selection theories in evolutionary biology — suggesting the feminist critique may be stronger for neuroscience specifically than for evolutionary biology generally.

### 8. Cordelia Fine, *Delusions of Gender* (2010) and *Testosterone Rex* (2017)
- **Link**: *Testosterone Rex* Wikipedia at https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Testosterone_Rex; critical review at https://quillette.com/2017/03/21/cordelia-fines-testosterone-rex-a-review/
- **Key content**: Fine attacks "neurosexism" — the inflation of small and often unreplicated brain-sex differences into culturally significant claims about innate gender. Her argument in *Testosterone Rex*: (1) The Bateman-Trivers framework (males as naturally promiscuous, females as naturally choosy) has serious methodological problems in the original fruit fly studies. (2) Even where the Bateman Gradient is real across species, its application to humans involves a speculative leap that ignores human behavioral plasticity and cultural variation. (3) Testosterone does not function as a simple male-dominance switch; its behavioral effects are context-dependent, reversible, and far less deterministic than popular accounts suggest. Fine explicitly states she is not claiming the brain is "asexual" or that sex has no biological relevance — she accepts that "genetic and hormonal differences between the sexes can influence brain development and function at every level."
- **Assessment**: Fine is a psychologist at the University of Melbourne; published in peer-reviewed journals and major trade presses. The Quillette review (critical from a right-of-center perspective) fairly identifies what it sees as the book's weakness: Fine sometimes relegates contrary evidence to endnotes and focuses on demolishing extreme positions while being less thorough on more nuanced ones. The criticism has merit. This is a limitation of the work that matters for assessing the full evidential picture. However, the Quillette review itself concedes Fine "does deal effectively" with "overblown claims." The consensus even among Fine's critics is that some neurosexist claims are overblown. Where Fine is on stronger ground: demonstrating that popular accounts of testosterone and sex differences in cognitive/behavioral domains are overstated relative to the evidence.

### 9. Sally Haslanger, "Gender and Social Construction: Who? What? When? Where? How?" (2006)
- **Link**: Paper available at https://sallyhaslanger.weebly.com/uploads/1/8/2/7/18272031/haslanger_2006_scwwww.pdf
- **Key content**: Haslanger provides the most rigorous analytic-philosophy account of what it means for gender to be socially constructed. Her taxonomy: (a) *idea-construction* — the concepts of "man" and "woman" are historical products, not natural kinds; (b) *object-construction* — individuals are constituted as gendered through classification and treatment (not just named as such); (c) *discursive construction* — entities become what they are partly because of how they are categorized. The crucial move: social construction does not mean "merely" fictional or unreal. Women and men are "fully real" social kinds, but their reality is constituted through social relations. This preempts the common objection that social construction means nothing is real or that biology is irrelevant. Haslanger is also explicit about the normative dimension: asking "whose interests are served" by a particular conceptual framework is not ideology — it is legitimate inquiry into the political functions of categorization. Applied directly to patriarchal ideology: the claim that women's "natural" inferiority justifies their subordination is best understood as a case of discursive construction — the framework that produces the evidence for female inferiority is the same framework used to justify female subordination.
- **Assessment**: Haslanger is Ford Professor of Philosophy at MIT, one of the foremost analytic feminist philosophers. The paper is in a widely-cited anthology. This is academically authoritative, peer-reviewed philosophy. Haslanger's framework is particularly valuable for this survey because it navigates between naive social constructionism (which cannot distinguish itself from idealism) and biological determinism. Her careful analysis of different senses of "construction" shows that the feminist claim does not require denying biology — only denying that biology alone determines social roles.

### 10. Judith Butler, *Gender Trouble* (1990) and *Bodies That Matter* (1993)
- **Link**: Overview: https://theconversation.com/judith-butler-their-philosophy-of-gender-explained-192166
- **Key content**: Butler's central concept is gender *performativity* — not performance (a conscious act) but the structural process by which gender is constituted through repeated stylized acts, practices, and institutional norms. The key philosophical move: Butler denies the sex/gender distinction that earlier feminism relied on (sex = biological, gender = social). For Butler, even "biological sex" as we encounter it is already interpreted through prior gender frameworks — we cannot access raw, pre-social bodies. When a doctor announces "it's a girl," this performative utterance begins constituting the child as a gendered being. This is not idealism about bodies; it is a claim about the conditions of intelligibility under which bodies are recognized. Applied to patriarchal argument: Butler's analysis shows that the appeal to "natural" sexual hierarchy depends on taking gender-coded observations about bodies as pre-social facts — precisely the circular move that social construction theory exposes.
- **Assessment**: Butler is Benjamin Opdyke Professor at UC Berkeley, globally influential. The Conversation overview by Szorenyi (2022) is a secondary source but accurate. Butler has been criticized from within feminism (Martha Nussbaum's critique in "The Professor of Parody," 1999, is the sharpest) for obscurantist prose and for an account that seems to leave little room for grounded political resistance. The criticism that Butler's account makes it hard to maintain the category "woman" for political purposes is taken seriously even by sympathetic interpreters. Butler's work is stronger as a critique of essentialist presuppositions than as a positive political theory.

### 11. Martha Nussbaum, capabilities approach (*Women and Human Development*, 2000)
- **Link**: Cambridge University Press (2000); PDF preview available. Also: https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/capability-approach/
- **Key content**: Nussbaum's capabilities approach is less a critique of patriarchal argument than an alternative normative framework. But it functions as a powerful rebuttal by framing the question entirely differently. Instead of asking "what is the natural function of women?" (the Aristotelian question that the patriarchal tradition exploits), Nussbaum asks "what are the central human capabilities that a just society must protect for all citizens?" Her ten central capabilities (life, bodily health, bodily integrity, senses/imagination/thought, emotions, practical reason, affiliation, other species, play, control over environment) are grounded in a threshold conception of human dignity, not in a fixed female nature. The argument against patriarchal natural law: even if we accept that men and women have different biological tendencies or capacities (a concession Nussbaum does not need to make), this cannot justify systematic deprivation of central human capabilities. The right to bodily integrity, to practical reason, to participation in political life — these do not depend on the absence of biological sex differences. Nussbaum is explicit that the capabilities approach can acknowledge biological variation without that variation grounding differential treatment. She also deploys her cross-cultural empirical research (India, Bangladesh) to show that the adaptive preferences of women in patriarchal societies — apparently endorsing their own subordination — are not evidence of free choice but of distorted preference formation under conditions of oppression. This "adaptive preference" argument is philosophically important: it responds to the libertarian-conservative objection that women who accept traditional roles are simply exercising their freedom.
- **Assessment**: Nussbaum is among the most authoritative voices in contemporary political philosophy (Ernst Freund Distinguished Service Professor, University of Chicago). The capabilities approach has been widely adopted in development economics (Amartya Sen) and human rights law. The work is empirically grounded, philosophically rigorous, and cross-culturally sensitive. Potential limitation: the capabilities list has been criticized as implicitly importing Western liberal values, particularly the emphasis on individuation and autonomy. Postcolonial feminist critics (e.g., in the Tapuya piece by Lamoureaux and Rottenburg, 2021) argue that the fixation on "bodily separateness" and autonomous selfhood may not be neutral with respect to non-Western ethical frameworks.

### 12. Sarah Richardson, "Sex Contextualism" (2022) — current philosophical consensus
- **Link**: DOI: 10.3998/ptpbio.2096; *Philosophy, Theory, and Practice in Biology*, GOLD open access
- **Key content**: Richardson's "sex contextualism" paper represents what may be the current best philosophical position on the biology-construction debate. The argument: rather than the binary (biological sex essentialism vs. social construction), sex contextualism proposes that operationalizations of "sex" in scientific research are *context-specific* and *pluralistic*. There is no single biological variable called "sex" that operates uniformly across all biological contexts. Which aspects of biological sex are relevant depends on the research question. This is philosophically important for the patriarchy debate: even granting that biological sex exists and matters biologically, this does not determine which social arrangements are appropriate, because the "sex" that matters for reproduction is not the same variable as the one that would matter for cognitive performance, social role assignment, or political authority.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed in a philosophy of biology journal; 97 citations in 3 years (high for the field). Richardson is at Harvard (history of science and philosophy of biology). This is the current frontier of the feminist philosophy of biology debate — moving beyond both simple constructionism and simple essentialism toward a more nuanced framework. Highly credible.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of knowledge

The feminist philosophical tradition has developed a layered, sophisticated rebuttal to patriarchal arguments across multiple levels of analysis:

**1. The genealogical-theological argument** (Filmer: God gave Adam paternal authority, which grounds all political and domestic hierarchy) was decisively refuted by Locke in 1689 and has not been seriously revived. The only remaining versions are non-philosophical: religious traditionalism of a non-philosophical character.

**2. The Aristotelian natural hierarchy argument** (women have defective rationality and are naturally suited to subordination) is undermined at two levels: (a) the empirical claim about defective rationality is refuted by the social construction argument — apparent differences in rationality are produced by differential education and opportunity, not nature; (b) the normative claim that natural differences justify hierarchy is independently contested by Nussbaum's capabilities framework and by the naturalistic fallacy objection (that even genuine biological differences do not straightforwardly yield normative conclusions about appropriate social roles).

**3. The social contract argument** (liberal political philosophy grounds legitimate authority in consent, which implicitly includes women) is internally complicated by Pateman's analysis. She shows that the major social contract theorists either explicitly excluded women or tacitly depended on their subordination. Pateman's critique is damaging to any argument that says "liberalism already solved the problem of gender hierarchy" — it shows the problem is embedded in the foundations.

**4. The biological-evolutionary argument** (natural selection shaped males toward dominance and competitiveness, females toward nurturing and compliance; this explains and may justify the observed hierarchy) is where the feminist case is most contested and most nuanced. The best feminist philosophers of science (Longino, Harding, Jordan-Young, Fine, Fausto-Sterling, Richardson) have not claimed biology is irrelevant — they have argued that (a) specific empirical claims in evo-psych and neuroscience are methodologically weak; (b) the inference from biological tendency to social normativity involves the naturalistic fallacy; and (c) the science in this area has been systematically shaped by androcentric background assumptions that compromise its evidential status. This is a genuinely strong critique but not a knockdown — it requires ongoing engagement with each specific empirical claim.

### Agreements across feminist sources

- The apparent inferiority of women in various domains is substantially (if not entirely) produced by conditions of subordination, not by nature.
- Biological differences, even where real, do not straightforwardly justify differential social roles.
- The mainstream Western political tradition (Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Kant) is far more deeply committed to gender hierarchy than its self-presentation as universalist suggests.
- Nussbaum's capabilities approach provides the strongest positive alternative to both natural law and liberal contractarian frameworks for thinking about gender justice.
- The sex/gender binary (sex = biology, gender = culture) has been complicated but not simply dissolved — the current consensus leans toward Richardson's sex contextualism: sex is real, plural, and context-specific, not a single fixed fact.

### Conflicts and tensions

- **Butler vs. Nussbaum**: A genuine philosophical dispute. Nussbaum ("The Professor of Parody," 1999) argues that Butler's performativity theory is so focused on critique that it provides no basis for the political action needed to actually improve women's lives. Butler responds that Nussbaum's capabilities approach uncritically imports liberal individualism. Neither has definitively won this debate.
- **Pateman's essentialism problem**: Pateman's framework has been criticized (including by Nancy Fraser) for treating "male sex-right" as a stable, cross-cultural constant rather than a historically variable phenomenon. This is a real tension — the critique of essentialism that feminism deploys against biology can be turned back on feminist arguments that posit fixed male dominance.
- **Strong constructionism vs. biological realism**: Jordan-Young and Fine are more cautious than some of their popular reception — they do not deny biological influence on behavior and cognition. But some feminist theorists (certain post-structuralists, some gender studies scholars) do make stronger claims that sex is "entirely" socially constructed. These stronger claims are philosophically unsupported and scientifically implausible; they are also strategically counterproductive because they are easily refuted.
- **Standpoint epistemology's epistemic privilege claim**: Harding's claim that marginalized groups have epistemic advantage in producing scientific knowledge is philosophically contested. Critics argue this risks a kind of identity-based essentialism about knowledge. The weaker version (diverse standpoints reduce systematic bias) is more defensible.

### Gaps and under-explored areas

1. **The Locke-Filmer debate's relevance to contemporary debates**: The philosophical argument between Filmer and Locke is the most decisive historical confrontation with patriarchal argument and is almost completely absent from contemporary public debate. More work connecting Locke's First Treatise to contemporary biological-evolutionary patriarchalism would be valuable.

2. **The naturalistic fallacy in biological arguments for gender hierarchy**: Most feminist critics note the naturalistic fallacy problem in passing, but a dedicated, philosophically rigorous analysis of how exactly evolutionary arguments for gender hierarchy commit this fallacy (and what versions avoid it) is underrepresented.

3. **Engaging the strongest contemporary biological arguments**: The feminist philosophical literature is strongest against naive evo-psych and pop-neuroscience. The most sophisticated contemporary defenses of biological contributions to sex differences (e.g., David Geary's careful work on evolved psychological differences) receive less engagement.

4. **The adaptive preferences problem in depth**: Nussbaum raises adaptive preferences but does not fully resolve the philosophical problem of when a preference should be discounted as "distorted" without collapsing into paternalism. This remains philosophically underworked.

5. **Intersectionality and the patriarchy argument**: Most of the sources surveyed focus on gender hierarchy in relative isolation from race and class. Pateman's critics (e.g., Charles Mills) note that the sexual contract is intertwined with racial contracts in ways that complicate the picture. The feminist critiques surveyed here are largely by and about white, Western women's experience.

### Surprises

1. **The historical significance of Locke's First Treatise is almost entirely absent from public feminist discourse** — even though it performed the definitive philosophical demolition of the strongest pre-modern patriarchal argument (Filmer). This is remarkable. The First Treatise is a significant feminist document by any reckoning, yet it is rarely discussed in feminist philosophy courses or texts.

2. **The best feminist philosophers of science do NOT claim biology is irrelevant** — Jordan-Young and Fine both explicitly accept that fetal hormones matter to the brain "somehow." The media representation of this debate, both on the feminist side (dismissing all biology) and the anti-feminist side (dismissing all critique of evo-psych), misrepresents the actual philosophical positions.

3. **Pateman's argument that liberal contractarianism conceals rather than solves patriarchy** is more devastating to mainstream progressive politics than to conservative patriarchalism — it is primarily a critique of liberalism, not of explicit patriarchy advocates.

4. **The naturalistic fallacy argument alone is not sufficient**: Even if feminists succeed in showing that biology contributes to sex differences in behavior, the normative conclusion ("therefore women should occupy subordinate roles") does not follow. This is widely noted but often treated as a slam-dunk when in fact sophisticated natural lawyers (following Aristotle, Aquinas, or MacIntyre) do have responses to simple naturalistic fallacy objections — they argue that the content of flourishing is determined by natural ends, not by is/ought reasoning alone. A deeper engagement with neo-Aristotelian natural law on its own terms is needed.
