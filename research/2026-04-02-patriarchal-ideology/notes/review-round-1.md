### Q1: What are the strongest philosophical arguments for patriarchal social order, and where do they break?

- **Coverage**: Excellent. Four distinct traditions (Aristotle, Filmer, Thomistic-Catholic, Arnhart's Darwinian natural right) plus Confucian role ethics and Reformed complementarianism. The range is appropriate and the reconstructions are careful.
- **Strengths**: The survey does exactly what the research design demands -- it steelmans before dismantling. The Aristotle reconstruction (via Karbowski 2013) is rigorous. The discovery that JP2's theology explicitly rejects male domination as a consequence of the Fall is a genuinely interesting finding that will surprise readers. The identification of the "inferential bridge" problem -- that no tradition successfully moves from natural difference to hierarchical authority -- is the thread that should run through the essay.
- **Gaps**: The survey notes that Roger Scruton's *Sexual Desire* (1986) is the more philosophically serious conservative text on gender but could not be accessed. This is a minor gap -- Scruton's position is better characterized as defending traditional marriage and sexual mores than defending patriarchal authority per se. Not worth a deep dive given time constraints. The "argument from social coordination" (gender roles as efficient coordination devices rather than claims about worth) is flagged as under-explored but is more of an essay-writing opportunity than a research gap -- the orchestrator can develop this line.

### Q2: Does evolutionary psychology provide a credible scientific basis for patriarchal norms?

- **Coverage**: Very thorough. The survey covers the major empirical findings (Hyde meta-analyses, Walter et al. mate preferences, Baron-Cohen E-S theory, Joel brain mosaic, gender equality paradox), the replication crisis data (Soto & Schimmack 2024), the biosocial alternative (Eagly & Wood), and the logical structure of the is/ought gap. The four "logical moves" taxonomy (naturalization, adaptive efficiency, free choice, immutability) is well-structured and will serve the essay directly.
- **Strengths**: The note that Buss and Schmitt themselves explicitly disown the normative argument is important -- it shows that the "evo-psych justifies patriarchy" move is being made primarily by popularizers (Peterson, Defant) rather than by the leading scientists. The Mosuo matriliny data (Mattison et al. 2020) is a strong concrete example. The replication crisis coverage grounds the argument empirically rather than just philosophically.
- **Gaps**: The survey does not deeply engage with David Geary's work, which is probably the most careful contemporary evolutionary account of cognitive sex differences. This matters because the essay's credibility depends on engaging the strongest version of the biological argument, not just Peterson and Defant. However, the survey already covers the core empirical landscape well enough through Hyde, Walter et al., and Baron-Cohen. This is a gap worth noting but not worth a full deep dive.

## The Key Gap: The Neo-Aristotelian Response to the Naturalistic Fallacy

Across all three surveys, the naturalistic fallacy is identified as the central logical failure of patriarchal arguments that appeal to nature (whether Aristotelian teleology, Thomistic natural law, or evolutionary psychology). The surveys correctly identify this. But the feminist rebuttals survey itself flags that "sophisticated natural lawyers (following Aristotle, Aquinas, or MacIntyre) do have responses to simple naturalistic fallacy objections -- they argue that the content of flourishing is determined by natural ends, not by is/ought reasoning alone."

This matters because the essay's argumentative integrity depends on engaging the strongest form of the opposition. If the essay says "the naturalistic fallacy defeats all appeals to nature" and there exists a serious philosophical tradition (virtue ethics, neo-Aristotelian naturalism -- Foot's *Natural Goodness*, MacIntyre's *Dependent Rational Animals*) that claims to bridge the is/ought gap through a teleological account of human flourishing, then the essay has a hole. The reader who knows this tradition will find the argument incomplete.

The question is whether this tradition actually supports patriarchal conclusions or whether it is co-opted. The philosophical arguments survey hints that Arnhart's Darwinian natural right and the complementarity tradition try to do this but fail. A deep dive should trace this specific question: does the neo-Aristotelian/virtue-ethics response to the naturalistic fallacy actually rescue patriarchal arguments, or does it -- like the complementarity argument -- prove at most that men and women differ without generating hierarchical conclusions?

## Deep-Dive Assignments

### Assignment: The neo-Aristotelian bridge -- does virtue ethics rescue the naturalistic fallacy for patriarchal arguments?

- **Question to answer**: Neo-Aristotelian ethicists (Foot, MacIntyre, Hursthouse, Arnhart) claim to bridge the is/ought gap by grounding normativity in natural teleology -- what counts as "good" for a creature depends on its nature. Does this bridge, even if philosophically sound, actually support patriarchal conclusions? Or does it, like the complementarity argument, at most support the claim that men and women have somewhat different flourishing profiles without entailing that one sex should govern the other?
- **Why it matters**: The naturalistic fallacy is the linchpin of the essay's argument against both the philosophical and evo-psych defenses of patriarchy. If the essay deploys this argument without engaging the strongest response to it, the argument is incomplete. This deep dive would either (a) show that neo-Aristotelianism does not rescue the patriarchal argument, strengthening the essay's conclusion, or (b) identify a genuine philosophical tension that the essay should honestly acknowledge.
- **Where to start**: Philippa Foot, *Natural Goodness* (2001); MacIntyre, *Dependent Rational Animals* (1999); Arnhart's "Darwinian Natural Right" (already surveyed); the feminist response from Nussbaum (who is herself neo-Aristotelian and explicitly rejects the patriarchal application of virtue ethics). Also look for any explicit engagement between feminist philosophers and the Foot/MacIntyre tradition on gender specifically.
