# Survey: What Are the Strongest Philosophical Arguments for Patriarchal Social Order, and Where Do They Break?

## Summary

The major philosophical traditions that have defended patriarchal or gender-differentiated social orders fall into four clusters: (1) Aristotle's teleological-biological argument for natural hierarchy; (2) Filmer's divine-right patriarchalism and its biblical genealogy of power; (3) contemporary natural law theory, including the Catholic complementarity tradition and Arnhart's Darwinian natural right; and (4) Confucian role ethics, which is a distinctive and genuinely different case. Each tradition has a real logical structure worth understanding. None survives sustained scrutiny intact. The most sophisticated are the Thomistic-complementarity position and Arnhart's naturalism — neither of which actually entails male rule, but is regularly co-opted to support it. The weakest, despite being historically most influential, is Filmer's, which collapses the moment its biblical premises are denied. Aristotle occupies a strange middle position: his argument has genuine philosophical architecture, but it rests on biological claims known to be false even in the 17th century.

## Key Sources

### Aristotle, *Politics* Book I — Teleological Hierarchy Argument

- **Link**: Reconstructed from multiple secondary sources (Karbowski, Connell, Reeve, LSE blog); primary text cited throughout scholarship
- **Key content**:

Aristotle's argument is not merely a cultural prejudice dressed up in philosophy — it has a real logical structure rooted in his broader teleological framework. The reconstruction:

**Premise 1**: In nature, the soul governs the body; reason governs passion. These are constitutive hierarchies of form over matter.

**Premise 2**: Different beings possess rational faculties to different degrees. Men, women, children, and natural slaves differ in their share of the deliberative faculty (*to bouleutikon*).

**Premise 3**: Women possess the deliberative faculty but it is *akuron* — "without authority" or "without the capacity to command" (Politics I.13, 1260a12–13). The exact meaning is contested: some scholars (Fortenbaugh) read it as women being unable to control their emotions; Karbowski's influential 2013 reading argues women's deliberative faculty is intrinsically limited, not merely culturally suppressed.

**Premise 4**: Political governance requires full deliberative authority. Therefore, women are fit for governance of the household (a restricted domain) but not the polis.

**Conclusion**: The natural order assigns women to domestic rule under male supervision, not because of convention, but because of their inherent rational constitution.

The argument is linked to Aristotle's biology: he believed females were "misbegotten males" (*Generation of Animals* II.3) — defective products of imperfect generative process. This biological claim undergirds the metaphysical claim about inferior rational capacity.

**Assessment**: Peer-reviewed scholarship, well-established. Karbowski's paper (Apeiron 2013, DOI 10.1515/APEIRON-2013-0061) is the most rigorous recent reconstruction. Connell's book (Cambridge, 2021) and the anthology of related papers provide the state of the field.

**Where it breaks**: Three independent failures.

(a) *The biological claim is simply false*. The premise that women have constitutionally inferior deliberative capacity rests on Aristotelian embryology (women as defective males) that has no basis in any form of modern biology. This is not a peripheral point — without the claim about women's constitutive rational deficiency, the argument for exclusion from politics cannot go through.

(b) *Internal inconsistency*: Aristotle simultaneously grants women enough deliberative authority to manage the household (a real and complex domain). If women can make reliable judgments about household economy, inheritance, child-rearing, and extended-family relations, the argument for their categorical exclusion from politics is already undermined by his own concessions.

(c) *The naturalistic inference*: Even if women did have somewhat less natural aptitude for public deliberation (an empirical question never settled), Aristotle's argument still requires the additional premise that political roles should be allocated by natural capacity rather than by developed virtue, education, or consent. He doesn't adequately defend this transition.

### Robert Filmer, *Patriarcha* (written c. 1640, published 1680)

- **Link**: Full text available, modernized edition at https://updatedworks.com/books/patriarcha-modern-translation/; Tessarolo (2024) "Rethinking the Political Thought of Robert Filmer" in *History of Political Thought* offers the best recent scholarly reanalysis
- **Key content**:

Filmer's argument is specifically about political authority, not directly about gender subordination — it is patriarchalism in the original sense (rule by fathers), not modern "patriarchy" in the feminist sense. But it constitutes the most systematic early-modern defense of male-headed governance through a specific argument structure.

**The triadic argument** (reconstructed from Filmer's text and the Reyes 2025 exposition):

**Theological argument**: God gave dominion first to Adam. This was an actual, historical grant of governing authority. Adam's lordship over his children is the seed of all legitimate political authority. Kings are the heirs of Adam through patrilineal succession — therefore political authority derives from paternal right, not popular consent.

**Philosophical argument**: Attacks social contract theory at its foundation. If all humans are naturally equal, then democracy (power to the whole people) is the only legitimate form. But the people cannot exercise power directly and must delegate it — which means their "natural power" is a fiction that produces illegitimate delegations. The premise of natural equality is thus self-defeating for liberal theorists.

**Historical argument**: Representative governments are historically shorter-lived, bloodier, and more corrupt than monarchies. The ancient democracies of Athens and Rome demonstrate this.

The *genealogical* structure: Political authority follows from fatherly authority, which descends from Adam. All national sovereigns are, in principle, the "fathers" of their peoples even if not literally. The son who creates his own household gains sovereignty over it (patrilineal transmission of authority). Marriage is within the father's jurisdiction, so the new household's authority descends from the patriarch's permission.

**Assessment**: Filmer is a genuinely rigorous thinker within his premises. Tessarolo's 2024 historical analysis distinguishes Filmer from cruder patriarchalists by noting his sophisticated distinction between ordinary fatherhood and "supreme" fatherhood as *dominium* (right of ownership). The Substack essay by Reyes (April 2025) clearly exposes the triadic structure for non-specialists. Locke's First Treatise demolished Filmer systematically; the scholarly consensus is that Filmer lost that exchange.

**Where it breaks**: Three terminal problems.

(a) *The scriptural premise is load-bearing but optional*. The entire genealogy — Adam's grant, Noah's division, patriarchal succession — only works if the Bible's historical account is treated as literal fact describing actual events. Remove that premise and the argument has no foundation. Locke's response focused here: even if Adam had unique divine grant, it cannot be transmitted to present kings because no one can identify the rightful heir.

(b) *The conflation of household authority with political authority*. Filmer's claim that there is no difference between economic power (domestic/family management) and political power is simply asserted, not demonstrated. Suárez's response — that family authority dissolves as children form independent households — is not adequately answered.

(c) *The gender implication is not actually the core*. Filmer's argument is about why fathers, not mothers, hold authority — but his explicit argument for that distinction is the weakest part. He relies on the biblical construction that God commanded the husband to rule, but this just restates the question.

### Thomistic-Catholic Complementarity (Aquinas through John Paul II and Ratzinger)

- **Link**: Vatican CDF Letter on the Collaboration of Men and Women (2004), signed by Ratzinger: https://www.vatican.va/roman_curia/congregations/cfaith/documents/rc_con_cfaith_doc_20040731_collaboration_en.html; Blake Johnson, "A Thomistic Approach to the Sexes" (Credo Magazine, December 2025): https://credomag.com/article/a-thomistic-approach-to-the-sexes/; Bamforth and Richards, *Patriarchal Religion, Sexuality, and Gender: A Critique of New Natural Law* (2007) on Grisez/Finnis/George tradition
- **Key content**:

This is the most philosophically developed contemporary tradition, and it splits into two partially distinct arguments:

**The complementarity argument** (Aquinas revised by JP2):

**Premise 1**: Sexual difference is ontologically real and irreducible. Male and female differ in their "non-identically divided power of generation" — each sex represents a different orientation toward the act of procreation.

**Premise 2**: A woman is the kind of being whose body is organized around the potential to gestate new life (maternity); a man is oriented toward paternity. This is not merely biological contingency but constitutive of what each sex *is*.

**Premise 3**: From hylomorphism (the soul-body composite): sexual difference is in the body, not the soul. Men and women share equal dignity as persons (imago Dei in both equally). But bodily difference has implications for social role.

**Premise 4**: The family is the foundational social institution. Within the family, complementary roles emerge from different bodily orientations — the mother's primary orientation is gestational care, the father's is provision and protection.

**Ratzinger 2004 letter's move**: The "genius of women" — their particular attunement to care, vulnerability, and relational life — is not a diminishment but a special contribution. Masculine and feminine "values" are both necessary for human flourishing; neither can be reduced to the other.

**Where traditional Thomism endorses subordination**: Aquinas accepts Aristotle's faulty embryology (woman as "misbegotten male") and thus concludes that men have greater rational faculty, making them suited to govern. He argues women should be "submissive" because "in man the discretion of reason predominates" (ST I, q. 92). This is now widely recognized, even by sympathetic interpreters, as grounded in false biology.

**JP2's revision**: John Paul II explicitly breaks with the older subordinationist reading. In *Mulieris Dignitatem* (1988) and the "Theology of the Body" catecheses, he argues that male domination of women is *not* natural law but rather the consequence of the Fall — distorted concupiscence, not God's design. The 2004 letter expressly states: "God's decisive words to the woman after the first sin express the kind of relationship which has now been introduced between man and woman... a relationship in which love will frequently be debased into pure self-seeking." Male rule is thus the sign of sin, not creation order.

This is a remarkable inversion: the Vatican tradition, properly developed, explicitly disavows patriarchal domination while retaining gender differentiation.

**The New Natural Law wing** (Grisez, Finnis, George): Arguments about gender roles are largely implicit — these philosophers focus mainly on sexual ethics (contraception, homosexuality) rather than explicitly arguing for male governance. Their position preserves gender-differentiated roles through claims about the "basic good" of marriage as intrinsically ordered toward procreation.

**Assessment**: The 2004 Vatican letter is an authoritative, sophisticated document. Blake Johnson's 2025 article is a fair sympathetic reconstruction. Bamforth and Richards (2007) offers the strongest secular critique of the Grisez-Finnis tradition. The complementarity argument, as developed by JP2, is philosophically more defensible than Aquinas's straightforwardly inegalitarian position — but it comes at the cost of surrendering the claim for male authority.

**Where it breaks**:

(a) *Complementarity does not entail hierarchical authority*. The argument that male and female are genuinely different in their orientations, even if accepted, does not by itself generate a conclusion about who should govern whom. You can have genuine complementarity with equal authority — the Vatican's own formulation (especially JP2) recognizes this.

(b) *The Thomistic residue*: Where traditional Thomism does argue for male authority (Aquinas's claim that men have superior reason), this depends on the same false Aristotelian biology Aquinas should not have relied on. Blake Johnson notes this tension explicitly: Aquinas's complementarian metaphysics contradicts his sex-polarity anthropology.

(c) *Essentialism without adequate empirical grounding*: The claim that female identity is constitutively organized around maternal potential runs into the fact that many women neither can nor do bear children; Thomists try to address this with the "potential" move, but critics note this is circular (you're defined by a potential that many people lack).

### Larry Arnhart, Darwinian Natural Right (1998) and "Darwinian Conservatism"

- **Link**: *Darwinian Natural Right: The Biological Ethics of Human Nature* (SUNY Press, 1998), https://sunypress.edu/Books/D/Darwinian-Natural-Right; Arnhart, "Darwinian Conservatism as the New Natural Law," *Perspectives on Political Science* 34.3 (2005), pp. 135-143; Arnhart's self-description in *Claremont Review of Books*, Spring 2004
- **Key content**:

Arnhart's project is to ground natural right in Darwinian biology, arguing that there are at least 20 "natural desires" universal to all human societies because they are biologically grounded. His chapter "Man and Woman" in *Darwinian Natural Right* includes sections titled "The Biology of Sex Differences," "Mating Desires," "Male Dominance and Male Vulnerability," and "The Moral Complementarity of Male and Female Norms."

**Arnhart's argument structure** (reconstructed from the table of contents and secondary sources):

**Premise 1**: Natural desires are biologically real universal inclinations documented across cultures and in non-human primates. The satisfaction of these desires constitutes objective human good.

**Premise 2**: Males and females have evolved different mating strategies (consistent with Darwinian parental investment theory — women invest more in offspring, men are more competitive for mating access). These differences are expressed in cross-cultural patterns.

**Premise 3**: "Male dominance" is a biological fact about human societies just as in other primates — but it is paired with "male vulnerability" (men are more expendable, have shorter lifespans, are exposed to more physical risk). These are complementary positions in a natural order.

**Premise 4**: Social arrangements that conform to these natural tendencies — the nuclear family with male provision and female primary childcare — satisfy natural desires more fully than utopian attempts to eliminate sex differences.

**Conclusion**: A Darwinian conservatism can defend traditional family structures not because God ordained them but because they reflect evolved human nature.

Barbara Smuts (University of Michigan, primatologist) published a direct critique of Arnhart: "Feminism, the Naturalistic Fallacy, and Evolutionary Biology," *Politics and the Life Sciences* 11.2 (1992). Her key point: Arnhart cannot move from "natural potential" to normative judgment about social arrangements without an additional premise he doesn't adequately defend — the naturalistic fallacy.

**Assessment**: Arnhart is a legitimate academic political philosopher (Northern Illinois University). His book received serious scholarly attention and endorsements from Robert Trivers and Roger Masters. The evolutionary biology he draws on is real science, not folk biology. However, his is primarily an argument against *utopian social engineering* rather than an affirmative argument for patriarchal authority specifically. He defends the nuclear family, not male dominance.

**Where it breaks**:

(a) *The naturalistic fallacy*: Smuts's critique is on target — moving from "natural" to "normatively good" requires additional justification that Arnhart does not fully provide. Many natural tendencies (aggression, xenophobia, short time horizons) are things we rightly work to overcome. "Natural" is not the same as "good."

(b) *Selective reading of biology*: The claim that male dominance is the "natural" pattern for humans ignores significant variation across human societies, including matrilineal and matrilocal societies. The primatological record shows considerable variation: bonobos vs. chimpanzees illustrate that closely related species can have radically different dominance structures. Smuts points out that interpreting animal behavior itself involves projections.

(c) *The argument proves too little*: Even if evolved sex differences are real (which they are, to a significant degree), they exist along a population distribution with enormous overlap between sexes. No individual's social role should be determined by population averages.

### Confucian Role Ethics and Gender

- **Links**: Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Gender in Confucian Philosophy" (Rosenlee, 2023), https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/confucian-gender/; Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy, "Gender in Chinese Philosophy," https://iep.utm.edu/gender-in-chinese-philosophy/; Wikipedia, "Three Obediences and Four Virtues"; Sor-hoon Tan, "China, Feminism and the Legacy of Confucius" (*IAI*, January 2025), https://iai.tv/articles/china-feminism-and-the-legacy-of-confucius-auid-3044
- **Key content**:

Confucian thought presents a distinctive case: it is not primarily an argument *for* patriarchal order in the way Western traditions argue. Rather, it is a system of role ethics grounded in relational identity. The question of whether Confucianism constitutes a philosophical defense of patriarchy is actively contested among contemporary scholars.

**The core Confucian structure**:

Confucian social order is built around the five key relationships: ruler-minister, father-son, husband-wife, elder brother-younger brother, friend-friend. Of these, husband-wife and father-son involve hierarchical asymmetry by design. The "Three Obediences" (Three Feminine Obediences: obey father, then husband, then son) formalized from the Han period codify women's role explicitly.

**The cosmological grounding**: Yin-yang cosmology assigns feminine/yin qualities (receptive, dark, soft, interior) and masculine/yang qualities (active, bright, hard, exterior) as complementary forces in the cosmos. Many Confucian texts emphasize yang's dominant characteristics. This is not hierarchy of worth but of *function* — yin without yang and yang without yin would each be incomplete.

**The role ethics argument**: For Confucians, identity is not individual but relational — you are constituted by your roles. A wife is constituted partly by her role as wife; a mother by her role as mother. This is not degradation but the nature of a relationally-defined self. The virtue of fidelity (zhong) and ritual propriety (li) apply to all parties in all relationships, including the superior party.

**Key classical texts**: Analects 17.25 ("It is only women and petty persons who are difficult to provide for") is the most-cited denigrating passage; but its interpretation is contested (Sor-hoon Tan notes it may refer to girls, concubines, or women denied education). Ban Zhao (49–120 CE), the first known female Chinese historian, elaborated the Three Obediences and Four Virtues in *Lessons for Women* — notably, she did this as a woman, suggesting she saw it as a complementary framework, not solely oppression.

**The contemporary revisionist position** (Tan, Li, Wawrytko): Confucian *ren* (benevolence/humaneness) and the emphasis on cultivation apply universally, not only to men. The care ethics implicit in Confucian relational philosophy has affinities with feminist ethics of care (Gilligan, Noddings). The patriarchal practices codified in Han and later Neo-Confucian texts are accretions, not the core logic.

**Assessment**: The IEP and SEP articles are authoritative academic sources. Tan's IAI piece (2025) represents current revisionist scholarship fairly. The Wikipedia entry on Three Obediences and Four Virtues documents the codified tradition well.

**Where the "patriarchal argument" in Confucianism breaks** (and what is distinctive):

(a) *The hierarchy is relational, not intrinsic to gender*: Unlike Aristotle's claim that women are biologically deficient, Confucian hierarchy is role-based and positional. A grandmother commands respect over her son in the same relational system. The hierarchy shifts with role, age, and context, not merely gender.

(b) *The cosmological grounding is not an argument*: Saying that the feminine corresponds to yin and the masculine to yang is a cosmological correlation, not a philosophical argument for male governance. It describes a pattern, not a prescription — and the IEP notes that yin and yang are equally necessary, so neither is superior.

(c) *Historical contamination*: The explicit subordination doctrines (Three Obediences) emerge strongly in the Han period under legalist-Confucian synthesis and are amplified in Neo-Confucian orthodoxy (Song-Ming periods). There is a genuine scholarly case that these are later accretions to early Confucian texts that do not follow from the core philosophical commitments.

(d) *Confucian feminism is a live tradition*: Unlike, say, Filmer, which is a dead argument defended only by monarchists, Confucian thought is actively reformulated by feminist scholars in East Asia who find resources for gender equality within it.

### Reformed Protestantism and Complementarianism (contemporary)

- **Link**: Interview with Zachary Garris on *Honor Thy Fathers* (New Christendom Press), published at *American Reformer* July 2024: https://americanreformer.org/2024/07/on-the-state-of-patriarchy-past-and-present/
- **Key content**:

Contemporary Reformed complementarianism (Garris, Grudem, Piper) constitutes a significant practical tradition defending male headship. Its argument is scriptural-hermeneutical:

**Core claim**: The Bible (1 Tim 2:11-12, 1 Cor 14:34-35, Eph 5:22-33, Gen 1-3) consistently prescribes male leadership in home, church, and implicitly civil government. This is not a post-Fall consequence of sin (contra JP2's interpretation) but part of creation order — male authority is established before the Fall.

**The exegetical argument**: Adam was created first (1 Tim 2:13); woman was created as "helper" (ezer) for man (Gen 2:18); the naming of woman by Adam signals authority (as in the naming of animals). These creation-order facts, not mere cultural accommodation, ground male leadership.

**Assessment**: This is a sincere theological tradition with real exegetical engagement. However, as a philosophical argument, it is entirely dependent on the authority of the biblical text and a specific interpretation of that text within a larger hermeneutical framework. It is not a philosophical argument in the tradition-neutral sense.

**Where it breaks philosophically**: The argument is valid only within a framework that accepts (a) the authority of the biblical text, and (b) a specific hermeneutical tradition. This is not a universal argument accessible to those outside the tradition. Moreover, the Catholic tradition (JP2) demonstrates that even within biblical Christianity, the same texts can be read to yield the opposite conclusion on male authority.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of knowledge

The philosophical literature on arguments for patriarchal order is substantial and well-developed, especially for the ancient and medieval periods. The contemporary scene divides into: (1) academic philosophy (Aristotle scholarship, natural law theory), which is critical and well-sourced; (2) theological/confessional traditions (Catholic, Reformed Protestant), which continue to produce serious internal defenses; (3) popular naturalist arguments (evo-psych adjacent), which are often less rigorous than academic versions.

### Agreements across sources

All serious philosophical traditions that touch patriarchal social order now concede that crude biological determinism (Aristotle's "misbegotten male" or claims of constitutively inferior female reason) is false. Even sympathetic expositors of Aquinas (like Blake Johnson) acknowledge the Aristotelian biology was wrong. This narrows the serious defense to either (a) differentiated-but-equal complementarity or (b) explicit religious prescription.

### Core conflicts

- **Does natural difference entail hierarchical authority?** The complementarity tradition (JP2) says no; traditional Thomism said yes; Arnhart's Darwinianism hedges.
- **Is male dominance a fact of natural order or a consequence of sin?** Catholic post-Vatican II tradition says the latter; Reformed complementarians say the former; Confucians sidestep the question by making hierarchy positional rather than sinful/natural.
- **Is Confucianism patriarchal at its philosophical core, or only in its historical accretions?** Genuine scholarly controversy.

### Under-explored areas

1. **The argument from social function**: There is a version of the patriarchal argument that doesn't appeal to inherent capacity but to social coordination — that asymmetric gender roles in the family are functionally efficient coordination devices, not claims about worth. This argument is almost never made rigorously and deserves more systematic examination. It is distinct from complementarity in not being about essential natures.

2. **The Roger Scruton angle**: Scruton's position on gender and family roles is not extensively documented in the sources found. His 2013 Guardian article on conservative values treats the family as crucial to national identity and social continuity but does not directly argue for male authority — he argues for marriage and family as institutions. His *Sexual Desire* (1986) is the more philosophically serious text but I was unable to access it directly.

3. **The gap between gender difference and gender hierarchy**: All the philosophical traditions surveyed either prove too much (arguing from natural difference to necessary subordination without the inferential bridge) or prove too little (showing that men and women differ without showing that this requires hierarchical authority). The inferential bridge is almost never explicitly defended.

### Surprises

1. **The Vatican tradition, properly developed, explicitly rejects male domination as a consequence of the Fall, not creation order**. This is a remarkable inversion that many people who cite "Catholic" support for patriarchy do not acknowledge.

2. **Aristotle's argument is far more philosophically scaffolded than expected** — it is not mere prejudice but a genuine philosophical argument about deliberative capacity, natural teleology, and the relationship between soul and body. It fails, but it fails for specific reasons that can be specified.

3. **Confucian role ethics is genuinely different in kind from Western patriarchal arguments**: It is not an argument from natural inferiority but from relational constitution. Whether this is better or worse is a different question — it may be less obviously wrong while being harder to argue against.

4. **Filmer, despite being the historically most influential explicit defender of patriarchal political order, is the easiest to defeat** — his argument depends entirely on accepting literal biblical history as political genealogy. Locke's rebuttal is devastating and has never been seriously answered.
