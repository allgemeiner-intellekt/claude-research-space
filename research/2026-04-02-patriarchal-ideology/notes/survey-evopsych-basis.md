# Survey: Does evolutionary psychology provide a credible scientific basis for patriarchal norms, or does it commit the naturalistic fallacy at scale?

## Summary

Evolutionary psychology (evo-psych) offers a genuinely interesting framework for investigating human sex differences, and some findings in this space are well-replicated. But when its proponents — and especially its popularizers — use those findings to argue that patriarchal social arrangements are "natural," "adaptive," or otherwise grounded in deep biology, the argument breaks down at multiple levels. The scientific case is weaker than often presented (many effect sizes are small, many specific claims have poor replication, the brain-mosaic literature undermines strong dimorphism), the logical move from "exists" to "justified" is almost never made explicit and almost never survives scrutiny when examined, and the claim of universal male dominance across human societies is empirically contested by anthropological data. The overall picture is: some genuine sex differences exist (particularly in mating behavior preferences, physical aggression, and certain visuospatial tasks), but these differences are mostly distributional overlaps rather than categorical divides, they are shaped by culture and context in profound ways, and they do not entail anything in particular about whether patriarchal norms should persist.

## Key Sources

### Hyde (2005) — "The Gender Similarities Hypothesis"
- **Link**: DOI 10.1037/0003-066X.60.6.581 (American Psychologist; open access at wisebrain.org)
- **Key content**: Meta-analysis of 46 meta-analyses covering psychological gender differences. Finding: males and females are similar on most psychological variables. Most effect sizes are small or close to zero. Large differences found only in motor performance (throwing velocity), sexuality (attitudes toward casual sex, masturbation frequency), and some types of aggression. "Only a few" differences are large. Hyde explicitly warns that overstatement of sex differences harms girls by creating stereotype threat.
- **Assessment**: Very high credibility — this is a rigorous quantitative synthesis in a top journal, widely cited (280+ citations). Provides the best panoramic view of the literature. The key methodological point: distributional overlap makes individual-level predictions from group averages unreliable. Even where differences exist (d=0.5 or below), the distributions overlap so heavily that sex is a poor predictor of any individual's traits.

### Hyde (2014) — "Gender Similarities and Differences"
- **Link**: DOI 10.1146/annurev-psych-010213-115057 (Annual Review of Psychology)
- **Key content**: Updated systematic review confirming the gender similarities hypothesis. Also summarizes competing theoretical frameworks: evolutionary theories, cognitive social learning theory, sociocultural theory. Key point: where differences exist, their magnitudes vary substantially across cultures — an argument against fixed biological determinism.
- **Assessment**: Authoritative review in a top-tier journal (737 citations). More nuanced than 2005 piece; acknowledges some robust differences while contextualizing them.

### Buss & Schmitt (2011) — "Evolutionary Psychology and Feminism"
- **Link**: DOI 10.1007/S11199-011-9987-3 (Sex Roles)
- **Key content**: Buss and Schmitt lay out evo-psych's meta-theoretical claims and defend them from feminist critiques. Crucially, the authors explicitly acknowledge: (1) human behavior is not "genetically determined"; (2) social and cultural inputs are "necessary and integral"; (3) evolutionary psychology predicts both similarities and differences; (4) some hypotheses will be falsified by empirical tests. They also discuss the "origins of patriarchy" as a co-evolutionary outcome of women's mate preferences for resourceful men + men's resulting competition for resources — not as a normative argument for its perpetuation. The authors explicitly state that evo-psych has "no political agenda."
- **Assessment**: This is the most careful primary statement from evo-psych proponents. Sophisticated. Important that they explicitly disclaim determinism and acknowledge the is/ought distinction. However, the public use of evo-psych rarely reproduces this careful framing.

### Walter et al. (2020) — "Sex Differences in Mate Preferences Across 45 Countries"
- **Link**: Psychology Science (Repositorio ISCTE open access)
- **Key content**: Large-scale replication (N=14,399 across 45 countries) of mate preference sex differences. Robust finding: men prefer younger, more attractive mates; women prefer older, financially resourceful mates. Cross-culturally, both sexes prefer mates closer to their own age as gender equality increases. Pathogen prevalence and gender equality did not robustly predict magnitude of sex differences. Authors interpret as evidence for universal evolved sex differences in mating preferences.
- **Assessment**: This is one of the stronger findings in the evo-psych canon — a large-N replication with cross-cultural scope (228 citations). However, even here there is interpretive complexity: the preferences are in terms of stated ideals, not behavior; average differences may be small relative to within-group variance; the biosocial role theory (Eagly & Wood) offers an alternative account in which these preferences reflect socialized roles rather than evolved dispositions, and both accounts are consistent with the pattern found.

### Baron-Cohen et al. (2018) — "Testing the Empathizing-Systemizing Theory in Half a Million People"
- **Link**: PNAS 115(48) (open access)
- **Key content**: Large-scale (N=671,606) test of the E-S theory. Confirms: typical females on average score higher on empathy quotient (EQ); typical males on average score higher on systemizing quotient (SQ). Autistic individuals show a "masculinized" profile. D-scores (EQ minus SQ) account for 19x more variance in autistic traits than demographic variables including sex.
- **Assessment**: High credibility due to massive sample size and replication dataset (247 citations). However, important caveats: (1) The E-S questionnaires were developed by Baron-Cohen's own group and have been criticized by Fine for socially biased question framing; (2) the difference is in group averages with massive individual overlap; (3) E-S scores reflect self-report, not direct neural measurement; (4) the theory says nothing normative about social roles. Fine's critique of Baron-Cohen's newborn face/mobile study (methodological flaws allowing experimenter bias) remains valid, even if the large-scale self-report data looks robust.

### Joel et al. (2015) — "Sex Beyond the Genitalia: The Human Brain Mosaic"
- **Link**: PNAS 112(50) (open access)
- **Key content**: Analyzed four large neuroimaging datasets. Finding: human brains are not divided into two types (male/female). Rather, each brain is a "mosaic" of features, some more common in females, some more common in males, many intermediate. Brains with consistent "all male" or "all female" features are rare. Even on highly gender-stereotyped behaviors (watching porn vs. using cosmetics), most individuals show mixed profiles. This undermines the "male brain / female brain" concept underlying much popular evo-psych.
- **Assessment**: High credibility (577 citations, PNAS). Sparked four published critiques and a response. The critiques generally argue that this only shows variation within each sex, not that sex differences in specific regions don't exist — but this response concedes the central point: there is no discrete "male type" or "female type" brain. This directly challenges claims that brain dimorphism translates into fixed cognitive dimorphism.

### Eagly & Wood (2016) — "Social Role Theory of Sex Differences"
- **Link**: (340 citations; encyclopedic entry, closed access)
- **Key content**: Biosocial construction theory argues that sex differences in behavior arise primarily from the social roles that men and women occupy, which are themselves partially shaped by biological differences (especially reproductive roles). The critical move: even if biology creates some initial tendencies, social structure amplifies them enormously. Evidence: sex differences in many traits (e.g., aggression, nurturance) are substantially larger in societies with greater gender inequality, which is the opposite of what a strict genetic determinism would predict (though this is contested — see below).
- **Assessment**: Eagly is one of the most rigorous empirical researchers in gender psychology. Social role theory has strong cross-cultural support. The key point for the is/ought question: even if biological differences exist, their manifestation in social roles is plastic — which means social arrangements can be changed independently of biology.

### Stoet & Geary (2018) — "The Gender-Equality Paradox in STEM"
- **Link**: Psychology Science (open access)
- **Key content**: Girls outperform boys in reading in nearly all countries; boys show relative advantage in science/math as intraindividual strength. Paradoxically, the sex difference in STEM representation is larger in more gender-equal countries (e.g., Finland has fewer women in STEM than Saudi Arabia). Authors interpret this as evidence for innate occupational preferences diverging from economic need when both sexes have genuine choice.
- **Assessment**: Highly cited (876 citations) but methodologically contested. Richardson et al. (2020) published a formal commentary in Psychology Science identifying errors: the "propensity" measure Stoet & Geary used to estimate women in STEM does not correspond to what they claimed it measured. This controversy matters: the "gender equality paradox" has become a major talking point in arguments for biological inevitability of gender gaps, but the original calculation was flawed. The paradox may be real (later replications find it robust with corrected measures) but its magnitude and mechanism remain disputed.

### Richardson et al. (2020) — "Is There a Gender-Equality Paradox in STEM?"
- **Link**: Psychology Science (commentary, partial open access)
- **Key content**: Formal critique of Stoet & Geary (2018), identifying a mismatch between their "women in STEM" numbers and UNESCO data. Their key measure was not what they said it was. Authors argue this undermines the paradox claim, or at least calls for reanalysis.
- **Assessment**: Published in the same journal as the original. Stoet & Geary issued a corrigendum. The debate is ongoing, but this source is important because it shows how quickly popular evo-psych arguments can outrun the quality of the underlying data.

### Soto & Schimmack (2024) — "Replicability Report for the Journal Evolutionary Psychology"
- **Link**: https://replicationindex.com/2024/09/06/2024-rr24-evopsy/
- **Key content**: Statistical analysis using z-curve method of all 800+ articles published in *Evolutionary Psychology* (2003–2023). Key findings: (1) Expected replication rate (ERR) 49–72%; (2) Observed discovery rate (68%) significantly exceeds expected discovery rate (49%), indicating selection bias in publication; (3) False discovery risk at conventional alpha = 5–15%; recommend adjusting to p<.01; (4) No improvement in replication metrics over time despite awareness of replication crisis; (5) "Robust results have been found for sex differences in sexuality; questionable results have been reported for menstrual cycle effects on behavior."
- **Assessment**: This is a careful, data-driven assessment from a prominent replication methodology researcher. It is not from a peer-reviewed journal but uses established methods (z-curve). Key takeaway: the replication crisis has hit evo-psych meaningfully, particularly for smaller studies. The specific finding that menstrual cycle studies are especially unreliable is relevant because these studies have been used to argue for dramatic hormonally-driven behavioral cycling. Effect size inflation due to publication bias means published claims overstate the true magnitude of sex differences.

### Peterson lobster argument critique (Washington Post, Bailey Steinworth, 2018)
- **Link**: https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/posteverything/wp/2018/06/04/jordan-peterson-needs-to-reconsider-the-lobster/
- **Key content**: Marine biologist explains the scientific errors in Peterson's lobster argument. Main points: (1) The common ancestor of humans and lobsters almost certainly lacked dominance hierarchies — so convergent evolution means the similarity is not evidence of shared deep homology; (2) Peterson cherry-picks lobsters (solitary, competitive) from a vast array of marine invertebrate social structures, including highly cooperative and hermaphroditic species; (3) Sea hares (also used in serotonin research) prefer the company of conspecifics and benefit from association; (4) Horseshoe crabs demonstrate that "satellite male" strategies mean even non-dominant males can reproduce successfully. The piece is journalistic/opinion but written by a domain expert.
- **Assessment**: Not peer-reviewed, but the core biological critique is solid. Peterson's argument commits the "lone fact" fallacy: selecting one example from nature to support a social conclusion while ignoring the enormous diversity of biological social arrangements. This is a textbook case of motivated reasoning dressed as naturalism.

### Quillette review of Fine's "Testosterone Rex" (anonymous, 2017)
- **Link**: https://quillette.com/2017/03/21/cordelia-fines-testosterone-rex-a-review/
- **Key content**: Critical review from a conservative-adjacent publication. Makes some legitimate methodological points: Fine is accused of being partisan in her summary of the Bateman gradient debate (a 2016 meta-analysis confirmed male-biased sexual selection across animal kingdom, appeared after Fine's book); Fine occasionally relegates contradicting evidence to endnotes; her own favored studies sometimes show the same methodological weaknesses she criticizes in opponents. Also notes Fine explicitly says "the brain is not asexual" — she's not denying all sex differences.
- **Assessment**: Published commentary from Quillette (political publication with a track record of favoring evo-psych arguments). Despite its political orientation, some methodological criticisms appear fair. Important to note: Fine's argument, and the review's rebuttal, are both engaging with the *magnitude* and *causal attribution* of sex differences, not with whether any differences exist. Both sides agree some differences exist.

### Buss (1995) — "Psychological Sex Differences: Origins Through Sexual Selection"
- **Link**: American Psychologist 50(3) (253 citations)
- **Key content**: Canonical early statement of the evo-psych view. Sexual selection (differential reproductive investment, parental investment theory) explains why males competed more for mates, leading to psychological differences in competitiveness, risk-taking, and resource-seeking. Women evolved greater choosiness due to higher parental investment per offspring.
- **Assessment**: Foundational but paradigmatic rather than empirical. Parental investment theory (Trivers 1972) has genuine biological support; applying it to human psychology requires many inferential steps, each of which can be contested. Buss's cross-cultural data on mate preferences are more empirically grounded than his explanatory framework.

### Eagly & Wood (1999/2009) — Social Role Theory as Biosocial Alternative
- **Link**: Multiple papers; "Sexual Selection Does Not Provide Adequate Theory of Sex Differences in Aggression" (2009, 13 citations)
- **Key content**: Argues that social roles created by physical sex differences (especially female reproductive biology and male physical strength) generate psychological sex differences as downstream effects, not as directly evolved adaptations. The prediction: as gender equality increases, sex differences in behavior should decrease if they're role-driven; evo-psych predicts they should remain stable or (perversely per gender-equality paradox) increase.
- **Assessment**: This is the main empirical battleground between evo-psych and social constructionism. The evidence is mixed: some differences decrease with gender equality (as biosocial theory predicts); others (the "gender equality paradox") appear to increase. Neither theory has a clean victory.

### Kong (2021) — "Evolutionary Psychology, Rape, and the Naturalistic Fallacy"
- **Link**: DOI from Journal of Society of Philosophical Studies (open access)
- **Key content**: Philosophical paper arguing that feminist critics of evo-psych are *not* committing the naturalistic fallacy when they oppose its normative implications, because evo-psych accounts themselves embed biased value judgments. Uses Thornhill & Palmer's "A Natural History of Rape" as case study. Three embedded biases identified: (1) adaptationist assumptions that predetermine conclusions; (2) willful ignorance of cultural/social context; (3) binary understanding of sex and gender.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed philosophy paper with rigorous argumentation. The key insight is that evo-psych is not a neutral descriptive enterprise from which feminists illegitimately draw normative conclusions — the value-laden assumptions are baked in at the methodological level.

### Mosuo matriliny study — Mattison et al. (2020), PNAS
- **Link**: https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2014403117
- **Key content**: The Mosuo of Yunnan/Sichuan, China live in matrilineal, matrilocal households where women control land and property. Study comparing Mosuo to neighboring patrilineal Han Chinese society found that gender disparities in inflammation and hypertension are *reversed* in the Mosuo: women have lower inflammatory markers and blood pressure than men (opposite of the patrilineal pattern). This biological outcome suggests that the health penalties often attributed to "female biology" are in significant part a product of social structure.
- **Assessment**: Published in PNAS, a high-prestige journal. This is a natural experiment using adjacent societies. The finding directly contradicts the claim that biological sex differences inevitably produce health hierarchies aligned with patriarchal structures.

### Defant (2025) — "Evolutionary Basis of Gender Dynamics: Understanding Patriarchy, the Pay Gap, and the Glass Ceiling"
- **Link**: Journal of Libertarian Studies 29(1) (open access)
- **Key content**: A recent paper that explicitly tries to use evo-psych to justify the persistence of patriarchal institutions. Argues that sex differences in evolved traits (risk-taking, competition, spatial ability) explain occupational sorting and pay gaps, and that these are not the result of discrimination. Uses the gender equality paradox (STEM data) as evidence that "natural" differences emerge more strongly under freedom.
- **Assessment**: Published in the *Journal of Libertarian Studies*, a politically ideologically-oriented publication, not a mainstream psychology or anthropology journal. This paper is notable primarily as an example of the naturalistic fallacy being explicitly committed: it goes from "sex differences exist" to "the social arrangements reflecting them are justified" without engaging the is/ought gap. The source credibility is low (ideological journal, no peer review in the usual sense).

## The Core Logical Moves: From Is to Ought

Proponents of using evo-psych to justify patriarchal norms typically employ one or more of these logical moves:

**Move 1: Naturalization as justification.** "X is natural (universal, ancient, evolved) therefore X is good or inevitable." This is the classic naturalistic fallacy. Even if male dominance hierarchies existed in our evolutionary past, this entails nothing about whether they should persist. Many "natural" features of human ancestral environments — parasites, infant mortality, violence — are things we work to eliminate.

**Move 2: Adaptive efficiency argument.** "Sex-differentiated roles were selected for because they were adaptively efficient, therefore organizing society around them is optimal." This commits both the naturalistic fallacy and a misunderstanding of natural selection: selection does not optimize for human wellbeing or justice, it selects for reproductive success in ancestral environments that no longer exist.

**Move 3: The "free choice" argument.** "In societies with more freedom (gender equality), sex differences in occupational choices persist or increase — so the differences must be innate preferences and social structure should accommodate rather than override them." This move is evidenced by the gender equality paradox literature (Stoet & Geary, Mac Giolla & Kajonius). But it conflates revealed preference with innate preference. Choices made under any social structure (even egalitarian ones) reflect the accumulated socialization of that structure, not pure biological expression.

**Move 4: Immutability argument.** "If the differences are biological, they cannot be changed, so social engineering is futile." This is explicitly rejected even by evo-psych proponents like Buss & Schmitt (2011), who note that environmental input is required at every step of behavioral development. It also conflicts with the massive cross-cultural variation in sex-differentiated behavior documented in the anthropological literature.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The empirical landscape is genuinely mixed. There are well-replicated findings:
- Men prefer younger, physically attractive mates across cultures; women prefer older, higher-status/resource-holding mates. Effect is real but varies in magnitude (Walter et al. 2020).
- Males show higher physical aggression on average across cultures (large effect). This is probably the most robust sex difference in behavior.
- Males and females differ in mating-related psychology (more male interest in casual sex, greater female choosiness) — consistent across many studies and evolutionary predictions.
- Baron-Cohen's E-S framework: group-level differences in self-reported empathizing and systematizing exist, though their neural basis is contested and their behavioral implications are less clear than often presented.

There are contested or unreliable findings:
- Menstrual cycle effects on attraction, mate preference, and risk-taking: the replication crisis has been especially damaging here (Soto & Schimmack 2024 report this as one of the most unreliable areas).
- Stereotype threat effects and their erasure of cognitive sex differences: the literature is mixed.
- Many specific evo-psych "adaptations" claimed for particular behaviors (e.g., fear of spiders as evolved, specific jealousy mechanisms) have weak or failed replications.
- Spatial ability sex differences: meta-analytically moderate (d~0.5 for mental rotation), but dramatically reducible by training or cultural factors.

### Agreements Between Sources

Even deeply opposing voices in this debate agree on some things:
- Some psychological sex differences exist and are real, not artifacts.
- The brain is not "asexual" — sex hormones and chromosomes influence neural development.
- Social and cultural context shapes sex-differentiated behavior substantially.
- Evolutionary psychology as a meta-theory is not inherently political or normative.

### Conflicts Between Sources

- **Magnitude and import**: Hyde vs. Buss/Schmitt debate is largely about how large and how consequential the differences are. Hyde says "mostly small"; Buss says "large and theoretically important in specific domains."
- **Causal attribution**: Is women's preference for resourceful mates an evolved adaptation or a response to economic dependence? Eagly & Wood say the latter; Buss says the former. The data cannot definitively distinguish them (both predict the same cross-cultural pattern; the question is mechanism).
- **Gender equality paradox**: Some sources treat this as strong evidence for innate differences (Stoet, Geary, Mac Giolla, Defant); critics (Richardson et al.) argue the original finding was methodologically flawed and alternative interpretations exist.
- **Mosaic vs. dimorphism**: Joel et al. argue brains are mosaics with massive overlap; critics argue that reliable group-level differences in specific brain features still exist.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

1. **Behavior genetics of sex differences**: Twin and adoption studies could in principle estimate heritability of sex-differentiated traits. This literature is thin relative to behavioral studies. What heritability evidence exists is rarely tied to specific evo-psych claims.

2. **Developing-world populations**: Much evo-psych research uses WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) samples. Cross-cultural replication is improving but uneven. The mate preference studies (Walter et al.) are an exception.

3. **Interaction effects**: How do biology and culture interact to produce sex differences? The biosocial approach (Eagly & Wood) theorizes this but empirical tests of the interaction mechanism are limited.

4. **Non-binary and intersex populations**: If sex differences are strongly biologically determined, intersex individuals and those with sex-atypical hormone profiles should show predictable intermediate profiles. This literature is thin and politically contentious.

5. **The normative argument made explicit**: There is surprisingly little serious philosophical work that tries to reconstruct the best possible naturalistic argument for patriarchal norms and then evaluate it rigorously. Most philosophical literature focuses on debunking weak versions.

### Surprises

1. **Buss explicitly disowns the normative argument.** His 2011 paper with Schmitt is quite clear that evo-psych has "no political agenda" and that evolved differences do not justify anything normatively. The aggressive public use of evo-psych for political normalization (Peterson, Kanazawa, Defant) is not well-supported by what the leading scientists in the field actually say.

2. **The replication crisis has hit evo-psych specifically hard in the areas most important to patriarchal arguments.** The most contested/unreliable findings (menstrual cycle effects, priming studies of masculine behavior, hormone-behavior links in naturalistic settings) are exactly the ones that get deployed most aggressively in popular culture arguments about "natural" gender roles.

3. **The Mosuo data (Mattison et al. 2020, PNAS) shows that biological health outcomes often attributed to female sex — higher inflammation, cardiovascular risk from stress — reverse in matrilineal societies.** This is a rare natural experiment showing that what looks like a sex difference in health is partly a structure difference.

4. **The gender equality paradox (larger sex differences in more egalitarian societies) is empirically real in some domains** but its interpretation is contested. It is frequently used as the trump card in the evo-psych-for-patriarchy argument, but: (a) the original STEM paper had errors; (b) the mechanism is genuinely unclear; (c) even if innate preferences are expressed more freely in egalitarian societies, this does not make the preferences immutable or socially unmodifiable.

5. **The lobster argument is emblematic.** Peterson's argument is not a minor metaphor — it is structurally representative of how popular evo-psych arguments work: find one biological example, claim deep evolutionary homology, generalize to human social organization. The methodological problem (cherry-picking from an enormous diversity of biological social structures) applies to many evo-psych claims, not just this one.

---

## Overall Assessment

Evolutionary psychology does not provide credible scientific grounding for patriarchal norms. The claim requires clearing three bars:

**Bar 1: Do the biological sex differences actually exist and are they robustly measured?** Partially yes. Some differences (mating preferences, physical aggression) are real and replicated. Most cognitive differences are small relative to within-group variation. Brain dimorphism is real at the level of group averages but does not produce discrete male/female types. The replication crisis has damaged some of the most normatively loaded claims (especially about hormonally-driven behavior cycles).

**Bar 2: Do these differences translate into the specific psychological profiles that would make patriarchal social arrangements "natural"?** Mostly no. The argument that dominance hierarchies reflect evolved male psychology is the central claim, but: (a) male dominance hierarchies across species are diverse and not universal; (b) dominance and prestige are distinct pathways to high status in humans, and prestige-based status is not gendered in the same way; (c) the magnitude of psychological differences in "dominance-seeking" is smaller than popular accounts suggest; (d) cross-cultural variation in gendered dominance is enormous.

**Bar 3: Even if sex differences exist, do they justify patriarchal norms?** No. This is the is/ought gap. No serious evolutionary psychologist defending evo-psych as a science makes this argument explicitly. The move from "natural" to "good" or "inevitable" is never defended; it is assumed. The closest the literature comes is the immutability claim (Fine's critique of "neurosexism" is partly that these claims foreclose possibilities by treating current differences as fixed), but even Buss & Schmitt explicitly reject behavioral determinism.

The most honest summary: evo-psych describes some real patterns in human sex differences, often inflated in popular culture; those patterns are causally complex (nature-culture interaction all the way down); they do not translate into either a mandate for or an inevitability of patriarchal social arrangements; and anyone who argues they do is smuggling normative commitments in under the cover of natural science.
