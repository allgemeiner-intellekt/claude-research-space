# Deep Dive: The Neo-Aristotelian Challenge to the Naturalistic Fallacy

## Question

Does the neo-Aristotelian philosophical tradition (Foot's *Natural Goodness*, MacIntyre's *Dependent Rational Animals*, Hursthouse's *On Virtue Ethics*) actually rescue patriarchal conclusions from the is/ought objection? Does grounding normativity in the human "life form" or natural teleology — rather than in simple biological facts — actually support fixed gendered social roles? Or does the framework, examined carefully, undermine rather than support patriarchy?

---

## Investigation

### The Problem Being Addressed

The main essay's argument against patriarchal appeals to nature relies heavily on the naturalistic fallacy: from the fact that something is natural or biologically typical, nothing normative follows directly. This is a good objection to crude biological determinism. But it faces a more sophisticated counter from neo-Aristotelian virtue ethics, which explicitly claims to derive normative conclusions from facts about a species' characteristic form of life — and to do so in a way that *is not* a simple is/ought error.

The question is whether this more sophisticated naturalism actually rescues patriarchal arguments, or whether it too collapses when pressed.

---

### Thread 1: Foot's Argument in *Natural Goodness* (2001)

Foot's central thesis is stated at the outset: "evaluations of human will and action share a conceptual structure with evaluations of characteristics and operations of other living things, and can only be understood in these terms. I want to show moral evil as 'a kind of natural defect.'" (Natural Goodness, p. 5)

The **logical structure** of her argument runs roughly as follows:

1. For any living thing, we can distinguish good from defective specimens by reference to what Foot calls "Aristotelian categoricals" — generalizations about what members of the species *characteristically* do and have. ("The wolf hunts in a pack," "the bee signals the location of food.")

2. These categoricals are not merely descriptive statistics. They pick out the life form of a species — what a member of that species needs to function well as *that kind of thing*.

3. A particular wolf that refuses to hunt, or a bee that finds nectar but doesn't signal, is *defective* — not just statistically unusual, but failing to be what its life form demands.

4. The same logic applies to humans, with the crucial modification that humans are *rational* animals. Our life form includes not just biological requirements (nutrition, immune function, reproduction) but the distinctively human capacities for language, practical reason, and social cooperation.

5. Therefore, evaluative claims about human beings — including moral ones — can be grounded in facts about the human life form, not in mere subjective preferences or arbitrary social conventions.

The key move is the replacement of a simple biological criterion with a life-form criterion. Foot explicitly says that "lack of capacity to reproduce is a defect in a human being. But choice of childlessness and even celibacy is not therefore shown to be defective choice, because human good is not the same as animal good." (p. 42) This is crucial: she builds a logical firewall between the biological baseline and the normative conclusion *precisely because* humans are rational.

**The is/ought bridge she offers**: It is not that "X is natural, therefore X is good." It is rather that "what it means for any organism to be *good* — excellent, non-defective — is determined by its life form, and for humans, this life form essentially includes rationality, language, and social cooperation." The evaluative claim is *constitutive* of what it is to be a human in good condition, not inferred from a separate premise about nature's authority.

**The problem this creates for patriarchy**: Notice the implication. If rationality and sociality are what distinguish the *human* life form from a merely biological one, then the normative baseline for human goodness is anchored in capacities that both men and women share. Foot explicitly treats "men and women" symmetrically when discussing human needs: "Men and women need to be industrious and tenacious of purpose not only so as to be able to house, clothe, and feed themselves, but also to pursue human ends having to do with love and friendship." (p. 44) There is no asymmetry in the life form on which her normativity depends.

**The open-question residue**: Critics (including philosopher Ari Armstrong in a careful reading of the book) argue that Foot never fully closes the loop. She establishes that *the framework* generates evaluative standards from life-form descriptions; but she does not show *why* humans should act in accordance with those standards rather than against them when their desires conflict. This is the residual is/ought gap that critics identify. However, this objection — while philosophically serious — cuts *against* using the framework to support patriarchy as much as it cuts against anything else Foot claims. If the framework is incomplete, it cannot rescue patriarchal conclusions either.

### Thread 2: MacIntyre's *Dependent Rational Animals* (1999)

MacIntyre's book is a significant departure from the purely rationalist Aristotelianism of his earlier *After Virtue* (1984). The central thesis is that human beings are essentially vulnerable, dependent animals — continuous with other animals in our embodied fragility — and that this vulnerability is not a philosophical embarrassment to be minimized but the very source of our distinctively human social and moral requirements.

**The argument about vulnerability and virtue**: MacIntyre argues that because we are vulnerable (as infants, in illness, in old age, in intellectual limitation), we are inescapably dependent on networks of giving and receiving care. This dependency is not contingent on particular social arrangements; it is characteristic of the human life form. From this he derives a suite of virtues that he calls "virtues of acknowledged dependence": justice (in the network of mutual care), generosity, and a kind of practical wisdom oriented toward the goods of others.

**What MacIntyre does NOT say**: He does not draw patriarchal conclusions. He does not argue that men's relative independence from certain forms of bodily vulnerability entitles them to authority over women. He does not gender the virtues of acknowledged dependence — there is no argument that women are more suited to care-giving roles and men to governing roles. The virtues he derives — justice, practical wisdom, generosity — are virtues required of all humans *because* all humans are vulnerable and all humans participate in networks of mutual support.

The dependency framework actually cuts against patriarchal structures, which characteristically concentrate the labor of care (including physical caregiving) on women while excluding them from public recognition and authority. A MacIntyrean analysis would flag this as a failure in the networks of giving and receiving that constitute human social flourishing — a structural injustice, not a natural arrangement.

**The Okin objection**: Feminist philosopher Susan Moller Okin criticized MacIntyre's *After Virtue* (1984) for using "falsely gender-neutral language" to disguise that the tradition cannot apply to women, and for reinforcing suspect patriarchal norms. Karen Stohr's survey of feminist virtue ethics (Routledge Companion, 2015) documents this debate: Annette Baier initially praised MacIntyre's openness to women's experience but modified her praise after Okin's critique; Okin specifically argued that virtues are embedded in a patriarchal framework and that MacIntyre's emphasis on community and tradition risks legitimizing those structures. This is a genuine criticism of MacIntyre's tendency to appeal to historical communities without sufficient scrutiny of the gender structures embedded in those communities.

However — and this is important for the essay — Okin's critique targets *After Virtue*'s communitarianism, not the argument from dependence in *Dependent Rational Animals*. The 1999 book is a substantive departure precisely because MacIntyre takes human vulnerability and embodiment seriously, which forces attention to those most burdened by vulnerability (women, the disabled, the elderly) rather than allowing the theory to float free into an idealized polis.

### Thread 3: Does Neo-Aristotelian Naturalism Support Fixed Gendered Social Roles?

Here we need to ask the crucial empirical-normative question: even *if* we accept Foot's life-form argument as philosophically sound, what does it actually say about gender roles?

**The "two sub-species" issue**: A patriarchal appropriation of Foot might argue: human males and females have different reproductive roles and therefore different "life forms" in the relevant biological sense; therefore their normative standards differ; therefore gender-differentiated roles follow. This is the analogical move from, say, the male deer (which grows antlers and fights for mates) to human men (who should lead and provide) versus human women (who should bear and nurture).

This move fails for a specific reason internal to Foot's framework, not just because it contradicts feminist sensibilities. Foot's account of *the distinctively human* good is precisely that it cannot be read off from the biological baseline alone. She explicitly says childlessness is not a defect in humans the way it would be in rabbits — *because* rationality transforms what human goodness consists in. If the normative standard were simply the biological, Foot's argument would add nothing to plain biological determinism. The whole point of the framework is that the human life form includes reason and sociality as constitutive features — and these capacities belong equally to both sexes.

**The sub-species move is also biologically ill-founded**: Even if one tried to run the argument from biological sex dimorphism, the evidence (extensively covered in the evo-psych survey) shows that the psychological and cognitive differences between sexes are small in magnitude, massively overlapping in distribution, substantially plastic in response to social environments, and not oriented toward the kind of complementary specialization (one sex rational/political, the other emotional/domestic) that the patriarchal argument requires. Foot's framework grounds normativity in the characteristic life form of the species as a whole. Human males and females are not separate species with separate life forms — they are members of one species whose characteristic life form is precisely defined by what they share: reason, language, social cooperation, affective capacity.

**The rationality pivot**: Here is the decisive point. In neo-Aristotelian naturalism, the specifically human life form is constituted by practical reason — the capacity to deliberate about ends, not just pursue them instinctively. This is what separates the human case from the bee or the wolf, and it is why Foot says celibacy is not a defect even though non-reproduction would be a defect in a merely biological creature. If practical reason is what makes humans distinctively human, and if women possess practical reason as fully as men (which Aristotle himself denied, but which is empirically indefensible to deny today), then the framework generates the same normative demands for both sexes: develop the virtues that enable rational animals in community to flourish.

This is precisely why Okin and others can argue that Aristotle's original sexism was not a feature of his *virtue ethics framework* but a factual error (about women's rational capacities) that can be corrected without abandoning the framework.

### Thread 4: Feminist Engagements with Neo-Aristotelian Naturalism

**Hursthouse's position**: Rosalind Hursthouse's *On Virtue Ethics* (1999) extends and develops the Foot/MacIntyre framework. Her treatment of the "four ends" (biological life and reproduction, individual characteristic pleasures, the life of the social group, reason) as the natural standard against which virtue is assessed does involve acknowledging biological facts including sex differences. However, she explicitly does not use this to generate gender-differentiated virtue requirements. The virtues she identifies as central — practical wisdom (phronesis), courage, justice, temperance — are universal human excellences not sex-differentiated ones. She acknowledges biological sex differences exist while treating them as providing context for the exercise of universal virtues, not as generating separate normative standards for men and women.

**Nussbaum's capabilities approach**: Martha Nussbaum is the most explicit feminist engagement with neo-Aristotelian naturalism, and her intervention is decisive. In *Women and Human Development* (2000) and *Sex and Social Justice* (1999), she develops a version of Aristotelian essentialism that explicitly rejects biological determinism as a basis for gender inequality. Her argument:

1. Aristotle is right that evaluating human lives requires asking what human beings can characteristically *do and be* — not just what preferences they contingently happen to have.
2. But the relevant standard is a list of *capabilities* (the ability to live a full lifespan, to have good health, to use the senses, to experience emotions, to exercise practical reason, to affiliate with others, to live with concern for other species, to play, to exercise control over one's environment) that apply *regardless of sex*.
3. The purpose of the framework is to identify where social arrangements systematically deny these capabilities — and patriarchal arrangements deny them to women structurally.

Nussbaum explicitly criticizes essentialisms that attribute fixed, intrinsic qualities to men and women as justifications for hierarchical arrangements. Her capabilities approach uses Aristotelian naturalism *against* patriarchal conclusions, not to support them. In her 1992 paper "Human Functioning and Social Justice," she defends Aristotelian essentialism precisely to show what women are being denied.

**The Stohr survey (2015)**: Karen Stohr's "Feminist Virtue Ethics" (Routledge Companion to Virtue Ethics, 2015) provides an excellent overview of where the intersection stands. Her conclusions:
- Neo-Aristotelian virtue ethics is compatible with feminist aims, despite Aristotle's own sexism.
- The key feminist objection is not to the framework's structure but to the risk that culturally specific conceptions of virtue (which in patriarchal cultures are gender-differentiated) get smuggled in under the guise of natural standards.
- Nussbaum's capabilities approach is identified as the most promising direction for feminist virtue ethics, precisely because it makes the universal standard explicit and uses it to critique rather than justify existing arrangements.
- The "burdened virtues" critique (Lisa Tessman, 2005) — that oppression makes it impossible to fully develop virtues and severs the connection between virtue and flourishing — is flagged as a challenge that cuts against using virtue ethics to justify oppressive structures.

**The Okin-MacIntyre dispute**: Okin's worry is that the communitarianism in MacIntyre's framework leads to idealizing historical communities whose gender arrangements were oppressive. This is a legitimate worry but it is a contingent one about *which* historical communities MacIntyre appeals to, not about the logical structure of neo-Aristotelian naturalism itself. A MacIntyrean who takes the vulnerability argument seriously has no grounds to celebrate communities that outsourced the burdens of vulnerability to women without reciprocal recognition.

### The Central Question: Does the Bridge Help Patriarchy?

Let me now give a direct answer to the question assigned.

**The neo-Aristotelian move does not rescue patriarchal conclusions. And here is why the framework actually undermines patriarchy rather than supporting it:**

1. **Foot's framework grounds normativity in the characteristically human life form, not in biological sex roles.** The whole point of her argument is that human goodness differs from animal goodness precisely because rationality transforms the normative standard. Both sexes share the rational capacities that define the human life form. The framework generates *one* normative standard for humans, not sex-differentiated standards.

2. **MacIntyre's vulnerability argument actively subverts patriarchal arrangements.** If human flourishing requires networks of mutual care and acknowledged dependence, then social arrangements that impose the labor of care on one sex while denying that sex political recognition and self-governance are failures of the very virtues MacIntyre derives. The framework's implications run directly counter to patriarchal social structures.

3. **Nussbaum's feminist neo-Aristotelianism is the most philosophically developed application of the framework to gender.** She uses the framework to *critique* patriarchy, not to support it. The capabilities approach identifies what humans need to flourish and identifies patriarchal arrangements as systematically blocking those capabilities for women.

4. **The "two sub-species" move fails internally.** The argument that sex differences in biology generate sex-differentiated normative standards misunderstands what Foot's framework is doing. It would require treating biological sex as generating separate life forms — but Foot's logic explicitly distinguishes the human normative standard from the merely biological baseline. The move to gendered normativity requires reintroducing the very biologism that Foot's framework was designed to avoid.

5. **The residual is/ought gap in Foot's framework is symmetric.** Critics argue Foot never fully closes the gap between life-form descriptions and normative demands. This objection, if correct, applies equally to all her normative conclusions — you cannot use the incomplete framework to derive patriarchal conclusions while disabling it against feminist ones.

---

### Is There Any Version of the Argument That Helps Patriarchal Conclusions?

There is one narrow possibility worth acknowledging. A neo-Aristotelian could argue:
- The human life form includes differential contributions to reproduction (gestation, lactation are female-specific).
- These differential contributions generate differential social roles as a matter of practical necessity.
- Therefore some degree of gendered division of labor follows from the human life form.

This argument is coherent within a weak version of the neo-Aristotelian framework. But it proves far less than patriarchal ideology requires:

- It might justify some social accommodation of pregnancy and lactation — which is not patriarchal but simply responsive to biological reality.
- It gives no grounds for political authority of men over women, economic dependency of women on men, or the moral subordination of women.
- It says nothing about the vast majority of human life activities that are not connected to reproduction.
- It cannot explain why men should govern or women should obey — it can at most explain why gestation and lactation are female activities.

Arnhart's "Darwinian Natural Right" (already surveyed) makes a related move — attempting to use natural teleology to defend complementary sex roles. The philosophical arguments survey rightly notes that this argument at most generates conclusions about average tendencies (which are themselves contested empirically) without generating anything about what individuals with different profiles should do, and without generating hierarchical authority rather than mere differentiation.

## Key Findings

- **Foot's logical structure** (strong evidence): The neo-Aristotelian framework grounds normativity in the human life form, which is defined by rationality and sociality rather than by biological sex roles. The framework generates one normative standard for humans, not sex-differentiated ones. Sources: *Natural Goodness* (2001), Gorevan (2008/2009) exegesis.

- **MacIntyre's vulnerability argument** (strong evidence): *Dependent Rational Animals* derives virtues from universal human vulnerability and dependency. These virtues — acknowledged dependence, justice in networks of care, practical wisdom — apply to both sexes, and the argument's logic implies that patriarchal structures that burden one sex with care labor while denying political recognition are failures of the required virtues. Sources: MacIntyre (1999), Bernacchio (2018), Bielskis (2012).

- **Nussbaum as the definitive feminist neo-Aristotelian** (strong evidence): Nussbaum explicitly uses Aristotelian naturalism to argue that patriarchal arrangements deny women the capabilities constitutive of human flourishing. Her capabilities approach is the most developed application of the neo-Aristotelian framework to gender, and it is explicitly anti-patriarchal. Sources: Nussbaum (1992, 2000), Stohr (2015).

- **The "two sub-species" move fails internally** (strong evidence): Applying Foot's framework to generate sex-differentiated normative standards requires treating biological sex as defining separate life forms, which contradicts the framework's key move — that human normativity transcends the merely biological baseline. This move would reduce the framework to the biological determinism it was designed to avoid.

- **The residual is/ought gap** (moderate evidence): Critics (Armstrong, Steele 2020) argue Foot never fully bridges the gap between life-form descriptions and normative demands. This objection, if correct, is symmetric — it cannot be selectively deployed against feminist conclusions while leaving patriarchal ones intact. The framework is either philosophically sound as a bridge (and then it does not rescue patriarchy) or it is not fully sound (and then it cannot rescue any normative conclusions from nature).

- **The Okin-MacIntyre dispute** (moderate evidence): Okin's feminist critique of MacIntyre's communitarianism is a legitimate warning about the risks of romanticizing historical communities with embedded patriarchal norms. However, this critique targets MacIntyre's application and choice of exemplary communities, not the logical structure of neo-Aristotelian naturalism. The vulnerability argument in *Dependent Rational Animals* is relatively immune to this critique.

- **Hursthouse's naturalism does not generate gendered virtues** (moderate evidence): Despite acknowledging biological sex differences as part of the natural context for virtue development, Hursthouse does not derive sex-differentiated normative standards. The four ends she uses to ground ethics generate universal human virtues, not gendered ones. Source: Stohr (2015) survey; Hursthouse (1999).

---

## Assessment

The neo-Aristotelian challenge to the naturalistic fallacy is **philosophically significant but does not rescue patriarchal conclusions**. In fact, on close inspection, it is one of the more powerful arguments *against* patriarchy available within the natural law tradition.

Here is the key insight the essay should use: Foot's framework, MacIntyre's vulnerability argument, and Nussbaum's capabilities approach all converge on the same structural point — that what grounds human normativity is not biological sex but the shared human capacities for reason, language, care, and social cooperation. When these capacities are recognized as the basis for evaluative judgments about humans, the framework generates obligations to *protect and develop* these capacities in all humans, regardless of sex. Patriarchal arrangements that systematically deny women the exercise of practical reason, political agency, and self-governance are, on this framework, not natural arrangements but natural *defects* — failures of the human life form.

The essay can therefore say something stronger than "the naturalistic fallacy defeats all appeals to nature." It can say: "Even the most sophisticated philosophical tradition that explicitly claims to bridge the is/ought gap — neo-Aristotelian naturalism — does not support patriarchal conclusions when its logic is traced carefully. The same framework that its most eminent feminist practitioners (Nussbaum, Hursthouse) deploy to argue for women's equal capabilities has been appropriated by a much cruder biological determinism. The appropriation fails because it depends on a mistake Foot herself explicitly warns against: reading human normativity off the biological baseline rather than off the characteristically human life form, which includes reason and sociality as constitutive features shared by both sexes."

One qualification should be noted honestly: there remains genuine philosophical debate about whether Foot's framework fully closes the is/ought gap or whether it merely relocates it (Armstrong's critique). If the framework is not philosophically sound, then it cannot rescue patriarchal conclusions — but it also cannot generate the anti-patriarchal conclusions Nussbaum draws. The essay's argument against patriarchy does not depend on accepting Foot's framework; the naturalistic fallacy objection stands independently. The neo-Aristotelian tradition is relevant to the essay not because it is a threat that must be neutralized but because its strongest practitioners demonstrate that even sophisticated naturalism does not yield patriarchal conclusions — and that is a point worth making.
