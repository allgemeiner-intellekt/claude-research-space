# Deep Dive: The Cognitive Mechanism of Excerpting as Thinking

## Question

Why does the specific practice of selecting passages, translating them, reordering them, and interpolating commentary generate original theoretical thought? Can we construct a plausible account of the cognitive mechanism from the evidence in the surveys, supplemented by scholarship on early modern note-taking traditions, Luhmann's Zettelkasten, cognitive science on generation and encoding, and translation as intellectual practice?

## Investigation

### The State of the Scholarship

The review agent correctly diagnosed the central problem: the scholarship on Marx's notebooks describes the phenomenon of excerpting becoming theory with precision and documents it across five cases, but does not explain *why* this happens. Musto, Heinrich, Bluhm and Rüdiger, Kangal — all trace the transformation and none theorize it. This is not accidental: the scholars are primarily philologists and historians, working inside the MEGA project's framework, and cognitive mechanism is simply outside their disciplinary brief. The deep dive therefore requires drawing on four distinct bodies of scholarship that the Marx philologists have not themselves connected: the early modern history of note-taking (Ann Blair), Luhmann's self-theorized Zettelkasten (Schmidt via Luhmann), cognitive science on encoding and the generation effect, and the specific case of translation as intellectual practice.

### Ann Blair and the Early Modern Excerpting Tradition

Blair's *Too Much to Know* (Yale UP, 2010) is the definitive study of early modern information management, covering 16th-17th century European scholarly practice. Her 2004 Critical Inquiry article "Note Taking as an Art of Transmission" provides the most accessible theoretical statement of her argument, and her 2010 Intellectual History Review article "The Rise of Note-Taking in Early Modern Europe" (DASH repository, doi:10.1080/17496977.2010.492611) covers the historical context.

The key finding for our purposes is what Blair's account does *not* contain. The early modern scholars Blair studies — Drexel, Erasmus, Sacchini — theorized note-taking almost entirely in terms of memory and retrieval, not generation. Drexel's 1638 *Aurifodina* (The Mine of All Arts and Sciences) advocates excerpting as a defense against memory's unreliability: "human memory is slow, narrow, volatile and unfaithful unless it is strengthened with memory aids." The memory function was explicitly "delegated to paper," as one Chavigny source says, because "too much memorizing can be harmful to the higher intellectual qualities." The key sentence from Blair's 2004 article: "Notes must be rememorated or absorbed in the short-term memory at least enough to be intelligently integrated into an argument; judgment can only be applied to experiences that are present to the mind."

That last clause is crucial but almost parenthetical in Blair: *judgment can only be applied to experiences that are present to the mind*. The early modern theorists of excerpting understood that notes served to make material available to the intellect — but they did not fully theorize what happens when judgment is actually applied. Bacon's two types of note-taking (epitome of a field across multiple books, or epitome of one book) both assume the note is prior to the thought: you excerpt, then you think. What Blair's account does not explain is why the act of excerpting itself — the process of selecting, the process of writing out — appears to generate thought rather than merely storing material for later use.

However, Blair's research reveals one important clue: the commonplace book tradition explicitly valued the *collocation* of excerpts under thematic headings as intellectually productive. Passages gathered under a "locus communis" did not just coexist — their proximity was meant to create connections the original authors had not made. This is structural juxtaposition as a cognitive tool, and it is important for what follows.

Blair does not theorize a cognitive mechanism, but her historical evidence documents that early modern scholars believed excerpting served *both* memory *and* judgment — the two functions were not cleanly separable. The act of selecting what to excerpt already required evaluative judgment; the act of transcribing fixed the passage to memory in a way that made it available to subsequent thought.

### Luhmann's Zettelkasten: A Theorized Comparison Case

Luhmann's card index is the best-theorized example of a writing-as-thinking system, and Luhmann himself theorized it explicitly — which no other note-taker in the tradition does. The primary source is Luhmann's 1981 essay "Kommunikation mit Zettelkästen: Ein Erfahrungsbericht" (Communication with the card index: An experiential report). The best secondary treatment is Johannes F.K. Schmidt's work: "Niklas Luhmann's Card Index: The Fabrication of Serendipity," *Sociologica* 12(1) 2018, doi:10.6092/issn.1971-8853/8350, a revised and shortened version of his 2016 chapter in Cevolini (ed.), *Forgetting Machines* (Brill), pp. 289-311.

Luhmann's self-account is precise: the Zettelkasten functioned as a "communication partner" that generated ideas through unexpected juxtapositions. The mechanism: "Without those cards, just by contemplating, these ideas would have never occurred to me. Of course, my mind is needed to note down the ideas, but they cannot be attributed to it alone." The file is, in Luhmann's phrase, a "surprise generator" — and Schmidt explains exactly how it produces surprises. The structural features enabling this are: (1) a non-hierarchical organization (avoiding "premature systematization") that allows any note to be adjacent to any other via cross-references; (2) a branching numbering system (1/1a, 1/1b, 1/1b1...) that creates "internal ramifications" — new thoughts about a note are inserted *adjacent* to it, not filed under a topic; (3) a "multiple storage" principle that allows any concept to appear in multiple contexts simultaneously; (4) a keyword index that serves as an entry point to a web of cross-references that quickly leads away from the initial search term to "a variety of other subjects that the user initially would not have associated with the first one." The Zettelkasten "makes serendipity possible in a systemically and theoretically informed way." Crucially, Luhmann himself states the fundamental principle: "Underlying the filing technique is the experience that without writing, there is no thinking" (Zettelkasten II, index card no. 9/8g, translated by Schmidt).

Now, the structural comparison with Marx's notebooks. The systems are profoundly different in architecture. Luhmann's system was *atomistic and spatial*: each card was a discrete unit with a fixed address in a non-hierarchical web; cross-references could jump anywhere; the system had no natural reading order and generated ideas through *navigation* of the network. Marx's system was *sequential and temporal*: notebooks read like manuscripts, with a natural order from front to back; commentary was interpolated into the running text; the "structure" was the sequence of reading, not a network. What the two systems share is more interesting than their differences: both treat writing as *constitutive* of thought rather than merely expressive of it. In Luhmann's case, the mechanism of generation is explicit: unexpected cross-connections between distant nodes. In Marx's case, the mechanism of generation is different — it operates through a dialogue structure, not through network navigation.

This is the key structural difference. Luhmann's system generated ideas by *spatial juxtaposition* (a cross-reference forces you to hold two distant ideas in mind simultaneously). Marx's system generated ideas by *temporal accumulation within a dialogue* (the excerpt from a source becomes an interlocutor; the commentary is an argument with that interlocutor; as commentary accumulates, the excerpt recedes and the argument stands on its own). The generative mechanism is not juxtaposition but *refutation and elaboration*: Marx argues with the passages he has extracted, and in arguing, he develops positions the source did not hold.

Schmidt's conclusion is worth noting for the comparison: "it is specifically not (only) the paths that Luhmann tread in his initial readings and note-taking that are constitutive of his filing system but rather the special filing technique and the (selective) relations established between his notes by means of his referencing technique that make it possible to retrieve more in a later query via the pivotal keyword index than what was intended when the notes were initially taken." This maps onto Marx precisely: what the notebooks make available is more than what was put in — and the surplus is theory.

### Cognitive Science: The Generation Effect and Levels of Processing

The cognitive science literature provides the deepest mechanistic account, even though none of it was written with excerpting or note-taking in mind.

The foundational finding is Slamecka and Graf's 1978 paper "The Generation Effect: Delineation of a Phenomenon," *Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Learning & Memory* 4(6): 592-604 (doi:10.1037/0278-7393.4.6.592, 1664 citations). Their core finding: information that subjects *generate* (even partially — e.g., completing a word from a partial cue) is remembered significantly better than information that is simply *read*. The effect is robust and has been replicated across hundreds of studies. The mechanism: generating information requires active processing at a semantic level, which creates richer, more distinctive memory traces.

This connects to Craik and Lockhart's "levels of processing" framework (1972, revised in their 1975 paper "Depth of Processing and the Retention of Words," doi:10.1037/0096-3445.104.3.268, 4001 citations). The core principle: shallow processing (structural features — does a word rhyme?) produces weak memory traces; deep processing (semantic features — does this word fit this sentence?) produces strong memory traces. The 2014 neuroimaging review by Galli ("What Makes Deeply Encoded Items Memorable?", *Frontiers in Psychiatry*, doi:10.3389/fpsyt.2014.00061) confirms this framework at the neural level: deep semantic encoding activates prefrontal cortex and hippocampal regions in ways that shallow encoding does not.

How does this apply to excerpting? The critical move is to recognize that each specific operation in Marx's excerpting practice maps onto a different level of processing:

*Selection* forces semantic evaluation at the highest level. To choose a passage for excerpting, you must assess its significance — you must answer the question "what does this matter?" This is exactly the kind of self-referential semantic processing that the levels-of-processing literature identifies as maximally "deep." You cannot select without understanding; you cannot understand without forming a judgment about relevance; forming a judgment about relevance requires relating the passage to your existing theoretical schema. The selection act is, cognitively, an act of categorization and evaluation — and both require and produce conceptual clarification.

*Translation* is the most powerful operation in Marx's specific practice, and it maps onto what Bjork (1994) has called "desirable difficulty" — the principle that effortful encoding conditions that appear to hurt immediate performance often significantly improve long-term retention and generalization. Translation while excerpting is not a "difficulty" in the sense of an obstacle; it is a re-encoding operation. You cannot translate a passage without understanding it at a syntactic and semantic level simultaneously. More importantly, translation into German from English or French forced Marx to find German conceptual equivalents for English economic concepts — and when no equivalent existed, he had to invent one, or use a German term that imported different connotations. The generation effect applies with full force: the German term Marx wrote was *generated*, not read. And the generation process forced conceptual work that passive copying would not have required.

*Reordering*, particularly in the Ethnological Notebooks case, is a structural interpretation act. Musto (2023, *Inter-Asia Cultural Studies*, doi:10.1080/14649373.2023.2156124) documents this precisely: Marx changed Morgan's chapter order from (1) inventions, (2) government, (3) family, (4) property to (1) inventions, (2) family, (3) property, (4) government, "in order to bring out more clearly the nexus between the last two." This is not an organizational convenience — it is a claim that the causal sequence runs differently from how Morgan presented it. The reordering is a theoretical argument about historical causation expressed through the act of reorganizing material. No commentary is necessary; the reordering *is* the commentary. In cognitive terms, this is structural elaboration: you impose a schema on the material that goes beyond what the material itself contains, and in doing so, you externalize a theoretical commitment.

*Interpolation of commentary* creates what I will call a dialogue structure. The excerpt-plus-commentary format is structurally a dialogue between Marx and the author he is reading: the excerpt speaks, and Marx responds. As Foucault reportedly said to Blair (and she quotes it), copybooks of quotations are "works on the self" — they document the subject's encounter with ideas. But in Marx's case, the encounter is not passive. The commentary *argues back*. And as commentary grows, the excerpt gradually becomes less a quotation and more an occasion for argument. The specific transition — from "Mill says X" to "but this is impossible given Y" to "therefore Z" — is the emergence of theory from the dialogue structure. Cognitively, this is a form of *elaborative interrogation*: you explain why the excerpt says what it says, what is true or false about it, and what follows from that assessment. Elaborative interrogation is one of the most robust predictors of deep encoding and conceptual transfer in the learning sciences literature.

*Problemgeschichtlich synthesis* (Bluhm and Rüdiger's third type of excerpting) is the operation that produces the highest-order cognitive products. Once Marx had assembled excerpts across multiple authors on the same problem — Ricardo's theory of rent, Say's law, the quantity theory of money — the act of reading these excerpts together forced him to notice contradictions, gaps, and dependencies that no single author had identified. This is exactly what the commonplace book tradition called "collocation under a locus communis," but Marx's version was dynamic: as he moved across authors, his understanding of the problem changed, which caused him to re-read earlier excerpts with new eyes. The problemgeschichtlich mode is, cognitively, an extended version of the generation effect: you are constantly generating connections and contradictions across a complex field.

### Translation as an Intellectual Mode: The French Capital Evidence

The French Capital episode (1872-75) is the strongest direct evidence that translation is a generative intellectual practice for Marx. The evidence is documented in Kevin Anderson's 1983 article "The 'Unknown' Marx's Capital, Volume I: The French Edition of 1872-75, 100 Years Later," *Review of Radical Political Economics* 15(4): 71-80, and in more detail in Rodrigo Maiolini Rebello Pinho's article "The Originality of Marx's French Edition of Capital: An Historical Analysis," *IMHO Journal* (2021, doi not recoverable from available sources but available at imhojournal.org).

What the French Capital documents is this: Marx did not translate Capital; he rewrote it. The French edition introduced substantive theoretical changes that Engels chose not to incorporate into the subsequent German editions, which remain the basis of most translations. The changes are not stylistic concessions to the French language (as Engels sometimes claimed). They include: restructuring from 7 sections/25 chapters to 8 sections/33 chapters; a changed title (dropping the subtitle "A Critique of Political Economy" entirely); a substantive reformulation in the Preface, changing "The country that is more developed industrially only shows, to the less developed, the image of its own future" to "The most industrially developed country only shows those that follow it on the industrial ladder the image of their own future" — a change Pinho identifies as shifting Marx's developmental theory from a single-path to a multi-path model; changes to the commodity fetishism section shifting "the fantastic form of appearance of production relations as exchange of things" to "the necessity of that form of appearance" (analyzed by Dunayevskaya 1982); and the deletion of a 40-line passage on formal and real subsumption that Rubel noted. Marx himself wrote in his Afterword to the French edition (28 April 1875) that it "possesses a scientific value independent of the original and should be consulted even by readers familiar with German."

The cognitive implication: translation did not simply transfer existing thought into a new linguistic medium. The constraint of French syntax, and the need to make the argument accessible to French readers unfamiliar with German economic concepts, forced Marx to clarify, simplify, and in some cases fundamentally revise his positions. The translation process was a site of theoretical work precisely because it forced re-encoding at a level of explicitness that German composition, with its capacity for compound nouns and embedded clauses, could evade. The "strait-jacket of modern French" (Engels's hostile phrase) was, from a cognitive standpoint, a desirable difficulty: it forced Marx to articulate what he meant in a more analytically disciplined way than German allowed.

### The Structural Account: A Synthesis

Drawing these threads together, I propose the following account of why Marx's specific excerpting practice generated original theory. The account has three levels.

**Level 1: The Operations Individually**

Each of the five operations in Marx's excerpting practice forces cognitive processing that passive reading does not:

- Selection: forced semantic evaluation (what matters?) — produces conceptual clarity about the problem
- Translation: forced re-encoding across linguistic systems — prevents passive copying and forces explicit understanding
- Reordering: forced structural interpretation (what causes what?) — externalizes theoretical commitments
- Interpolation: forced dialogic engagement (what do I think about this?) — transforms the source into an interlocutor
- Problemgeschichtlich synthesis: forced comparative assessment (how do these authors agree/disagree?) — generates structural insights unavailable from any single source

**Level 2: The Accumulation Effect**

The key to why commentary overtakes extraction is an accumulation dynamic. Each operation above forces a level of cognitive engagement that produces outputs — arguments, objections, alternative formulations — that require more space than the excerpt itself. As Marx wrote commentary in response to an excerpt, the commentary often required further argument, which required referring to other excerpts, which generated further commentary. The dialogue structure is self-amplifying: each response creates the occasion for further responses. Eventually the excerpt serves only as a triggering occasion, and the argument stands on its own. This is the textual equivalent of what cognitive scientists call "knowledge-telling" being replaced by "knowledge-transforming" (Bereiter and Scardamalia, 1987): the initial goal of recording information is displaced by a generative process of constructing new knowledge.

**Level 3: The Medium Effect**

Both Luhmann ("without writing, there is no thinking") and Blair ("judgment can only be applied to experiences that are present to the mind") point at the same phenomenon: written notes function as a cognitive prosthetic that makes thoughts available for further processing. Cognitive science calls this "extended cognition" (Clark and Chalmers, 1998) — the notebook extends the mind's working memory by externalizing information that would otherwise be lost. But for Marx, the notebook was not simply a memory extension; it was a space where thinking *occurred*. The difference is important: a memory extension stores what you already know; a thinking space is where you discover what you think. The dialogue structure of the excerpt-commentary format transforms the notebook from storage into a site of intellectual production.

### The Distinctive Feature of Marx's Method Compared to Luhmann's

The key difference between Marx's sequential notebooks and Luhmann's Zettelkasten is not one of efficiency or scale but of generative mechanism.

Luhmann's system generates ideas through *spatial juxtaposition* of distant nodes via cross-references. The surprise comes from the unexpected connection between two ideas that happen to be linked in the network. The productive operation is *navigation*: moving from one card to another reveals connections that neither card contains alone.

Marx's system generates ideas through *temporal accumulation in dialogue*. The productive operation is *engagement*: arguing with a source, refuting it, extending it, contradicting it. The surprise comes not from unexpected connections between pre-existing cards but from the dialectical pressure that arises when you are forced to take a position on everything you read.

This means that Luhmann's system is optimized for *synthesis across a wide field* — it is excellent at revealing that two distant ideas are related, which is the operation most useful for building a comprehensive social theory. Marx's system is optimized for *depth on a single problem* — it is excellent at generating increasingly precise theoretical formulations through iterative engagement with a source, which is the operation most useful for political-economic critique. The Grundrisse is the purest product of Marx's system: a set of notebooks that is not yet a book but is already a theory, produced through an escalating dialogue with Ricardo, Mill, and the other political economists.

## Key Findings

- **Ann Blair's account of early modern excerpting** (Blair 2010, Blair 2004) establishes that the tradition theorized note-taking primarily as memory management, not as a generative intellectual practice. The one exception is the implicit claim that collocation of excerpts under thematic headings enabled intellectual connections the original authors had not made — which is a weak form of the structural juxtaposition mechanism that Luhmann made explicit. Confidence: strong evidence for what the tradition *said*; moderate evidence for the implicit cognitive function.

- **Luhmann's Zettelkasten as comparison** (Schmidt 2018, doi:10.6092/issn.1971-8853/8350) provides the best theorized parallel case. The system generated "fabricated serendipity" through cross-reference networks enabling unexpected spatial juxtaposition of distant concepts. Luhmann stated explicitly: "without writing, there is no thinking." The structural difference from Marx: Luhmann's system is a non-hierarchical network enabling synthesis across a wide field; Marx's system is a sequential dialogue enabling depth on a specific problem. Confidence: strong evidence (primary sources via Schmidt, directly quoted).

- **Generation effect and levels of processing** (Slamecka and Graf 1978; Craik and Lockhart 1972) show that information that is *generated* — even partially — is encoded more deeply and retained better than passively read information. Translation while excerpting is a generation operation: the German rendering must be *produced*, not copied. Selection while excerpting is a deep semantic evaluation operation. Both map onto the deepest levels in Craik and Lockhart's hierarchy. Confidence: strong evidence from cognitive psychology for the general mechanism; moderate evidence for the specific application to excerpting (the cognitive science literature does not study excerpting directly).

- **Desirable difficulty** (Bjork 1994) provides a framework for why translation is specifically productive: it imposes the kind of effortful re-encoding that produces durable and transferable understanding. Confidence: moderate (well-established general principle; not directly tested on scholarly translation).

- **French Capital episode** (Anderson 1983; Pinho 2021) documents that Marx's translation of Capital into French was a site of substantive theoretical revision, introducing changes to the developmental theory, the commodity fetishism analysis, and the structural organization of the work that were not in the German editions. Marx himself called the French edition a work with "scientific value independent of the original." This is strong direct evidence that translation functioned as theoretical work for Marx, not merely linguistic transfer. Confidence: strong evidence (primary sources documented by multiple scholars).

- **Ethnological Notebooks reordering** (Musto 2023) shows that Marx's reordering of Morgan's chapter sequence from (inventions, government, family, property) to (inventions, family, property, government) was itself a theoretical act, making explicit a causal claim about the relationship between property and government that Morgan had not drawn. Confidence: strong evidence (directly documented from the notebooks).

- **Dialogue structure as generative mechanism**: The excerpt-commentary format creates a self-amplifying argumentative dynamic in which each response to an excerpt generates further argument, gradually displacing the excerpt itself. This is the mechanism by which "commentary overtakes extraction." Confidence: moderate (inferred from the documented cases; not directly tested as a cognitive mechanism).

## Assessment

The cognitive mechanism of Marx's excerpting practice is not mysterious once the individual operations are mapped onto their cognitive functions. The mechanism operates at three levels simultaneously:

First, at the level of individual operations: each act of selecting, translating, reordering, or commenting requires a form of cognitive processing that is deeper — in the Craik-Lockhart sense — than passive reading. The generation effect ensures that information processed through these operations is encoded more richly, and richer encoding means that more of the material is available for subsequent thought.

Second, at the level of dialogue structure: the sequential notebook format, with excerpts and commentary interleaved, creates an ongoing dialogue with the sources in which Marx is compelled to respond to each passage. This dialogue structure is self-amplifying: responses generate further positions, which require further argument, which eventually becomes autonomous. The excerpt becomes an occasion rather than a subject; the notebook becomes a manuscript.

Third, at the level of medium: writing externalizes thought, making it available for further processing in a way that purely mental deliberation cannot. Luhmann put this most precisely: "without writing, there is no thinking." The notebook is not a record of thinking that happened elsewhere; it is a site where thinking occurs.

The comparison with Luhmann clarifies what is distinctive about Marx's method. Both men used writing as a thinking tool; both created systems that generated more than was put into them; both described their systems as "communication partners." But the mechanisms are different. Luhmann's system generates ideas by unexpected spatial juxtaposition across a non-hierarchical network — the productive unit is the cross-reference that reveals a distant connection. Marx's system generates ideas by temporal accumulation in a sequential dialogue — the productive unit is the argument that refutes, extends, or inverts the source passage.

This difference is not incidental. It reflects the difference between the intellectual tasks each man was pursuing. Luhmann was building a comprehensive social theory that needed to hold many different domains in relation to one another — his system needed to be good at finding connections across a wide field. Marx was doing political-economic critique that needed to find the contradictions latent in existing theory and derive consequences from them — his system needed to be good at sustained engagement with a limited number of sources to exhaustion. The system each man built was optimized for his intellectual project, not merely for note-taking in general.

The weakest point in this account is the specification of how exactly the transition from excerpting to theory happens at the textual level — where the dialogue tips from response-to-source to autonomous argument. The survey noted this gap, and the deep dive cannot fully close it without the textual analysis of a specific notebook transition (which is Assignment 2 from the review). The French Capital evidence provides the strongest available proxy: Marx's translation of his own text was a site of revision that altered his theoretical commitments, suggesting that the re-encoding process is productive even when the "source" is one's own prior work. But the exact textual mechanism — what specific passage or contradiction triggers the shift from excerpting to theory — remains incompletely documented.

The overall verdict: the excerpting-as-thinking mechanism is well-supported at the level of individual cognitive operations (strong evidence), at the level of historical comparison (strong evidence from Luhmann and Blair), and at the level of documented outputs (strong evidence from French Capital and Ethnological Notebooks reordering). The specific mechanism of the accumulation dynamic and the dialogue-to-autonomy transition is moderately supported — plausible and consistent with the evidence but not directly demonstrated at the textual level.
