# Deep Dive: The Comments on James Mill as a Case Study in Theory-from-Excerpting

## Question

Can we trace, section by section, how Marx's *Comments on James Mill* (1844) transitioned from excerpting to original theory? Where exactly did commentary begin to overtake extraction, and what triggered it?

## Investigation

### Sources Read

The full text of the *Comments on James Mill* from marxists.org (the Clemens Dutt translation, MECW Vol. 3, pp. 211–228), which preserves the two digressions as presented in MEGA² IV/2; the table of contents of James Mill's *Elements of Political Economy* (marxists.org edition of the 1844 third edition, Chapter 3 in full); the Lixin Han chapter "The Alienation of Intercourse in *Comments on James Mill*: The Turning Point of Marx" (Springer, 2020); the Chinese translation of Jürgen Rojahn's 2002 *Rethinking Marxism* article "The Emergence of a Theory: The Importance of Marx's Notebooks Exemplified by Those from 1844" (NJU, 2012 translation by Zhao Yulan); and the abstract and references of the original Rojahn article (*Rethinking Marxism* 14.4, 2002, pp. 29–46). Secondary orientation was provided by Marcello Musto's "Marx in Paris: Manuscripts and Notebooks of 1844" (*Science and Society* 73.3, 2009).

### What the Marxists.org Edition Presents

The edition note is itself revealing: "Following a lengthy selection of Mill excerpts, Marx suddenly 'veered off' and began developing a larger, tangential thought. After writing his thoughts out, Marx returned to more Mill transcription. Soon, a second digression followed. Upon its completion, Marx finished up his summarizing." The marxists.org version omits most of the opening and all of the closing Mill transcriptions and presents only the two digressions and the Mill material sandwiched between them — which is itself instructive about what later readers found significant. The original notebook material (MEGA² IV/2) starts on page 25 of the fourth notebook and continues into the fifth, meaning we are dealing with a span of at least two physical notebooks.

### Structural Map of the Text as Preserved

By tracking page-reference citations and the shift in register, the text's architecture becomes clear:

**Zone 1 (positions ~0–4600 in the text): Mill excerpts on money as medium of exchange**
Marx is excerpting and lightly summarizing Mill's Chapter 3, Sections VI–VIII of *Elements of Political Economy*, the sections on "Convenience of a Particular Commodity as a Medium of Exchange," "What Regulates the Value of Money," and "What Regulates the Quantity of Money." The excerpts follow Mill's page references closely: p. 93, p. 95, pp. 99–101, p. 101. The register is almost purely expository. Marx is reporting Mill.

**Zone 2 (positions ~4600–6200): First transitional commentary — technical critique of Ricardo**
The first break in pure excerpting comes with a paragraph beginning "In the compensation of money and value of metal, as in his description of the cost of production as the only factor in determining value, Mill commits the mistake — like the school of Ricardo in general — of stating the abstract law without the change or continual supersession of this law." This is still analytical commentary in a technical economic register, not yet theoretical philosophy. Marx is criticizing Mill's methodology: Ricardo-school economics states laws as abstractions while suppressing the movement by which those laws arise and are continually superseded. The key claim here is dialectical but still disciplinary: "The true law of political economy is chance, from whose movement we, the scientific men, isolate certain factors arbitrarily in the form of laws." This is a critique of economic methodology, not yet a theory of alienation.

**Zone 3 (positions ~6200–33700): First major digression — theory of money, credit, and estranged intercourse**
The pivot sentence is precise: "Mill very well expresses the essence of the matter in the form of a concept by characterising money as the medium of exchange." The word "essence" (*Wesen*) is the trigger. Marx takes Mill's technical definition — money as medium of exchange — and asks what it means that the *activity* of mediation has been alienated from man and transferred to an object. This is no longer commentary on Mill. It is an independent philosophical argument that uses Mill's formulation as a launchpad to depart from political economy entirely.

The argument that follows has its own internal logic and moves through several stages that are worth mapping precisely because they show how quickly the trajectory accelerates away from Mill:

(a) The mediating activity — "the human, social act by which man's products mutually complement one another" — has been estranged from man and become the attribute of money, a material thing outside him. This is the core alienation claim: mediation itself is alienated.

(b) The Christ analogy: "Christ represents originally: 1) men before God; 2) God for men; 3) men to man. Similarly, money represents originally, in accordance with the idea of money: 1) private property for private property; 2) society for private property; 3) private property for society." This is a structural analogy between religious and economic alienation that has no warrant in Mill whatsoever. It is pure original theory.

(c) The critique of the monetary system versus its "enlightened" critics: Marx argues that the modern economist who says money is just a commodity like any other has not transcended the monetary superstition but merely substituted a "refined superstition" for a "crude" one. Both share the same root.

(d) The theory of credit as the completion and intensification of alienation: credit does not abolish the estrangement of money; it intensifies it because now "man himself, instead of metal or paper, has become the mediator of exchange, not however as a man, but as the mode of existence of capital and interest." The credit analysis runs for approximately 5,000 words and includes the four-point analysis of how the credit system deepens alienation through moral judgment, hypocrisy, counterfeiting-of-self, and the domination of banking. This is extended original political-philosophical theory with no corresponding Mill passage.

(e) The section on "exchange as species-activity" returns to a higher level of abstraction: "Exchange, both of human activity within production itself and of human product against one another, is equivalent to species-activity and species-spirit." From here Marx develops the theory of estranged social intercourse, the argument that political economy defines "the estranged form of social intercourse as the essential and original form corresponding to man's nature."

(f) The theory of alienated private property: a systematic account of how exchange under conditions of private property necessarily alienates and fragments human social being, culminating in the claim that "through the mutual exchange of the products of human activity" — appearing as barter and trade — "the mutual completion and exchange of the activity itself appears as division of labour, which turns man as far as possible into an abstract being, a machine tool."

The first digression ends abruptly at around position 33,700 with a schematic note about "the separation of work from itself — separation of the worker from the capitalist — separation of labour and capital" followed by a brief schema of the forms of estrangement in production, distribution, exchange, and consumption.

**Zone 4 (positions ~33,700–42,200): Return to Mill excerpts — Sections IX–XVI of Chapter 3 (money, bills of exchange, paper money) and Chapter 4 Section I–III (consumption)**
Mill's page references resume: pp. 101, 102; Sections IX, X, XI, XII, XIII, XVI. These are summaries of Mill's discussion of government money policy, bills of exchange, paper money, and the relation between nations as merchants. Then Chapter 4, Sections I, II, III, on productive and unproductive consumption, the annual consumption = production identity, and Say's Law on the equivalence of demand and supply. The register is again extractive. Marx is back to summarizing Mill, though in a condensed form.

**Zone 5 (positions ~42,200–53,500): Second major digression — theory of exchange, alienated intercourse, and the vision of non-alienated production**
The trigger is Mill's Section III of Consumption, which Marx summarizes and then immediately follows with: "With his customary cynical acumen and clarity, Mill here analyses exchange on the basis of private property." The phrase "customary cynical acumen" is the hinge. Marx has been excerpting Mill's analysis of why production necessarily involves exchange and demand; Mill's argument, given with "clarity," is that man produces only in order to have, that demand is always equivalent to supply. Marx now takes this as a definition to be reversed.

The second digression is the most philosophically complete section of the entire text. It proceeds:

(a) "Man produces only in order to have — this is the basic presupposition of private property." Marx takes Mill's description and treats it as the axiom of an entire social ontology rather than as an innocent economic observation.

(b) A long reconstruction of why, under private property, exchange cannot be mutual recognition. The register shifts to second person — "you" and "I" — as Marx stages an imaginary dialogue between two commodity owners: "I have produced for myself and not for you, just as you have produced for yourself and not for me." The commodity relation is shown to reduce every apparent social act to mutual plundering and deception: "The social relation in which I stand to you, my labour for your need, is therefore also a mere semblance."

(c) The famous passage on alienated language: "The only intelligible language in which we converse with one another consists of our objects in their relation to each other. We would not understand a human language and it would remain without effect."

(d) The counterfactual — what production would look like if "we had carried out production as human beings": four numbered points on objectified individuality, conscious satisfaction of human need, species-mediating activity, and reciprocal confirmation of human nature. "Our products would be so many mirrors in which we saw reflected our essential nature." This utopian positive vision has no basis in anything Mill says; it is Marx's original contribution, the affirmative complement to all the critical analysis that preceded it.

(e) The final comparative schema: "My work would be a free manifestation of life, hence an enjoyment of life. Presupposing private property, my work is an alienation of life, for I work in order to live, in order to obtain for myself the means of life. My work is not my life."

**Zone 6 (after position ~54,800): Return to Mill excerpts and closing note**
The edition note explains: "The rest of the conspectus contains further excerpts from Mill's book. After the excerpts dealing with the question of land rent, capital profit and wages as sources of taxation and state revenues, Marx wrote" a brief note on Mill and Ricardo on land rent as the basis for taxation. The closing returns to excerpting and the land-rent question; the text ends on a minor economic point.

### Where the Shift Happens

The shift is not gradual in a smooth sense, but it is also not a single rupture. There are two distinct moments, each triggered by a specific concept in Mill.

The first trigger is Mill's concept of money as "medium of exchange." When Marx hits this term he does not critique the concept — he extracts the philosophical consequence the concept implies but does not acknowledge: that if money is the mediator of human social exchange, then the mediation of human beings by one another has been exteriorized into a thing. Mill names the function without seeing what it means about the human beings involved. Marx sees the structure behind the function.

The second trigger is Mill's account of production-for-exchange, the passage where Mill explains, with admirable clarity, that each producer produces not for himself but for exchange — that "his demand is exactly equal to the amount of what he has produced and does not mean to consume." Here Marx again extracts the human meaning of an economic description. Mill has articulated the basic presupposition of private property society — production-for-having rather than production-for-being — without understanding it as a presupposition, as something chosen and thus potentially unchosen.

In both cases the mechanism is the same: Mill's technical precision creates a concept whose philosophical content Mill cannot see. Marx reads the concept philosophically.

### Physical Marks and Textual Distinction

The marxists.org edition does not systematically preserve any physical notation scheme distinguishing excerpts from commentary (the MEGA² critical apparatus would contain this). However, the textual markers are clear from register alone. Mill quotes are presented in quotation marks with page references. Marx's own commentary has no quotation marks and no page references. The first commentary paragraph begins mid-passage without any heading or transitional marker — it simply begins "In the compensation of money and value of metal..." immediately following a quoted passage about cost of production determining value. This abruptness is itself telling: the commentary does not announce itself. It erupts.

### The Rojahn Evidence on Sequence and Emergence

Rojahn's 2002 article argues that the 1844 Paris notebooks should not be read as raw material preliminary to the *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts* but as an integral part of the thinking process itself. In the Chinese translation accessed, Rojahn makes the important philological point that the Soviet editors' claim — that Marx wrote the EPM *after* reading and compiling extracts from a large corpus — is wrong; the actual sequence was an "alternation of writing and extracting," with excerpts punctuating the entire Parisian period. The Han (2020) chapter confirms the scholarly consensus that the writing sequence of the *Paris Manuscripts* as a whole was: First Manuscript → *Comments on James Mill* → Second Manuscript → Third Manuscript. This means the *Comments* were written not as a preliminary or supplementary text but at an intermediate point in the theoretical development — after the estranged labour theory of the First Manuscript but before the more synthetic treatments of the Second and Third.

This sequence matters for interpreting the digressions. The first digression's theory of money and credit as alienated mediation represents something Marx had not worked out in the First Manuscript: a theory of exchange-alienation (as opposed to production-alienation). The second digression's positive vision of non-alienated production is the fullest elaboration of that counter-image in all the 1844 writings. Both are made possible by the specific content of Mill's text — Mill's Chapter 3 on money and Chapter 4 on consumption are the precise chapters that prompted these developments, which is why the Mill notebook is unique among the Paris notebooks in generating such extended original writing.

### The Structure of Mill's Triggering Chapters

Mill's *Elements of Political Economy* Chapter 3, Section VI is titled "Convenience of a Particular Commodity as a Medium of Exchange." It defines the medium of exchange in the language Marx excerpted: "a medium of exchange is some one commodity which, in order to effect an exchange between two other commodities, is first received in exchange for the one, and is then given in exchange for the other." The formal clarity of this definition is exactly what triggers Marx's first digression. Mill defines the mediating function in abstraction from the human beings performing and experiencing it. Chapter 4, Section III, "Consumption is Co-Extensive with Production," defines demand as always equivalent to supply because every producer's capacity to demand is exactly equal to what he has produced and does not himself consume. This is Say's Law stated with characteristic Mill economism. Marx reads this as the philosophical definition of a world in which human beings relate to one another only through their products, never directly as persons.

## Key Findings

- The *Comments on James Mill* has a clear two-digression structure: a first digression (~27,500 words in the text) on money, credit, and estranged mediation, triggered by Mill's definition of money as "medium of exchange"; a return to excerpting; then a second digression (~11,000 words) on the nature of alienated exchange and the positive vision of non-alienated production, triggered by Mill's Section III on Say's Law. (Strong evidence — read directly from primary text.)

- The trigger mechanism in both cases is the same: Marx reads a technically precise economic formulation and extracts the philosophical content the formulation contains but its author cannot see. The shift from excerpting to theory is not triggered by disagreement with Mill but by an act of interpretive excess: seeing what Mill's own language implies. (Strong evidence — traceable in the text itself.)

- The first digression begins not with a headline or transition but mid-paragraph, immediately after a Mill quote about cost of production. The commentary simply erupts from the excerpt without announcement. The second digression is slightly more marked — Marx uses the evaluative phrase "With his customary cynical acumen and clarity" before shifting into his own voice — suggesting increasing self-consciousness about the digressive move. (Moderate evidence — textual analysis of the edition we have, which may not fully reproduce the physical notebook's layout.)

- Philological scholarship (Rojahn 2002, Han 2020) places the *Comments on James Mill* in the middle of the 1844 writing sequence, after the First Manuscript and before the Second. This means the two digressions represent theoretical advances — the theory of exchange-alienation and the positive vision of unalienated production — that Marx had not yet worked out elsewhere. The *Comments* is not a supplement to the EPM; it is part of its genesis. (Strong evidence — Rojahn's philological analysis, confirmed by Han.)

- The marxists.org edition editorially omits the bulk of the opening and closing Mill excerpts, presenting only the two digressions and the intervening Mill material. This means most readers of the *Comments* read a text that has been pre-selected for its theoretical content and never see how immersed in conventional excerpting the text mostly is. The "veering off" is thus structurally hidden in most editions. (Strong evidence — explicit in the edition note.)

- The Christ/money analogy ("Christ represents originally: 1) men before God; 2) God for men; 3) men to man") and the second-person address in the second digression ("I have produced for myself and not for you") are formal innovations — rhetorical strategies not found in conventional excerpting practice or in Mill — that mark the full transition into original writing. (Strong evidence — textual analysis.)

- The closing formal schema "My work would be a free manifestation of life / Presupposing private property, my work is an alienation of life" is the most condensed articulation of Marx's 1844 theory of alienated labour anywhere in the 1844 texts — more condensed than the First Manuscript's "Estranged Labour" section. It was generated in a notebook. (Strong evidence — direct reading.)

## Assessment

The *Comments on James Mill* is the clearest case in Marx's entire output of theory emerging from excerpt-work because the architecture is visible: excerpt, eruption, return to excerpt, second eruption, return to excerpt. The text did not begin as a theoretical essay that got filled in with Mill material; nor did it begin as pure note-taking that happened to generate some marginal reflections. The theory is produced *by* the excerpting, in the precise sense that Mill's formulations contain latent philosophical commitments that Marx's excerpting practice causes him to surface.

The first trigger — "medium of exchange" — works because Marx's prior reading in Feuerbach and Hegel had given him a category (species-activity, mediation, externalization) that could be applied to Mill's concept. When Mill says "medium of exchange," Marx hears "alienated mediator of human social intercourse." The Hegelian-Feuerbachian framework acts as a lens on Mill's technical vocabulary. The excerpt practice is what brings the lens into contact with the technical vocabulary: Marx has to write out Mill's sentence to process it, and writing it out activates the theoretical frame.

The second trigger — Say's Law applied to the individual producer — works slightly differently. Here Mill's formulation does not just name something that needs a different name; it states, with admirable self-evidence, the operational logic of capitalist production. "Man produces only in order to have" is Mill's neutral description. Marx reads it as the axiom of a mode of being — and because he has spent weeks developing an account of human species-being from which this axiom is a deviation, he can immediately trace what the world looks like when this axiom holds. The result is the long second-person staging of commodity exchange as mutual plundering, and then the counterfactual vision of what production could be.

What does this single case show about Marx's note-taking as a thinking method? Several things. First, excerpting is not passive collection; it is a form of slow reading that forces contact between the text and the reader's prior theoretical frameworks. Writing out Mill's sentence about the medium of exchange takes longer than reading it and creates more opportunity for the sentence to catch on something in Marx's mind. Second, the excerpt notebook is a protected space for theoretical elaboration — Marx does not have to discipline his digressions into the architecture of a publishable argument, so they can run as long as they need to. The first digression runs approximately 27,000 words and develops a full theory of money, credit, and estranged social intercourse with no obligation to be economical. Third, the return to excerpting after each digression suggests that the process is genuinely bidirectional: theory generates questions that send Marx back to the text, and the text generates more theory. This is a spiral, not a sequence. The notebooks are where the spiral is visible.

One important uncertainty remains: the physical layout of the notebook as preserved in MEGA² IV/2. The marxists.org edition does not report whether Marx used any physical marks (vertical lines in the margin, headings, ink changes) to distinguish his commentary from his excerpts. Scholars working with MEGA² note that Marx's notebooks often used spatial layout — indentation, double columns, marginal notations — to signal different registers of writing. Without examining the facsimile pages, we cannot confirm whether the two digressions were flagged in any way before they began, or whether they genuinely erupted without prior marking. This is a genuine open question that only the MEGA² IV/2 apparatus can resolve.
