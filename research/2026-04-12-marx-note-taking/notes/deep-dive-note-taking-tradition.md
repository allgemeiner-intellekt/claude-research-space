# Deep Dive: Marx's Excerpting Practice and the Note-Taking Tradition

## Question

How does Marx's excerpting practice relate to the early modern and 19th-century tradition of scholarly note-taking? Was his method unusual for a 19th-century German scholar, or was it standard academic practice pursued with unusual intensity? What specifically was distinctive about it?

## Investigation

### What I searched and found

I traced five intersecting threads: (1) Ann Blair's scholarship on the early modern excerpting tradition; (2) the commonplace book tradition, especially Locke's 1706 method; (3) Niklas Luhmann's Zettelkasten as a structural comparator; (4) the German scholarly tradition of Exzerpieren as documented by Helmut Zedelmaier and the IZEA research group; and (5) the specific analyses of Marx's practice by Bluhm and Rüdiger and by Kangal.

The primary sources accessed were: Ann Blair, *Too Much to Know* (Yale UP, 2010) via the Page 99 Test blog and the open-access "Reading Strategies for Coping with Information Overload" (JHI 2003) and "Note Taking as an Art of Transmission" (Critical Inquiry 2004); Luhmann's original paper "Communication with Noteboxes" (undated; revised English translation by Manfred Kuehn and Scott Scheper); the IZEA (Halle) research project on "Excerpting, Citing, Plagiarizing" led by Décultot with Zedelmaier; the Bluhm and Rüdiger Soziopolis essay "Exzerpieren als Basis: Marxens Produktionsweise" (2018); the same authors' peer-reviewed article "Von der Philosophie- zur Sozial- & Wirtschaftsgeschichte: Zwei Varianten des Exzerpierens beim jungen Marx" (Berichte zur Wissenschaftsgeschichte 43, 2020); Kangal's "Marx's Bonn Notebooks in Context" (Historical Materialism 2020); and Marx's own 1837 letter to his father (Marxists.org primary source). The special issue *Towards a History of Excerpting in Modernity* (Berichte zur Wissenschaftsgeschichte 43/2, 2020), edited by Décultot, Krämer, and Zedelmaier, and Zedelmaier's monograph *Werkstätten des Wissens zwischen Renaissance und Aufklärung* (2015) provided the scholarly frame.

### The tradition Marx inherited: excerpting from antiquity to the 19th century

The practice of extracting passages from books and storing them in notebooks is among the oldest scholarly technologies in Western intellectual history. Ann Blair's work documents its continuous development from the medieval florilegium through the Renaissance humanist tradition and into the 18th century. The core function was consistent: to cope with more books than any scholar could retain, and to create a portable, accessible store of the best material for future use in one's own compositions. As the IZEA project description states, "Since the Renaissance, European scholars were required to keep excerpt books — collections of reading notes. These excerpt books...served, on the one hand, as a storage space for selected extracts, citations, tropes and ideas. On the other hand, they were used as quarries, from which materials for the production of the writer's own works could be extracted."

The tradition had an explicit pedagogical literature. Key texts included: Jeremias Drexel's *Aurifodina artium et scientiarum omnium* (1638), which distinguished three kinds of notes — lemmata (bibliographic references), adversaria (copied quotations), and historica or exempla (illustrative examples) — and argued that note-taking delegated memory to paper; Francesco Sacchini's *De ratione libros cum profectu legendi libellus* (1614, reprinted through the 18th century); and Vincent Placcius's *De arte excerpendi* (1689). The Göttingen professor Christoph Meiners also produced textbooks on reading and excerpting as academic instruction topics — and the Zedelmaier project specifically mentions a textbook by a Göttingen professor on the subject as evidence that "Lesen, Exzerpieren und Schreiben waren bis zum 18. Jahrhundert Gegenstände des akademischen Unterrichts" (Reading, excerpting, and writing were subjects of academic instruction up through the 18th century). The tradition of *ars excerpendi* as a teachable genre flourished around 1700 but then declined — one of the Forgetting Machines volumes (Brill) documents "the flourishing and disappearance of a pedagogical genre" around that time.

This decline is significant: by the early 19th century, when Marx was studying, excerpting was no longer formally taught as a distinct discipline. The IZEA project explicitly flags the Enlightenment as a turning point: the 18th century was a period in which "on the one hand, the ars excerpendi was sharply criticized as a simple exercise in copying, but on the other, intensive use was made of traditional (and also new) forms of knowledge acquisition." The critique was that excerpting in the classical tradition amounted to passive accumulation and imitation rather than original thought — an attack bound up with the Enlightenment's emphasis on authorial originality.

### The commonplace book tradition: topical organization

The dominant form of the traditional excerpt book was the *commonplace book* (Latin: *loci communes*, German: *Kollektaneenbuch*). Its defining structural feature was topical organization: passages were filed under thematic headings rather than in the order they were read. Locke's famous 1706 method was a refinement of this tradition. His innovation was an indexing system that could span an entire commonplace book: a two-page matrix organized by initial letter and first vowel, allowing the reader to find the correct topical heading for any entry. Locke described the procedure: "When I meet with any thing worth putting into my Common-Place-Book, I presently look for a proper Head." The entry was then written in the body of the book with the heading prominent in the margin. Entries from across different books and different reading sessions thus accumulated under shared topics, building up a cross-temporal, cross-source thematic archive.

The cognitive logic of this system was retrieval and combination: you would consult your commonplace book when composing, and find all your notes on "Justice" or "Memory" or "Commerce" together regardless of where you had originally read them. Robert Darnton's description, quoted in the historyofinformation.com entry on Locke, captures the mental habit well: early modern scholars "broke texts into fragments and assembled them into new patterns by transcribing them in different sections of their notebooks. Then they reread the copies and rearranged the patterns while adding more excerpts. Reading and writing were therefore inseparable activities." The system privileged *topical synthesis* — the moment of use was organized around categories and themes.

What the commonplace book system did not preserve was the *chronological experience of reading* or the *sequential encounter with an argument*. Once filed, an excerpt lost its context in the flow of its source. The system was optimized for retrieval at the point of composition, not for following a line of thought through a text.

### Marx's sequential notebook system: a structural contrast

Marx's system had a fundamentally different architecture. His notebooks were *chronological* and *sequential*: he wrote through each notebook from front to back in reading order, starting with a new notebook when the previous was full, numbering them with Roman numerals, and providing front-matter tables of contents and prospective reading lists. The notebooks captured the intellectual experience of reading through a text — the encounter with arguments, the moments of resistance, the accrual of commentary — rather than atomizing passages into a topical grid.

This is a structural decision of the first importance, and it is not the classical tradition. The commonplace book tradition assumed that the point of excerpting was to build up thematically organized material that would be useful when the scholar came to write. Marx's sequential organization instead preserves the *direction* of thought — the shape of an argument in the source, his own evolving reactions to it. This shifts the cognitive function of the notebooks from *archive for retrieval* toward *laboratory of encounter*.

Bluhm and Rüdiger describe the front-matter apparatus in some detail: Marx reserved special notebooks for "Philosophische Aphorismen," used tables of contents, prospective reading lists, and multiple stages of annotation and reworking. The key sentence in their essay: "Nun ist Exzerpieren zwar zu Marx' Zeit eine tradierte wissenschaftliche Praxis, wird aber an der Universität nicht gelehrt, weshalb er seine eigene Form entwickelt und kultiviert." (Admittedly, excerpting is in Marx's time a traditional scholarly practice, but it is not taught at university, which is why he develops and cultivates his own form.) This is the authoritative scholarly verdict on the question: excerpting was standard practice, but Marx's specific form was self-developed.

### The three-form taxonomy and the multi-stage process

Bluhm and Rüdiger, following Ernst-Theodor Mohl, document three forms of excerpting in Marx's practice: *statarisch* (line-by-line, exhaustive — used for major theoretical interlocutors like Hegel and Adam Smith), *kursorisch* (selective, faster — most common in the economic notebooks), and *problemgeschichtlich* (problem-historical synthesis — a method Marx develops gradually, where excerpting becomes the vehicle for tracing a problem across authors and texts).

The taxonomy of stages is also distinctive: Bluhm and Rüdiger identify at least three: a primary stage of selective extraction from a text; a secondary stage of evaluation, commentary, and annotation (in a different reading); and a tertiary stage of autonomous reworking where the excerpted material becomes the basis for new writing. They write: "man kann bei Marx eine primäre, stets selektive Aneignung des Textes von einer sekundären Ver- und Bearbeitung mit expliziter Bewertung und Kommentaren unterscheiden, von der schließlich die tertiäre, durch eigenständige Verarbeitung und Adaption gekennzeichnete Aneignung abgehoben werden kann." This three-stage process is not described in the classical ars excerpendi literature, which generally treated the excerpt as a product rather than a stage.

The classical literature (Drexel, Sacchini, Placcius) distinguished kinds of excerpts but not stages of processing. For them, the excerpt was a final product — a passage stored under a heading for future use. The idea that the excerpt was a *preparatory stage* that would be reworked, commented upon in a second pass, and then subjected to autonomous rewriting represents a fundamentally different theory of what excerpting does. It makes the notebook a phase in a production process rather than a storage system.

### Luhmann's Zettelkasten as structural comparator

Niklas Luhmann (1927–1998) designed his Zettelkasten system in direct opposition to the commonplace book logic. His original paper "Communication with Noteboxes" explains the core choice: he "decided against the systematic ordering in accordance with topics and sub-topics and chose instead a firm fixed place (Stellordnung)." Each notecard (octavo-size paper, written on one side) received a permanent fixed number. This enabled internal branching (a note 57/12 could be extended by 57/12a, 57/12b, etc.) and unlimited cross-linking. The entire system was designed to produce "unexpected connections" — its value lay precisely in its ability to surprise its user with non-obvious relations between notes made at different times and on different topics.

Luhmann described the system as a "communication partner" and a "second mind": "There may be equally apt ways to reach this goal. The described reduction to a fixed, but merely formal order of placement and the resulting combination of order and disorder is, however, one of these ways." The Zettelkasten could become generative because its organizational logic did not bind it to a prior conceptual scheme: "Every note is only an element which receives its quality only from the network of links and back-links within the system."

The structural contrast with Marx is illuminating along several axes. Marx used *bound notebooks*, Luhmann *loose cards*. Marx's units were *multi-page reading sessions*, Luhmann's were *atomic notes* on single ideas. Marx's organization was *chronological reading order*, Luhmann's was *arbitrary fixed addresses*. Marx's retrieval relied on *front-matter tables of contents*, Luhmann's on *keyword registers and cross-references embedded in the cards themselves*. Marx's system was designed to *process sources*, Luhmann's to *generate unexpected connections*.

Most importantly, they reflect different theories of what thinking requires. Luhmann's system is optimized for lateral connection — finding non-obvious relations across a dispersed system that has grown over decades. Marx's system is optimized for *depth of engagement with an argument* — the sequential notebook preserves the experience of working through a body of thought, accumulating commentary that builds on prior commentary. Luhmann's notes are designed to become autonomous of their source. Marx's notes are designed to remain in dialogue with their source while gradually asserting independence through the layers of annotation.

This is confirmed by the outcome: Luhmann's Zettelkasten generated a famously unexpected range of connections, which he then assembled into books on systems theory. Marx's notebooks generated works that are in direct and explicit dialogue with the sources excerpted — Capital reads like an extended argument with Ricardo, Smith, and the political economists, not like a synthesis assembled from decontextualized atomic notes.

### The Winckelmann comparison: another German case

Décultot's work on Winckelmann provides a closer comparison — a German 18th-century scholar for whom excerpt notebooks were also constitutive of the published work. Winckelmann spent his pre-Rome years filling excerpt books from texts he could not otherwise access, and Décultot demonstrated that what were taken to be his original coinages (including "edle Einfalt und stille Größe") derived from his excerpts. Winckelmann used his notebooks much more like the classical tradition — they were a repository that enabled him to write later — and he claimed to be moving *away* from bookish learning toward direct experience of art. The review in FAZ captures this: his "Originalitätspathos verschleierte seine Verwicklung mit seinen Quellen." The excerpt notebooks were his scholarly infrastructure; he wished they weren't.

Marx had the opposite relationship to his notebooks. He never claimed to have transcended the act of close reading and engagement with sources; he remained a lifelong excerpteur who acknowledged in 1837 that he had "made a habit of the practice of excerpting." The notebooks were not embarrassing preconditions but a deliberate method. He continued them after his major works had established him, filling notebooks on chemistry, mathematics, geology, and ethnology until the final years of his life.

### What was standard and what was distinctive

**Standard for 19th-century German scholars**: Keeping a reading journal or excerpt notebooks was common scholarly practice. Marx was doing something that had been culturally normal since the Renaissance, and that German universities had formally taught through the 18th century (even if they no longer did so in his student days at Berlin and Bonn). The use of notebooks for intellectual work, the practice of excerpting major authors, and even the use of front-matter tables of contents were all part of the scholarly habitus of a well-trained German student of the 1830s-40s.

**Unusual in his time**: The formal tradition of the ars excerpendi had declined by the early 19th century. The Enlightenment critique of excerpting-as-mere-copying had weakened the explicit pedagogical emphasis on systematic excerpting methods. Bluhm and Rüdiger are explicit: it was not taught at university. Marx developed his own system. He was in this sense a practitioner of a tradition that had fallen out of formal instruction, who developed a highly deliberate personal methodology without a pedagogical template.

**Distinctively Marxian**: Several features appear to be genuinely distinctive rather than standard:

1. The *three-form taxonomy* (statarisch/kursorisch/problemgeschichtlich) is not documented in the classical literature. It is a taxonomic self-awareness about reading modes that the classical tradition, which focused on the kind of note taken rather than the mode of reading, did not articulate.

2. The *multi-stage processing* — primary extraction, secondary commentary, tertiary autonomous reworking — is more elaborate than what the classical tradition envisioned. The classical account treated the excerpt as an endpoint; Marx treats it as a beginning.

3. The *commentary overtaking the extraction* pattern. In mature notebooks, Marx's own voice increasingly dominates. Bluhm and Rüdiger observe this throughout the middle period. The classical tradition aimed at preserving the source's language (adversaria meant copied quotations); Marx increasingly treats the source as material for his own argument.

4. The *scale and range* — 200+ notebooks over 45 years, in multiple languages, across philosophy, law, economics, history, ethnology, mathematics, chemistry — exceeds any documented comparator in the tradition.

5. The *language* dimension: the multilingual excerpting, with Marx translating as he reads (translating Aristotle's Rhetoric, making his first English-language economic excerpts from a French translation of Smith, gradually developing his English in the notebooks themselves), is both a record of language acquisition and a record of conceptual appropriation through translation. The translation layer is not mentioned in the classical literature and was not a standard feature of the German excerpting tradition.

6. The *notebooks-as-major-works* phenomenon. The Grundrisse and the manuscripts that became Capital exist as elaborate excerpt-and-commentary documents. The notebooks were not preparatory material that was later transcribed into polished texts; they *were* the primary theoretical texts in many cases. This is an inversion of the classical logic, in which the excerpt book was preparatory material to be consumed in the making of a published book.

## Key Findings

- The practice of keeping reading notebooks and excerpting sources was standard scholarly practice in 19th-century Germany, continuously present since the Renaissance and formerly the subject of dedicated pedagogical literature (ars excerpendi). (Strong evidence: Blair, Zedelmaier, IZEA project.)

- The classical excerpting tradition organized notes *topically* (commonplace book logic: file under a heading). Marx's sequential chronological notebooks represent a structurally different approach that preserves the experience of reading through an argument rather than atomizing passages. (Strong evidence: Blair, Locke primary source, Bluhm-Rüdiger, structural analysis.)

- By Marx's student era (1830s-40s), the ars excerpendi as a formally taught genre had declined. Bluhm and Rüdiger state explicitly that excerpting "was not taught at university" in Marx's time, so "he develops and cultivates his own form." (Strong evidence: Bluhm-Rüdiger 2018.)

- Marx's three-form taxonomy (statarisch/kursorisch/problemgeschichtlich) and three-stage processing (primary extraction, secondary commentary, tertiary autonomous reworking) go beyond anything in the classical ars excerpendi literature, which addressed kinds of notes but not reading modes or processing stages. (Moderate evidence: Bluhm-Rüdiger documented in detail; no classical comparator found.)

- Luhmann's Zettelkasten, the other famous German-language note-taking system, is structurally designed for the *opposite* purpose: lateral unexpected connections rather than depth of engagement with a single source's argument. The comparison clarifies what is distinctive about Marx's system by contrast. (Strong evidence: Luhmann's original paper.)

- Winckelmann provides a 18th-century German near-comparator who also relied heavily on excerpt notebooks, but his relationship to them was apologetic (they were infrastructure he wished to transcend); Marx's was celebratory (they were deliberate method). (Moderate evidence: Décultot 2000/2005.)

- Marx announced his excerpting habit in a letter to his father in November 1837 at age 19 and maintained it for 46 more years. The habit preceded his specific methodological elaborations but was treated as a "habit" from the start, suggesting internalized practice rather than formal instruction. (Strong evidence: Marx letter primary source.)

- The phenomenon of notebooks-as-major-works (Grundrisse, ethnological notebooks) inverts the classical logic in which excerpts were preparatory raw material to be consumed in the making of a polished published text. In Marx's case, the notebooks were often the most theoretically rich documents he produced. (Strong evidence: scholarship on Grundrisse and MEGA2 Section IV.)

## Assessment

Marx's excerpting practice was *standard in genus but distinctive in species and scale*. The genus — reading books, extracting important passages, writing commentary, accumulating notebooks — was unremarkable for a German scholar of his generation. Any well-trained student of the 1830s-40s would have kept reading notes.

What was unusual was the structural choice to organize these notes *sequentially by reading order* rather than *topically by subject*, the development of a *multi-stage processing model* that the classical tradition had not articulated, the *three-form taxonomy* of reading modes, the *commentary-overtaking-extraction* pattern that eventually produced some of his most theoretically significant documents, and the sheer *scale and multilingual range* of the enterprise.

The Luhmann comparison is analytically clarifying rather than historically relevant (they are a century apart), but it isolates what is distinctively Marxian: his system was designed to go deep on a source and accumulate layers of engaged reading over time, not to generate unexpected lateral connections across a vast dispersed archive. The sequential notebook is an instrument of *depth*, not *breadth or lateral surprise*.

The Enlightenment critique of excerpting-as-mere-copying is relevant here. That critique targeted the classical commonplace book tradition's tendency to produce scholars who accumulated without synthesizing. Marx's practice was, in effect, an answer to that critique: he used the excerpt as the starting point of synthesis, not the endpoint of collection. The commentary that gradually overtook the extraction in the mature notebooks represents excerpting consciously transformed into something the Enlightenment critics would have recognized as genuine scholarly work.

The most historically significant and genuinely distinctive thing about Marx's practice may be the least often noted: the notebooks did not *become* his major works — they frequently *were* his major works, in forms he never polished into publishable texts. The classical tradition assumed the excerpt book would be consumed in the making of a book; Marx's notebooks accumulated a theoretical output that exceeded, in density and originality, anything he managed to formally publish.
