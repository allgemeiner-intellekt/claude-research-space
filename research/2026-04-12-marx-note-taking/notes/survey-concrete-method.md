# Survey: Marx's Concrete Note-Taking System — Physical Method and Practice

## Summary

This survey found substantial, usable evidence about Marx's physical note-taking system, drawing primarily from: (1) the IISG Amsterdam archive content list for Marx-Engels Papers (which gives physical descriptions of notebooks), (2) the MEGAdigital editorial guidelines (which describe notation conventions in detail), (3) the editorial introductions to MEGA² Section IV volumes (especially IV/19, translated by Timm Grassmann), (4) Marcello Musto's philological scholarship on specific notebook periods, and (5) Harald Bluhm and Axel Rüdiger's 2018 essay "Exzerpieren als Basis: Marxens Produktionsweise" — the most directly methodologically focused source found. A 2025 Springer chapter specifically titled "Notes on a Crisis: The Exzerpthefte and Marx's Method of Research and Composition" (de Paula et al.) also appeared in searches but was paywalled. The Rüdiger 2020 article "Von der Philosophie- zur Sozial- & Wirtschaftsgeschichte: Zwei Varianten des Exzerpierens beim jungen Marx" (Berichte zur Wissenschaftsgeschichte, 2020) also appeared but was paywalled. The overall picture is fairly detailed on numbering, language practices, and distinction of commentary from excerpts, but thinner on exact physical dimensions and the specific indexing practices within individual notebooks.

## Key Sources

### IISG Amsterdam Archive Content List: Karl Marx / Friedrich Engels Papers

- **Link**: https://search.iisg.amsterdam/Record/ARCH00860/ArchiveContentList
- **Key content**: The IISH (International Institute of Social History) in Amsterdam holds the original Marx-Engels Papers and has published a detailed content list. The Exzerpthefte (excerpt notebooks) are catalogued as B-items (B_1 through approximately B_168). Each entry lists: the notebook's IISG-assigned letter-number (e.g. "IISG-Original B 19"), Marx's own sequential roman numeral designation for the notebook (e.g. "Heft XIX"), the date(s) of composition, the language(s) used, the total number of pages (Seiten, "S"), and an itemized list of sources by page range. The B-section begins with Heft I (1840) and runs through the late notebooks of the 1880s. By the London Notebooks period (1850–53), numbering had reached Heft XLIV–L and beyond. The IISG catalogue directly confirms: (a) that Marx paginated his own notebooks with his own roman numeral "Heft" numbers; (b) that the physical page counts are given (e.g. B_44 = "63 S" = 63 Seiten); (c) that language was frequently mixed (e.g. "deutsch, englisch," "franz., deutsch, englisch"). Crucially, entries B_19 (Heft XIX, 1844, 25 Seiten) and B_24 (Heft XXV, 1844–45, 25 Seiten) both show a **two-column page layout**: "S. 1-25 (linke Spalte): J.B. Say..." and "S. 1-4 (rechte Spalte): Frédéric Skarbek..." This confirms Marx used two vertical columns on some pages — a practice attested specifically in the 1844 Paris notebooks. Some notebooks include a title page ("Umschlag"), a table of contents ("Inhaltsverzeichnis") written at the start, and a list of books to be read ("Verzeichnis der zu exzerpierenden Werke"). The early philosophical notebooks (B_1–13, 1840–42) are partly in a copyist's hand ("Hand des Kopisten") with only some sections in Marx's own hand.
- **Assessment**: Primary archival source. This is the actual finding-aid for the physical manuscripts. It is authoritative for format facts (page counts, languages, notebook numbering). Its limitation is that it does not give physical dimensions (cm) for the notebooks, nor does it describe the writing materials in detail. The description "Umschlag" (cover sheet) confirms that many notebooks had a wrapper/title page. The IISH has digitized many originals and links are provided via MEGAdigital.

### MEGAdigital Editorial Guidelines (Grassmann, Lindenberg, Grabsch)

- **Link**: https://megadigital.bbaw.de/about/intro.xql?id=M0001265
- **Key content**: The MEGAdigital editorial introduction gives precise information about how Marx's notation conventions are handled in the digital edition, which in turn describes what the original manuscripts contain. Key facts: (1) Marx's quoted material from sources is rendered with inverted French quotation marks (» «) in the edition — i.e., "wenn Marx exzerpierten Text in mit » « dargebotenen Anführungszeichen gesetzt hat, handelt es sich in der Regel um ein fast wörtliches Zitat." (2) Annotations and marginalia: "Marx' Unterstreichungen im exzerpierten oder ausgeschnittenen Text werden ebenfalls als Unterstreichung wiedergegeben. Randanstreichungen werden im Edierten Text durch durchgezogene, gepunktete und gekreuzte senkrechte Linien in den Farben, die auch Marx benutzt hat, gekennzeichnet." This confirms Marx used margin markings in multiple colors. (3) Distinction of own commentary: "In den Erläuterungen werden die gegenüber der exzerpierten Quelle eigenen Anteile am Edierten Text von Marx und Engels – in Form von zum Beispiel Kommentaren, Zusätzen, abweichender Begrifflichkeit und Umstellungen – nachgewiesen." The apparatus section explicitly identifies where Marx adds commentary, reorders material, or uses different terminology than his source. (4) Language mixture: the notebooks contain German, English, French, Russian, Italian, Spanish, Dutch, Latin, and Ancient Greek. (5) Marx used various shorthand and abbreviations for words (e.g. "dch" for "durch," "währd" for "während," "Engld" for "England"), which the edition expands. (6) Page beginnings and Marx's own pagination are marked in the edited text. (7) Pasted newspaper clippings appear in some notebooks (rendered with a "yellowed paper" background in the digital edition). (8) The apparatus section "Entstehung und Überlieferung" (Origin and Tradition) for each notebook describes the physical manuscript in detail — this is where physical format information lives in each individual MEGA² volume.
- **Assessment**: Authoritative (official editorial document). Establishes that Marx used quotation marks to distinguish verbatim excerpts; used margin annotations in multiple colors; mixed languages extensively; heavily abbreviated words. The convention of distinguishing his own additions is documented at the apparatus level. This source does not directly describe dimensions but confirms notation practices.

### MEGA² IV/19 Editorial Introduction (Timm Grassmann), translated via GitHub Gist

- **Link**: https://gist.github.com/michaelt/a20705940a00c6154f7d474612414ee9 (English translation by michaelt)
- **Original**: https://megadigital.bbaw.de/exzerpte/intro.xql?id=M0001262 (MEGA² IV/19, 2021)
- **Key content**: This is the most detailed editorial introduction I was able to read fully. It covers the four excerpt notebooks Marx produced from October 1868 to March 1869 (plus a Notizbuch from 1869–71 and three newspaper-clipping notebooks made by his daughter Jenny). Key method details: (1) **Self-titling**: "Marx titled all four excerpt notebooks himself: They are called 'London. 1868,' '1868,' '1869 I Heft,' and 'Heft II. 1869.'" This is direct evidence that Marx personally labeled notebooks by city, year, and sequential number. (2) **Systematic source coverage**: Within a single notebook ("London. 1868"), Marx excerpted from 102 of 104 issues of the 1866/67 volumes of The Economist, and from most issues of the Money Market Review published between May 1866 and December 1867 — suggesting a deliberate, near-comprehensive reading program, not sampling. (3) **Multiple sources in one notebook**: The four notebooks each contain excerpts from multiple sources, and crucially, Marx sometimes worked on two notebooks simultaneously or returned to an earlier notebook for new entries after months or years. (4) **Structured thematic collection within notebooks**: Marx "compiles in an independent structure materials on the movement of the money market from 1866 to 1868 from various columns of the 'Money Market Review' under a total of 158 headings" — this is a within-notebook subject organization, distinct from chronological excerpting. This is close to an internal index. (5) **Mixed use**: A single notebook ("1869 I Heft") contains excerpts from periodicals, then excerpts from Goschen's book on exchange rates, then part of excerpts from a German arithmetic textbook — very different subjects in sequence. (6) **Newspaper clippings by proxy**: Marx commissioned his daughter Jenny to create three notebooks of pasted newspaper clippings on specific themes, which he then used as working material. This shows Marx treated others' labor as part of his own research apparatus.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, scholarly (official MEGA² editorial apparatus, published by De Gruyter). Very strong credibility. Limitation: describes one late period (1868–69) only; does not give physical dimensions.

### Harald Bluhm and Axel Rüdiger, "Exzerpieren als Basis: Marxens Produktionsweise — ein Essay" (2018)

- **Link**: https://www.soziopolis.de/exzerpieren-als-basis.html (published in Soziopolis, October 2018); full paper also at Academia.edu
- **Key content**: This is the most methodologically focused secondary source found. It explicitly theorizes Marx's excerpting as his "mode of (intellectual) production." Key findings: (1) **Seven-aspect analytical framework**: The authors identify seven analytical dimensions of Marx's excerpting: (a) occasion/reason; (b) selection of authors/texts; (c) appropriation techniques (selecting, marking, copying, summarizing, commenting, translating, collecting); (d) form (statarisch vs. kursorisch vs. problemgeschichtlich); (e) scope/intensity; (f) stages of reworking; (g) evaluative placement in disciplinary contexts. (2) **Three forms of excerpting**: Statarisch (line-by-line, used for texts of high importance like Hegel or Smith), kursorisch (selective, faster), and problemgeschichtlich (problem-historical — a thematic synthesis across multiple authors). These map onto different physical approaches in the notebooks. (3) **Two-stage reworking**: Marx routinely made excerpts at a primary stage, then reworked them at a secondary stage. The first Manuscript of the Paris Manuscripts (1844) is itself a secondary reworking of the Smith excerpt notebooks: quotations are rearranged, translated into German, and organized under new headings (Kapital, Grundbesitz, Arbeitseinkommen). (4) **Language progression**: Marx initially used German, French, Ancient Greek, and Latin; from 1844/45 he began working into English. The Manchester Notebooks (1845) start with German translations of English originals, then shift to mixed German-English. From the London Notebooks (1850), the mixed form with comments and condensates in the original language becomes characteristic. Later, Italian, Spanish, and Russian were added. (5) **Seamless transition to commentary**: The authors note "the oft-observed phenomenon of seamless transition into brief comments" — Marx's commentary on his sources typically flows without formal separation from the excerpts. (6) **Lifelong habit**: Marx states in a letter of 1837 (age 19) that he had developed the habit of "making excerpts and scribbling down reflections alongside them." (7) **The Grundrisse as excerpting**: The authors treat the Grundrisse partly as an extension of excerpting practice — it represents "a phase of excerpting followed by rough drafts for self-clarification." (8) **London Notebooks 1850 as threshold**: From the London Notebooks onward, the mixed English-German form in comments and condensates becomes characteristic.
- **Assessment**: Essay-level scholarship (Soziopolis is a peer-reviewed German sociology portal; the full paper is published in academic venues). The authors are historians of political thought at German universities. Very strong on method classification. Limitation: not always specific about physical format of individual notebooks. Does not directly address the two-column layout.

### Axel Rüdiger, "Von der Philosophie- zur Sozial- & Wirtschaftsgeschichte: Zwei Varianten des Exzerpierens beim jungen Marx" (2020)

- **Link**: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/bewi.201900027 (Berichte zur Wissenschaftsgeschichte 43:2, 2020)
- **Key content**: Paywalled; abstract and search results confirm it focuses specifically on two variants of excerpting practice in the young Marx, distinguishing the philosophical from the socioeconomic excerpting styles. The title directly addresses the question of how Marx's method differed across disciplinary domains.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed (Wiley, Berichte zur Wissenschaftsgeschichte). Relevant but inaccessible full text.

### Marcello Musto, "Marx in Paris: Manuscripts and Notebooks of 1844" (Science & Society, 2009)

- **Link**: DOI 10.1521/siso.2009.73.3.386 (Science & Society 73:3, pp. 386–402)
- **Key content**: Musto examines the Paris notebooks philologically. Key finding: the Paris notebook period produced both the Exzerpthefte (excerpt notebooks) and the parallel Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts, and these two streams were intertwined. Crucially, Musto's article reconstructs the chronological order of composition of both manuscripts and notebooks (Table at end). His 2009 abstract specifies he examines "the close connection between the Economic and Philosophical Manuscripts of 1844 and the parallel notebooks of excerpts." The Jürgen Rojahn 2002 article in Rethinking Marxism (cited by Musto) is titled "The Emergence of a Theory: The Importance of Marx's Notebooks Exemplified by Those from 1844" — suggesting Rojahn specifically documents how the Paris notebooks show the theory taking shape physically in the notebook form.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, major journal of Marxist scholarship. Musto has worked directly with the MEGA² manuscripts. Limitation: I could not read the full text; the abstract focuses on intellectual-historical rather than physical-format questions, but Musto's work is known to describe format.

### Marcello Musto, "A Reappraisal of Marx's Ethnological Notebooks" (Inter-Asia Cultural Studies, 2023)

- **Link**: https://marcellomusto.org/marxs-ethnological-notebooks/ (published in Inter-Asia Cultural Studies 24:1, 2023, pp. 117–128)
- **Key content**: Full text accessible. Key method details: (1) The Ethnological Notebooks (1880–81) consist of "a hundred densely packed pages" on Morgan's Ancient Society, plus approximately another hundred sheets on Money, Phear, and Maine — total ~200 pages. (2) Marx uses square brackets to insert his own commentary within excerpted material: for example "It will be a revival, in a higher form (of society)" — where the words in brackets are Marx's. Musto explicitly notes "the words in brackets were added by Marx." (3) Marx reorders Morgan's book structure: Morgan organizes as (1) inventions, (2) government, (3) family, (4) property; Marx changes this to (1) inventions, (2) family, (3) property, (4) government — physically rearranging the structure in his excerpt to highlight what he considers the causal logic. (4) Marx's commentary is often a "crescendo" of sarcasm and critique embedded within the excerpt: "the blockheaded Englishman started not from the gens but from the Patriarch"; "the ass bases everything on private families!" These are not separated from excerpts but interpolated. (5) Footnote 3 explicitly directs readers: "To learn about the way Marx used to work and take notes from the books he used to read see Musto (2020c)" — referring to his Contemporary Sociology 2020 article on MEGA². (6) Citation to the IISH for the Egypt notes: "Karl Marx, IISH Amsterdam, Marx-Engels Papers, B 168, 11-18" — confirming the B-numbering system for the manuscripts.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed. Musto is a leading Marx scholar who has worked with the manuscripts. The footnote confirmation that Marx used square brackets for his own commentary within an excerpt is very important evidence for method.

### Marcello Musto, "New Profiles of Marx after the Marx-Engels-Gesamtausgabe (MEGA²)" (Contemporary Sociology, 2020)

- **Link**: DOI 10.1177/0094306120946339; open access at York University repository
- **Key content**: Musto states: "ever since his time at university, it was his habit to fill notebooks with summaries, quotations and reflections from the works he was reading... the some 200 notebooks bear witness to Marx's 'truly encyclopedic labors,' with excerpts from 1838 to 1882 written in eight languages on a wide range of subjects." He also notes that Engels, after Marx's death, found "piles of quotations in no kind of order, collected simply with a view to future selection" (quoting Engels to Bebel, 1883) — implying that the Capital manuscript drafts were not as organized as the Exzerpthefte. The article's emphasis is on content/significance of MEGA², not physical format.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed (Contemporary Sociology). Strong survey-level source for establishing scope.

### MEGA² IV/19 London Notebooks description: Libcom review and related scholarship

- **Link**: https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/9783050076850-014/html
- **Key content**: Table of contents for MEGA² IV/8 (Marx's London Notebooks, March–June 1851). Shows that within a single MEGA² volume, there are multiple named Hefte: "Heft VII März 1851 (S. 121–286)," "Heft VIII April bis Mitte Mai 1851 (S. 287–470)," "Heft IX Mitte Mai bis Mitte Juni 1851 (S. 471–659)," "Heft X Mitte Juni bis Anfang Juli 1851 (S. 660–755)." ## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The physical and methodological details of Marx's Exzerpthefte are now well-established among a small community of MEGA² scholars, but this knowledge has not widely penetrated Anglophone scholarship, partly because most MEGA² editorial apparatus is in German, and partly because the content of the notebooks draws more scholarly attention than their form. The best sources are: the IISG archive finding-aid (for physical facts); the MEGAdigital editorial guidelines (for notation conventions); the individual MEGA² editorial introductions (for period-specific detail); and Bluhm/Rüdiger 2018 (for systematic methodological analysis).

### What Can Be Said with Confidence

**Notebook numbering and titling**: Marx numbered his Exzerpthefte continuously with Roman numerals (Heft I, II, III...) from around 1840 onward. The IISG archive records show notebooks numbered through at least Heft XCVI (96) and beyond. Within MEGA², the London Notebooks alone run from approximately Heft XLII to Heft LVII or higher. For the later London/money-market notebooks (1868–69), he switched to a date-plus-location format ("London. 1868," "1869 I Heft," "Heft II. 1869"). Each notebook typically begins with a title page (Umschlag) and often an Inhaltsverzeichnis (table of contents) or a "Verzeichnis der zu exzerpierenden Werke" (list of books to be excerpted). This means Marx planned ahead — the opening list of books to read functioned as a kind of prospective index.

**Page layout — the two-column format**: The IISG archive entries for the 1844 Paris notebooks (B_19 = Heft XIX, B_24 = Heft XXV) explicitly show two-column layouts: one source in the left column, another in the right column. This practice appears in notebooks where Marx was reading two closely related texts simultaneously (e.g. Say's Traité in the left column, Skarbek's Théorie in the right). This is a deliberate comparative layout, not random notation. It appears to have been used mainly in the early Paris period; later notebooks seem to move away from this in favor of sequential single-column excerpts.

**Distinction of own commentary from excerpts**: Three mechanisms are confirmed: (1) **Quotation marks**: verbatim or near-verbatim quotes are set off with quotation marks (rendered as » « in MEGA²). (2) **Square brackets**: Marx's own additions within or adjacent to excerpts are placed in square brackets — confirmed by Musto for the Ethnological Notebooks (1880–81) and implied by MEGA² editorial guidelines for all periods. (3) **"NB" annotations**: the early editorial apparatus from the IISG content list itself uses "NB" (nota bene) to flag cross-references, and Marx uses this convention internally. For the London Notebooks, the MEGA² apparatus explicitly identifies Marx's "eigene Anteile" (own contributions) as comments, additions, terminological variations, or reorderings.

**Multilingual practice**: The progression is: Ancient Greek + Latin (dissertation notebooks, 1839–40) → German-French-Latin (Bonn/Kreuznach, 1842–43) → French-dominant with German commentary (Paris 1844) → German-English mixture with German translations (Manchester, 1845) → mixed German-English in source language (London Notebooks from 1850 onward) → later addition of Italian, Spanish, Russian (from 1850s onward). The shift is not merely incidental — Bluhm/Rüdiger argue the process of translating while excerpting accelerated the filtering effect: Marx was forced to be more selective because translation was laborious. From the London period, Marx generally stops translating into German and excerpts directly in the source language, with his commentary switching between languages freely.

**Multiple sources in one notebook**: Very common. The IISG entries show that a single notebook regularly contains excerpts from 3–20 different sources, sometimes in a single reading period and sometimes over years. The Krisenhefte (1857–58, MEGA² IV/14) is an example of a period-specific notebook focused on one event (the 1857 crisis) but drawing from numerous newspaper sources and analytical texts simultaneously. The later London notebooks (1868–69) contain excerpts from two periodicals plus multiple books, organized chronologically by when Marx worked on them.

**Internal organization and indexing**: There is evidence of several organizational devices: (a) The front-matter "Inhaltsverzeichnis" (table of contents listing source by page range) appears in many notebooks from the 1843–45 period onwards (confirmed in IISG entries for Hefte XVI, XVII, XVIII, XXVII, XXVIII, XXXII, XXXIII, XXXIV, XXXV). (b) The list of books to excerpt at the start. (c) The 158 thematic headings Marx used in "London. 1868" to organize material from the Money Market Review — this is a within-notebook subject index. (d) For the Grundrisse manuscripts (1857–58), Marx made a separate notebook (Heft M) containing two versions of an "Index zu den 7 Heften" — a cross-reference index to seven notebooks, one of the clearest examples of active cross-notebook indexing. (e) The "Heft L" (London, 1850) begins with "Liste zu exzerpierender Bücher" (list of books to be excerpted) followed by "Inhaltsverzeichnis." This is strong evidence that Marx both planned ahead and organized retrospectively.

**Forms of excerpting**: Bluhm/Rüdiger distinguish three: statarisch (line-by-line, used for canonical authors like Hegel and Smith), kursorisch (selective browsing), and problemgeschichtlich (problem-historical, synthesizing across authors). The second-stage reworking of excerpts is also documented: what appears in the Paris Manuscripts' first notebook is a secondary processing of the Smith excerpt notebooks, with quotations translated, rearranged under new headings, and set in relation to other authors.

**Newspaper clipping notebooks**: From 1868, Marx commissioned his daughter Jenny to produce notebooks of pasted newspaper clippings on specific subjects. These were physically distinct from his handwritten excerpt notebooks but functioned as part of the research apparatus, catalogued alongside the Exzerpthefte.

**Physical format — what is uncertain**: The exact dimensions of the notebooks (in cm) are not given in any source I found. The MEGA² editorial apparatus uses the standard phrase "Entstehung und Überlieferung" (Origin and Tradition) for physical descriptions, which includes paper type, watermarks, size, binding — but I could not access these apparatus sections directly. The published secondary literature does not regularly cite dimensions. The format appears to vary: the early philosophical notebooks were small enough for a copyist to duplicate (suggesting booklet size); the Paris Manuscripts occupied three or four distinct "Hefte"; the London Notebooks appear to be larger booklets (63+ pages per notebook for Heft L). The MEGA² volumes reproduce facsimile images on covers — the image from MEGA² IV/19 shows a notebook page with relatively dense handwriting, consistent with a standard 19th-century exercise-book or quarto format, but no scale is given.

### Agreements and Conflicts Between Sources

All sources agree on: the Roman numeral Heft numbering; the multilingual character of the notebooks; the presence of tables of contents and lists of books to excerpt; the seamless transition from excerpt to commentary in the text. There is no scholarly conflict on the basic method facts.

A potential point of tension: Some sources (Engels letter to Bebel 1883) suggest the Capital drafts were disorganized ("piles of quotations in no order"), while the Exzerpthefte themselves appear relatively organized (tables of contents, numbered Hefte, thematic headings in some notebooks). This tension is not really a conflict — it reflects the different character of research notebooks (the Exzerpthefte, which were organized working tools) vs. draft manuscripts (Manuskripte, which were more chaotic). The MEGA² distinction between these two types is important.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

**Physical dimensions**: No source gives the actual centimeter size of notebooks. This data exists in the "Entstehung und Überlieferung" sections of individual MEGA² volumes but was not accessible in this survey.

**Ink and writing implements**: Whether Marx used different colored inks or pencils at different reading stages — implying re-reading and annotation — is mentioned in the MEGAdigital guidelines (margin annotations "in colors that Marx also used"), but not systematically described in secondary literature.

**The role of the two-column layout over time**: The two-column format is confirmed for the 1844 Paris notebooks. Whether it persisted into the London period or later is unclear. The IISG entries for the London Notebooks (B_47, B_54, B_55, etc.) do not mention column layouts. This suggests the two-column format may have been specific to an early phase when Marx was comparing two closely related economic texts.

**The indexing question more precisely**: The Grundrisse index (Heft M) and the 158 thematic headings in "London. 1868" are the best documented indexing practices. But whether Marx made cross-notebook indexes of the kind one might expect from a large-scale project — a master reference system connecting, say, Heft XXV to Heft L to the Krisenhefte — is not documented in accessible sources. The "Krisenhefte" itself constitutes a kind of retrospective indexing by crisis year (1857), but this is chronological organization, not conceptual cross-referencing.

**The Russian-language notebooks**: Marx began serious study of Russian from 1869–70. The IISG entries for later notebooks (B_140s, B_160s range) should show Russian-language content, but I did not access these late entries in detail. The MEGAdigital guidelines confirm Russian is one of the languages in the excerpt notebooks. Anderson's and Musto's work on the late Marx confirm extensive Russian reading, but neither describes the physical format of Russian-language notebooks specifically.

**Comparison with Engels's method**: The MEGA² project covers both Marx and Engels. Engels's notebooks are also in Section IV. Whether their methods differed substantially is not addressed in the sources I found.

### Surprises

1. **The two-column layout**: The explicit evidence of left-column/right-column formatting for comparing two sources simultaneously (confirmed for Paris 1844) was more specific than expected. This is not often discussed in secondary literature about Marx's method.

2. **The Grundrisse index**: The fact that Marx created not one but two versions of a cross-notebook index (both preserved in Heft M) for the seven Grundrisse notebooks is striking. This is an unusually systematic organizational practice, though whether it was regular or exceptional is unclear.

3. **The degree of planning**: The regular appearance of front-matter "Inhaltsverzeichnis" and "Verzeichnis der zu exzerpierenden Werke" from the 1843 period onward suggests more advance planning than is often assumed. Marx was not simply reading and copying — he was maintaining a reading program and organizing materials prospectively.

4. **The "Exzerpieren als Basis" essay as under-cited**: Bluhm and Rüdiger's 2018 essay treats Marx's excerpting practice systematically as a research object in itself, distinguishing three forms and multiple stages of reworking. This conceptual framework is not widely known even among Anglophone Marx scholars — it exists mainly in the German sociology literature. It is potentially the single most useful methodological framework for thinking about what the notebooks ARE as a practice.

5. **Jenny Marx's clipping notebooks**: The fact that Marx commissioned his daughter to produce organized newspaper-clipping notebooks as part of his own research apparatus reveals that the "notebook system" extended beyond his own hand. This has implications for thinking about the system as a collaborative, distributed practice.
