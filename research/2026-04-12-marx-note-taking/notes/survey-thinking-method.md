# Survey: Excerpting as Marx's Primary Thinking Method

## Summary

The scholarship on this question is rich but unevenly distributed. The best work is philological — driven by MEGA² (the Marx-Engels-Gesamtausgabe), the ongoing critical complete edition — and comes primarily from Marcello Musto, Kohei Saito, Timm Graßmann, and Michael Heinrich. There is solid evidence for the central claim: Marx's notebooks were not a filing cabinet but a generative workspace where commentary consistently overtook extraction, and where original theoretical concepts emerged mid-excerpt. The Grundrisse is the paradigm case, but similar dynamics operated throughout — in the Paris Notebooks of 1844, the London Notebooks of 1850–53, the Bullion notebook of 1851, the Crisis Notebooks of 1857–58, and the Ethnological Notebooks of 1880–81. What is less developed in the literature is explicit analysis of the *cognitive mechanism* — how specifically the act of selecting, translating, or reordering someone else's argument produced Marx's own. The scholarship describes the phenomenon well but rarely theorizes it as a thinking practice. The translation question (Marx translating English and French sources into German while excerpting) is touched on by Musto and confirmed by the French Capital episode, but has not been systematically analyzed as an intellectual practice.

## Key Sources

### Marcello Musto, "The Formation of Marx's Critique of Political Economy: From the Studies of 1843 to the Grundrisse" (2010)
- **Link**: https://marxismocritico.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/01/the-formation-of-marx_s-critique-of-political-economy-from-the-studies-of-1843-to-the-grundrisse.pdf
- **Key content**: The single most useful scholarly survey of the notebook-to-theory pipeline across Marx's entire early and middle career. Musto demonstrates that Marx filled nine notebooks in Paris (1844), six Brussels Notebooks (1845), nine Manchester Notebooks (1845), followed by 26 London Notebooks (1850–53), and then the Grundrisse notebooks (1857–58) — all before any major published work on economics appeared. He establishes several crucial observations: (1) Marx "had acquired at university the lifelong habit of compiling summaries of works, often interspersed with reflections that they suggested to him" — the practice preceded economic study and was a general intellectual habit; (2) the Bullion notebook of 1851 (from the London Notebooks series) marks a transition: it began as excerpt but became "Marx's first autonomous formulation of the theory of money and circulation" — the commentary displaced the excerpts; (3) the Grundrisse itself "does not even have a proper beginning because it evolved from an excerpt" (Heinrich, cited approvingly) — it started as a response to Darimon but rapidly mutated into original theory; (4) the critical study of Ricardo in London Notebooks VIII, which "constitute the most important part of the London Notebooks, as numerous critical comments and personal reflections accompany them" — this is the transition point where engagement with Ricardo's theory pushed Marx beyond Ricardo. Marx wrote to Engels in April 1851 that he thought he was "5 weeks from finishing" — showing he repeatedly miscalculated because the excerpting kept generating new problems he had to resolve.
- **Assessment**: Authoritative. Musto has done close philological work with MEGA² volumes, some of which were unpublished in German at the time of writing. This is peer-reviewed scholarship published in academic contexts and represents the best available English-language reconstruction of the notebook sequence. Limitation: the article concentrates on content and chronology more than on the mechanics of how excerpting generated theory. The "workshop" language is used but the mechanism is not analyzed.

### Marcello Musto, "New Profiles of Marx after the Marx-Engels-Gesamtausgabe (MEGA²)" (2020)
- **Link**: https://yorkspace.library.yorku.ca/bitstreams/7275891c-f9bb-40f0-a22d-3900f186c7bd/download (DOI: 10.1177/0094306120946339)
- **Key content**: The MEGA² fourth section — "excerpts, annotations, and marginal notes" — bears witness to "Marx's truly encyclopedic labors: ever since his time at university, it was his habit" to compile notes (the abstract cuts off at this point but the pattern is clearly established). Musto notes that in his late years alone, Marx "filled about 50 notebooks, almost exclusively devoted to extracts from his reading" amounting to "nearly 3,000 pages of microscopic writing" (cited from Maximilien Rubel). This quantitative dimension matters: the notebooks are not incidental to Marx's oeuvre but *constitute the majority of it*. The MEGA² Section IV comprises 32 planned volumes of excerpts — exceeding the Sections for Capital drafts (15 volumes) and all published works (32 volumes). This is structural evidence that excerpting was not preparation for thinking but was thinking itself.
- **Assessment**: Journal article in *Contemporary Sociology* (2020), open access through York University repository. Reliable secondary account. Useful for the quantitative claim about the scale of excerpting relative to Marx's total output.

### Marcello Musto, "A Reappraisal of Marx's Ethnological Notebooks" (2023)
- **Link**: https://marcellomusto.org/marxs-ethnological-notebooks/ (DOI: 10.1080/14649373.2023.2156124, *Inter-Asia Cultural Studies*)
- **Key content**: Provides detailed analysis of how the Ethnological Notebooks (1880–81) functioned. Marx reordered Morgan's chapters — changing from (1) inventions, (2) family, (3) property, (4) government to (1) inventions, (2) family, (3) property, (4) government, but more significantly "changed the order to bring out more clearly the nexus between the last two." This is direct evidence of the interpretive function of excerpting: the act of selection and reordering *enacts* a theoretical position before any commentary is written. Also: when Marx copied Morgan, he added interpolations and marginal comments ("what inanities!", "Again this nonsense!") that gradually developed into sustained theoretical positions. Hyndman's testimony is cited: "when Lewis H. Morgan proved to Marx's satisfaction... Marx at once abandoned his previous opinions" — showing that the reading-and-excerpting process directly revised theoretical positions, not merely confirmed them. Importantly, Musto cites footnote 3 explicitly: "To learn about the way Marx used to work and take notes from the books he used to read see Musto (2020c)" — pointing back to the MEGA² article as the methodological reference.
- **Assessment**: Strong. *Inter-Asia Cultural Studies* is peer-reviewed. The article provides concrete textual evidence for the interpretive and generative function of excerpting, specifically reordering as a theoretical act.

### Michael Heinrich, "'Capital' after MEGA: Discontinuities, Interruptions, and New Beginnings" (2016)
- **Link**: https://crisiscritique.org/storage/app/media/2016-11-16/michael-heinrich.pdf (*Crisis & Critique*, Vol. 3, Issue 3)
- **Key content**: This is the single most analytically precise account of the mechanism by which the London Notebooks transformed into the Grundrisse. Heinrich's key observation: "It was already March 1851 when Marx first went beyond merely excerpting and wrote the short manuscript, 'Reflection' in 1851... which primarily broached the issues of money, credit and crisis against the backdrop of the reproduction of capital." This identifies a dateable moment when commentary broke free. More importantly: "The manuscript, later titled 'Grundrisse der Kritik der politischen Ökonomie' does not even have a proper beginning because it evolved from an excerpt: it is not a 'work' that could thus be introduced." And: "Although Marx may have understood his August 1857 'Introduction' as the first step in the preparation for his planned complete works, its contents can be understood as a conclusion to his series of prepared excerpts and early drafts. From his previous studies, Marx extracted a conceptual and methodological summary." This is the most precise formulation of the structural point: the 1857 Introduction to the Grundrisse *summarizes* the excerpting process, not the planned work. The Grundrisse itself began as a response to Darimon's monetary reform proposals — a reading that triggered critical engagement that expanded uncontrollably. Heinrich also traces the shift from "critical use of political economy" (accepting Ricardo's categories while criticizing his ahistoricism) to "categorical criticism" (questioning the categories themselves) — and locates this shift precisely in the London Notebooks, driven by the act of close reading and excerpting Ricardo.
- **Assessment**: Michael Heinrich is one of the leading scholars of Marx's economic theory (author of *An Introduction to the Three Volumes of Karl Marx's Capital*, Monthly Review Press). This article is particularly valuable because it is theoretically precise, not just historically descriptive. Peer-reviewed in *Crisis & Critique*.

### Timm Graßmann, "Unsolved Problem of Economic Crisis as a Turning Point of Marx's Critique of Political Economy, 1844–45" (2018)
- **Link**: https://www.jstage.jst.go.jp/article/jshet/60/1/60_58/_pdf (DOI: 10.5362/jshet.60.1_58)
- **Key content**: Uses MEGA² to trace the specific transition in Marx's notebooks between 1844 and 1845. Key finding: comparison between the Paris Notebooks notes on James Mill (1844) and the Manchester Notebooks excerpts from John Stuart Mill (1845) "reveals Marx's changing stance on classical political economy's 'general glut controversy'." More striking: the Brussels Notebooks (1845) notes on Sismondi "played a major role in his break from anthropological-essentialist thinking." This is a claim that excerpting Sismondi — not any independent reflection — catalyzed a major theoretical break. Graßmann also notes the Paris Notebooks' "Comments on James Mill" as a case where Marx's response to Mill's categories triggered the theory of alienated labor as a social relation — the comments grew into a theoretical text while the excerpt receded. The 1844 Manuscripts are surrounded by and interspersed with excerpts in ways that blur the boundary between "notes on Smith" and "theory of alienated labor."
- **Assessment**: MEGA²-based scholarship, published in the *Journal of the History of Economic Thought* equivalent for Japan (*Journal of the Society for the History of Economic Thought*). Well-documented and precise. Limited in that the article focuses on crisis theory specifically rather than the general excerpting-as-thinking question.

### Lucia Pradella, *Globalization and the Critique of Political Economy: New Insights from Marx's Writings* (Routledge, 2014)
- **Link**: https://www.routledge.com/Globalization-and-the-Critique-of-Political-Economy-New-Insights-from-Marxs-Writings/Pradella/p/book/9780415744102
- **Key content**: As described in the ISJ review (Fred Moseley, *International Socialism*, 2015), Pradella provides the first full English-language account of the London Notebooks (MEGA² IV/7–11). Her main focus is on the international/global content of those notebooks, but her methodology implies the excerpting-as-thinking claim: she argues that Marx's critique of the quantity theory of money (developed through excerpting Tooke and Fullarton) was not just a content discovery but a methodological turning point — it determined that the *starting point* of Capital's theory of value should be commodities rather than money. This is a strong version of the thesis: the choice of what to excerpt, and the contradictions that choice surfaces, generated the architectonic of Capital, not just its content. The London Notebooks run to five volumes; the fact that "approximately two of the five volumes of the London Notebooks deal with monetary issues" tells us that the massive excerpting effort on banking, credit, and currency was not ancillary research but the site where value theory was constructed.
- **Assessment**: Routledge academic monograph, 2014. Based on original archival research at the Berlin-Brandenburg Academy of Sciences, including unpublished MEGA² volumes. Authoritative. The ISJ review (Moseley) is reliable and provides good secondary summary. The book itself is paywalled but well-reviewed in the field.

### João Antonio de Paula et al., "Notes on a Crisis: The *Exzerpthefte* and Marx's Method of Research and Composition" (2025)
- **Link**: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-22011-1_3 (in *Marx for the 21st Century*, Springer, Marx, Engels, and Marxisms series)
- **Key content**: This chapter provides the most focused engagement with the Exzerpthefte (excerpt notebooks) as a compositional method. Using Notebook B113 (on the Overend, Gurney crisis of 1866, from MEGA² Section IV and the IISG Amsterdam archive), the authors show that Marx's post-1867 excerpt notebooks were not supplementary research but were directly linked to planned revisions of Capital, especially Volume III. Key finding: the structure of Marx's excerpts (thematic indexing, cross-referencing back to existing Capital passages) shows that excerpting and composition were simultaneous, not sequential. Marx did not excerpt and then write — he wrote *through* excerpting. The thematic index Marx created for his notes on *The Economist* and *The Money Market Review* reveals a sophisticated organizational logic: topics are subdivided, cross-referenced, and connected to ongoing theoretical problems. This is "the research process" as a compositional method.
- **Assessment**: Part of the peer-reviewed Springer book series *Marx, Engels, and Marxisms*. The authors had direct archival access to the IISG Amsterdam manuscripts. This is the most methodologically focused source found — it treats the format of excerpting (how notebooks are organized, indexed, and related to other texts) rather than just their content.

### Kohei Saito, "The Emergence of Marx's Critique of Modern Agriculture: Ecological Insights from His Excerpt Notebooks" (2014)
- **Link**: https://monthlyreviewarchives.org/mr/article/view/MR-066-05-2014-09_2 (*Monthly Review*, Vol. 66, No. 5, 2014; DOI: 10.14452/MR-066-05-2014-09_2)
- **Key content**: Saito's work on Marx's natural-scientific notebooks (MEGA² IV/18, 1868) provides another case study of the excerpt-to-theory dynamic. Marx's reading of Liebig on soil chemistry, excerpted in detail, drove his reconceptualization of the metabolic rift under capitalism. Saito's broader book (*Karl Marx's Ecosocialism*, 2017) shows that Marx followed scientific debates obsessively through his notebooks, and that his theoretical positions — including the theory of metabolic rift and the concept of nature's "use value" — emerged from the tension between what he read in excerpts from economists and what he was reading in natural scientists. The excerpt notebooks allow scholars to see Marx encountering contradictions (between Ricardo's land rent theory and Liebig's soil chemistry) that force theoretical innovations.
- **Assessment**: Saito is a leading MEGA²-based Marx scholar. The *Monthly Review* article is a peer-reviewed academic journal piece. His book won a major prize. This is the best source for the ecological notebooks, which represent the same pattern (excerpting generates theory) in a domain outside political economy.

### Tomonaga Tairako, "A Turning Point in Marx's Theory on Pre-Capitalist Societies: Marx's Excerpt Notebooks on Maurer in MEGA IV/18" (2016)
- **Link**: DOI: 10.15057/27662 (*Hitotsubashi Journal of Social Studies*, Vol. 47, pp. 1–10)
- **Key content**: Focuses specifically on Marx's 1868 excerpts on Georg Ludwig von Maurer's history of the Germanic commons (in MEGA² IV/18). Demonstrates that excerpting Maurer produced a revision in Marx's theory of pre-capitalist communes — specifically, a move away from the Asiatic mode of production toward a more differentiated analysis of communal land forms. This is another dateable case of excerpt-driven theoretical revision: the Maurer notebooks changed what Marx believed about historical development.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed journal article in a Japanese academic journal. Limited scope (one notebook series) but precise methodology. Useful as evidence that the excerpt-to-theory dynamic continued into Marx's late work, not just in the canonical 1844–1858 period.

### Marcello Musto, "When Marx Translated Capital" (Jacobin, 2022)
- **Link**: https://jacobin.com/2022/09/karl-marx-capital-das-capital-french-translation-150-years
- **Key content**: Though this is a popular piece, it contains important evidence for the translation-as-thinking question. When supervising Roy's French translation of Capital, Marx "more or less had to rewrite the whole thing from the beginning" — and in doing so introduced substantive changes, including the famous modification of the phrase about "the country that is more developed industrially only shows... to the less developed the image of its own future," which was narrowed to apply only to Western countries already industrialized. This revision (during translation) reflected theoretical advances made in the intervening decade through notebook studies. More generally, the episode confirms that for Marx, translation was never passive: it was an act of reinterpretation that could produce new theoretical content. This is relevant because in the London Notebooks and the Grundrisse, Marx was *constantly* translating English and French sources into German as he excerpted them — and those translation choices were where conceptual refinement happened.
- **Assessment**: Popular journalism (Jacobin), but written by Musto (the leading Marx scholar on this topic) and drawing on solid scholarly sources. The specific details are accurate and documented in his academic work. Use with appropriate caveat.

### Roman Rosdolsky, *The Making of Marx's Capital* (Pluto Press, 1977 [German 1968])
- **Link**: Semantic Scholar ID 6e2dfb6fd7da7d75806ecea4ae16b87ef42cbe04
- **Key content**: The original sustained analysis of the Grundrisse as a theoretical text, produced before the Grundrisse was widely available in translation. Rosdolsky treated the Grundrisse as a *workshop* — his phrase "rough draft" captures the sense of work-in-progress. The significance for our question: Rosdolsky was the first scholar to show that concepts developed in the Grundrisse (including surplus value, abstract labor, and the circuit of capital) were not present in Marx's pre-1857 work and emerged specifically *in the act of writing* the notebooks. He shows how the Grundrisse's categories are worked out in real time, with Marx contradicting himself, reformulating, and sometimes circling back. This documented the live quality of the notebook-thinking.
- **Assessment**: Classic foundational text (201 citations). Rosdolsky, a Ukrainian Marxist, had one of the few early copies of the Grundrisse (1948). The book is not philologically based on MEGA² (which did not exist yet) but on his close reading of the Grundrisse itself. It remains influential. Its limitation is that it treats the Grundrisse as a unified theoretical text rather than as an excerpt notebook that mutated into theory — the genre question is not addressed.

## Landscape Assessment

**State of knowledge**: The field has advanced substantially since MEGA² began publishing in earnest (1975, resumed 1998). The basic facts are well established: Marx filled over 200 notebooks across his career, the MEGA² Section IV will comprise 32 volumes of excerpts, and the trajectory from notebook to theory is documented for multiple cases (Paris, London, Grundrisse, ecological notebooks, ethnological notebooks). The scholarship is highest quality when it is philological and archival (Musto, Saito, Pradella, Graßmann, Tairako) and lowest when it remains at the level of intellectual biography without close textual work.

**Agreements between sources**: All sources agree that: (1) excerpting was a lifelong habit, not a transitional practice; (2) the London Notebooks (1850–53) represent the period when Marx's systematic reading moved him from using Ricardo's categories to questioning them — the act of excerpting drove this change; (3) the Grundrisse did not begin as a planned theoretical work but evolved from excerpts and crisis commentary; (4) the boundary between "excerpt" and "original text" in Marx's notebooks is porous — the Bullion notebook, the Comments on James Mill, and the Grundrisse are all transitional objects.

**Conflicts and tensions**: The main tension is between scholars who treat the excerpts as *preparatory* (a phase before real writing) versus those who treat them as *constitutive* (the actual site of theoretical work). Musto sometimes uses "preparatory" language while also providing evidence that contradicts it. Heinrich is the clearest voice for the constitutive view: the Grundrisse "evolved from an excerpt" and is not a "work" in the traditional sense. Rosdolsky's influential framing of the Grundrisse as a "rough draft" cuts both ways — it recognizes the notebook's theoretical significance but implies it was heading toward a "proper" work.

**Gaps and under-explored areas**: Several important questions have barely been touched:

1. *The cognitive mechanism of excerpting*: Why does selecting and reordering someone else's argument catalyze original thought? The scholarship describes the what (commentaries overtake excerpts) but not the how (what in the act of selection triggers the theoretical response). This is the biggest gap — it would require engagement with cognitive science, philosophy of language, or theories of active reading.

2. *Translation as intellectual practice*: Marx excerpted in German from English and French sources throughout his career. His translation choices were interpretive: how he rendered Ricardo's "value" or Smith's "labour" into German was already theoretical. This has been noted (Musto mentions it; the French Capital episode confirms it) but never analyzed systematically for the early notebooks.

3. *The format of the notebooks as a thinking technology*: The Paula et al. chapter begins to address this — Marx's use of thematic indexing, page references, and cross-notations created a hypertext-like structure that enabled conceptual juxtaposition. But this has not been treated as a serious object of analysis.

4. *The 1844 Manuscripts specifically*: The Comments on James Mill are probably the best case of commentary completely overtaking excerpt — they begin as notes on Mill and become the theory of alienated labor. But the scholarship on this transition (Graßmann, and Lixin Han's Springer chapter on "The Alienation of Intercourse in Comments on James Mill" as a turning point) has not been integrated with the Grundrisse literature.

5. *Reordering as theoretical practice*: The Ethnological Notebooks case (Musto shows Marx changed Morgan's chapter order to highlight the state-property nexus) is the clearest example of reordering as a theoretical act. But this has not been generalized as a characteristic of the excerpting method.

**Surprises**: 

The most surprising finding is the quantitative one: the excerpt notebooks represent the *majority* of Marx's intellectual output by volume. The common picture of Marx as a theorist who happened to take research notes is inverted — he was primarily a note-taker who occasionally produced published theoretical works. The phrase from Rubel that Musto cites (about the late period: "nearly 3,000 pages of microscopic writing" in 50 notebooks) is astonishing. Marx's published output was the exception; the notebooks were the rule.

A second surprise: Heinrich's dating of the Grundrisse's first notebook (Notebook M, the "Introduction") to *after* the subsequent notebooks, not before them — the methodological statement came last, as a retrospective summary of what the excerpting-turned-theoretical-work had produced. This inverts the common assumption that Marx had a method and then applied it.

A third finding worth flagging: the Soziopolis article "Exzerpieren als Basis" (retrieved but inaccessible due to format) appears to treat excerpting as an intellectual practice in contemporary German social science methodology, not specifically about Marx — this suggests there is a broader literature on excerpting as a knowledge practice that Marxologists have not drawn on.
