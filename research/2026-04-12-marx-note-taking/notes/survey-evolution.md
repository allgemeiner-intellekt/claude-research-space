# Survey: How Did Marx's Note-Taking Practice Change Across His Intellectual Life?

## Summary

The literature on this question is surprisingly rich, though scattered across biographical scholarship, MEGA² editorial prefaces, and thematic monographs. The clearest finding is that excerpting was Marx's lifelong constant — a habit he described in his own words as early as 1837 and maintained until months before his death in 1883 — but the *purpose*, *structure*, and *relationship between excerpts and original writing* changed dramatically across the six major phases of his career. The student period (1835-41) produced philosophical notebooks and literary exercises. The Kreuznach period (1843) marked a decisive turn toward systematic historical research as the empirical substrate for philosophical critique. Paris (1844) introduced the hybrid form of interspersed excerpts and original commentary that characterizes the EPM. The London decades (1850-65) saw an industrialization of the notebook method: vast, disciplined accumulation organized with indices, designed as raw material for the Capital project. The late period (1868-83) shows a puzzling expansion rather than contraction: Marx's notebooks diversified into ethnology, mathematics, geology, and world history, pursuing threads the Capital project had not been able to absorb. The MEGA² project — particularly its Section IV (Excerpts, Notes, and Marginal Notes) — has transformed scholarly understanding by making this material systematically available for the first time, though much remains unpublished or untranslated.

## Key Sources

### Kaan Kangal, "Marx's 'Bonn Notebooks' in Context" (Historical Materialism 28.4, 2020, pp. 102–138)
- **Link**: DOI:10.1163/1569206X-12341901; PDF accessed via PhilArchive at https://philarchive.org/archive/KANMBN
- **Key content**: The most detailed scholarly analysis available in English of Marx's early notebook practice (1839-1842). Kangal establishes several things crucial to the evolution question: (1) Marx described his excerpting habit explicitly in the November 1837 letter to his father — "I made a habit of the practice of excerpting passages from out of all the books that I read … and at the same time scribbled down my own reflections" (confirmed directly from the letter below); (2) the Bonn Notebooks (seven notebooks, spring-autumn 1841/42) were compiled as preparatory research for a joint project with Bruno Bauer on religious art, organized thematically around art and religion from Carl Friedrich von Rumohr, Charles de Brosses, Böttiger, Meiner, Benjamin Constant, and others; (3) the notebooks were driven by a specific publishing agenda — they were meant to supply empirical content for Bauer's *Trumpet* project — but Marx exceeded and eventually broke free of that framework; (4) MEGA² editors date the notebooks to April-May 1842, but Kangal argues persuasively that some sections were written earlier (summer/autumn 1841); (5) the notebooks show Marx already using marginalia and thematic cross-referencing (he compiled subject indices), but the commentary is sparse — he was, Kangal suggests, in a state of "lostness" entering a new disciplinary field. The article also usefully identifies that Marx's practice at this stage was still tightly tied to a live intellectual collaboration and a specific publication plan — his excerpting had an external organizing purpose that later gave way to a more autonomous research logic.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed, published in Historical Materialism (one of the leading Marxology journals). Uses MEGA² sources throughout. Kangal cites Michael Heinrich's intellectual biography and the MEGA² editorial apparatus. Highly credible for the early period.

### Marx, Letter to His Father (November 10, 1837)
- **Link**: https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1837-pre/letters/37_11_10.htm
- **Key content**: The foundational primary source for understanding Marx's early intellectual practice. The 20-year-old Marx describes his first year in Berlin: he was reading law (Heineccius, Thibaut, Pandects), philosophy (Hegel, Kant, Fichte), and literature simultaneously, and states explicitly: "I made a habit of the practice of excerpting passages from out of all the books that I read … I also translated Tacitus' Germania, Ovid's Tristria, and started learning English and Italian." He describes burning his early literary notebooks after rejecting them as too "idealistic." The letter shows a mind producing notes in several registers simultaneously: juridical systematization (he drafted a 300-page philosophy of right), philosophical dialectics (the "Cleanthes" dialogue), translation exercises, literary composition, and excerption. The letter is also notable for showing that his early note-taking had no single disciplinary focus — he moved between law, philosophy, poetry, and linguistics in a single notebook session. The observation that he read Hegel "from beginning to end" while ill (and after burning his earlier work) marks a significant intellectual reorientation; the excerpting habit emerges in this letter as his primary instrument for managing the resulting cognitive overload.
- **Assessment**: Primary source. Confirms the habit of excerption is literally the oldest documented feature of Marx's intellectual practice, antedating the Kreuznach notebooks by six years. Published in Die Neue Zeit (1897) and multiple subsequent editions.

### Marx, Notebooks on Epicurean Philosophy (1839)
- **Link**: https://www.marxists.org/archive/marx/works/1839/notebook/index.htm (seven notebooks)
- **Key content**: Seven notebooks surviving from Marx's doctoral research, organized around Greek atomist philosophers (Epicurus, Democritus), compiled 1839-1841. These are the most academically structured of the early notebooks — they resemble scholarly preparatory materials for the doctoral thesis. Published in MECW Vol. 1 (1975). The structure (systematic excerption from primary and secondary sources, organized by theme/author) suggests a more formalized academic note-taking practice than the more scattered practice described in the 1837 letter.
- **Assessment**: Primary source available in MECW. Establishes what the student notebooks actually looked like before the Kreuznach turn.

### Niu Yuanyuan, "The Impetus of the Kreuznach Notebooks to Marx's First Ideological Transformation" (Advances in Philosophy 13.5, 2024)
- **Link**: https://image.hanspub.org/Html/8-2381973_86897.htm
- **Key content**: A Chinese-language academic article (with English abstract) that provides the most detailed structural description of the Kreuznach notebooks available. Key findings: the five Kreuznach notebooks total 255 pages and were written July-August 1843 (confirmed by Marx's own date markings); they cover over 20 works on state history spanning 2,500 years of European history (England, France, Germany, Sweden, Poland, etc.); Notebook 1 contains Marx's own subject headings (*Inhaltsverzeichnis*) for what he is excerpting; Notebook 4 contains a thematic index (*Sachregister*) as well as a longer original commentary (the famous note on Hegel and the French constitution: "main subject becomes predicate, and predicate becomes subject"); Marx's own bibliographic records of the sources are also included; the notebooks were written non-sequentially (Marx cross-cut between the five volumes simultaneously according to his research interests); the French Revolution receives by far the most extensive treatment across all five books. The article argues that the Kreuznach notebooks drove Marx's shift from idealism to materialism precisely because immersion in historical facts showed him that constitutional forms (*Staatsformen*) follow material interests, not rational principles — a conclusion he could not derive from within the Hegelian framework alone. This article draws on MEGA² IV/2 (the published Kreuznach volume) and Chinese secondary literature.
- **Assessment**: Chinese peer-reviewed journal (Hans Publishers). Works directly with MEGA² IV/2. The article is primarily interpretive (focused on the ideological transformation) rather than codicological, but it provides the most specific structural description of the notebooks' physical organization available in my searches.

### MECW Volume 3 Preface (Progress Publishers / Institute of Marxism-Leninism)
- **Link**: https://marxists.architexturez.net/archive/marx/works/cw/volume03/preface.htm
- **Key content**: The editorial preface to the MECW volume covering March 1843 to August 1844 explicitly addresses the Kreuznach notebooks and their relationship to the Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right. Key passage: "Marx studied a broad range of problems in the history and theory of the state and law, world history, the history of separate countries (England, France, Germany, the USA, Italy, Sweden), the English Revolution of the seventeenth century, and the French Revolution at the end of the eighteenth century. All this was reflected in his manuscript and in his notebooks of excerpts (the so-called Kreuznach Notebooks). Although he was strongly influenced by Feuerbach's materialism, Marx did not approach the criticism of Hegel through an analysis of religion, as Feuerbach had done, but through an investigation of social relations." Also notes that in Paris (late 1843 onward) Marx "made numerous excerpts from the works of Adam Smith, Ricardo, Say, Skarbek, List, James Mill, Destutt de Tracy, McCulloch, Boisguillebert, Lauderdale, Schütz and other economists… in many cases accompanying these excerpts with his own comments and critical remarks." The Paris excerpts are identified as qualitatively distinct from Kreuznach: they accompany active *theory formation* rather than historical survey — the "Comments on James Mill" represent the most theoretically developed output from this period's excerpting.
- **Assessment**: Authoritative Soviet editorial apparatus; orthodox Marxist interpretive framing but reliable on bibliographic and chronological questions. Published by Progress Publishers (Moscow) and Lawrence and Wishart.

### Fred Moseley, review of Lucia Pradella's *Globalization and the Critique of Political Economy* (International Socialism 148, 2015)
- **Link**: https://isj.org.uk/marxs-research-notebooks-and-globalisation/
- **Key content**: A review that doubles as one of the best English-language overviews of the London Notebooks (1850-53). Establishes: (1) the London Notebooks are MEGA² Section IV, Volumes 7-11, covering 1850-53; (2) they comprise 24 booklets (*Hefte*) of excerpts; (3) approximately two of the five main volumes deal with monetary theory alone; (4) they were the foundation for the Grundrisse (1857-58) but also contain vast material on banking, credit, economic history, current economic conditions, and anti-colonial movements; (5) Marx read John Gray's "labour money" proposal in these notebooks — showing he was actively tracking competing socialist economic proposals; (6) the Gülich excerpts (September 1846-December 1847), filling over 900 pages in MEGA² IV/6, show Marx working through a five-volume world economic history immediately after writing *The German Ideology*; (7) none of the Section IV notebooks appear in the MECW. Notes that Pradella's book (2014) is "the first description in English of the contents of these early notebooks." The London Notebooks mark a clear methodological shift: Marx was no longer excerpting to solve a specific philosophical problem but building an encyclopedic base of economic data for a sustained systematic theory project.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed journal article in *International Socialism*. Moseley is a specialist on Marx's economic manuscripts. Reliable on MEGA² structure and notebook contents.

### Michael Heinrich, *Capital After MEGA: Discontinuities, Interruptions, and New Beginnings* (libcom.org, from *Crisis and Critique*)
- **Link**: https://libcom.org/article/capital-after-mega-discontinuities-interruptions-and-new-beginnings-michael-heinrich
- **Key content**: Heinrich's analysis — based on his multi-volume intellectual biography and years of MEGA² editorial work — is the most sophisticated account of how Marx's research practice evolved during the London period. Key points extracted from the WebFetch summary: (1) the London Notebooks (1850-53) involved "merely excerpting" before Marx transitioned to original composition; the 1851 "Reflection" manuscript was the first time he moved "beyond merely excerpting" to develop independent theoretical work; (2) Heinrich distinguishes "formation periods" (like the Grundrisse in 1857-58, written "almost obsessively") from "implementation phases" (careful manuscript preparation for publication); (3) Marx's research exhibits "sharp breaks" rather than linear development — the 1863 shift from six-book plan to four-book Capital structure, and the abandonment of "capital in general" as an organizing principle, are discontinuities within the notebook practice; (4) the 1870s brought a "new formation period" — Marx simultaneously revised previous volumes, explored new topics (US industrial development, Russian agriculture, environmental issues), and conducted specialized banking/credit studies. This represents "expansion rather than completion — many new research processes start even as earlier ones remain unfinished." Heinrich's framework challenges the simple story of steady methodological maturation; it suggests Marx's note-taking oscillated between phases of broad accumulation and phases of intense theoretical synthesis, and that the late notebooks represent a new formation cycle rather than a winding-down.
- **Assessment**: Heinrich is one of the most authoritative Marx scholars working today, with decades of MEGA² editorial experience and a multi-volume intellectual biography. The summary was from a WebFetch and may not capture all nuance, but the findings are consistent with his published work. Very high credibility.

### Michael R. Krätke, "Marx and World History" (*International Review of Social History*, 2018)
- **Link**: https://cominsitu.wordpress.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/marx_and_world_history.pdf (Cambridge Core canonical version: DOI:10.1017/S0020859017000657)
- **Key content**: Crucial for the late period. Describes the four "Chronological Extracts" notebooks (1881-82) in precise physical detail: A5-format, marble cardboard covers, black-cloth binding, narrow-ruled blue lines, written "with a sharp quill (*Feder*) and thus relatively readable"; text is a mixture of German, English, Latin, Italian, French, Spanish, and Russian; excerpts cover Schlosser's 18-volume *Weltgeschichte für das deutsche Volk* and Botta's *Histoire des peuples d'Italie*; cover approximately 96 CE to 1648 CE; preserved at IISG Amsterdam (Sign. B 108-111/B 157-160); planned for MEGA² IV/29. Krätke argues these notebooks are not a scholarly curiosity but a serious engagement with the question of how modern European states and the state system arose — a prerequisite for understanding capitalism's origins. The notebooks show Marx reading European world-history with the eye of someone building the broader historical scaffolding for *Capital*'s theoretical framework. They also demonstrate his multilingualism as a research tool: he takes notes in the language most natural for a given topic. Krätke explicitly says "The excerpts and occasional comments are characteristic of his way of working" — implying the method itself is consistent even while the subject matter has expanded. The article also contextualizes these notebooks in Marx's ongoing capital project and his Russian agrarian studies.
- **Assessment**: Published in *International Review of Social History* (Cambridge Core), a leading journal for historical social science. Peer-reviewed. Krätke worked with the IISG archive directly. Excellent credibility; the physical description of the notebooks is unique in the English-language literature.

### Kevin B. Anderson, "Marx's Late Writings on Non-Western and Precapitalist Societies and Gender" (*Rethinking Marxism*, 2002)
- **Link**: https://kevin-anderson.com/wp-content/uploads/docs/anderson-article-marx-late-writings.pdf
- **Key content**: Anderson's essay tracks three strands in Marx's last decade (1872-83): changes to the French *Capital* edition removing unilinear passages; new writings on Russia; and the 1879-82 notebooks (many still unpublished in any language, planned for MEGA² IV/27). These notebooks cover Indian history and village culture, Dutch colonialism in Indonesia, gender and kinship among Native Americans, and communal/private property in Algeria and Latin America. Anderson's main argument — that Marx moved from Eurocentrism toward multilinear development models — is well-known. For the question of note-taking evolution, what matters is his documentation that these notebooks represent a *thematic* broadening of the excerpting practice: Marx is no longer solely focused on political economy but is building a comparative anthropology of pre-capitalist social formations. Krader's 1972 edition of the Ethnological Notebooks is the key published source; Anderson confirms that much surrounding material (Kovalevsky, geological, mathematical notebooks) remains largely unstudied.
- **Assessment**: Peer-reviewed article. Anderson is the leading English-language scholar on Marx's late intellectual development. Draws on Krader (1972) and early MEGA² volumes. Reliable; the interpretive thesis (multilinearism) is controversial but the bibliographic information is sound.

### Marcello Musto review-summary in "New Profiles of Marx after the MEGA²" (*Contemporary Sociology*, 2020)
- **Link**: https://www.asanet.org/wp-content/uploads/attach/journals/sept20csfeature.pdf
- **Key content**: The ASA article (accessed via PDF) was not directly readable due to a 403 error and token limits. However, from ancillary sources (the MLToday review of Musto's *Last Years* and the Green Left review) the key contributions of Musto's late-Marx scholarship can be reconstructed: (1) Musto's *The Last Years of Karl Marx* (Stanford University Press, 2020) covers 1881-83 and shows a Marx still conducting rigorous research across agricultural chemistry, physiology, mathematics, anthropology, ancient history, and banking; (2) regarding the mathematical notebooks specifically: Marx began studying calculus partly to correct errors he had found in the Grundrisse, and what started as a "detour to algebra" became a major "moral consolation" and "refuge" during periods of illness and grief; (3) Musto's chronology shows the late notebooks were produced under conditions of severe physical illness (lung disease, periodic hospitalization), emotional trauma (death of wife Jenny 1881, death of daughter Jenny Caroline 1883), and political engagement — suggesting the broadening of notebook themes was in part a response to his inability to concentrate on the sustained theoretical work Capital required; (4) Musto argues, against earlier biographers like Nicolaievsky/Maenchen-Helfen (1933), that Marx's last years were not a period of "waiting to die" but of genuine intellectual activity, even if it no longer resulted in completed publications.
- **Assessment**: Musto is a leading Italian-Canadian Marx scholar based at York University. His late-Marx scholarship draws directly on MEGA² materials and is peer-reviewed. The Stanford UP monograph is the definitive recent work on this period.

### Carlos L. Garrido, review of Musto's *The Last Years of Karl Marx* (MLToday, 2022); Barry Healy review (Green Left, 2021)
- **Links**: https://mltoday.com/book-review-the-last-years-of-karl-marx-an-intellectual-biography/; https://www.greenleft.org.au/content/biography-sheds-light-marxs-last-years
- **Key content**: Two review articles of Musto's *Last Years* that provide accessible summaries of the book's key findings on Marx's late notebooks. Confirms the Ethnological Notebooks material (Morgan's impact on Marx's views of the family and state, Kovalevsky notebooks on Russian commune, Marx's critical stance on transferring European categories), the mathematical manuscripts as "moral consolation," and the Chronological Extracts as "an annotated year-by-year timeline of world events from the first century BC on, summarizing their causes and salient features" (Green Left review quoting Musto). Healy's review (Green Left) quotes the damning 1933 Nicolaievsky/Maenchen-Helfen assessment that after 1873 "Marx remained the insatiable reader that he had always been; he continued indefatigably making extracts from what he read, he went on collecting material, but he no longer had the capacity to organise it" — a view Musto explicitly refutes by showing the organization *was* there, it just wasn't in publishable form.
- **Assessment**: Non-peer-reviewed reviews, but both summarize a peer-reviewed monograph accurately. Useful for accessible synthesis of the late period.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of Knowledge

The evolution of Marx's note-taking across his career is an active research question, concentrated in the MEGA² scholarly community (Berlin-Brandenburg Academy, IISG Amsterdam) and in monographic scholarship by Musto, Heinrich, Anderson, Saito, Krätke, and Pradella. The picture that emerges across these sources is internally consistent on chronology and general periodization, with some interpretive disagreements about what the evolution means.

**What is well established:**
The six-phase periodization proposed in the research brief maps reasonably well onto the scholarly consensus. The habit of excerption is documented from 1837 onward (Marx's own testimony in the letter to his father) and is confirmed by MEGA² editors as a lifelong constant. The Riazanov-era observation (cited in the "Marx in the MEGA" article) that Marx's notebooks are "a very important source for the study of Marxism on the whole and for the critical history of individual works by Marx in particular" remains the bedrock of current scholarship. Richard Sperl's characterization of the notebooks as the "key" to Marx's "intellectual workshop" and a "basis of sources" providing insight into his methods and orientation is widely endorsed.

**Student period (1835-1841):** The 1839 Epicurean philosophy notebooks survive and are in MECW Vol. 1. The Bonn Notebooks are in MEGA² I/1 and have now received their definitive study in Kangal (2020). The 1837 letter documents the general habit. These sources show a student-style note-taking practice: excerption to support specific research projects (doctoral thesis, the Bauer collaboration), organized by author and theme, with sparse original commentary. The habit of keeping separate notebooks for translation, poetry, and philosophy already suggests categorical thinking about different registers of intellectual work.

**Kreuznach period (1843):** The five Kreuznach notebooks are in MEGA² IV/2. They represent the first instance of what will become Marx's characteristic mature note-taking: vast systematic excerption from a defined literature (here, European political history), organized with subject indices and bibliographic records, in service of a specific theoretical project (here, the Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right). The Chinese article (Niu 2024) describes their physical organization in useful detail. The MECW Vol. 3 preface confirms the interpretive consensus that these notebooks drove the transition from idealist to materialist critique. The key structural feature is that the notebooks served as the empirical substrate for the philosophical manuscript being written alongside them — the notebooks and the manuscript are best understood as two faces of a single research process.

**Paris period (1844):** The Paris notebooks — including the EPM — are in MEGA² IV/2 (excerpts from political economists) and MEGA² I/2 (the manuscripts). The MECW Vol. 3 preface identifies a new form here: Marx excerpts from economists (Smith, Ricardo, Say, Mill, etc.) and then writes extended critical commentary within or immediately alongside the excerpts. The "Comments on James Mill" represents the most developed instance of this form. This is a hybrid mode — the excerpts and the original writing are more deeply interwoven than in the Kreuznach notebooks, where the excerpts were mostly subordinated to a separate manuscript.

**Brussels/Manchester period (1845-1847):** The Gülich notebooks (900+ pages, MEGA² IV/6) represent one of the most labor-intensive excerpting projects in Marx's career: he read a five-volume world economic history while simultaneously working on *The German Ideology*. This period shows the note-taking practice becoming more encyclopedic in scope — no longer organized around a single philosophical problem but beginning to build a database of economic history.

**London notebooks (1850-1865):** The most prolific period. MEGA² Section IV, Volumes 7-18, contains the London Notebooks (1850-53, 24 booklets) and subsequent economic manuscripts. Heinrich's periodization is crucial here: he distinguishes "formation periods" (the Grundrisse, 1857-58, written in a frenzy of ten months) from the more systematic work on the 1861-63 manuscript and Capital Vol. 1 drafts. The 1857 Introduction (*Einleitung*) to the Grundrisse is Marx's most explicit methodological self-reflection — it articulates the method of "rising from the abstract to the concrete" as the correct scientific method. It was written for the Grundrisse notebooks themselves, as a kind of orientation paper. Heinrich's finding that the first genuinely original theoretical composition (the 1851 "Reflection" manuscript) emerged *out of* the London excerpt notebooks is an important structural finding: the notebooks were the precondition for independent theory, not just its backdrop.

**Late period (1868-1883):** The picture here is complex and not fully studied. The Ethnological Notebooks (ed. Krader, 1972) are the best-studied late notebooks. The Chronological Extracts (described physically by Krätke) are barely studied. The Mathematical Manuscripts (published in Russian 1968, English 1983) have been studied somewhat. Anderson's article identifies further late notebooks on India, Indonesia, Algeria, and Latin America that are still largely unpublished. The geological and agrarian chemistry notebooks (Saito's work) are also relevant. The consensus (Musto, Anderson, Heinrich) is that the late period represents a new formation cycle rather than intellectual decline: Marx was expanding his material base for topics (*Capital* had not been able to absorb) and responding to new political situations (Russia, the Paris Commune aftermath, Irish nationalism).

### Agreements and Conflicts

**Agreements:** All sources agree that excerption was a lifelong constant. All agree that the MEGA² project has fundamentally transformed our understanding by making the sheer volume and variety of the notebooks visible. All agree that the London period is the most prolific and that the Grundrisse marks a watershed (the "1857 Introduction" as Marx's methodological self-portrait). All agree that the late period shows broadening rather than narrowing.

**Conflicts:** The main interpretive disagreement is about what the late notebooks' broadening *means*. Older biographers (Nicolaievsky/Maenchen-Helfen) read it as intellectual disintegration — "the capacity to organise it" gone. Musto, Anderson, and Heinrich read it as evidence of continuing intellectual vitality and genuine theoretical development. This debate is not purely empirical; it depends on what one expects finished intellectual work to look like and whether dispersed note-taking counts as "work."

A secondary disagreement concerns the Kreuznach notebooks specifically. Orthodox Soviet scholarship (MECW preface) treats them primarily as a step on the road to historical materialism. Kangal (2020) and recent German scholarship (Heinrich's biography) read the early period more discontinuously — Marx had not yet committed to any settled method and was working through several competing frameworks simultaneously.

### Gaps and Under-Explored Areas

**The Brussels/Manchester period (1845-1847):** The Gülich notebooks are described by Moseley as important but there is almost no English-language scholarship treating them codicologically or methodologically. MEGA² IV/6 is published but untranslated.

**The 1848-1850 hiatus:** There is very little scholarship on whether and how Marx took notes during the revolutionary period itself, when he was editing the *Neue Rheinische Zeitung* and engaged in direct political work. The notebook practice during periods of intense political activity is poorly documented.

**Comparative codicology of the London notebooks:** While the London Notebooks (1850-53) are described structurally (24 booklets, organized indices), there is no detailed English-language study comparable to Kangal's analysis of the Bonn Notebooks that traces how a specific notebook was organized, how Marx moved between sources, and how the structure evolved notebook by notebook.

**The relationship between the 1857 Introduction and actual notebook practice:** The Introduction articulates the "rising from abstract to concrete" method, but it is a programmatic statement. How closely does the Grundrisse's actual organization (and the subsequent manuscript practice) track this stated method? This is an under-explored question that would require close reading of the manuscripts against the Introduction.

**The mathematical manuscripts as a window into method:** Musto mentions that Marx turned to calculus to "correct errors" in the Grundrisse and that this became a major intellectual preoccupation. There is virtually no English scholarship on whether the mathematical notebooks shed light on Marx's understanding of scientific method more broadly (e.g., did his work on differential calculus inform his thinking about rates of change in economic analysis?). The Wikipedia article notes that Marx's definitions "anticipated some 20th-century mathematical advances in nonstandard analysis" — an extraordinary claim that merits investigation.

**The physical/material dimension:** Krätke describes the late Chronological Extracts notebooks physically (A5 format, marble covers, narrow-ruled blue lines, sharp quill). But almost no source addresses the material history of Marx's notebooks more systematically: how did the physical format of his notebooks change? Did he use pre-ruled notebooks, blank paper, standard academic formats? This level of codicological attention has not been applied systematically across the periods.

**Why excerption?** No source I found directly engages the question of *why* Marx excerpted so obsessively as a mode of intellectual work — as opposed to, for example, writing marginal notes in books directly (which he also did, but less centrally) or taking synthetic notes. This question has methodological significance: excerption as a practice involves selecting, juxtaposing, and implicitly interpreting sources without committing to a fixed interpretation. It is an inherently open-ended mode of accumulation that defers synthesis. Understanding why Marx chose (and retained) this mode might illuminate something about his whole intellectual approach.

### Surprises

The most striking finding from this survey is the complete reversal of the old "late decline" narrative. The Nicolaievsky/Maenchen-Helfen reading — that after 1873 Marx could no longer organize what he accumulated — has been thoroughly refuted by Musto and others. If anything, the late notebooks show *more* disciplinary range and *more* explicit organizational structures (indices, chronological frameworks, thematic registers) than earlier periods. The decline narrative was a projection onto the notebooks of the absence of publication, not a reading of the notebooks themselves.

A second surprise: the 1837 letter to his father is far richer as a methodological document than its reputation suggests. Most citations of it note the excerpting habit. But the letter also shows Marx already thinking explicitly about the failures of his own early note-taking practice (the "clumsy and incorrect division" of his philosophy of right, the critique of his own tripartite schema as "a desk with drawers into which I afterwards poured sand"). The 20-year-old Marx was already critically reflective about method — the 1857 Introduction's self-conscious methodological discussion has genuine roots in this earlier self-examination.

A third surprise: the Gülich notebooks (1846-47, 900+ pages, MEGA² IV/6) are completely absent from English-language discussion of Marx's note-taking, despite being one of the most intensive excerpting projects of his career and being written immediately after *The German Ideology*. This is a significant gap.
