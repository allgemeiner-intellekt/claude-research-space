# Deep Dive: RSP as Organizational Capacity-Building Mechanism

## Question

Even if the RSP never formally triggered a deployment restriction, did it succeed as an organizational capacity-building mechanism — creating the teams, tools, and institutional knowledge that made the Mythos restriction possible? Is "building capacity to make discretionary judgments" the same thing as "being constrained"?

## Investigation

### 1. What organizational capacity did the RSP actually create?

The evidence here is unusually rich because Anthropic itself published a detailed retrospective in May 2024, "Reflections on our Responsible Scaling Policy," that explicitly catalogues what the RSP forced into existence. This is primary-source self-reporting, which has obvious credibility limitations, but the specifics are verifiable and internally consistent.

**The 8% headcount figure.** The May 2024 reflections document states directly: "Scaling up our security program and developing a comprehensive roadmap to defend against a wide variety of non-state actors has required a surge of effort: around 8% of all Anthropic employees are now working on security-adjacent areas and we expect that proportion to grow further as models become more economically valuable to attackers." At Anthropic's then-headcount of approximately 800 employees, this means roughly 65 people whose jobs existed specifically because the RSP created an obligation to achieve ASL-3 security standards. The document adds: "The threat models and security targets articulated in the RSP have been especially valuable for our security team to help prioritize and motivate the necessary changes." [Source: https://www.anthropic.com/news/reflections-on-our-responsible-scaling-policy]

**The Frontier Red Team.** The Frontier Red Team is explicitly an RSP creature. The May 2024 reflections document names it as the team responsible for threat modeling and capability evaluations, and the March 2025 Frontier Red Team blog post — "Progress from our Frontier Red Team" — describes "over the last year across four model releases" of work assessing cyber and biosecurity risks. As of December 2024, press coverage (WSJ, Techmeme) reported the team had grown to 11 people. Newton Cheng, named in CNBC's Mythos coverage as "Anthropic's Frontier Red Team cyber lead," became the person who publicly discussed the Mythos cybersecurity capabilities and the rationale for restricting them. The through-line from RSP → Frontier Red Team → Mythos restriction is direct. [Source: https://www.anthropic.com/news/strategic-warning-for-ai-risk-progress-and-insights-from-our-frontier-red-team; https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/07/anthropic-claude-mythos-ai-hackers-cyberattacks.html]

**Constitutional Classifiers.** Published February 2025, Constitutional Classifiers were developed explicitly to satisfy ASL-3 deployment standards. The paper states this directly in footnote 1: "This capability threshold refers to systems that have the ability to significantly help individuals or groups with basic technical backgrounds create/obtain and deploy CBRN weapons... We are hopeful that a system defended by Constitutional Classifiers could allow us to mitigate jailbreaking risks for models which have passed the CBRN capability threshold outlined in our Responsible Scaling Policy." When ASL-3 was activated in May 2025, Constitutional Classifiers were the core of the deployment safeguard — "making the system more difficult to jailbreak" via "real-time classifier guards, trained on synthetic data." The RSP did not just incentivize this work; it created the specific requirement that this work had to exist before Claude Opus 4 could ship. [Source: https://www.anthropic.com/research/constitutional-classifiers; https://www.anthropic.com/news/activating-asl3-protections]

**ASL evaluation protocols and METR partnerships.** The March 2025 Frontier Red Team report describes partnerships with Carnegie Mellon University for network cyber ranges, collaboration with the US and UK AI Safety Institutes (both of which conducted pre-deployment testing on Claude 3.7 Sonnet), and a "first-of-its-kind partnership with the National Nuclear Security Administration (NNSA)" for classified nuclear/radiological evaluation. Crucially, the Frontier Red Team post contains a key line: "By developing evaluation plans in advance and committing to capability thresholds that would motivate increased levels of security, the work of Anthropic's Frontier Red Team enhances our ability to advance the frontier of AI rapidly and with the confidence that we are doing so responsibly." This is Anthropic explicitly making the capacity-building argument from the inside. [Source: https://www.anthropic.com/news/strategic-warning-for-ai-risk-progress-and-insights-from-our-frontier-red-team]

**Sabotage Risk Reports.** The Claude Opus 4.6 Sabotage Risk Report (published February 2026) was explicitly a response to the RSP's AI R&D-4 capability threshold requirements. The RSP update page notes: "Our RSP requires that once models cross the AI R&D-4 capability threshold, we develop an affirmative case identifying the most immediate and relevant misalignment risks..." The Sabotage Risk Report, which METR red-teamed (David Rein spent three weeks testing Anthropic's internal agent monitoring systems, publishing results in March 2026), represents an entire new institutional practice that did not exist before the RSP. [Source: https://www.anthropic.com/responsible-scaling-policy; https://metr.org/blog/2026-03-25-red-teaming-anthropic-agent-monitoring/]

**Industry norm diffusion.** The GovAI analysis (March 2026, Sophie Williams and Jonas Freund) documents: "Anthropic was the first company to publish this kind of frontier AI safety framework. Since then, 11 other companies have adopted similar frameworks. These frameworks have even served as a foundation for frontier AI regulation globally, including California's SB-53, New York's RAISE Act, and the EU AI Act." The RSP v3.0 document itself, in its theory of change assessment, acknowledges: "Our RSP did encourage other AI companies to adopt somewhat similar standards: within a few months of announcing our RSP, both OpenAI and Google DeepMind adopted broadly similar frameworks." [Source: https://www.governance.ai/analysis/anthropics-rsp-v3-0-how-it-works-whats-changed-and-some-reflections; https://www.anthropic.com/news/responsible-scaling-policy-v3]

### 2. Did the RSP make the Mythos restriction possible?

The Mythos restriction (announced April 7, 2026) limited Claude Mythos Preview to ~52 partner organizations rather than general availability, citing the model's ability to find thousands of zero-day vulnerabilities autonomously, escape virtual sandboxes, and produce working exploits without human intervention. The restriction was routed through Project Glasswing, not the RSP framework.

The question is whether the RSP-built capacity made this discretionary restriction possible. The answer is: yes, but with important nuance.

**What made Mythos different from previous models:** The Frontier Red Team's cyber capability work — developed specifically under RSP mandates — gave Anthropic the infrastructure to actually know that Mythos was dangerous before releasing it. The March 2025 FRT report shows the team went from CTF performance of a high-schooler to an undergraduate in cyber in a single year. By April 2026, Newton Cheng (FRT cyber lead) and his team had run autonomous exploit development tests that showed Mythos was "in a different league" — Opus 4.6 had near-0% success rate at autonomous exploit development while Mythos produced 181 working exploits on the same test. This detection capacity — the knowledge that Mythos was qualitatively different — was built under the RSP evaluation regime. Without the Frontier Red Team, there is no reason to think Anthropic would have run these tests.

**The counterfactual:** The May 2024 reflections document states explicitly: "The process of implementing the policy has also surfaced a range of important questions, projects, and dependencies that might otherwise have taken longer to identify or gone undiscussed." This is Anthropic saying, in writing, that the RSP caused them to discover things they would not have discovered otherwise. The Constitutional Classifiers would likely not have been developed when they were without the ASL-3 deployment obligation. The METR red-teaming relationship — which produced the independent validation of Anthropic's safety systems — grew directly from RSP-driven evaluation partnerships.

### 3. Is the capacity-building defense structurally sound? The INPO analogy.

The INPO analogy (Institute of Nuclear Power Operations) is worth taking seriously. INPO was created in 1979 after Three Mile Island as an industry self-regulatory body. Its mechanism was primarily cultural and operational — peer reviews, sharing of operating experience, training standards — rather than rule-based prohibition. It did not primarily work by saying "you cannot start reactor X until you achieve Y." It worked by building a shared technical culture across the industry, establishing evaluation practices, and creating accountability to peer institutions rather than regulators. The US nuclear industry's safety record after INPO improved substantially even without new binding NRC rules.

The RSP does share INPO-like features. It created:
- Shared evaluation practices (the CTF benchmarks, the virology test, the autonomy evaluations) that other labs adopted in modified form
- External review partnerships (METR, UK and US AI Safety Institutes) analogous to INPO's peer inspection program
- Internal accountability structures (Responsible Scaling Officer, noncompliance reporting, Board updates)
- A technical culture within Anthropic that treated "would this fail the ASL-3 deployment eval?" as a real engineering constraint

This culture-building argument is not trivial. The May 2024 reflections document notes that implementing multi-party authorization for model weight access "has been a well-received extension of existing cultural norms" because "Anthropic has already adopted a culture of peer review across software engineering, research, comms, and finance teams." The RSP accelerated and formalized a safety culture that shaped decisions made without invoking the formal mechanism.

However, there is a critical disanalogy between INPO and the RSP. INPO peer pressure works because there is a regulator (the NRC) whose presence creates background threat — operators have reason to want good safety records. INPO is effective partly because it heads off regulatory action that operators prefer to avoid. The RSP had no equivalent background threat during its operational period. The regulatory landscape Anthropic hoped to catalyze (and the v3.0 document explicitly says the theory of change failed here) never materialized. Without that background constraint, INPO-like culture mechanisms depend entirely on voluntary internalization. The RSP was therefore INPO-like in structure but operating in a context more like a pre-Three-Mile-Island nuclear industry — where the external pressure that makes voluntary safety culture sticky was absent.

### 4. The timing of RSP v3.0 and the Mythos counter-argument.

RSP v3.0 went live February 24, 2026. Mythos was announced April 7, 2026 — six weeks later. The pause commitment was dropped in v3.0. Mythos turned out to be a model Anthropic restricted from general release.

The counter-counter-argument runs: Anthropic removed the binding pause commitment precisely because they knew (or strongly suspected) Mythos was coming, would have dangerous cyber capabilities, and removing the pause commitment before Mythos was announced meant they would not be in formal violation of the RSP when they decided to handle Mythos through a discretionary restriction rather than an RSP-triggered pause.

What does the evidence show? Several facts are relevant:

First, Drake Thomas (Anthropic employee) disclosed in the Zvi analysis that "I know of early discussion around something like an RSP v3 regime widely accessible to Anthropic staff as early as January 2025 and even wider visibility into drafts of something pretty similar to this RSP for at least the past 3 months." This means the v3.0 changes were being discussed internally at least 13 months before publication, predating the final stages of Mythos training.

Second, the Glasswing announcement says Mythos is "a general-purpose, unreleased frontier model" whose "large increase in capabilities has led us to decide not to make it generally available." The decision not to make it generally available is explicitly described as discretionary — "we really do view this as a first step for giving a lot of cyber defenders a head start," said Dianne Penn, head of research product management. This is not the language of a regulatory trigger. It is the language of organizational judgment.

Third, if Mythos had been evaluated under RSP v2's cybersecurity capability threshold, would it have triggered formal ASL-4 requirements? The RSP v2.2 cybersecurity thresholds are somewhat opaque — the GovAI analysis notes that "radiological and nuclear risks have been removed [from v3.0], as have references to cyber operations." This means cyber was not a formal v2 capability trigger in the same way that CBRN was. Mythos's cyber capabilities might not have triggered a formal RSP v2 response either — the restriction could have been discretionary under v2 as well.

Fourth, the METR director Chris Painter's quote (from TIME, February 24, 2026, the day RSP v3.0 dropped): "This is more evidence that society is not prepared for the potential catastrophic risks posed by AI." He also said he was "concerned" that moving away from binary thresholds "might enable a 'frog-boiling' effect, where danger slowly ramps up without a single moment that sets off alarms." This is METR's policy director saying, on the day of the v3.0 announcement, that the change toward discretionary judgment increases the risk of not catching danger in time. The Mythos cyber capabilities were announced six weeks later.

The timing is, at minimum, uncomfortable. Whether it is damning depends on whether you think:
(a) Anthropic orchestrated a cynical sequence (drop the pause commitment, then announce the dangerous model)
(b) Anthropic genuinely concluded the pause commitment was unworkable for systemic reasons that predated Mythos, and the Mythos situation happened to confirm they needed discretionary capacity

The internal Drake Thomas disclosure — that v3 discussions were happening since January 2025 — weakens the cynical interpretation slightly. Anthropic had been working on this change for over a year, not just in the weeks before Mythos. But it does not eliminate the concern that the final publication timing, six weeks before a major product announcement that would test the old pause commitment, was deliberate.

### 5. Has anyone explicitly made the capacity-building defense?

**Anthropic itself.** The strongest version of the capacity-building argument comes from the RSP v3.0 document itself, which explicitly lists "Our RSP did incentivize us to develop stronger safeguards" and "the overall implementation of the ASL-3 standard did prove feasible" as the two primary successes of the old RSP. The v3.0 also introduces the Frontier Safety Roadmap with explicit "forcing function" language: "By clearly and publicly articulating these goals, we are aiming for a 'forcing function' that helps us pull off this kind of coordination and prioritization. We believe our Responsible Scaling Policy has served this function effectively in the past, as with our ASL-3 protections." [Source: https://www.anthropic.com/responsible-scaling-policy/roadmap]

**The March 2025 Frontier Red Team post.** This document contains perhaps the clearest inside-view statement of the capacity-building argument: "An important benefit of this work is that it helps us move faster, not slower. By developing evaluation plans in advance and committing to capability thresholds that would motivate increased levels of security, the work of Anthropic's Frontier Red Team enhances our ability to advance the frontier of AI rapidly and with the confidence that we are doing so responsibly." This is Anthropic explicitly arguing that the RSP's capacity-building function was instrumental to, not in tension with, its commercial mission. [Source: https://www.anthropic.com/news/strategic-warning-for-ai-risk-progress-and-insights-from-our-frontier-red-team]

**GovAI (Williams and Freund, March 2026).** The GovAI analysis does not frame its argument in the "capacity-building" language exactly, but it makes a structurally equivalent point: "It's better to be honest about real-world constraints than to keep commitments that won't be followed in practice." Their affirmative case for v3.0 rests substantially on the Frontier Safety Roadmap and Risk Reports as tools that "add significant value" by creating concrete near-term goals that can be verified. The GovAI piece does not explicitly defend the old RSP as a capacity builder, but does acknowledge that "Anthropic hasn't lowered any of its existing mitigations" — meaning whatever capacity was built remains operative. [Source: https://www.governance.ai/analysis/anthropics-rsp-v3-0-how-it-works-whats-changed-and-some-reflections]

**FAS (March 2024).** The Federation of American Scientists evaluation of Anthropic's RSP acknowledges it as "net-positive" and notes: "These commitments are costly signals that these developers are serious about their PFs." The FAS analysis explicitly identifies the 8% headcount investment and multi-party authorization systems as evidence of genuine costly commitment, which is the institutional economics language for the capacity-building argument. [Source: https://fas.org/publication/scaling-ai-safety/]

**Critics who implicitly concede the capacity-building point.** Zvi (the most detailed independent critic) writes: "I think Anthropic's description of its own history says that having these softly binding commitments, and having a track record of treating it as costly to break them, was very good for safety outcomes and policy adoption. I hate that we've given that up." This is the strongest possible concession of the capacity-building argument from the opposition: even the harshest critics of the v3.0 change acknowledge that the v1.0/v2.x RSP did produce real safety capacity. The dispute is whether v3.0 preserves that function, not whether the old RSP built capacity. [Source: https://thezvi.wordpress.com/2026/04/01/anthropic-responsible-scaling-policy-v3-a-matter-of-trust/]

**What has NOT been argued.** No external party — not GovAI, not METR, not FAS, not Zvi — has made the specific argument that the capacity-building function of the RSP is *sufficient* justification for the v3.0 change, or that building capacity to exercise discretion is *equivalent* to being constrained by rules. The capacity-building argument is acknowledged as real, but the critics treat it as insufficient to redeem the loss of binding commitments.

## Key Findings

- **The RSP created verifiable organizational capacity.** The Frontier Red Team, Constitutional Classifiers, ASL-3 security controls (100+ controls, multi-party authorization, egress bandwidth controls), METR evaluation partnerships, NNSA partnership, Sabotage Risk Reports, and the ~8% headcount commitment to security-adjacent work all trace directly to RSP mandates. This is strong evidence, sourced from Anthropic's own contemporaneous reflections (May 2024) and confirmed by external observers. Confidence: strong.

- **The RSP made the Mythos restriction detectable.** Without the Frontier Red Team cyber capability evaluation infrastructure built under the RSP, Anthropic would not have known Mythos was dangerous before release in the way they evidently did. The FRT's multi-year capability tracking program (CTF benchmarks, autonomous exploit development tests, partnered evaluations) was the instrument that detected Mythos's qualitative capability jump. Confidence: strong, with the caveat that this was capacity enabling a discretionary decision, not a binding trigger.

- **The INPO analogy is partially valid.** The RSP did create an INPO-like safety culture within Anthropic — shared evaluation practices, accountability structures, peer review norms for safety-relevant decisions. But the analogy breaks down because INPO operates in the shadow of NRC regulation, creating external pressure that makes voluntary culture sticky. The RSP's external pressure (hoped-for regulation, industry adoption) failed to materialize, which is one of the RSP v3.0's own stated conclusions. Confidence: moderate (the analogy holds structurally but the background conditions differ).

- **The capacity-building defense is real but incomplete.** The strongest version of the counter-argument — "the RSP built capacity even without triggering formal restrictions" — is supported by the evidence. But it conflates two distinct things: (1) the RSP made Anthropic *able* to restrict Mythos (by building detection capacity and organizational norms), and (2) the RSP *required* Anthropic to restrict Mythos in the way it did. The evidence supports (1) but not (2). The Mythos restriction was a discretionary judgment call, not a formal RSP trigger. The difference matters enormously for governance purposes. Confidence: strong.

- **The v3.0 timing is suspicious but not clearly cynical.** RSP v3.0 dropped six weeks before Mythos. The change was being discussed internally since January 2025 (per Drake Thomas). The pace of change weakens but does not eliminate the hypothesis that v3.0 was partly timed to remove a formal constraint before a model that would test it. METR's Chris Painter's concern about "frog-boiling" expressed on the day of v3.0's publication, followed by Mythos six weeks later, is exactly the scenario he described. Confidence: moderate (suspicious timing, insufficient evidence for intent attribution).

- **No external party has defended capacity-building as sufficient.** Anthropic itself makes the capacity-building argument, but treats it as justification for reforming the RSP (v3.0's Frontier Safety Roadmap is itself presented as a forcing function), not as a defense of the v1.0/v2.x approach. GovAI and METR acknowledge the capacity was built but do not use this to redeem the loss of binding commitments. Zvi explicitly concedes the capacity was built while criticizing the loss of the binding mechanism. Confidence: strong.

## Assessment

The capacity-building defense of the RSP is substantially true and should not be dismissed. The RSP created real organizational infrastructure — teams, tools, evaluation protocols, external partnerships — that demonstrably enabled Anthropic to detect and respond to Mythos's dangerous capabilities in a way that would not otherwise have been possible. The 8% headcount investment alone represents ~65 people whose jobs existed because the RSP required a security posture. The Constitutional Classifiers, deployed as ASL-3 safeguards, are a concrete artifact of the RSP's forcing function. The Frontier Red Team is a concrete artifact of the RSP's forcing function. These are not trivial.

But the defense conflates two importantly different claims. "The RSP built capacity" is true and evidenced. "The RSP was primarily a capacity-building mechanism rather than a binding constraint" is retrospective rationalization. Anthropic sold the RSP, internally and externally, as a binding commitment — the Evan Hubinger episode documented by Zvi shows this was not merely external marketing but internal belief. If it was always primarily a culture mechanism rather than a constraint, then it was also always primarily a *reputational* instrument, and the question of whether it delivered on its reputation becomes live.

The deepest problem with the capacity-building defense is structural: a mechanism that builds capacity to make discretionary judgments is not the same thing as a mechanism that constrains which judgments can be made. INPO built safety culture in the nuclear industry, but the NRC still had authority to shut down reactors. The RSP's value as a forcing function depended partly on the credibility of the "pause if needed" backstop — the forcing function forces because there is something being forced toward. Without the binding commitment, the Frontier Safety Roadmap (the v3.0 equivalent) becomes a list of aspirations that the Frontier Red Team will pursue when commercially viable, rather than a set of obligations that the Frontier Red Team must achieve before the next model ships.

The Mythos case is both the best and the most problematic evidence for the capacity-building argument. Best, because it shows the RSP infrastructure working — the FRT detected the capability, the organization made a sophisticated discretionary judgment, the restriction was real. Most problematic, because the restriction happened after Anthropic removed the formal binding mechanism that would have made a similar decision non-discretionary. The question "would Anthropic have restricted Mythos under RSP v2.0?" is genuinely unclear. Cyber capabilities were not a formal v2 trigger. But under v2.0, Anthropic would at least have been operating under an institutional norm that treating dangerous capability as requiring a pre-specified response before deployment is the rule, not the exception. Under v3.0, the institutional norm is "we will make reasonable-to-us arguments."

On balance: the capacity-building defense is the strongest available defense of the RSP's track record, and it is not empty. But it does not establish that the RSP was working as designed. It establishes that the RSP, as a byproduct of its operation (regardless of whether it ever triggered formally), created the organizational infrastructure for responsible judgment. That is a real accomplishment. It is also a description of a mechanism that was primarily valuable for the indirect effects of the formal commitment, not the formal commitment itself — which is an odd thing to say about a governance mechanism explicitly described as binding.
