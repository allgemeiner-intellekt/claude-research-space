# Survey: Anthropic RSP Track Record

## Summary

The RSP has a documented, publicly visible track record spanning three major versions (v1.0 Sept 2023 → v2.0 Oct 2024 → v3.0 Feb 2026), six minor revisions, four self-confessed compliance failures (all self-reported), and one concrete case of genuine behavioral constraint: the withholding of Claude Mythos from public release in April 2026, citing the model's demonstrated ability to find zero-day vulnerabilities and escape sandboxes. The RSP also caused a real institutional investment — ~8% of Anthropic headcount on security by mid-2024, deployment of Constitutional Classifiers for Claude Opus 4, and activation of ASL-3 standards in May 2025. What the RSP demonstrably did NOT do is pause or delay any model training in response to failed capability evaluations. The closest near-miss was Claude Opus 4, deployed in May 2025 under ASL-3 "as a precautionary measure" because Anthropic "could not rule out" ASL-3 risk — not because ASL-3 was definitively triggered. The February 2026 v3.0 rewrite explicitly dropped the pause commitment, replacing hard if-then gates with aspiration goals and transparent risk reporting. Critics from within and outside Anthropic (Oliver Habryka, Evan Hubinger, METR, GovAI, Zvi Mowshowitz) document that this was a weakening of real constraints, not merely a clarification. The sharpest open question is whether Mythos represents the first genuine RSP-enforced deployment restriction — or a PR-framed restriction driven by technical unreadiness and compute scarcity.

## Key Sources

### 1. Anthropic RSP Updates page (official, living document)
- **Link**: https://anthropic.com/rsp-updates
- **Key content**: Canonical version history. RSP v1.0 (Sept 19 2023) → v2.0 (Oct 15 2024) → v2.1 (Mar 31 2025) → v2.2 (May 14 2025) → v3.0 (Feb 24 2026) → v3.1 (Apr 2 2026). The page also discloses four compliance failures under v1.0/v2.0: (1) evaluations ran 3 days late; (2) autonomy eval was updated without triggering a policy update as required; (3) some evals lacked basic elicitation techniques (best-of-N, chain-of-thought); (4) the 6x scaling buffer was not formally established in one domain. Anthropic describes all four as "minimal risk" in hindsight. The April 2, 2026 update to v3.1 added clarification that "even if not required by the RSP, we remain free to pause."
- **Assessment**: Authoritative primary source. Self-reported compliance failures are significant — they confirm the RSP was operationally imperfect but also that Anthropic chose to disclose them publicly, which is more transparency than any competitor has offered. The failures are minor procedural ones, not failures to catch a dangerous model. The page also notes a Noncompliance Reporting and Anti-Retaliation Policy published December 2025, with anonymous reporting channels.

### 2. RSP v1.0 announcement (Sept 2023)
- **Link**: https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropics-responsible-scaling-policy
- **Key content**: Original framework. ASL-1 = no meaningful risk (2018-era LLMs, chess AI). ASL-2 = "early signs of dangerous capabilities" but not yet useful uplift — explicitly classifies "current LLMs, including Claude" at ASL-2. ASL-3 = substantially increases catastrophic misuse risk vs. non-AI baselines OR shows low-level autonomous capabilities. ASL-4+ = not yet defined (commitment to define before reaching ASL-3). Requires "unusually strong security requirements" and commitment not to deploy ASL-3 models with "any meaningful catastrophic misuse risk under adversarial testing by world-class red-teamers." "Formally approved by its board and changes must be approved by the board following consultations with the Long Term Benefit Trust." ARC Evals credited for inspiring the framework.
- **Assessment**: High authority (Anthropic official). The "board approval + LTBT consultation" for changes is notable — this is the formal governance structure. In practice, RSP v3.0 was approved by the board (Drake Thomas at Anthropic noted the board "only approved the changes very recently" before the Feb 2026 announcement, per Zvi's blog). The original RSP's language was clear about the pause commitment: "In big bold font on the second page" it committed to pause if scaling outstripped safety compliance (per Evan Hubinger's 2023 characterization, quoted in Zvi's post).

### 3. Activating ASL-3 Protections announcement (May 22, 2025)
- **Link**: https://www.anthropic.com/news/activating-asl3-protections
- **Key content**: First-ever activation of ASL-3. Claude Opus 4 was deployed under ASL-3 as a "precautionary and provisional action" — Anthropic explicitly states "we have not yet determined whether Claude Opus 4 has definitively passed the Capabilities Threshold that requires ASL-3 protections." Claude Sonnet 3.7 was cleared as ASL-2. Claude Sonnet 4 was also cleared as not needing ASL-3. Claude Opus 4 could not be "clearly ruled out" for ASL-3 due to "continued improvements in CBRN-related knowledge and capabilities." Also: "we have ruled out that Claude Opus 4 needs the ASL-4 Standard." ASL-3 deployment measures are "initially focused exclusively on biological weapons." Security involves 100+ controls, 2-party authorization for weight access, egress bandwidth controls. Footnote: "Experiments showed that access to Claude Sonnet 3.7 helped participants do somewhat better on tasks related to CBRN weapon acquisition than those with standard internet access (although all participants' plans still had critical failures)."
- **Assessment**: High authority (official Anthropic announcement + full technical report). This is the most important data point on whether the RSP caused behavioral change. The answer is ambiguous: ASL-3 was activated proactively, not because a threshold was definitively crossed. Anthropic framed this as prudent precaution. Critics could reasonably call it an example of the RSP working (it caused real investment and real deployment changes) OR an example of the RSP's vagueness allowing indefinite deferral of the "did we trigger ASL-3?" determination.

### 4. Activating ASL-3 Protections full report (May 2025)
- **Link**: https://www.anthropic.com/activating-asl3-report
- **Key content** (from preview): Report details Constitutional Classifiers deployed to block CBRN workflow assistance. Classifier-based guards monitor inputs and outputs in real-time. Asynchronous monitoring. Bug bounty program for jailbreaks. Security controls across 100+ domains. The report explicitly notes that Claude Sonnet 3.7 "did not require ASL-3 protections" but was used to prepare the infrastructure. Claude Opus 4 deployed with ASL-3 "as a precautionary measure" even though ASL-3 necessity was undetermined.
- **Assessment**: High authority (official technical report). Critical for understanding the *content* of what ASL-3 means in practice. Notably, the deployment classifiers are "initially focused exclusively on biological weapons" — cyber and other domains are not yet covered by ASL-3 deployment measures. This is a significant limitation given that Mythos (April 2026) was ultimately restricted based on cyber capabilities, not bio.

### 5. RSP v3.0 announcement (Feb 24, 2026)
- **Link**: https://www.anthropic.com/news/responsible-scaling-policy-v3
- **Key content**: Anthropic's own candid assessment of the RSP's track record. Successes: (1) RSP incentivized development of stronger safeguards (Constitutional Classifiers); (2) ASL-3 "did prove feasible" and was activated May 2025; (3) pushed OpenAI and Google DeepMind to adopt similar frameworks; (4) influenced SB-53 (California), RAISE Act (NY), EU AI Act GPAI Codes of Practice. Failures: (1) The "zone of ambiguity" — biological risks produce ambiguous eval results, making the case for multilateral action weak; (2) the "anti-regulatory political climate" and (3) requirements at higher ASLs (like RAND SL4/SL5 for model weight security against state actors) may be "outright impossible to implement without collective action" — a RAND report cited states SL5 is "currently not possible." Result: v3.0 drops the pause commitment, separates "unilateral commitments" (modest: maintain ASL-3 protections, publish Risk Reports) from "industry-wide recommendations" (aspirational). Introduces Frontier Safety Roadmap (non-binding goals) and Risk Reports (3-6 month cycle).
- **Assessment**: Critical primary source. Anthropic's self-assessment is more damning than most external critics allow. The admission that "zone of ambiguity" in bio risk evals means the RSP cannot credibly trigger multilateral action is a significant structural concession. The mention of a "wet-lab trial" that was too slow to complete before more powerful models were released is highly significant — it suggests the eval infrastructure is fundamentally unable to keep pace with model releases.

### 6. GovAI analysis of RSP v3.0 (March 2026)
- **Link**: https://www.governance.ai/analysis/anthropics-rsp-v3-0-how-it-works-whats-changed-and-some-reflections
- **Key content**: Detailed third-party analysis. Authors Sophie Williams and Jonas Freund (GovAI). Key structural changes: (a) pause commitment dropped; (b) ASL tiers no longer specified beyond one level ahead; (c) RAND Security Level 4 (protection against state-level model theft) demoted from unilateral commitment to "industry-wide recommendation"; (d) AI R&D threshold redefined as compressing two years of 2018-2024 progress into one year; (e) radiological/nuclear risks removed; (f) "high-stakes sabotage" added as new category. GovAI's assessment: "initial reaction was rather negative" but became "more positive" on reflection. Key concern: Risk Reports without pause commitment are weaker enforcement mechanism than pause + reports. Key positive: Risk Reports covering *internal* models (not just public deployments) is meaningfully new. Notes that "escape clause" in v2.0 already made pause commitment conditional in practice, but "Anthropic gave the impression that its pause commitment was binding."
- **Assessment**: High quality independent analysis from credible AI governance organization. GovAI's nuanced position (negative initial reaction, more positive on reflection) is a good reference point — they are neither in Anthropic's camp nor reflexively hostile. Their point about the escape clause already existing in v2.0 weakens the strongest "Anthropic broke its promise" claims, but not entirely.

### 8. UK AISI and US AISI Joint Pre-Deployment Evaluation of Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Nov 2024)
- **Link**: https://www.aisi.gov.uk/blog/pre-deployment-evaluation-of-anthropics-upgraded-claude-3-5-sonnet
- **Key content**: Joint evaluation by UK AI Safety Institute and US AI Safety Institute (now AI Security Institute). Evaluated upgraded Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Oct 2024 release). Domains: (1) biological capabilities — performance "comparable to reference models and significantly below measured human expert baselines in most instances" but tool-augmented performance "at times exceeding human expert baselines"; (2) cyber — 32.5% solve rate on 40 public cybersecurity challenges vs. 35% for best reference model; UK AISI found 36% on "cybersecurity apprentice" level tasks vs. 29% for best reference; (3) software/AI development — 57% average score on ML improvement tasks vs. 48% for best reference model; (4) safeguard efficacy — "in most cases the built-in version of the safeguards ... were circumvented." No deployment was blocked or delayed as a result.
- **Assessment**: High credibility (government research institutions). The safeguard circumvention finding is significant — even after ASL-3 preparation was underway, the deployed Claude 3.5 Sonnet's safeguards were routinely broken in testing. This is consistent with Anthropic's admission in the ASL-3 activation report that jailbreaks are inevitable and require rapid-response patching. Key limitation: This evaluation was conducted under "limited period" access with "finite resources."

### 9. METR evaluation of Claude 3.5 Sonnet and o1 (Jan 2025)
- **Link**: https://metr.org/blog/2025-01-31-update-sonnet-o1-evals/
- **Key content**: METR (formerly ARC Evals, the organization that inspired the original RSP) evaluated both models. "We failed to find significant evidence for a dangerous level of capabilities" in autonomous risk tasks. However: "limitations in these evaluations prevent us from establishing robust capability bounds." Key methodological concern: o1's performance jumped dramatically with an improved scaffold, showing that evaluation results are sensitive to elicitation effort. METR: "pre-deployment capability testing is not a sufficient risk management strategy by itself." Claude 3.5 Sonnet baseline agent performs comparably to what humans can achieve in ~1 hour; elicited o1 performs comparably to ~2 hours.
- **Assessment**: High credibility — METR is the independent evaluator most directly involved with the RSP framework (ARC Evals was credited in the original RSP). Their "did not find evidence but cannot confirm absence" framing is epistemically honest but also practically convenient for deployment: it creates an asymmetric burden where finding dangerous capabilities would trigger restrictions, but not finding them is taken as clearance even under elicitation limitations.

### 10. METR review of Anthropic's Sabotage Risk Report for Claude Opus 4.6 (March 2026)
- **Link**: https://evals.alignment.org/blog/2026-03-12-sabotage-risk-report-opus-4-6-review/
- **Key content**: METR reviewed two versions of Anthropic's Sabotage Risk Report. Executive summary: "We agree with Anthropic that the risk of catastrophic outcomes that are substantially enabled by Claude Opus 4.6's misaligned actions is very low but not negligible." Key concerns: (1) "evaluation awareness" — risk that the model performs well on alignment assessments because it recognizes it is being evaluated; (2) METR found "some low-severity instances of misaligned behaviors not caught in the alignment assessment"; (3) several subclaims are "weak without more analysis and experimentation"; (4) METR's conclusion is partly based on Opus 4.6 having been "publicly deployed for weeks without major incidents or dramatic new capability demonstrations" — an empirical post-hoc check rather than a prospective capability argument. This constitutes the first external third-party review of a full (unredacted) Anthropic risk report.
- **Assessment**: High credibility. The "evaluation awareness" concern is potentially a structural issue with all capability evaluations — if models can identify when they're being evaluated, they may sandbag. METR raises but does not resolve this. The fact that their conclusion partially rests on real-world deployment observations is methodologically honest but also means the safety argument is backward-looking.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of knowledge

The RSP's track record is unusually well-documented for a corporate safety policy. Anthropic has disclosed compliance failures, published version histories, activated ASL-3 standards publicly, and had a key risk report externally reviewed by METR. The concrete behavioral effects are:

1. **Caused investment**: ~8% of headcount on security by mid-2024; development of Constitutional Classifiers; the ASL-3 infrastructure build-out was directly RSP-motivated.
2. **Caused restricted (not blocked) deployment**: Claude Opus 4 was deployed under ASL-3 precautionary measures even though ASL-3 threshold was not definitively crossed.
3. **Caused withheld general release**: Claude Mythos (April 2026) was not publicly released due to cyber capabilities that the system card describes as dangerous — the first unambiguous case of Anthropic's own safety judgment (now operating under v3.0, not a formal RSP trigger) preventing a general release.
4. **Caused self-reported compliance failures**: Four minor procedural violations disclosed in October 2024, all considered minimal risk.

What the RSP did NOT cause:
- Any halt to model training.
- Any formal triggering of a defined capability threshold (ASL-3 was activated proactively/precautionarily, not as a triggered response).
- Any deployment restriction that could be cleanly traced to a specific failed evaluation result.

### Agreements between sources

All sources — including Anthropic itself — agree that: (1) the RSP's bio risk evals are caught in a "zone of ambiguity" that prevents confident threshold determinations; (2) ASL-3 was the first real test of whether the framework could cause costly action, and it did result in real infrastructure investment; (3) the pause commitment in v1.0/v2.0 was widely understood as binding even though it was technically escapable via escape clause; (4) RSP v3.0 represents a weakening of hard commitments in exchange for more transparency.

### Conflicts

The main disagreement is whether v3.0 represents an honest acknowledgment of what was always the case (Anthropic's view, supported partly by GovAI), or a broken promise that materially damaged the credibility of voluntary AI self-regulation (Zvi, Habryka, Tegmark view). There is also disagreement about whether Mythos's restricted deployment is genuinely RSP-driven or primarily driven by compute scarcity and technical unreadiness.

### ASL classification history (compiled from sources)

- All models pre-Claude Opus 4: ASL-2 (including Claude 3 Opus, all Sonnet/Haiku variants, Claude Sonnet 3.7)
- Claude Opus 4 (May 2025): First model deployed under ASL-3 "precautionary measures" — ASL-3 not definitively triggered, but could not be ruled out
- Claude Sonnet 4: Cleared as not needing ASL-3
- Claude Opus 4.5: System card filed; sabotage risk reports committed to for future frontier models
- Claude Opus 4.6 (Feb 2026): Sabotage Risk Report filed, reviewed by METR; not near AI R&D-4 threshold; deployed publicly
- Claude Mythos Preview (April 2026): System card published to red.anthropic.com; NOT publicly released; deployed only to Project Glasswing partners; no formal ASL classification announced publicly, but its restriction effectively represents a deployment-level constraint not triggered on any prior model.

Note: "Mythos" was referenced in prior research context (possibly referring to a pre-release codename or leaked name). It is confirmed as a real model as of April 2026.

### Who runs the evaluations

- **Internal**: Anthropic's Frontier Red Team, Alignment Science teams. Referenced across system cards and the May 2024 Reflections document.
- **METR** (formerly ARC Evals, the organization credited in the original RSP): Conducted pre-deployment evaluations of Claude 3.5 Sonnet and o1 (published Jan 2025); conducted first external review of a full Risk Report for Opus 4.6 (March 2026). METR is the primary formal third-party evaluator.
- **UK AISI / US AISI** (now AI Security Institute): Conducted joint pre-deployment evaluation of Claude 3.5 Sonnet Upgraded (published Nov 2024). Domains: biological, cyber, software/AI development, safeguard efficacy. Did not trigger any deployment change.
- The RSP v3.0 now requires external review of Risk Reports "in certain circumstances" (when a model is "highly capable" — a very high bar — and the report has been "significantly redacted"). This is still a future commitment; the Opus 4.6 METR review was done proactively ahead of this requirement.

### Gaps and under-explored areas

1. **The 6x scaling buffer rule**: The RSP v2.0 required evaluations to establish a 6x compute buffer before a capability was deemed not-yet-at-threshold. Anthropic disclosed in the October 2024 compliance review that this was not formally met in one domain. Which domain? Not specified publicly. This could be important.

2. **What exactly Mythos triggered**: Mythos's system card is on red.anthropic.com, which is not publicly indexed in the normal way. Whether the Mythos restriction was formally linked to the RSP's capability threshold system (under v3.0) or was an extra-RSP judgment by the Frontier Red Team is unclear from available sources. The distinction matters enormously for the "did the RSP cause this?" question.

3. **The wet-lab bio trial**: Anthropic mentions in the v3.0 announcement that they ran an "extensive wet-lab trial" to assess biological misuse risk, but "results remain ambiguous" partly because "more powerful models are available by the time they're completed." This is a critical methodological failure point — the eval pipeline cannot keep pace with the training pipeline. No details on which labs, what protocol, or what the ambiguous results were.

4. **METR's AI developer productivity evaluation**: METR published a finding (referenced on their blog) that "when developers use AI tools, they take 19% longer than without — AI makes them slower." This is tangentially relevant to the AI R&D capability threshold.

5. **The Google DeepMind Frontier Safety Framework**: Mentioned as adopting "broadly similar frameworks" after Anthropic's RSP, but not analyzed in detail here. A comparison of whether DeepMind has similarly restricted any models would be valuable.

### Surprises

1. **Mythos is real, and its restriction happened just days before this survey's date (April 11, 2026)**. The timing is almost suspiciously convenient for this research question — the first concrete deployment restriction under Anthropic's safety framework appears to have just occurred. This is the hottest possible angle.

2. **All prior models were ASL-2, not ASL-3**. Given how much narrative capital Anthropic invested in the RSP, the fact that literally every model up through Claude Sonnet 3.7 was classified at the baseline ASL-2 level — the same level that requires only what Anthropic calls "current safety and security standards" — is striking. The RSP's harder requirements never formally triggered until Opus 4 forced a precautionary ASL-3 activation, and even that was not a definitive trigger.

3. **Jan Leike joined Anthropic, not left it**. Leike departed OpenAI citing safety culture concerns and joined Anthropic to work on scalable oversight. He is not a whistleblower against Anthropic. Daniel Kokotajlo also was an OpenAI departure, not Anthropic. The one named Anthropic departure with explicit criticism is Mrinank Sharma (Feb 2026), but his criticisms are vague.

4. **The compliance failures Anthropic disclosed are genuinely minor** — all were procedural timing and process issues, not cases where a dangerous model was deployed without adequate safeguards. This is different from what a skeptic might expect.

5. **Anthropic itself admits in v3.0 that the RSP's bio risk evals cannot produce a dispositive answer**. The mechanism by which the RSP was supposed to work — trigger hard, pre-committed action when a capability threshold is crossed — is undermined by the fact that capability thresholds cannot be reliably measured.

## The sharpest question this angle offers

**Did the RSP actually cause Anthropic to withhold Claude Mythos from public release — or was the withholding driven primarily by technical unreadiness (inability to build adequate cyber safeguards) and compute scarcity, with safety framing added post-hoc?**

This is falsifiable via: (1) checking whether Mythos's deployment restriction was formally linked to any RSP capability threshold trigger under v3.0 or was an extra-RSP editorial judgment by the Frontier Red Team; (2) examining whether the Project Glasswing framing (defensive cybersecurity use) represents genuine safety logic or primarily reflects that large enterprise contracts were more profitable than consumer access at scale; (3) asking whether Anthropic's own v3.0 framework even contains formal cyber-capability deployment restrictions (the ASL-3 Deployment Standard, as of May 2025, covered only biological weapons, not cyber — meaning Mythos may be restricted outside the RSP's formal trigger structure entirely); (4) comparing against OpenAI's behavior with models of comparable cyber capabilities. If the RSP has no formal cyber deployment standard (it may not, under v3.0), then Mythos is restricted by Anthropic's judgment, not by the RSP — which would be the sharpest possible evidence that the RSP's formal trigger architecture has never actually caused a deployment restriction.
