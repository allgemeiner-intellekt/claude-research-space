# Survey: Anthropic Governance & the Long-Term Benefit Trust (LTBT)

## Summary

The LTBT is a Delaware purpose trust holding Class T Common Stock with voting power to elect a majority of Anthropic's board. It formally achieved its 3-of-5 majority power in November 2024. As of April 2026, the board has expanded to 6 members, raising the immediate question of whether the LTBT's 3-of-5 majority is maintained or diluted. The trust has been exercised in practice: it appointed Jay Kreps (May 2024), Reed Hastings (May 2025), and arguably a third LTBT seat (Liddell, February 2026 — electing authority ambiguous). The trust document has never been published, the supermajority override threshold is disclosed only as "85% of voting power" per a third-party tracker, and Amazon/Google hold non-voting shares that are explicitly excluded from the supermajority calculation — though this claim rests solely on Anthropic general counsel Brian Israel's oral representation to Time magazine. No independent verification of the charter's actual voting structure has been published. Two of five trustee seats were vacant as of March 2026, a quiet opacity that contradicts the trust's governance-as-accountability framing.

## Key Sources

### Anthropic: "The Long-Term Benefit Trust" (Sep 2023)
- **Link**: https://www.anthropic.com/news/the-long-term-benefit-trust
- **Key content**: Primary document describing LTBT structure. Class T stock grants power to elect "a portion of our Board that will grow over time (ultimately, a majority of our Board)." Phase-in is time- and funding-based. Explicitly states the trust can be amended "without the consent of the Trustees if sufficiently large supermajorities of the stockholders agree." Also notes that Series C investors get "a new director seat that will be elected by the Series C and subsequent investors" — confirming at least one perpetual investor-elected seat on the board. Describes failsafe provisions as increasing in threshold over time. Initial five trustees: Jason Matheny (RAND), Kanika Bahl (Evidence Action), Neil Buddy Shah (Clinton Health Access Initiative, Chair), Paul Christiano (Alignment Research Center), Zachary Robinson (Effective Ventures US).
- **Assessment**: Primary source, authoritative on structural intent. Deliberately vague on key enforcement thresholds. No trust agreement published.

### Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance: "Anthropic Long-Term Benefit Trust" (Oct 2023)
- **Link**: https://corpgov.law.harvard.edu/2023/10/28/anthropic-long-term-benefit-trust/
- **Key content**: Written by John Morley (Yale Law), David Berger and Amy Simmerman (Wilson Sonsini) — the lawyers who designed the structure. Confirms the Class T stock is held by the Trust; economic rights of Class T are "very small." Clarifies the amendment mechanism: by Trustees + company; by Trustees + stockholders; by Trustees + directors (before majority phase-in); or by "a supermajority of stockholders" alone (failsafe). Notes the Trust is a purpose trust under Del. Code tit. 12 §3556. Confirms an Administrative Trustee (a Delaware institution) holds narrow administrative powers. Key ambiguity: "the Trust Agreement also authorizes the Trust to be enforced by the company and by groups of the company's stockholders who have held a sufficient percentage of the company's equity for a sufficient period of time" — the authors themselves do not explain what this means for Trustee independence.
- **Assessment**: Highest-credibility legal analysis — authors drafted the document. Still discloses no numerical thresholds. Confirms the core tension: failsafe = stockholder supermajority can override Trustees.

### Time Magazine: "How Anthropic Designed Itself to Avoid OpenAI's Mistakes" (May 2024, updated Jan 2026)
- **Link**: https://time.com/6983420/anthropic-structure-openai-incentives/
- **Key content**: Reports, based on Anthropic's incorporation documents reviewed by Time, that LTBT held 1/5 seats at publication, would rise to 2/5 in July 2024 and 3/5 in November 2024 — i.e., the majority milestone was tied to fundraising milestones already surpassed. Critically: Anthropic GC Brian Israel says Amazon and Google "do not own voting shares in Anthropic," meaning they cannot vote in the supermajority needed to rewrite the LTBT. Confirms this claim but does not reproduce the charter language. Adds nuance: even LTBT-elected directors "owe fiduciary obligations to Anthropic's stockholders," limiting the board's ability to take drastic action against investor interests. Notes that LTBT-elected directors "could probably not pull off an action as drastic as" the OpenAI firing.
- **Assessment**: Best reported piece with document access. Key facts on voting rights rest on GC representation, not published documents. Significant that the reporter had the incorporation documents but does not publish them.

### Longterm Wiki: LTBT Phase Timeline (multiple fact entries)
- **Link**: https://www.longtermwiki.com/factbase/fact/f_ltbt_board_phase2
- **Key content**: Tracks the phase-in timeline: Phase 1 (Sep 2023) = 1 of 5 seats; Phase 2 (Jul 2024) = 2 of 5; Phase 3 (Nov 2024) = 3 of 5 (majority). Amendment threshold disclosed as 85% of voting power. As of March 2026, Trust has 3 of 5 voting trustee seats filled and 2 vacant. Board seats controlled listed as "3 of 5."
- **Assessment**: Non-authoritative third-party tracker, but well-sourced from Anthropic announcements. The 85% amendment threshold figure does not appear in any primary Anthropic document I found — its source needs verification. Two vacant trustee seats as of March 2026 is significant.

## Landscape Assessment

### State of knowledge

The LTBT's formal structure is reasonably well-documented through Anthropic's own releases and the Harvard Law Forum piece by its designers. What remains opaque is enforcement. The trust agreement itself has never been published. The only public claim that Amazon and Google lack votes to reach the supermajority threshold comes from Anthropic's general counsel speaking to a journalist — there is no independent legal analysis or charter excerpt confirming this. The 85% supermajority threshold cited by Longterm Wiki lacks a verifiable primary source. Two of the five trustee seats were vacant as of March 2026, which quietly undermines the trust's functioning capacity.

The LTBT has demonstrably exercised its power: Kreps (May 2024) and Hastings (May 2025) were explicitly announced as LTBT-appointed directors, confirming the mechanism is operational and not a paper construct. The majority milestone (3 of 5) was reached in November 2024 per Time's reporting on Anthropic's incorporation documents. This is the clearest positive evidence the structure has real teeth.

### Agreements across sources

All sources agree: (1) Amazon and Google have significant financial stakes but the structure was designed to exclude them from governance control; (2) LTBT-elected directors nonetheless retain fiduciary duties to stockholders, limiting how aggressively they can act against investor interests; (3) the failsafe/supermajority provision exists and could in principle allow stockholder override; (4) the LTBT cannot directly fire the CEO — only the board can, and only LTBT-appointed directors are accountable to the trust.

### Conflicts and tensions

The central conflict is between Anthropic's marketing of the LTBT as a robust safeguard and the AI Lab Watch / Stein-Perlman critique that the undisclosed supermajority threshold could make the trust structurally subordinate to stockholder interests. Anthropic's GC says Amazon/Google lack votes for a supermajority — if true, this resolves the main concern. But this is an unverified claim, and no one outside the company has read the charter. The Time reporter reviewed incorporation documents but did not publish the relevant sections on voting rights.

A second tension: the LTBT's original EA/longtermist composition (Christiano, Robinson, Matheny) has fully turned over. All three were replaced with national security and governance establishment figures (Fontaine, Cuéllar, with Shah the only original remaining). This is not a betrayal of mission per se — the trust's stated purpose is broad — but it changes the probability distribution of how trustees would vote on hard cases. Christiano would likely veto a contract with DARPA for autonomous weapons AI; Fontaine (CNAS) is institutionally inclined toward defense applications. No public discussion of this ideological shift exists in mainstream coverage.

A third tension: the board expanded from 5 to 6 members (Liddell, Feb 2026) without any public disclosure of whether the LTBT's "3 of 5 majority" translates to "3 of 6" or whether a new seat was added for stockholder election. If the board is now 6 and LTBT holds 3 seats, LTBT has 50% — not a majority. Anthropic's announcement said "elected by stockholders and the Long-Term Benefit Trust" without seat breakdown. The Longterm Wiki still shows "Board Seats Controlled: 3 of 5" without updating to reflect the 6-member board. This is either an unresolved data gap or the board was always contemplated to expand.

### Gaps and under-explored areas

1. **The trust agreement itself.** No journalist, academic, or regulator has published or analyzed the actual text. The entire assessment of the trust's enforceability rests on a) Anthropic's self-descriptions and b) its lawyers' description of their own work.

2. **Yasmin Razavi's seat.** Razavi (Spark Capital, led Series C) sits on the board but no announcement identifies whether her seat is the "investor-elected" Series C seat created by the charter amendment, or a Trustee-elected seat. If it is the permanent Series C investor seat, then the board composition is: 2 founders + 1 investor seat (Razavi) + 3 LTBT seats (Kreps, Hastings, and either Liddell or a to-be-determined third). Liddell's election mechanism is unconfirmed.

3. **Amazon's actual governance rights.** Amazon committed $8B+ and is described as holding non-voting preferred stock. But Amazon's investment agreement almost certainly contains contractual governance rights beyond formal share voting — information rights, approval rights on certain transactions, anti-dilution provisions. These are not public. The FTC and UK CMA have both probed this relationship without concluding that it constitutes de facto control.

4. **The IPO question.** Multiple sources note that investor liquidity provisions have been negotiated in later rounds. An IPO or conversion to public trading would require new Delaware corporate filings and potentially charter amendments. Whether the LTBT's Class T stock survives an IPO — or would be redeemed, converted, or grandfathered — is completely undiscussed in public sources.

5. **Whether the LTBT has ever prevented anything.** All confirmed exercises of LTBT power involve board appointments. There is no public record of the LTBT receiving notice of a "significant action," deliberating on it, and either approving or blocking it. The "advance notice" protective provision has either never been triggered, or was triggered and the outcome was not made public.

### Surprises

The most significant surprise is the board expansion to 6 members simultaneous with the $30B Series G — and the complete silence about how this changes LTBT's majority. Under the original "3 of 5" design, the LTBT is the majority. With 6 members and 3 LTBT seats, it is a tie. Anthropic's charter reportedly contemplated the LTBT eventually electing a "majority" — if the board has grown, either the charter requires LTBT to elect 4 seats on a 6-person board, or the majority provision was not automatically scaling.

The second surprise is how rapidly the trustee composition has shifted to a national-security/Beltway profile. The founding trust included two EA-movement figures (Robinson, Christiano) and was explicitly framed as an experiment in longtermist governance. By March 2026, the trust is chaired by Buddy Shah (Clinton Health Access Initiative) and includes Fontaine (CNAS) and Cuéllar (Carnegie Endowment) — profiles better suited to advising on geopolitics and diplomacy than adjudicating AI safety trade-offs.

The third surprise is the $380B valuation. At $380B, Anthropic is larger than many Fortune 500 companies and attracting institutional capital at a scale that makes the LTBT's governance protections more, not less, economically significant to override. The incentive to neutralize the LTBT grows with valuation.

## The sharpest question this angle offers

**Has the LTBT's formal majority power been diluted below 50% by the expansion of Anthropic's board from 5 to 6 directors (February 2026), and if not, which of the six current directors was elected by the LTBT as its third board seat?**

This question is falsifiable: Anthropic's certificate of incorporation specifies either (a) the LTBT elects a fixed number (3) of directors regardless of board size, or (b) it elects a majority fraction that scales with the board. If (a), a 6-person board means the LTBT now holds exactly 50% — not a majority — silently reversing the governance design without any public announcement. If (b), the LTBT must elect 4 of 6, and one of the current six directors must be a fourth LTBT appointee not yet publicly acknowledged. Either answer is revealing: (a) would mean the $30B Series G round was used to structurally weaken the trust's control via a quiet board expansion; (b) would mean Anthropic has a fourth LTBT-appointed director whose election was never publicly announced. Answering this requires obtaining Anthropic's certificate of incorporation or getting a direct statement from Anthropic specifying which seat Liddell occupies and what happens to LTBT's percentage on a 6-person board — information Anthropic has conspicuously failed to volunteer.
