# Review -- Round 1

## Overall Assessment

The investigation is strong. The six notes collectively build a coherent, well-sourced argument that the RSP's formal trigger architecture has never caused a deployment restriction, and that Mythos was restricted by discretionary judgment rather than a mechanistic safety gate. The theoretical framing (credible commitment, Crawford-Sobel, Kydland-Prescott, INPO/Responsible Care comparisons) is genuinely sophisticated and well-matched to the question. The Mythos deep dive is the standout piece -- it identifies and weighs four competing explanations (RSP mechanism, compute scarcity, commercial strategy, technical unreadiness) with appropriate uncertainty. The material is sufficient for a strong report. There are two specific gaps that, if filled, would elevate the essay from "very good analysis" to "genuinely novel argument." Both are tractable.

## Per-Question Evaluation

### Has the RSP's formal mechanism ever caused a deployment restriction?

- **Coverage**: Excellent. The survey traces every model from ASL-2 through Opus 4's precautionary ASL-3 to Mythos. The deep dive on Mythos motives establishes that RSP v3 is discretionary, not binding. The critical insight -- that the ASL-3 Deployment Standard covers only biological weapons, not cyber, meaning Mythos's restriction for cyber capabilities is formally extra-RSP -- is well-sourced and appears in both the survey and the deep dive.
- **Strengths**: Primary source documentation is thorough (RSP v1-v3 texts, Anthropic announcements, GovAI analysis, METR reviews, system cards). The four-hypothesis structure in the Mythos deep dive is analytically clean. The compute cost evidence ($25/$125 pricing, leaked blog language) is concrete. The commercial logic of Glasswing is well-documented through Silicon Report and TechCrunch.
- **Gaps**: One significant gap remains. The notes establish that ASL-3's deployment standard covers only bio, not cyber. But they do not confirm whether RSP v3.0 introduced any new capability thresholds or deployment standards for cybersecurity specifically. The v3.0 announcement mentions "high-stakes sabotage" as a new risk category. Does this cover cyber exploitation? If v3.0 added a cyber-relevant threshold that Mythos plausibly triggered, the "formally extra-RSP" conclusion weakens. If v3.0's sabotage category is about AI-on-AI sabotage (alignment concern) rather than offensive cyber, the conclusion holds. This distinction needs to be nailed down from the v3.0 text itself.

### Was the Mythos withholding driven by the RSP's trigger architecture or ad hoc judgment?

- **Coverage**: Strong. The deep dive concludes the RSP provided "legitimizing vocabulary" rather than a causal mechanism. The evidence supports this: no ASL level is cited as triggered, no specific threshold is referenced, and the Frontier Red Team's framing is qualitative ("due to its cybersecurity capabilities") rather than referencing a formal evaluation result.
- **Strengths**: The finding that "Mythos would have been restricted even without the RSP" due to compute cost, technical unreadiness, and enterprise strategy is the essay's sharpest claim and is well-supported. The system card's own alarming content (sandbox escape, concealment features, credential escalation) establishes that the safety concerns are real even if the RSP didn't formally cause the restriction.
- **Gaps**: The notes do not adequately address one important counter-argument: that the RSP created the institutional infrastructure (Frontier Red Team, evaluation protocols, system card norms, Risk Report framework) that enabled the Mythos restriction to happen at all. A sophisticated defender of the RSP would argue that the relevant question is not "did a bright-line rule fire?" but "did the RSP's institutional apparatus produce the information and organizational capacity that led to the restriction?" This is the difference between the RSP as a rule-based mechanism and the RSP as an organizational culture intervention. The notes treat only the rule-based reading. The culture-intervention reading is the strongest counter-argument and needs to be engaged with, even if ultimately the essay argues the distinction matters.

### Is the RSP a credible commitment device or elaborate cheap talk?

- **Coverage**: Excellent. The self-regulation theory deep dive is the strongest piece in the collection. The four-criteria framework (costly signaling, external enforcement, reputation stakes, modification foreclosed) is applied rigorously. The five industry comparisons (Responsible Care, INPO, Basel, platform T&S, pharma) are well-chosen and well-sourced. The mechanism design taxonomy (constitution/contract/norm/cheap talk) is analytically precise.
- **Strengths**: The INPO comparison is the essay's most powerful analytical move -- it shows exactly what effective voluntary safety regulation looks like (joint liability, insurance pricing, real sanctions, inspectors with skin in the game) and demonstrates the RSP has none of these features. The King and Lenox finding on Responsible Care (zero effect without verification, 1,636 citations) is a devastating comparator. The Pentagon standoff analysis as a Spence-Fearon costly signal is genuinely novel.
- **Gaps**: The notes are weakest on the "race to the top" question. The self-regulation deep dive mentions Anthropic's v3.0 claim that the RSP influenced OpenAI, DeepMind, SB-53, and the EU AI Act. But it doesn't evaluate this claim. If Anthropic's RSP genuinely caused competitors to adopt similar frameworks (even weak ones), that is a real externality that complicates the "cheap talk" verdict. The race-to-top survey agent failed, and this angle was never recovered. It is not essential -- the essay can note it as a claimed externality without deep investigation -- but the current notes leave this as an unexamined Anthropic talking point.

## Deep-Dive Assignments

### Assignment: RSP v3.0 Cyber Threshold Verification

- **Question to answer**: Does RSP v3.0 (or the associated Frontier Safety Roadmap) define any deployment standard, capability threshold, or risk category that formally covers offensive cybersecurity capabilities? Specifically: does the "high-stakes sabotage" category added in v3.0 encompass cyber exploitation, or is it limited to AI-on-AI alignment concerns? And does the Mythos system card reference any specific RSP threshold as having been crossed?
- **Why it matters**: The essay's central empirical claim is that the RSP's formal mechanism never caused a deployment restriction. If v3.0 introduced a cyber-relevant threshold that Mythos plausibly triggered, this claim is wrong. If it didn't, the claim is airtight. This is the load-bearing factual question and it must be verified from the actual RSP v3.0 text and the Mythos system card, not from secondary commentary.
- **Where to start**: The RSP v3.0 full text at anthropic.com/news/responsible-scaling-policy-v3; the Mythos system card at red.anthropic.com/2026/mythos-preview/; the GovAI analysis which lists new risk categories. Focus on whether "high-stakes sabotage" or any other v3.0 category covers offensive cyber exploitation by the model itself (as opposed to misuse by users for cyber attacks, which is a different risk category).

### Assignment: The RSP as Organizational Infrastructure (Strongest Counter-Argument)

- **Question to answer**: Is the strongest defense of the RSP not that it works as a trigger mechanism, but that it created the institutional apparatus -- Frontier Red Team, evaluation protocols, system card norms, Risk Report framework, the 8% headcount investment -- that enabled the Mythos restriction? In other words: even if no bright-line rule fired, would the restriction have happened without the organizational infrastructure the RSP built? What does the credible commitment literature say about the distinction between rules-as-mechanisms and rules-as-organizational-culture?
- **Why it matters**: The essay currently argues the RSP is between "elaborate cheap talk" and "early-stage norm." A sophisticated reader will object: "You're judging the RSP as a rule system and finding it fails as a rule system. But maybe it succeeds as an organizational capacity-builder -- it created the teams, processes, and norms that produced the Mythos restriction, even though no specific rule triggered." This is the steelman. The essay must engage with it to be persuasive to readers who are not already RSP-skeptics.
- **Where to start**: The May 2024 "Reflections on our RSP" document (8% headcount, Responsible Scaling Team, evaluation infrastructure); the Brundage et al. auditing paper on organizational vs. rule-based safety governance; the INPO comparison (INPO works partly through organizational culture change, not just sanctions). The question is whether organizational capacity-building that can be defunded or redirected at the builder's discretion counts as a "credible commitment" in the institutional economics sense, or whether it's a different kind of thing entirely.

These two deep dives address the strongest factual vulnerability (does v3.0 have a cyber threshold?) and the strongest argumentative vulnerability (the RSP-as-infrastructure steelman).
